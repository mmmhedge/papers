# Proactive Inpatient Bed Requests for Emergency Department Admissions

**Authors:** QIan Cheng, Nilay Tanik Argon, Aniruddhan Ganesaraman, Serhan Ziya
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.15432v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15432v1)

## Summary
This paper tackles emergency department (ED) boarding — the costly problem where admitted patients wait in the ED for inpatient beds — by proposing a proactive bed-request system. Using machine learning to predict admission probability and disposition time, the authors formulate the problem as a decision process and compare three approaches (dynamic programming, reinforcement learning, and a newsvendor heuristic). Simulations show 30–70% reductions in boarding time with modest tradeoffs in bed idle time.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two main streams of work. First, it uses **Markov decision processes** (MDPs) — mathematical frameworks for sequential decision-making under uncertainty — to model the ED's bed-allocation problem. Second, it draws on **approximate dynamic programming** and **reinforcement learning** (learning optimal policies by trial-and-error interaction with an environment) as solution methods. The newsvendor approach is a classical heuristic from operations research for inventory problems: deciding how much stock to hold when demand is uncertain. The work also relies on **predictive analytics** — using historical data to forecast patient admission likelihood and time to discharge — which is now standard in healthcare operations.

## Core Contributions
1. Formulates ED bed allocation as an MDP that aggregates individual patient predictions into a single proactive bed-request decision.
2. Proposes and compares three concrete policies: an approximate dynamic programming solution, a reinforcement learning agent, and a lightweight newsvendor-style heuristic.
3. Demonstrates via realistic simulation (calibrated on real ED data) that proactive requests achieve 30–70% reductions in admitted-patient boarding time and 6–15% reductions in overall ED length of stay.
4. Shows that the simple newsvendor heuristic often outperforms more complex methods on the practical tradeoff between ED delays and bed idle time — a valuable meta-insight about when simpler methods should be preferred.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of **[[reinforcement-learning]]** applications in healthcare operations and classical **[[optimization]]** in hospital management. It contrasts reinforcement learning with simpler myopic heuristics, echoing broader debates about when complex versus simple policies are appropriate. The use of predictive models to inform operational decisions relates to growing work on data-driven hospital operations (e.g., ED triage, bed management systems). The aggregation of individual predictions into a single control action is akin to optimal stopping and resource-allocation problems in queueing theory.

## Practical Applications
1. **Real-time ED decision support**: Deploy the newsvendor or RL policy in an actual ED to trigger bed requests when the system predicts high admission probability, reducing patient wait times.
2. **Hospital capacity planning**: Use the framework to recommend optimal staffing and bed allocation across inpatient units based on ED demand forecasts.
3. **ED-to-inpatient workflow automation**: Integrate with hospital information systems to automatically notify bed managers and housekeeping when a bed request is triggered.
4. **Performance benchmarking**: Help ED administrators set realistic targets for boarding time reduction and identify bottlenecks.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **ED Bed Orchestration SaaS**: A cloud-based platform that ingests real-time ED patient data, runs RL-based or newsvendor policies, and sends automated bed-request alerts to hospital staff — targeting multi-facility health systems.
2. **Predictive ED Analytics Toolkit**: A hospital analytics product that combines patient admission probability models with this optimization framework, competing with existing ED forecasting tools.
3. **Hospital Operations AI Assistant**: An agent that monitors ED crowding, predicts boarding risk, and recommends proactive bed requests + staffing adjustments in real time.
4. **ED-Inpatient Coordination Marketplace**: A platform matching ED patients awaiting beds with available inpatient capacity across partner hospitals, using this optimization logic to minimize system-wide wait times.
5. **Consulting/Optimization Services**: Engage healthcare systems to model and implement tailored bed-request policies (newsvendor vs. RL vs. dynamic programming) based on their specific cost structure and operational goals.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Data requirements**: The approach requires accurate real-time predictions of admission probability and disposition time; if these are poorly calibrated, the policy may request beds too early or too late.
2. **Simulation vs. reality**: Results rely on simulation; real-world implementation may face issues like staff resistance to automated requests, unmeasured bottlenecks in bed turnover, or unpredictable surges.
3. **Aggregation assumption**: The framework aggregates individual patient forecasts into a single bed request. This may lose information about patient severity, acuity, or special requirements that should influence bed allocation.
4. **Myopic horizon**: The MDP likely uses a finite or discounted horizon; very long-term effects (e.g., chronic bed shortages) may not be fully captured.
5. **Stationarity**: The framework assumes patient arrival and disposition processes are stationary; seasonal effects, holidays, or pandemic shocks are not addressed.
6. **Implementation friction**: Even with perfect predictions, staff adoption and process changes can stall if not carefully managed.

## Open Questions
1. How do these policies perform when admission predictions are misspecified or drift over time? Is there a robust adaptation mechanism?
2. Can the framework incorporate patient acuity or bed-type requirements (ICU vs. general ward) rather than treating all beds as interchangeable?
3. How do results generalize across different hospital types (urban vs. rural, small vs. large, teaching vs. non-teaching)?
4. Can the RL agent be trained end-to-end using real hospital data and deployed online, or does simulation-based training suffice?
5. What is the optimal way to communicate bed requests to inpatient units? Does the request pattern (smooth vs. bursty) affect staff behavior or turnover efficiency?
6. How does this framework interact with other ED interventions (e.g., fast-track units, early discharge protocols)?

## Key Takeaway
Predictive proactive bed requests, optimized via simple heuristics or reinforcement learning, can cut ED boarding time by 30–70% with minimal cost to inpatient operations.
