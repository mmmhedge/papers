# To Retain or to Adapt? Generalizing Continual Learning

**Authors:** Giulia Lanzillotta, Mandana Samiei, Doina Precup, Razvan Pascanu, Claire Vernade
**Published:** 2026-07-06 | **arXiv:** [2607.05609v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05609v1)

## Summary
This paper challenges a foundational assumption in continual learning: that lifelong learners should retain all previous knowledge to match a Joint-Task Learning (JTL) solution. Instead, the authors argue that in changing environments, prioritizing retention can actually hurt real-time performance. They formalize this tradeoff through a new metric (Average Lifelong Error) and prove that historical knowledge becomes a liability once task duration exceeds a critical threshold. The work matters because it reframes a 20-year-old problem—most continual learning papers optimize for preventing forgetting—and shows this goal may be misaligned with practical performance.

## Theoretical Background
Continual learning studies how agents learn from a stream of tasks without forgetting earlier ones. The field has been dominated by research on **catastrophic forgetting**—the observation that neural networks rapidly overwrite old knowledge when learning new tasks. Prior work assumes the optimal solution is to approximate **Joint-Task Learning (JTL)**, where you train on all tasks simultaneously. This paper builds on online optimization theory (especially work on predictable sequences) and draws parallels to the stability-plasticity dilemma in neural networks. The authors use tools from optimization theory to decompose learning error into two competing forces: **Instability** (bias from conflicting past experience) and **Transient Error** (the cost of learning a new task from scratch without warm-start).

## Core Contributions
1. **Theoretical reframing**: Shows JTL is not always the right target—retention can be suboptimal. Introduces the Critical Task Duration theorem: a closed-form formula predicting when historical knowledge transitions from helpful to harmful.
2. **Transfer Efficiency decomposition**: Quantifies the tension between Instability and Transient Error, making the retention-vs-adaptation tradeoff explicit and measurable.
3. **Predictive Continual Learning (PCL)**: Proposes a new algorithm class that optimizes for expected future performance under a dynamically updated model of upcoming tasks—not just retention of the past.
4. **Window algorithm**: A concrete PCL instantiation that interpolates between JTL and Independent-Task Learning (ITL), shown to outperform both on vision and RL benchmarks.

## Connections to Other Work
This work directly challenges the implicit objective of most continual learning papers (preventing catastrophic forgetting). It connects to [[online learning]] frameworks and predictable sequences theory from the optimization literature. The stability-plasticity tradeoff echoes classical neural network theory. The move toward task-specific modeling relates to [[meta-learning]] and task-aware approaches. The RL experiments ground the theory in [[reinforcement-learning]] settings where non-stationary task distributions are common. This is complementary to recent work on [[plasticity]] in neural networks—which investigates *why* networks forget—whereas this paper argues we should sometimes *want* forgetting.

## Practical Applications
1. **Robotics in changing environments**: A robot deployed in a warehouse that handles new product types weekly. Instead of retaining all old picking strategies, it should adapt quickly to new items—the Window algorithm predicts when to forget old patterns.
2. **Continual video classification**: A surveillance system monitoring a factory floor that must adapt to new machinery or layouts over months. Real-time detection matters more than perfect recall of rare events from year one.
3. **Adaptive recommendation systems**: E-commerce platforms where user preferences drift. Retention of old preference patterns from months ago actively harms prediction; fresh models outperform memory-based approaches.
4. **Medical imaging with evolving scanners**: Hospitals that upgrade imaging hardware; knowledge from the old machine can confuse new task learning. The Critical Task Duration helps decide when to reset.
5. **Language models in evolving domains**: A model fine-tuned on financial news from 2020 learns domain syntax that becomes stale by 2024; retention impedes adaptation to new market vocabulary.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Continual Learning-as-a-Service platform**: A SaaS tool that automatically measures the Critical Task Duration for users' data streams and recommends whether to retrain from scratch or fine-tune. Subscription for real-time predictions.
2. **Adaptive robotics control layer**: Middleware that sits between robot learning modules and tasks, using the Window algorithm to decide when to reset vs. retain knowledge. Target: industrial automation companies.
3. **Dynamic model selection for non-stationary data**: B2B ML ops tool that monitors dataset drift (e.g., in financial, medical, or e-commerce applications) and selects between continual learning, retraining, and catastrophic forgetting based on predicted task duration.
4. **Benchmark suite for continual learning evaluation**: A hosted testing platform that benchmarks users' CL algorithms against the theoretical predictions and various baselines, helping practitioners choose strategies.
5. **Enterprise transfer learning optimizer**: Cloud service that automatically tunes the retention-adaptation tradeoff for fine-tuning on customer data, optimizing for business KPIs (latency, accuracy, drift resistance) rather than academic metrics.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Convergence assumptions**: The theoretical results (Critical Task Duration) hold under "mild convergence conditions"—the paper doesn't fully specify when these break. Neural network training is not always convex; the guarantees may not transfer to deep learning in practice.
2. **Task stationarity and task boundary detection**: The theory assumes tasks are discrete and their duration is known or predictable. Real environments have continuous drift; the method needs a principled way to detect task boundaries.
3. **Scalability of Predictive CL**: Maintaining and updating a model of future tasks adds computational overhead. On high-dimensional problems (large images, massive RL state spaces) this may be prohibitive.
4. **Limited RL evaluation**: Most experiments are on vision tasks. RL results are lighter; it's unclear how the theory scales to high-dimensional policy learning or multi-agent settings.
5. **Forgetting metric**: Average Lifelong Error (ALE) is new; no comparison to other metrics (e.g., forward/backward transfer). It's unclear if practitioners will adopt it or if it generalizes across domains.

## Open Questions
1. **Adaptive Critical Task Duration**: Can we estimate the Critical Task Duration online, without prior knowledge of task statistics? The closed-form formula requires knowing bias and optimization rates.
2. **Multi-task interactions**: What happens when tasks are not independent? If task N+1 shares structure with task M (M << N), should we selectively retain M's knowledge? How does the theory extend?
3. **Task boundary detection**: In a continuous data stream (no explicit task labels), how do we reliably detect task switches to apply the Window algorithm?
4. **Scaling to transformers**: Do the theoretical predictions hold for large language models or vision transformers? The paper tests on smaller networks; modern deep learning may have different dynamics.
5. **Integration with other CL approaches**: Can Predictive CL be combined with replay-based or regularization-based continual learning methods? Or are they fundamentally incompatible?

## Key Takeaway
Retention of all past knowledge is not always optimal in continual learning—there exists a task-duration threshold beyond which old knowledge hurts future performance, and the best strategy is to selectively forget and adapt.
