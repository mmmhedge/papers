# NeuralChaos: Optimal Adapted Approximation of Square Integrable Predictable Processes

**Authors:** Anastasis Kratsios, Giulia Livieri, Philipp Schmocker
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.14361v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14361v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces NeuralChaos, a neural network architecture that efficiently represents predictable stochastic processes — random quantities that evolve over time in ways driven by Brownian motion. The method solves a fundamental problem in mathematical finance and control: traditional approaches require computing prohibitively expensive high-order integrals, while NeuralChaos achieves the same theoretical guarantees using only finitely many evaluations of the underlying randomness. This matters because stochastic processes are central to pricing derivatives, optimal trading, and reinforcement learning in continuous time.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on Wiener-chaos expansions — a classical decomposition technique from stochastic analysis that writes any square-integrable predictable process as an infinite sum of special basis functions (chaoslets) involving iterated integrals of Brownian motion. **Predictable processes** are those whose value at any time can be known from information available up to (but not including) that moment — a crucial constraint in finance and control. **Malliavin calculus** (a differential calculus for stochastic processes) is used to characterize regularity and approximation rates. The paper also engages with [[RLHF]]-adjacent themes: how neural networks can be trained to output objects from specialized function spaces rather than just vectors or images.

## Core Contributions
1. **NeuralChaos architecture**: A neural operator that directly produces elements of $\mathcal{H}^2_T(\mathbb{R}^d)$ (square-integrable predictable processes) using only finitely many Brownian samples, while respecting predictability constraints.
2. **Density and approximation guarantees**: Proves NeuralChaos is dense in the space of all such processes and achieves optimal $N$-term approximation rates for compressible and Malliavin–Sobolev regular processes.
3. **Fundamental structural result**: Shows that finite-dimensional Markovian neural SDE models (a popular class in practice) form a negligible subset — compressible processes are generically preferred.
4. **Sub-Gaussian sampling theory**: Establishes that compressibility is typical under non-degenerate random sampling, providing a bridge between theory and practice.

## Connections to Other Work
This work extends classical [[Wiener chaos]] expansions into the neural network era. It relates to recent advances in [[neural operators]] (architectures that map function spaces to function spaces) and connects implicitly to [[DeepONet]]-style architectures. The stochastic optimal control application links to [[reinforcement learning]] in continuous time; the dynamic hedging application links to [[derivatives-pricing]] and [[market-microstructure]]. The critique of standard neural SDEs — showing they are too restrictive — is a subtle negative result about a popular architecture class.

## Practical Applications
1. **Quantitative hedging**: Banks can use NeuralChaos to compute optimal dynamic hedging strategies for complex derivatives without computing intractable iterated integrals over high-dimensional asset spaces.
2. **Portfolio optimization**: Asset managers can optimize trading strategies in continuous time more efficiently, capturing non-Markovian effects missed by standard neural SDEs.
3. **Risk management**: Real-time computation of Value-at-Risk (VaR) and expected shortfall for portfolios with path-dependent derivatives.
4. **Reinforcement learning in continuous time**: Training agents in physics-based or financial simulations where the environment evolves via stochastic differential equations.
5. **Calibration of volatility models**: Fitting complex stochastic volatility models (e.g., rough volatility) to market data without solving expensive numerical PDE systems.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Computational cost**: While more efficient than brute-force chaos expansions, training NeuralChaos still requires solving stochastic optimization problems with high-dimensional path integrals; scalability to very high dimensions (100+) is untested.
2. **Model selection**: No clear guidance on choosing architecture hyperparameters (network width, depth, chaos truncation level) for new problems; practitioners will need cross-validation.
3. **Convergence in practice**: Theoretical density results are asymptotic; experiments are limited to relatively low-dimensional problems (2D–3D states). Behavior in 20+D is unknown.
4. **Sampling regime**: Theory assumes non-degenerate sub-Gaussian sampling; breaks down if the driving Brownian motion has special structure or if sample paths are sparse/irregular.
5. **No uncertainty quantification**: The architecture produces point estimates of processes; no principled way to quantify uncertainty in the learned representation (unlike Bayesian neural networks).
6. **Limited discussion of computational time vs. accuracy tradeoffs**: How does wall-clock time scale with process dimension and desired approximation accuracy?

## Open Questions
1. **Curse of dimensionality**: Can NeuralChaos efficiently represent high-dimensional predictable processes (50+D)? What is the actual sample complexity as dimension grows?
2. **Comparison to neural SDE hybrids**: Could a two-stage model (Markovian backbone + NeuralChaos correction) outperform pure NeuralChaos? When is the Markovian assumption actually harmless?
3. **Adaptive training**: Can the architecture dynamically identify which chaos orders matter most (active learning on chaos degree)?
4. **Multi-scale processes**: How does NeuralChaos perform on processes with multiple timescales (e.g., fast mean reversion + slow trends)?
5. **Real data experiments**: Applications to actual market data (equity, FX, credit) with parameter learning and out-of-sample validation.
6. **Gradient flow analysis**: What do learned NeuralChaos models look like geometrically? Can we interpret the learned chaos basis visually?

## Key Takeaway
Neural networks can now efficiently represent any stochastic process without computing expensive iterated integrals, enabling faster quantitative finance and reinforcement learning algorithms.
