# When Local Monitors Miss Compositional Harm: Diagnosing Distributed Backdoors in Multi-Agent Systems

**Authors:** Yibo Hu, Ren Wang
**Published:** 2026-07-13 | **arXiv:** [2607.11751v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11751v1)

## Summary

This paper reveals a critical vulnerability in runtime safety monitors for multi-agent LLM systems: attackers can split harmful payloads across multiple agents so each individual message passes safety checks, but the combined result is malicious. The authors prove that once attack fragments appear benign to a monitor's view, no detector using that view can catch them — no matter how strong — unless the monitor can see the assembled payload in its final representation.

## Theoretical Background

The paper builds on two key foundations. First, it draws from [[alignment]] research, which focuses on ensuring AI systems behave safely despite potential adversarial inputs. Second, it extends ideas from [[mechanistic-interpretability]] — the study of how neural networks' internal representations encode information — by showing that the *representation level* where information is visible fundamentally determines what can be detected.

The core insight leverages the concept of an *observability boundary*: a formal limit on what a monitor can distinguish from benign traffic given its view of the system. This echoes classical information theory: if two inputs are indistinguishable to an observer, no algorithm can separate them based only on that observer's data.

## Core Contributions

1. **Formalization of distributed backdoors in multi-agent systems**: The paper formally defines how harmful payloads can be split across agents such that each fragment appears individually benign.

2. **Observability boundary theorem**: A proof that once attack fragments become indistinguishable from benign traffic in a monitor's view, no detector (however powerful) can catch them using only that view.

3. **Empirical validation of representation dependence**: Experiments show monitors fail exactly when local evidence disappears, and succeed only when given access to the representation where the payload is exposed — demonstrating the theorem's practical relevance.

4. **Quantitative recovery metrics**: Demonstrates that benign-traffic-only trained monitors can recover attack structure across held-out encodings (0.874 AUROC), but full-trace monitors still fail unless they reach the critical representation.

## Connections to Other Work

This work sits at the intersection of several research areas:

- **Safety monitoring and [[alignment]]**: Extends the assumption that local checks guarantee global safety — a foundational assumption in current multi-agent safety architectures.
- **[[mechanistic-interpretability]]**: Directly invokes representation-level analysis to explain why monitors fail, connecting detectability to neural network semantics.
- **Adversarial robustness**: Relates to work on adversarial examples and compositional attacks, but in the multi-agent setting where the "attack surface" is distributed.
- **Formal verification**: Uses formal observability analysis similar to control theory and program verification.

The paper challenges the implicit assumption in current runtime monitoring that checking each step independently is sufficient.

## Practical Applications

1. **LLM agent tooling platforms** (e.g., Anthropic's tool-using systems, OpenAI function calling): Need to implement monitors that see composed outputs, not just individual tool calls.

2. **Workplace automation systems**: When multiple AI agents collaborate (e.g., procurement + approval + execution), monitors must check final assembled actions, not intermediate steps.

3. **Security-critical multi-robot systems**: Autonomous systems that coordinate across multiple agents need end-to-end safety verification, not agent-local checks.

4. **Supply chain and logistics coordination**: Multi-agent planning systems orchestrating vendors, shipping, and delivery require global consistency checks.

5. **Content moderation at scale**: Detecting coordinated harmful content split across multiple AI generators or personas.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **Representation-aware safety middleware**: A drop-in monitor that automatically identifies the "canonical representation" where payloads are most visible, and enforces safety checks at that level. Market: AI infrastructure.

2. **Compositional attack detection service**: SaaS product that uses benign-traffic baseline learning to detect distributed backdoors in customer multi-agent systems. Market: enterprise AI safety.

3. **Multi-agent audit toolkit**: Developer tools that automatically surface compositional harms by exhaustively checking all assembly paths in agent workflows. Market: AI safety compliance.

4. **Representation-layer debugger**: IDE-like tool that lets engineers visualize what different safety monitors can and cannot see, solving the hard problem of finding safe representations. Market: AI debugging/observability.

5. **Federated safety coordination protocol**: Lightweight framework allowing distributed agents to enforce global safety properties without a central bottleneck. Market: decentralized AI systems.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Representation discovery is unsolved**: The paper identifies that monitors must reach "the" representation where harm is exposed, but provides no general method for finding it. In practice, complex LLM systems may have many possible representations, and choosing the wrong one still fails.

2. **Scalability to real systems**: Experiments use controlled testbeds and end-to-end agent runs. Real-world systems with hundreds of agents, complex state, and partial observability may have exponentially many compositional pathways.

3. **Assumption of benign baseline data**: The monitor training assumes access to sufficiently representative benign traffic. Adaptive attackers could craft attacks that look like rare-but-legitimate behavior.

4. **No solution for zero-day compositional attacks**: The paper shows recovery on held-out *encodings* of known attack *structures*. It does not address novel attack compositions unseen during training.

5. **Overhead of full-trace monitoring**: Seeing "more" (full traces, decoded representations) is computationally expensive and may be impractical for high-throughput systems.

6. **Multi-representation attacks**: Nothing prevents attackers from distributing harm across multiple safe representations simultaneously.

## Open Questions

1. **Representation learning for safety**: Can we automatically learn or identify the minimal sufficient representation where all harmful patterns are observable? This seems to be the core hard problem.

2. **Compositional safety specifications**: How do we formally specify what makes a composition safe when individual fragments are benign? Current formal methods don't handle this well.

3. **Adaptive attackers**: What if an attacker knows the monitor's representation and crafts attacks that are benign in that representation but harmful when executed? Can monitors be robust to that?

4. **Efficiency-safety tradeoff**: Can monitors catch distributed backdoors without full-trace visibility? Is there a middle ground between local checks and monitoring everything?

5. **Generalization across agent architectures**: Do the same attack techniques and detection methods work across different types of agent systems (tool-using LLMs, reinforcement learning agents, classical multi-agent planning)?

6. **Human-in-the-loop detection**: When automated monitors fail, what are the minimal hints or summaries that would let a human operator catch distributed backdoors?

## Key Takeaway

Local safety checks miss attacks split across agents—monitors must see the composed payload's actual representation to catch them.
