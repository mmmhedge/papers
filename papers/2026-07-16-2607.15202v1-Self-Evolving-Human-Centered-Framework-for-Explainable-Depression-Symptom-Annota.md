# Self-Evolving Human-Centered Framework for Explainable Depression Symptom Annotation

**Authors:** Hoang-Loc Cao, Van Pham, Truong Thanh Hung Nguyen, Phuc Truong Loc Nguyen, Phuc Ho et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.15202v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15202v1)

## Summary
This paper presents a framework for creating high-quality, explainable datasets for depression diagnosis by combining LLM-assisted annotation with expert feedback. The key innovation is a dual-memory system that learns from expert corrections without retraining, producing datasets aligned with clinical standards (DSM-5-TR) and traceable reasoning for each diagnosis decision.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two intersecting areas: (1) **annotation quality in AI** — the recognition that model performance is limited by training data quality, especially in high-stakes domains like mental health; and (2) **explainable AI (XAI)** — the principle that critical decisions should be transparent and auditable. It also draws on **human-in-the-loop learning**, where human experts iteratively refine AI outputs, and **large language models (LLMs)** — neural networks trained on vast text that can follow instructions and generate structured reasoning. The clinical foundation is the DSM-5-TR, a standardized diagnostic manual that defines Major Depressive Disorder through specific criteria.

## Core Contributions
1. **Self-evolving annotation framework**: A three-stage process (evidence selection → criterion analysis → case synthesis) that produces structured, justified labels rather than binary assignments.
2. **Dual-memory architecture**: Example Memory (past cases) and Reflection Memory (expert feedback) that allow the system to improve annotations across cycles without model retraining.
3. **End-to-end explainability**: Every annotation is exported with clinical evidence, reasoning traces, and edit histories, enabling full auditability and downstream model interpretability.
4. **DSM-5-TR alignment**: Annotation methodology explicitly grounded in clinical diagnostic criteria, bridging AI and psychiatry standards.

## Connections to Other Work
This work relates to [[RLHF]] (reinforcement learning from human feedback) in its use of expert corrections to improve outputs, though it uses memory-based learning rather than gradient updates. It connects to broader **human-centered AI** and [[human-in-the-loop]] paradigms. The explainability emphasis aligns with work on [[mechanistic-interpretability]] and **model transparency**. It also addresses a known problem in medical AI: the "annotation curse" where unlabeled or weakly labeled datasets undermine clinical deployment. The dual-memory mechanism is reminiscent of retrieval-augmented approaches used in knowledge management systems.

## Practical Applications
1. **Clinical decision support systems**: Mental health platforms (teletherapy, counseling apps) that recommend diagnoses with auditable, clinician-reviewable reasoning.
2. **Psychiatric research datasets**: High-quality, ethically transparent datasets for depression research, publishable with full annotation provenance.
3. **Electronic health record (EHR) analysis**: Automated depression screening in primary care settings where clinicians need to verify reasoning, not just trust a score.
4. **Insurance and disability assessment**: Structured diagnostic documentation for claims processing, reducing disputes and improving consistency.
5. **Training and education**: Medical students/residents using the framework to learn diagnostic reasoning alongside AI suggestions.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Mental Health Analytics Platform**: SaaS tool for clinics/hospitals to annotate and audit patient records with LLM-assisted explainable classification, ensuring compliance and improving diagnostic consistency.
2. **Clinical Data Marketplace**: Create premium, DSM-5-aligned mental health datasets with full provenance; sell to researchers and model developers wanting reliable training data.
3. **Regulatory Compliance as a Service**: White-label annotation platform for pharmaceutical companies and clinical trials needing FDA-defensible diagnostic labeling with traceable evidence.
4. **Psychiatrist-in-the-Loop AI Assistant**: Real-time diagnostic co-pilot for primary care doctors flagging depression cases with reasoned, editable summaries that reduce clinician burden.
5. **Mental Health Insurance Underwriting Tool**: Automated yet explainable depression severity assessment for underwriting, with full reasoning export for appeals and transparency.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Evaluation incomplete**: The paper explicitly defers evaluation of the dual-memory system across multiple feedback cycles, leaving its actual learning efficacy unvalidated.
2. **Scalability unclear**: Requires expert-in-the-loop verification, which doesn't scale to millions of records; the framework's cost-effectiveness at scale is unproven.
3. **LLM hallucination risk**: LLMs can confidently generate false evidence or reasoning; the framework relies on expert review to catch these, but may miss subtle errors.
4. **Dataset bias**: If early expert feedback encodes biases (e.g., demographic disparities in diagnosis), the memory architecture will amplify them without explicit bias detection.
5. **Generalization**: Trained on depression; unclear whether the framework transfers to other mental health conditions or languages.
6. **Causality vs. correlation**: The framework aligns with DSM-5-TR criteria but doesn't guarantee causal diagnosticity; symptom presence ≠ clinical validity.

## Open Questions
1. How much expert feedback is needed per cycle for the memory system to plateau in improvement? What is the cost-benefit frontier?
2. Does the framework actually improve downstream model interpretability, or just annotation transparency? Can you empirically measure this?
3. How do the Example and Reflection memories interact when they conflict (e.g., an old example vs. recent expert correction)?
4. Can the system detect and flag when it is uncertain or out-of-distribution, rather than confidently wrong?
5. How does performance vary across demographic groups? Are there hidden biases in the DSM-5-TR alignment itself?
6. Could adversarial users game the example/reflection memories to steer future annotations?

## Key Takeaway
LLM-assisted annotation with expert feedback and memory improves depression dataset quality, explainability, and auditability for clinical AI.
