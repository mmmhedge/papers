# Data Analysis in the Wild: Benchmarking Large Language Models Against Real-World Data Complexities

**Authors:** So Hasegawa, Shailaja Keyur Sampat, Lei Liu, Wei-Peng Chen
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06482v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06482v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces DataGovBench, a benchmark that tests how well large language models (LLMs) perform on real-world data analysis tasks using government open datasets. The benchmark covers two challenges: answering complex questions about multi-table datasets and discovering expert-level insights through exploratory analysis. The work matters because existing benchmarks focus on simple fact retrieval from small tables and miss the messy reality of data analytics — the paper shows that state-of-the-art LLMs still fall short on these practical tasks.

## Theoretical Background
Large language models (LLMs) — neural networks trained on massive text corpora that can generate text and perform reasoning tasks — have recently been applied to data analysis problems. Prior work has focused on "Table QA" (question-answering over tables) but typically used small, clean datasets. The paper builds on the observation that benchmarks like these often miss real-world complexity: datasets in practice are large, span multiple related tables, require external knowledge, and demand exploratory insight generation rather than simple fact lookup. The paper also considers agentic frameworks (systems where LLMs decompose tasks into steps and call external tools), which represent an emerging paradigm for making LLMs more capable at structured reasoning.

## Core Contributions
1. **DataGovBench**: A new benchmark dataset derived from real governmental open data (not synthetic or simplified), with two distinct task types that reflect practical analytics workflows.
2. **Table QA with complexity**: Questions require decomposition across multiple tables and integration of external knowledge, not just retrieving facts from a single table.
3. **Table Insight generation**: A novel evaluation task that requires LLMs to perform exploratory data analysis and articulate non-obvious findings — a capability rarely benchmarked before.
4. **Empirical evidence of performance gaps**: Systematic experiments showing that both standard LLMs and agentic LLM systems underperform on real-world data analytics, establishing a clear research target.

## Connections to Other Work
This work extends the [[Table QA]] and [[semantic parsing]] literature by grounding evaluation in realistic data — moving away from simplified benchmarks like WikiTableQuestions or Spider. It relates to broader efforts in [[LLM evaluation]] (e.g., HELM, Big-Bench) that seek to measure model capabilities against practical demands rather than isolated tasks. The inclusion of agentic frameworks connects to [[agents]] research and tool-use paradigms like [[ReAct]] and [[chain-of-thought prompting]]. The exploratory insight task draws on data science methodology and represents a distinct challenge from traditional NLP — closer to [[reasoning]] and [[knowledge synthesis]] than pure language understanding.

## Practical Applications
1. **Automated business intelligence**: Helping non-technical users query and explore corporate datasets (financial records, operational metrics) by asking natural-language questions.
2. **Government transparency tools**: Enabling citizens and journalists to extract insights from public datasets without programming.
3. **Research data analysis**: Assisting researchers in exploratory phases of analysis before detailed statistical modeling.
4. **Compliance and audit**: LLM-powered systems that analyze transaction or regulatory datasets to flag anomalies or answer investigative questions.
5. **Customer analytics platforms**: SaaS tools that allow product teams to query user behavior data conversationally without SQL expertise.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **DataChat for Enterprises**: A conversational interface (powered by LLMs and DataGovBench insights) that lets any business user run ad-hoc analysis on company data warehouses, with auto-generated charts and written summaries of findings.
2. **PublicDataGPT**: A search engine and chatbot for government datasets that automatically discovers and explains policy-relevant insights from open data, selling to agencies and NGOs.
3. **Research Copilot**: A tool for academic researchers that takes raw datasets (CSV, Excel, databases) and generates exploratory summaries, outlier detection, and hypothesis generation before formal modeling.
4. **Insurance Insights Engine**: An internal tool for insurers to rapidly analyze claims, pricing, and fraud patterns through natural language — reducing time from data to decision.
5. **DataExplainer**: A consulting service that uses LLM-powered analysis to help mid-market companies unlock their own data, positioned as a cheaper alternative to hiring junior analysts.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability to truly large datasets**: The benchmark may not fully test LLMs on datasets with millions of rows or hundreds of tables in real enterprise settings; computational and context-length limits remain unresolved.
2. **Data privacy and sensitivity**: Real government data may be anonymized or contain sensitive fields; the benchmark doesn't measure how well systems handle privacy constraints or comply with regulations.
3. **Insight evaluation is subjective**: The "expert-level findings" task relies on human judgment of what constitutes a good insight — makes fine-grained reproducibility and automatic evaluation hard.
4. **Limited domain coverage**: Government datasets have specific structures and vocabularies; results may not generalize to financial, medical, or scientific data with different complexities.
5. **Reliance on pre-trained knowledge**: LLMs may succeed partly by memorizing information about well-known public datasets rather than true reasoning; unclear how much is genuine analysis vs. retrieval.
6. **Tool availability and chain-of-thought brittleness**: Agentic systems depend on well-designed tools and stable reasoning chains; real-world tool APIs and data schemas are messier than the benchmark setup.

## Open Questions
1. How do LLMs perform on *truly novel* datasets they've never seen during training, and how much does memorization vs. reasoning contribute to the benchmark results?
2. Can agentic frameworks be made more robust — what happens when external tools fail, return unexpected formats, or have high latency?
3. How do different LLM architectures, scales, and training strategies rank on this benchmark, and what minimal capabilities are sufficient for good performance?
4. Can humans reliably and consistently evaluate "insight quality," and would inter-rater agreement be high enough to use as a metric?
5. How does performance scale with dataset size, number of tables, and domain specificity? Are there scaling laws?
6. Can fine-tuning on data analysis tasks improve performance significantly, and would it be more practical than pure prompting approaches?
7. What kinds of reasoning errors do LLMs make most often — joining tables, aggregation, filtering, or conceptual misunderstandings of the domain?

## Key Takeaway
Real-world data analysis demands that LLMs handle multi-table datasets, external knowledge, and exploratory reasoning — not just fact lookup — and current models fall short, establishing an important benchmark for closing this practical capability gap.
