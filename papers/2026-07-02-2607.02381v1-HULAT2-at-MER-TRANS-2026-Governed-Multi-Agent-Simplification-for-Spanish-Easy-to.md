# HULAT2 at MER-TRANS 2026: Governed Multi-Agent Simplification for Spanish Easy-to-Read Generation

**Authors:** Lourdes Moreno, Paloma Martínez, Marco Antonio Sanchez-Escudero, Miguel Domínguez-Gómez
**Published:** 2026-07-02 | **arXiv:** [2607.02381v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02381v1)

## Summary
This paper presents a multi-agent system for converting Spanish text into Easy-to-Read format—simplified language designed for people with cognitive disabilities or low literacy. The authors combine multiple language models (Gemini 2.5 Flash and RigoChat-7B-v2) in a workflow that routes decisions through quality signals, outperforming a simpler baseline and showing that intelligent orchestration of agents beats linear regeneration for this accessibility task.

## Theoretical Background
Easy-to-Read translation aims to reduce syntactic and lexical complexity while preserving meaning—a subtask of text simplification. The paper builds on recent work in [[large-language-models]] orchestration and multi-agent workflows (using LangGraph, a framework for composable agent logic), combining it with prompt engineering and parameter-efficient adaptation (LoRA — Low-Rank Adaptation, which fine-tunes models by updating only small learned matrices). The evaluation framework uses established machine translation and simplification metrics: BLEU (n-gram overlap), SARI (simplification-specific metric rewarding deletions and paraphrases), and BERTScore (semantic similarity via pretrained embeddings).

## Core Contributions
1. **Signal-guided multi-agent routing**: Rather than chaining agents sequentially, the authors use Event-Condition-Action rules to route text through different processing branches based on internal quality signals (semantic fidelity, readability, lexical simplicity, syntactic clarity, factual consistency).
2. **Parallel generation with controlled editing**: Runs 1 and 2 generate simplifications in parallel and apply traceable editing decisions, making the process interpretable.
3. **Lexical support layer**: Run 2 adds a glossary-based resource to guide lexical choices, though empirical results show this didn't help reference-based metrics.
4. **Empirical finding**: Signal-guided multi-agent routing (SARI: 44.05) outperformed linear generate-evaluate-regenerate (SARI: 38.51), suggesting orchestration strategy matters for this task.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to broader work in [[agents]] orchestration and [[multi-agent-systems]] (e.g., routing, workflow design), accessible language generation (a specialized branch of [[nlp]]), and [[generative-models]] adaptation via LoRA. The paper sits at the intersection of machine translation and accessibility—distinct from standard MT but using similar evaluation frameworks. The internal quality signals echo [[mechanistic-interpretability]] concerns in reverse: instead of understanding what models learn, the authors expose and guide what models produce.

## Practical Applications
1. **Accessible government communication**: Automating Easy-to-Read versions of official documents, laws, and public health information for citizens with cognitive disabilities.
2. **Inclusive healthcare**: Simplifying medical instructions and patient consent forms in hospital systems.
3. **Educational support**: Auto-generating simplified textbooks or learning materials for students with dyslexia or intellectual disabilities.
4. **Social media moderation**: Pre-processing user-facing content (terms of service, community guidelines) for clarity.
5. **Accessibility as a service**: Building an API or plugin that publishers or government agencies can integrate to auto-generate accessible versions of content.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Easy-Read-as-a-Service (ERaaS)**: Cloud API that accepts Spanish (or multilingual) documents and returns certified Easy-to-Read versions, with audit trails and quality signals exposed to users.
2. **Accessible Document Suite**: Browser extension or Microsoft Word plugin that instantly generates Easy-to-Read summaries or full versions of any webpage or document.
3. **Government Accessibility Compliance Tool**: SaaS for public administrations ensuring all official content meets Easy-to-Read standards automatically, with human-in-the-loop review.
4. **Inclusive Publishing Platform**: CMS for educational publishers that auto-generates multiple readability tiers (standard, simplified, very simple) from one source document.
5. **Accessibility Consulting + Automation**: Service combining the multi-agent pipeline with human expert review and user testing, targeting disability organizations and large employers.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Metric-reality gap**: BLEU and SARI rely on reference texts; Easy-to-Read quality is subjective and user-dependent (different for different cognitive abilities). Official results don't report human evaluation.
2. **Lexical support hypothesis fails**: Adding a glossary hurt performance, suggesting the approach may be brittle or the glossary misaligned with the models' strengths.
3. **Scalability unclear**: No discussion of latency, cost, or throughput—multi-agent workflows with API calls (Gemini 2.5) may be slow or expensive at scale.
4. **Language scope**: Only Spanish; generalization to other languages unknown.
5. **Factual consistency**: Noted as unmeasured ("further analysis required"), but crucial for accessibility—simplification that changes meaning is harmful.
6. **No user validation**: No study with actual users with cognitive disabilities; accessibility claims are indirect.

## Open Questions
1. How do humans with varying cognitive abilities actually rate these outputs? What readability level is "Easy-to-Read"?
2. Why did lexical support *hurt* metrics? Was the glossary misaligned, or is the signaling mechanism flawed?
3. Does signal-guided routing remain superior at document level (vs. segment level)? Do discourse-level simplifications help?
4. How do costs and latencies of the multi-agent pipeline compare to simpler fine-tuned models?
5. Can the internal quality signals be made quantitative and automatically optimized (e.g., via reinforcement learning)?
6. How do these systems perform on other languages and non-European writing systems?

## Key Takeaway
Intelligent routing of text through multiple agents based on interpretable quality signals outperforms linear generation-regeneration for Spanish Easy-to-Read simplification, but metric-based evaluation masks real accessibility needs and requires human validation.
