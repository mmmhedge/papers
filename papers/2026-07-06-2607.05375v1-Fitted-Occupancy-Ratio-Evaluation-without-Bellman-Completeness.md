# Fitted Occupancy-Ratio Evaluation without Bellman Completeness

**Authors:** Lars van der Laan, Nathan Kallus
**Published:** 2026-07-06 | **arXiv:** [2607.05375v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05375v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes FORE (Fitted Occupancy-Ratio Evaluation), a new method for estimating occupancy ratios in offline reinforcement learning — a setting where you learn from fixed, collected data rather than active exploration. The key insight is that you can estimate these ratios using a simpler recursion (an "adjoint Bellman" equation) that only requires the occupancy ratio itself to be representable in your function class, avoiding stronger technical assumptions that prior methods needed. This matters because occupancy ratios are fundamental tools for correcting bias when evaluating policies on offline data.

## Theoretical Background
Offline reinforcement learning deals with learning policies from a fixed dataset, which introduces distribution shift: the data was collected under a different policy, so you can't trust direct policy value estimates. Occupancy ratios are a principled way to reweight data to correct this shift — they measure how often a state-action pair would be visited under a target policy relative to the data-collection policy. Prior work on estimating these ratios (e.g., using primal-dual or minimax approaches) typically enforces "occupancy-balance moments" over a critic class and often requires strong assumptions like Bellman completeness (roughly: the function class is closed under the Bellman operator) or projected-operator stability. This paper builds on the idea of fitted fixed-point methods — iteratively refining an estimate by solving a simpler problem on one-step transitions — similar to how fitted Q-evaluation works in value-based RL, but applies it to ratio estimation through an adjoint Bellman recursion.

## Core Contributions
1. **FORE algorithm**: A fitted fixed-point method that estimates occupancy ratios by iteratively solving KL-divergence density-ratio objectives on one-step transitions, using an adjoint (transpose) Bellman recursion rather than the standard Bellman operator.
2. **Weaker realizability condition**: Shows that only the discounted occupancy ratio itself needs to be representable in the function class — no Bellman completeness or projected-operator stability required, significantly weakening prior assumptions.
3. **Contraction analysis**: Proves the population recursion contracts in relative entropy (KL divergence) under occupancy-ratio realizability alone, because the adjoint Bellman operator is a KL-contraction.
4. **Finite-sample bounds**: Establishes convergence rates showing error scales with log-ratio approximation error plus statistical error from the ratio hypothesis class complexity.
5. **Plug-and-play integration**: Demonstrates how the fitted ratio enables three downstream tasks — direct value estimation via reward reweighting, occupancy-weighted fitted Q-evaluation, and doubly-robust estimation combining the ratio with a fitted Q-function.

## Connections to Other Work
This work directly advances [[off-policy evaluation]] in offline RL by simplifying assumptions compared to existing density-ratio and occupancy-ratio methods. It parallels the theory of [[fitted Q-evaluation]] but for ratio estimation, replacing value-function realizability + Bellman completeness with ratio realizability alone. The use of adjoint operators and KL-contraction analysis connects to the broader literature on operator-theoretic RL. The doubly-robust estimation component echoes classical causal inference techniques and prior [[doubly robust]] approaches in RL. It also relates to importance-sampling and density-ratio estimation methods in domain adaptation and causal learning, though applied here specifically to the offline RL setting.

## Practical Applications
1. **Offline policy evaluation in healthcare**: Evaluate new treatment policies on historical patient data without running costly or risky trials; the fitted ratio corrects for differences between how treatments were historically prescribed and the new policy.
2. **Autonomous systems safety auditing**: Assess the performance of a proposed autonomous driving or robotic policy against logged real-world interactions, accounting for the distribution shift between logged conditions and the new policy's behavior.
3. **Recommender system validation**: Estimate the performance of a new recommendation strategy on historical user interaction logs, correcting for the bias introduced by the previous system's recommendations.
4. **Financial strategy backtesting**: Evaluate historical trading strategies on market data, correcting for survivorship bias and distribution shifts in market regimes.
5. **Batch reinforcement learning in industrial control**: Estimate whether a new control policy will improve performance in a manufacturing or robotics setting, using only offline simulation or past operational logs.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Offline RL evaluation SaaS**: A platform that ingests offline interaction data and a candidate policy, uses FORE to estimate policy value with confidence intervals, and visualizes risk/return tradeoffs — target: healthcare and finance where online testing is expensive or unethical.
2. **Recommendation system A/B testing alternative**: A tool that predicts recommendation algorithm performance without deploying to real users, using FORE on historical logs — reduce experiment time and risk for e-commerce and content platforms.
3. **Autonomous vehicle policy validator**: Software that certifies new AV behaviors against naturalistic driving logs, quantifying safety and performance gains with statistical guarantees using occupancy-ratio weighting.
4. **Robust offline RL library/API**: Open a managed service offering FORE and related offline RL algorithms as an API, handling data ingestion, model selection, and confidence calibration — for teams without deep RL expertise.
5. **Batch RL for robotics consultancy**: Combine FORE with simulation-to-real transfer to help robotics companies evaluate new policies on logged task demonstrations before hardware deployment, reducing iteration cycles.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Realizability assumption is strong in high dimensions**: While weaker than Bellman completeness, assuming the occupancy ratio is perfectly representable in a finite function class is still restrictive for high-dimensional state spaces; approximation error when this fails is not characterized.
2. **KL-divergence as the sole geometry**: The method's contraction and convergence guarantees rely on KL divergence; it's unclear how results extend to other divergences (e.g., Wasserstein, chi-squared) that might suit different downstream tasks better.
3. **Single-level optimization per iteration**: Solving the density-ratio objective exactly at each iteration is assumed in theory; practical algorithms use finite sample approximations whose interaction with the outer recursion is not fully analyzed.
4. **Limited handling of function approximation mismatch**: The finite-sample bounds scale with hypothesis class complexity, but don't cleanly separate errors due to choosing the wrong class versus optimization error; practitioners must tune the class size.
5. **No guarantees on downstream task performance**: The method provides convergence to the true occupancy ratio, but end-to-end regret or value estimation error for doubly-robust or Q-learning variants is not directly characterized.
6. **Scalability to large MDPs**: The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity; iterative recursions over large state-action spaces could be slow, and the method's practical scaling behavior is unknown.

## Open Questions
1. **Approximation error under misspecification**: What happens when occupancy-ratio realizability fails? Can the method be made robust with regularization or a relaxed realizability condition?
2. **Adaptive function class selection**: How should practitioners choose the hypothesis class for the log-ratio? Can the method be extended with online model selection or early stopping that maintains guarantees?
3. **Extension to continuous control**: The paper works in the tabular or finite-class setting; how do neural network approximations (a practical standard) behave, and what sample complexity should we expect?
4. **Comparison with other divergences**: How do variants using alternative divergences (Wasserstein, f-divergences) compare in terms of sample efficiency and robustness?
5. **Multi-step off-policy evaluation**: Can FORE be extended to evaluate multiple steps ahead, or combined with model-based methods to reduce variance?
6. **Connection to implicit models**: FORE uses an implicit adjoint recursion; can it be interpreted as implicit optimization or score-matching, and does that perspective yield new algorithmic insights?
7. **Finite-sample tightness**: Are the regret bounds tight? Can lower bounds be established to characterize fundamental limits?

## Key Takeaway
FORE replaces hard-to-verify completeness assumptions in offline RL with a simple realizability condition on the occupancy ratio itself, enabling practical occupancy-ratio estimation for policy evaluation with cleaner theoretical guarantees.
