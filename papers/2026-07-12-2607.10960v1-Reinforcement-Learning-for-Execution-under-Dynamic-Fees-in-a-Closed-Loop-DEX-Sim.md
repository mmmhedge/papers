# Reinforcement Learning for Execution under Dynamic Fees in a Closed-Loop DEX Simulator

**Authors:** Wen-Ting Wang
**Published:** 2026-07-12 | **arXiv:** [2607.10960v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10960v1)

## Summary
This paper studies how traders should execute orders in automated market makers (AMMs) when the platform charges dynamic fees that vary with market conditions. Because real historical data can't show how traders would respond to fee changes that don't actually exist in the past, the authors built a realistic simulator with dynamic fees, multiple liquidity pools, and arbitrage logic, then trained a deep reinforcement learning agent (DQN) to find optimal execution strategies. The DQN agent outperforms hand-tuned baseline strategies by reducing execution costs, especially when fees are dynamic.

## Theoretical Background
This work sits at the intersection of market microstructure (the study of how financial markets function in detail) and reinforcement learning (RL — algorithms that learn by trial and error in interactive environments). Key concepts:

- **Automated Market Makers (AMMs)**: decentralized exchanges where prices are set by mathematical formulas (like constant-product pools) rather than order books. Users trade against liquidity pools.
- **Dynamic fees**: transaction fees that change based on market state (e.g., rising when volatility is high), in contrast to fixed fees.
- **Execution problem**: finding the best way to trade a large order without moving the price too far against you (minimizing "implementation shortfall").
- **Closed-loop simulation**: a realistic simulator where the agent's actions feed back into future prices and opportunities, so past decisions matter.
- **DQN (Deep Q-Networks)**: a foundational RL algorithm that learns value functions using neural networks.

The paper uses equilibrium (economic balance between supply and demand) as a "closure principle" — a way to make the simulator realistic without assuming traders are perfectly rational.

## Core Contributions
1. **Minimal but realistic simulator**: The first credible testbed for studying execution under dynamic AMM fees, with closed-loop feedback (agent actions affect future prices), arbitrage dynamics, and fee-sensitive order flow.
2. **Empirical RL benchmark**: Demonstrates that a small DQN beats a carefully tuned ladder of baselines (ranging from simple one-step routing to tabular policies), with statistically significant gains in implementation shortfall.
3. **Counterfactual evidence**: Shows that RL's advantage is *concentrated in dynamic-fee regimes*; under constant fees, RL and baselines are indistinguishable. This cleanly isolates what RL learns.
4. **Quantified improvement**: 13.3 basis points reduction in execution cost under the pre-specified agent-last ordering, on a held-out test set of 1,000 seeds with completion forced to 100%.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper addresses a gap between [[RLHF|supervised learning]] approaches (which rely on historical data) and true sequential decision-making. It echoes broader themes in:

- **RL for finance**: prior work on execution, market-making, and portfolio optimization using RL, but most studied simpler settings (e.g., constant fees, single-pool markets, or academic models with unrealistic assumptions).
- **Market microstructure**: classical work on optimal execution (e.g., Almgren–Chriss) in order-book markets; this extends those ideas to AMMs.
- **Mechanism design for fees**: recent proposals for dynamic fee structures in DeFi; this paper's simulator operationalizes them for RL study.
- **Multi-agent systems**: the simulator includes noise traders and arbitrageurs, echoing [[multi-agent-systems|multi-agent RL]] but in a controlled, non-learning environment.

The key novelty is recognizing that **historical backtesting cannot answer counterfactual questions** about fees traders have never faced, so simulation + RL is the right tool.

## Practical Applications
1. **DEX execution routing**: Market makers or traders using protocols like Uniswap could deploy learned execution policies to minimize slippage when facing dynamic fees.
2. **AMM fee optimization**: Protocol designers could use similar RL agents to understand how different fee schedules (e.g., rising with volatility) affect trader surplus and protocol revenue.
3. **Large order execution on-chain**: Asset managers executing large trades through multiple AMM pools could use RL-trained policies to split orders across pools and time dynamically.
4. **Flash loan arbitrage**: High-frequency traders could use learned policies to execute arbitrage between CEX and AMM, accounting for dynamic AMM fees.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Execution-as-a-Service for DeFi**: A SaaS product that trains RL agents on your AMM's fee schedule and liquidity profile, then offers white-label execution routing to traders (API endpoint that queries the trained policy).
2. **Dynamic Fee Optimization Dashboard**: A tool for AMM governance (Uniswap DAOs, etc.) to simulate and A/B test fee structures using the closed-loop simulator and RL agents, showing projected revenue and trader surplus.
3. **Cross-DEX Execution Router**: An aggregator that learns execution strategies across multiple AMMs with different fee curves in real-time, using lightweight RL agents updated daily.
4. **Institutional On-Chain Order Flow Provider**: Build a yield farming strategy that learns to execute large treasury rebalances on-chain at minimal cost, then license to DAOs and on-chain funds.
5. **Trading Bot Marketplace**: Offer pre-trained RL execution agents (tuned per DEX, fee regime, order size) for retail traders, with a usage-based fee model.

## Technical Limitations
- **Simulator realism**: The "equilibrium-inspired" fee rule and noise-trader model are stylized; real market dynamics are messier (regime shifts, vol clustering, strategic latency).
- **Generalization**: The trained DQN is tuned to the specific simulator; transfer to live markets is untested. Fee schedules, pool liquidity, and arbitrage costs vary across chains and over time.
- **Latency & execution risk**: The simulator assumes discrete time steps; real DEX execution involves asynchronous blockchain confirmation, MEV (miner extractable value), and sandwich attacks — none modeled here.
- **Scalability of DQN**: DQN is a relatively simple RL algorithm; more modern approaches (e.g., actor-critic, model-based RL) might yield further gains, but the paper doesn't compare them.
- **Statistical power**: Gains are concentrated in dynamic-fee settings; the paper doesn't explore how sensitive these gains are to fee schedule design or order-flow assumptions.
- **Causality vs. correlation**: The result is model-conditioned (only valid in the simulator); it's not direct evidence about real human trader behavior or equilibrium selection.

## Open Questions
1. **Transfer and robustness**: Does the learned policy degrade gracefully when deployed on a different DEX with different fee curves, liquidity, or arbitrage costs? How much retraining is needed?
2. **Larger order sizes and market impact**: The paper doesn't specify order size relative to pool depth. How do gains scale with order notional? At what point does the AMM's constant-product formula break down?
3. **Multi-agent equilibrium**: If many traders use RL execution agents simultaneously, do they reach a new equilibrium? Does fee design still matter?
4. **Real-time fee adaptation**: Can the agent adapt within a single large order as fees change in real-time, or only plan at the order level?
5. **Comparison with modern RL**: How do more sophisticated algorithms (PPO, SAC, model-based RL) perform? Is DQN just a convenient baseline?
6. **Realistic order flow**: How sensitive are results to the noise-trader model? Can the simulator be calibrated to real on-chain data?

## Key Takeaway
Deep RL agents learn to execute trades cost-effectively under dynamic AMM fees, outperforming hand-tuned baselines in a closed-loop simulator.
