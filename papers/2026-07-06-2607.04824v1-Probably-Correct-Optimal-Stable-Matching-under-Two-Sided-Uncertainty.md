# Probably Correct Optimal Stable Matching under Two-Sided Uncertainty

**Authors:** Andreas Athanasopoulos, Anne-Marie George, Christos Dimitrakakis
**Published:** 2026-07-06 | **arXiv:** [2607.04824v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.04824v1)

## Summary
This paper studies how to learn and identify the optimal stable matching in two-sided markets (like job–candidate or student–school assignments) when both sides' preferences are unknown and must be learned from noisy feedback. The key innovation is handling *two-sided uncertainty* — where neither side reveals their full preferences upfront — while still finding the best matching efficiently. The work matters because real matching markets operate with incomplete information, and this provides theoretical guarantees on how many queries are needed to find a provably good solution.

## Theoretical Background
Stable matching is a classical problem in economics and computer science, originating from the [[Gale-Shapley algorithm]], which finds matchings where no pair of agents would prefer to leave their assigned partners. This paper frames matching as a sequential learning problem, combining ideas from:

- **Bandit algorithms** (decision-making under uncertainty with limited feedback) — specifically semi-bandit feedback, where an algorithm observes rewards only for the matching it chooses, not for all possible alternatives
- **Pure exploration** — a learning regime focused on identifying the best solution rather than maximizing immediate rewards
- **Stable matching theory** — the mathematical framework ensuring no pair has incentive to deviate

The core challenge is that prior work either assumed one side's preferences were known, or that full preference information arrived with each query. Here, both sides are initially unknown, and feedback is partial (noisy and incomplete).

## Core Contributions
1. **Two-sided uncertainty handling**: Extends prior stable matching + learning results (which typically assume one side's preferences are known) to the fully uncertain case.

2. **Pervasive stable matching**: Introduces a novel concept that identifies when a matching is optimal *even under partial preference information*, without needing to learn complete preferences. This is a structural insight that enables efficient algorithms.

3. **Elimination-based algorithms**: Proposes algorithms that actively eliminate sub-optimal matchings using stopping criteria tailored to learned partial preferences, with refined sample-complexity bounds.

4. **Regret bounds without dependency on minimum reward gap**: Achieves regret minimization guarantees that don't blow up when the gap between best and second-best matching becomes arbitrarily small — a technical improvement over standard approaches.

## Connections to Other Work
This bridges:
- **Stable matching** (economic theory and algorithmic game theory) with **online learning** (machine learning)
- **Pure exploration in bandits** (a well-studied problem in ML) with matching market design
- Related to [[active learning]] and [[best-arm identification]] problems, which also aim to identify good solutions efficiently
- Contrasts with works that assume known preferences on one side or full feedback models; this is more realistic for two-sided markets

The "pervasive stable matching" concept is a novel structural tool — analogous to how [[RLHF]] or [[preference learning]] frameworks leverage partial preference signals in other domains.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Assumes transitive, rational preferences**: The stable matching framework assumes preferences follow certain rationality axioms; real human preferences may violate these (e.g., cycles, context-dependence).

2. **Semi-bandit feedback only**: The algorithm only learns from matched pairs; doesn't use information from unmatched comparisons. Real systems often have richer feedback (e.g., "I viewed but rejected this candidate").

3. **Noisy preference assumption**: Assumes noise is random and well-behaved (likely i.i.d. with known distribution); real preference signals are often adversarial, biased, or depend on match context.

4. **Computational complexity not addressed**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of running elimination algorithms at scale; computing stable matchings itself is polynomial but can be expensive with large agent pools.

5. **Sample complexity may still be large**: While avoiding $\Delta_{\min}$ dependence, sample complexity likely still depends on problem dimension (number of agents); may be prohibitive for very large markets.

6. **No strategic behavior**: Assumes agents answer preference queries truthfully; doesn't handle strategic manipulation or preference misreporting.

## Open Questions
1. **Can this be extended to one-to-many or many-to-many matching** (e.g., students choosing multiple universities, hospitals hiring multiple residents)? Does pervasive stable matching still apply?

2. **How does computational complexity scale** with the number of agents? Are there approximation algorithms that trade off sample complexity for computation time?

3. **What if preferences change over time** (non-stationary markets)? Does the algorithm gracefully adapt or need full relearning?

4. **Can you incorporate side information or features** (e.g., candidate resume similarity, job description similarity) to reduce sample complexity — essentially, a hybrid of preference learning and content-based matching?

5. **Empirical validation**: How does this algorithm perform on real marketplace data (job matching, dating, housing) compared to current heuristics? What's the actual sample complexity in practice?

6. **Strategic robustness**: What happens if agents strategically misreport preferences or collude? Can the algorithm be made robust to manipulation?

## Key Takeaway
This paper shows how to provably and efficiently learn the best stable matching in two-sided markets with unknown preferences on both sides — a theoretically sound framework for real-world matching platforms that must learn and optimize simultaneously.
