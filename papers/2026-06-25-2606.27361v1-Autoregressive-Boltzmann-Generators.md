# Autoregressive Boltzmann Generators

**Authors:** Danyal Rehman, Charlie B. Tan, Yoshua Bengio, Avishek Joey Bose, Alexander Tong
**Published:** 2026-06-25 | **arXiv:** [2606.27361v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27361v1)

## Summary

This paper introduces Autoregressive Boltzmann Generators (ArBG), a new generative modeling framework for efficiently sampling molecular systems at thermodynamic equilibrium. By replacing the normalizing flow architecture typically used in Boltzmann Generators with an autoregressive approach, ArBG overcomes expressivity and computational bottlenecks, achieving state-of-the-art performance on peptide sampling tasks and demonstrating significant improvements over existing flow-based methods.

## Theoretical Background

**Boltzmann Generators** are a recent framework combining generative models with importance sampling to draw uncorrelated equilibrium samples from molecular systems. They rely on computing exact likelihoods under the model to correct for distribution mismatch via reweighting. Traditional BGs use **normalizing flows (NFs)**, which map data to a simple base distribution through a sequence of invertible transformations. However, NFs face a fundamental trade-off: discrete-time flows (e.g., coupling layers) are fast but expressively limited due to strict invertibility constraints, while continuous-time flows (Neural ODEs) offer greater expressivity but require expensive likelihood computations via trace calculations.

The paper builds on:
- Classical statistical physics and importance sampling theory
- Modern generative modeling (flows, autoregressive models)
- Large Language Model architectures (which have proven highly effective in sequential generation tasks)

## Core Contributions

1. **Architectural Departure**: Proposes replacing flow-based BGs with an autoregressive framework that avoids invertibility constraints entirely, enabling richer function families without topological limitations.

2. **Inference-Time Interventions**: ArBG allows sequential, flexible interventions during generation (conditioned sampling, iterative refinement) that are difficult or impossible in flow models.

3. **Scalability via LLM Architectures**: Leverages transformer-like designs proven effective in language modeling, enabling practical scaling to larger systems.

4. **Robin Model**: Introduces a 132M parameter transferable model trained with ArBG that achieves 60%+ improvement in zero-shot energy error (E-W₂ metric) on 8-residue peptides over prior state-of-the-art.

5. **Empirical Validation**: Comprehensive benchmarking showing consistent improvements over flow-based BGs, especially pronounced on larger systems (e.g., 10-residue Chignolin).

## Connections to Other Work

This work sits at the intersection of several fields:

- **[[Normalizing Flows]]**: Directly challenges the flow-based paradigm for molecular sampling by demonstrating autoregressive alternatives.
- **[[Generative Models]]**: Bridges discrete generative modeling (autoregressive) with continuous physical systems.
- **[[Importance Sampling]]**: Inherits the importance sampling correction mechanism from BG literature but applies it to autoregressive distributions.
- **[[Transformers]] / Large Language Models**: Adopts architectural lessons from NLP, suggesting that sequential generation with attention mechanisms transfers to molecular domains.
- **Molecular Dynamics & MCMC**: Offers a complementary approach to traditional equilibrium sampling methods, potentially faster for large systems.
- **Energy-Based Models**: Related to score-based/diffusion approaches, though this paper uses explicit likelihood and importance weighting.

## Practical Applications

1. **Drug Discovery**: Faster conformational sampling of peptides and proteins accelerates virtual screening and binding affinity estimation.

2. **Materials Science**: Efficient sampling of molecular crystal or polymer configurations for property prediction and optimization.

3. **Protein Structure Prediction**: Sampling plausible conformations as a post-processing step for structure prediction models (e.g., AlphaFold variants), improving confidence in equilibrium properties.

4. **Computational Chemistry**: Reducing wall-clock time for molecular simulations in force-field parameterization and thermodynamic calculations.

5. **Transferable Foundation Models**: Robin and similar pre-trained models could serve as drop-in replacements in molecular simulation pipelines, similar to how pretrained LLMs reduce fine-tuning cost.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Limited to Equilibrium Sampling**: ArBG is designed for equilibrium systems; non-equilibrium dynamics or kinetics are outside scope.

2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: Like all deep generative models, ArBG likely requires careful tuning; sensitivity to hyperparameters and training data not fully explored.

3. **Transferability Boundaries**: Robin is trained on specific systems and force fields. Generalization to unseen molecular types, larger proteins, or different force fields is unclear.

4. **Computational Cost of Likelihood**: Autoregressive models require sequential forward passes; sampling speed per molecule may still be slower than simple MCMC for very small systems.

5. **Importance Sampling Variance**: If the learned model diverges far from the target distribution, importance weights can have high variance, reducing effective sample size. Paper does not deeply analyze weight distributions.

6. **Scalability to Large Proteins**: Experiments focus on peptides (8-10 residues). Scaling to full proteins or multi-chain complexes remains unexplored.

7. **Comparison Fairness**: Flow-based baselines may not represent recent advances in continuous normalizing flows; comparison could be strengthened.

## Open Questions

1. **Scalability**: How does ArBG perform on realistic protein-sized systems (50+ residues)? What is the computational cost scaling?

2. **Cross-Domain Transfer**: Can models trained on one protein family or force field transfer to distant domains without retraining?

3. **Generative Capacity**: What is the learned model's actual coverage of the target Boltzmann distribution? Are there modes missed?

4. **Conditioning and Control**: Can ArBG be effectively conditioned on properties (e.g., generate low-energy conformations satisfying distance constraints)?

5. **Uncertainty Quantification**: Can the model provide reliable confidence intervals or calibrated uncertainties on sampled energies?

6. **Hybrid Approaches**: Would combining ArBG with lightweight MCMC refinement further improve quality/speed trade-offs?

7. **Mechanistic Understanding**: Which architectural components (attention, depth, width) are most critical? Ablation studies would strengthen the contribution.

## Key Takeaway

Autoregressive models can outperform normalizing flows for molecular equilibrium sampling by trading invertibility for expressivity and enabling flexible sequential generation, opening a new paradigm for combining deep learning with statistical physics.
