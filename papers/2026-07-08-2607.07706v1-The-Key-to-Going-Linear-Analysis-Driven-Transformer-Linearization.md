# The Key to Going Linear: Analysis-Driven Transformer Linearization

**Authors:** Anna Kuzina, Paul N. Whatmough, Babak Ehteshami Bejnordi
**Published:** 2026-07-08 | **arXiv:** [2607.07706v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07706v1)

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental bottleneck in transformer inference: the quadratic computational cost of self-attention when processing long contexts. The authors analyze what makes attention mechanisms work, then systematically redesign their components to achieve linear-time inference while preserving model quality. They demonstrate their approach scales to 32-billion-parameter models and matches the performance of more complex adaptive caching systems.

## Theoretical Background

Transformers rely on self-attention — a mechanism where each token attends to all previous tokens by computing similarity scores (using softmax) and aggregating information. This is powerful but expensive: computing attention over a sequence of length *n* requires *O(n²)* operations and memory. 

Recent work has proposed "linearization" — replacing softmax attention with simpler, linear-time alternatives (often called linear transformers or state-space models). However, these alternatives typically degrade model quality, and it's been unclear which architectural choices matter most. This paper takes a mechanistic approach: rather than apply post hoc fixes, it analyzes *why* softmax works and uses that insight to design linearized variants that preserve its essential properties.

## Core Contributions

1. **Mechanistic analysis of softmax**: The authors prove that softmax attention performs key-dependent, rank-1 orthogonal projections — a structural insight explaining why purely linear accumulation (gating) underperforms delta-style networks (which combine additive and gated paths).

2. **Targeted linearization interventions**: Rather than replacing attention entirely, they propose three concrete fixes: sink tokens (learnable tokens that absorb off-topic attention), short convolutions (to capture local context without quadratic cost), and fixed-budget cache routing (selecting which tokens to keep in a finite cache based on learnable routing).

3. **Empirical validation at scale**: They scale the approach to LLaMA and Qwen models (up to 32B parameters) and benchmark on MMLU (knowledge evaluation) and long-context retrieval tasks, showing competitive or superior results to post hoc baselines.

## Connections to Other Work

This builds on the emerging literature of [[Linear Transformers]] and [[State-Space Models]] (e.g., Mamba), which seek subquadratic inference. It differs by grounding linearization in mechanistic analysis rather than pure empiricism. The work also relates to [[Attention Is All You Need]] and modern understanding of what makes attention special, as well as to [[Sparse Attention]] methods that try to approximate full attention with fewer operations. The sink token idea echoes techniques in [[Mixture of Experts]] routing and cache management in systems like vLLM.

## Practical Applications

1. **Long-document processing**: Enabling LLMs to ingest full books, codebases, or datasets in a single context window for summarization or search.

2. **Real-time inference on edge devices**: Linear-time attention reduces latency and memory footprint, making it feasible to run billion-parameter models on mobile or embedded hardware.

3. **Continuous stream processing**: Customer service bots, live transcription systems, or monitoring systems that must maintain unbounded context without reloading the model.

4. **Cost reduction in cloud inference**: Lower compute cost per token for long-context tasks, directly improving margins for API providers (e.g., Claude, ChatGPT long-context tiers).

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **"LinearLLM API"**: A drop-in replacement inference service (like Together AI or Replicate) optimized for long-context tasks, charging per-token but with linear scaling instead of quadratic, allowing arbitrarily long inputs at fixed cost.

2. **Embedded LLM runtime**: SDKs for on-device inference (mobile, IoT, edge compute) leveraging linear transformers to run 7B–32B models with sub-100ms latency and <2GB memory.

3. **Context-as-a-Service**: A caching layer that manages and routes long documents through linearized models, priced by cache size rather than queries, for knowledge bases and RAG systems.

4. **Research tool for mechanistic interpretability**: A commercial interpretability suite (similar to Anthropic's work) that applies analysis-driven linearization to make models more auditable and controllable.

5. **Financial/legal document analyzer**: A specialized SaaS for processing 10k SEC filings, contracts, or regulatory documents in full, leveraging cheap long-context inference for compliance or due diligence.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Frozen-backbone regime**: The analysis assumes pre-trained weights are fixed; it's unclear whether linear approximations hold or improve when fine-tuning on downstream tasks.

2. **Rank-1 approximation assumption**: Real softmax attention may deviate from rank-1 behavior in certain layers or domains; the extent to which sink tokens and convolutions fully recover this is empirically validated but not theoretically guaranteed.

3. **Cache routing overhead**: Fixed-budget cache routing requires learnable routing decisions; this adds a new set of hyperparameters and may not generalize across different sequence length distributions or domains.

4. **Limited long-context benchmarks**: MMLU and retrieval tasks are standard but relatively narrow. Behavior on truly long-context reasoning (e.g., multi-hop reasoning over 100k+ tokens) is not extensively tested.

5. **Comparison fairness**: Baselines are prior post hoc methods; direct comparison with other mechanistic or linear-attention designs (e.g., Mamba, S4) at the same scale is absent.

## Open Questions

1. How does linearization interact with [[RLHF]] or fine-tuning? Does the rank-1 structure hold or degrade during alignment?

2. Can the analysis extend beyond causal attention to prefix/non-causal settings (e.g., BERT-style encoders)?

3. What is the theoretical approximation error bound as a function of cache size and sequence length?

4. How sensitive are sink tokens and routing to the choice of initialization and training schedule?

5. Can similar mechanistic insights be applied to other quadratic bottlenecks (e.g., [[cross-attention]] in sequence-to-sequence models or video transformers)?

6. How does the approach perform on tasks requiring very long-range dependencies (e.g., code completion, theorem proving)?

## Key Takeaway

By analyzing the structure of softmax attention mathematically, rather than treating linearization as a purely empirical problem, this paper achieves linear-time inference that rivals standard transformers—showing that principled mechanistic understanding can outperform post hoc engineering.
