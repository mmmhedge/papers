# Autorelevance function and other feature relevance measures for univariate time series

**Authors:** Julian Cardenas, Jamie Arjona, Pedro Delicado
**Published:** 2026-07-02 | **arXiv:** [2607.01959v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01959v1)

## Summary

This paper introduces methods to measure which past time steps (lags) matter most for predicting future values in time series forecasting. The authors combine ideas from explainable AI—specifically Shapley values and "ghost variables"—to create relevance scores that work across any forecasting model, from classical statistical models to neural networks. This matters because understanding which historical data points drive predictions can improve model design, reduce unnecessary features, and build trust in automated forecasting systems.

## Theoretical Background

The paper builds on several established ideas. Shapley values (from cooperative game theory) assign each feature a "contribution score" to a model's prediction by averaging its impact across all possible feature combinations. Ghost variables are a technique where missing features are replaced with synthetic values (often from a learned imputation) rather than discarded, preserving the coalition structure. Additive importance measures assume a model's output can be decomposed into independent feature contributions. The authors extend these frameworks to time series, where the key innovation is treating lags (past values at time t-1, t-2, etc.) as features and defining how to handle "absent" lags in coalition-based methods using one-step-ahead forecasts from the model itself.

## Core Contributions

1. **Autorelevance and partial autorelevance functions**: Formal definitions of lag importance tailored to time series, where lags are naturally ordered and dependent.

2. **Novel coalition handling for time series**: Instead of using fixed imputation values for missing lags in Shapley calculations, the authors propose using one-step forecasts from the same model. This is more principled for sequential data where future predictions depend on learned dynamics.

3. **Model-agnostic empirical validation**: Demonstration that these measures recover known lag structures in both simulated data and real cases, across both ARIMA-family models and RNNs, showing the methods work regardless of model type.

4. **Unified framework perspective**: Positioning ghost variables, Shapley values, and additive measures as complementary tools specifically suited for time series interpretation.

## Connections to Other Work

This work sits at the intersection of [[interpretability]] (understanding what models learn) and [[time series forecasting]]. It extends Shapley-value-based interpretation methods (popular in [[SHAP]] and similar frameworks) to the temporal domain, where feature interactions are structural rather than arbitrary. The use of one-step forecasts for coalition imputation is conceptually related to [[causal inference]] approaches in time series, though not explicitly framed that way. The paper also implicitly addresses the challenge of [[feature selection]] in forecasting—understanding relevance helps identify which lags to include.

## Practical Applications

1. **Demand forecasting**: Retailers can identify whether weekly, monthly, or seasonal patterns drive predictions, enabling inventory planning targeted at relevant horizons.

2. **Energy load forecasting**: Utilities can determine whether weather-lag effects dominate or recent consumption patterns matter more, guiding sensor placement and data collection.

3. **Financial risk models**: Banks can measure which historical volatility or price-lag windows contribute most to Value-at-Risk estimates, validating regulatory compliance.

4. **Epidemiological forecasting**: Public health analysts can identify whether disease forecasts rely primarily on recent case counts or older historical trends, informing intervention timing.

5. **Model debugging**: Machine learning engineers can detect when a model over-relies on superficial patterns (e.g., day-of-week artifacts) rather than fundamental drivers, triggering feature engineering or data quality reviews.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **Time Series Interpretability-as-a-Service**: A cloud platform where users upload forecasting models and datasets; the system computes lag relevance, generates visual reports showing which time windows matter, and flags suspicious dependencies (e.g., over-reliance on noise).

2. **Automated Feature Engineering for Forecasting**: A tool that uses autorelevance scores to suggest optimal lag sets and interactions, automatically retraining models with pruned features to reduce compute and improve generalization.

3. **Forecasting Model Governance Dashboard**: Enterprise software for regulated industries (finance, energy, pharma) that continuously monitors whether model predictions rely on stable, interpretable lags or drift into unexplainable behavior—alerting analysts when relevance changes.

4. **Academic Benchmarking Platform**: A repository and competition framework where researchers submit time series forecasting models, and the platform automatically computes and compares lag relevance profiles across architectures, building a curated database of "what works for which problems."

5. **Explainable IoT Analytics**: A middleware layer for industrial IoT systems that ingests sensor streams, forecasts equipment failures or anomalies, and explains predictions in business terms ("failures predicted mainly from temperature lag-2, not vibration"), enabling faster diagnostics.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Computational cost**: Shapley values require many model evaluations (exponential in lag depth for exact computation); the paper doesn't discuss scalability to very long time series or approximation trade-offs.

2. **Assumption of stationarity or weak trend**: The methods assume lag relevance is interpretable; in highly non-stationary systems, relevance may shift over time, and the paper doesn't address how to detect or handle this.

3. **One-step forecast as imputation**: Replacing missing lags with model predictions introduces a subtle circularity—relevance scores depend on how the model learns to forecast, which may bias results toward lags the model already relies on. No validation of this bias is provided.

4. **Limited model diversity**: Evaluation focuses on ARIMA and RNNs; modern architectures (Transformers for time series, attention-based models, diffusion models) are not tested, leaving generalization unclear.

5. **No multivariate extension**: The paper explicitly addresses univariate series; extending to multivariate forecasting (where lag relevance across variables matters) is non-trivial and not explored.

6. **Interpretation over prediction**: High relevance scores don't guarantee predictive power; the paper shows recovered lag structures match intuition but doesn't compare prediction accuracy to other feature selection methods.

## Open Questions

1. **Computational approximations**: Can the authors propose and validate efficient Shapley approximations (e.g., sampling-based) for long time series without sacrificing insight?

2. **Non-stationary dynamics**: How should autorelevance functions be defined or estimated when lag dependencies change over time? Can local or adaptive versions be developed?

3. **Causal interpretation**: Under what conditions does high autorelevance imply causal influence rather than mere correlation? Can the framework integrate causal inference tools?

4. **Multivariate extension**: How do cross-lag relevance and lagged-cross-variable dependencies generalize the framework? Would separate univariate analyses suffice, or is joint measurement necessary?

5. **Comparison to alternatives**: How do these Shapley-based measures compare to classical lag selection methods (ACF, PACF) and modern deep-learning feature importance (attention weights, gradient-based saliency) in terms of computational cost, stability, and actionability?

6. **Real-time / streaming setting**: Can relevance be updated incrementally as new observations arrive, and how sensitive are scores to the choice of training window?

## Key Takeaway

Lag relevance in time series forecasting can be rigorously measured using Shapley values and one-step forecast imputation, offering a model-agnostic, interpretable alternative to black-box forecasting.
