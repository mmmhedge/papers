# Digital Pantheon: Simulating and Auditing Coalition Formation with LLM Agents

**Authors:** Dylan Van Mulders, Matthias Bogaert, Dirk Van den Poel
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.15095v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15095v1)

## Summary
This paper builds a multi-agent simulation where LLM-based agents representing political parties negotiate to form coalitions, mirroring real-world government formation. The authors solve a critical problem: standard LLMs trained with RLHF are too neutral and helpful to sustain partisan positions needed for realistic political negotiation. They introduce interpretability tools (lineage tracing, influence scoring) that make the emergent agreements auditable and grounded in party manifestos.

## Theoretical Background
Coalition formation is a classic problem in computational social science and game theory — how do multiple rational agents with conflicting preferences reach binding agreements? Large Language Models (LLMs) — neural networks trained on vast text corpora to predict and generate language — have recently enabled new approaches to agent-based simulation because they can engage in natural dialogue and reasoning. However, RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) — a technique that fine-tunes models using human preferences to reduce harmful outputs — introduces a neutrality bias that conflicts with the adversarial, ideologically steadfast behaviour required in political negotiation. This paper combines three techniques to address this: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) adapts models to specific tasks, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) instils strong partisan personas, and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) — fetching relevant documents to ground model outputs — ensures agents stay faithful to official party positions.

## Core Contributions
1. **Partisan Agent Framework**: A three-stage fine-tuning pipeline (SFT + DPO + per-party RAG) that overcomes RLHF neutrality to create genuinely partisan LLM agents capable of sustained ideological commitment.

2. **Multi-Layered Information Lineage Topology (MILT)**: An interpretability method that traces every clause in a negotiated agreement back to its source party manifesto and classifies it into five provenance states (e.g., faithful, modified, hallucinated), answering *which party shaped the final outcome*.

3. **Coalition Influence Score (CIS)**: A quantitative metric that aggregates MILT lineage data to rank parties by their actual influence on the agreement.

4. **Real-World Grounding Validation**: A benchmarking pass comparing simulated provisions against the historically adopted 2019 Flemish coalition agreement, showing that manifesto-anchored content predicts real materialization while hallucinations do not.

5. **Empirical Stability**: Across three independent simulations of the 2019 Flemish election, the framework produces consistent winner rankings (N-VA > CD&V > Open Vld), demonstrating repeatability.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[multi-agent reinforcement learning]], computational political science, and [[mechanistic interpretability]]. It addresses a fundamental challenge: how to align LLM agents toward specific objectives (here, partisan ideology) without sacrificing the neutrality that [[RLHF]] was designed to achieve — a tension also explored in [[alignment]] research. The lineage tracing approach parallels work on [[attention]] visualization and model attribution in [[transformers]], but applies it to emergent multi-agent negotiation outcomes. It extends earlier agent-based political simulations by replacing hand-coded heuristics with learned, dialogue-capable agents grounded in real documents.

## Practical Applications
- **Electoral Commission & Democratic Analysis**: Governments and electoral commissions can use this to audit coalition negotiations ex-ante, understanding which parties have negotiating leverage and where compromises are likely.
- **Policy Impact Forecasting**: Think tanks and civil society organizations can simulate how their policy proposals would fare in real coalition negotiations.
- **Institutional Design Testing**: Constitutional reformers and parliamentarians can test alternative formateur selection rules, negotiation structures, or voting thresholds before implementing them.
- **Journalism & Public Transparency**: News organizations can run simulations to explain to the public which party priorities likely shaped a final coalition agreement, with manifesto-backed evidence.
- **International Diplomacy**: Similar frameworks could simulate multilateral treaty negotiations, trade deals, or UN consensus-building.

## Technical Limitations
- **Manifesto Completeness**: RAG grounds agents in official manifestos, but these documents may be vague, aspirational, or silent on critical negotiation issues. Agents still face off-manifesto decisions where hallucination is possible.
- **Formateur Design**: The hub-and-spoke negotiation model (one formateur mediating all parties) may not reflect real multi-party back-channel deals or asymmetric coalition power dynamics.
- **Persona Stability**: DPO instils partisan personas, but it is unclear how robust these are to adversarial prompts or whether they degrade over extended multi-turn negotiations.
- **Real-World Validation**: The paper validates against one historical election (2019 Flanders). Generalization to other parliaments, electoral systems, or cultural contexts is untested.
- **Computational Cost**: Scaling to large parliaments (e.g., Germany's Bundestag) with many parties and complex manifesto RAG pipelines may become expensive.
- **Attribution Ambiguity**: MILT's five provenance states are defined post-hoc; it is not clear whether human annotators would agree, or whether the classification is reproducible across different agreements.

## Open Questions
- **Can partisan agents sustain coherent ideology under real negotiation pressure?** Does DPO-induced partisanship erode when agents face repeated concessions or time pressure?
- **How does agent ordering and negotiation structure affect outcomes?** Does the choice of formateur or negotiation sequence systematically bias results? Can you design mechanisms to minimize this bias?
- **Can MILT generalize to other domains?** Does manifesto-anchored lineage work for treaty negotiations, corporate board decisions, or other multi-party settings where alignment matters?
- **What is the ground-truth benchmark for "good" coalition prediction?** The paper validates against one historical outcome, but are there other elections where the framework could be tested rigorously?
- **How do you handle ideological drift within parties over time?** If manifestos are 4 years old and public opinion has shifted, do agents remain credible negotiators?
- **Can you extend this to explain *why* certain coalitions form?** Current work shows *which party influenced what*, but not *what constraints or preferences made certain coalitions impossible*.

## Key Takeaway
LLM agents can simulate realistic coalition negotiations if grounded in party manifestos and audited for where each policy came from.
