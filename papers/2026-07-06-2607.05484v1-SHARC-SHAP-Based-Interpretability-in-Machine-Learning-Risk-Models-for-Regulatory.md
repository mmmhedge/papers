# SHARC: SHAP-Based Interpretability in Machine Learning Risk Models for Regulatory Capital under ICAAP and CCAR

**Authors:** Ujjwala Vadrevu
**Published:** 2026-07-06 | **arXiv:** [2607.05484v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05484v1)

## Summary
This paper solves a critical regulatory problem: machine learning models like Gaussian Process Regression predict financial risk better than traditional methods, but regulators can't audit them because they're "black boxes." The authors apply SHAP (a game-theory-based explanation technique) to make these models transparent, decomposing stress-test capital requirements into interpretable components that regulators can trace and validate.

## Theoretical Background
Financial institutions use stress testing under ICAAP (Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process) and CCAR (Comprehensive Capital Analysis and Review) to estimate capital buffers needed for extreme scenarios. Traditionally, these estimates come from parametric models (linear equations with known coefficients) that regulators can inspect. Gaussian Process Regression—a non-parametric machine learning method that learns flexible patterns from data without assuming a fixed formula—produces better predictions but is opaque. SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) comes from cooperative game theory: it treats each input feature as a "player" and calculates how much each contributes to the model's output while satisfying mathematical fairness axioms (Local Accuracy, Missingness, Consistency, Efficiency). This paper applies SHAP to Stressed Value-at-Risk (SVaR)—a measure of maximum loss under stress—to make GPR capital estimates auditable.

## Core Contributions
1. **SHARC framework**: A novel application of SHAP specifically designed for regulatory capital workflows, decomposing SVaR into three interpretable components: baseline, mean-driven (directional loss), and volatility-driven (uncertainty).
2. **Empirical finding on capital drivers**: Under stress, mean return (directional losses) dominates variance (volatility) in determining capital levels—a quantitative insight with strategic implications for hedging and position limits.
3. **Regulatory alignment**: Demonstrates that the hybrid GPR-HS architecture with SHARC overlay satisfies FRTB (Fundamental Review of Trading Book), ICAAP Pillar 2, and CCAR transparency requirements, removing a major adoption barrier.
4. **Three stress-test case studies**: Validation across West Asia War, Climate Risk, and AI Bubble scenarios, proving framework fidelity under diverse macro conditions.

## Connections to Other Work
This work bridges [[mechanistic-interpretability]] (explaining what black-box models actually do) with [[risk-management]] practice. It complements recent fintech efforts to use ML in capital estimation (e.g., deep learning for market risk) but focuses on the governance layer that has prevented adoption. The use of SHAP relates to broader [[alignment]] concerns in machine learning—ensuring model decisions match human values, here extended to regulatory requirements. Unlike post-hoc explanations for credit scoring or hiring (common SHAP applications), this targets systematic risk under extreme scenarios, where auditability is legally mandated, not optional.

## Practical Applications
1. **Regulatory capital optimization**: Banks can adopt faster, more accurate ML models for stress testing without losing supervisor approval—reducing capital buffers by 5–10% through better predictions while maintaining compliance.
2. **Internal risk dashboard**: Risk officers and traders gain actionable insight into which market scenarios or positions drive capital requirements, enabling data-driven hedging decisions.
3. **Regulator-bank dialogue**: SHARC outputs (decomposed SVaR drivers) become the common language for regulators and financial institutions in stress-test reviews, reducing back-and-forth and accelerating approval cycles.
4. **Climate & scenario risk governance**: Banks can transparently link climate scenarios (e.g., carbon price paths) to capital outcomes, supporting ESG disclosures and internal risk governance.

## Technical Limitations
1. **SHAP computational cost**: For large portfolios, exact SHAP can be slow; the paper doesn't address scalability to millions of positions or real-time stress testing.
2. **Hybrid GPR-HS specificity**: SHARC is tailored to this architecture; adapting to other ML models (neural networks, random forests) requires re-validation and may not satisfy regulators equally.
3. **Scenario selection bias**: The three macro scenarios (West Asia War, Climate Risk, AI Bubble) are illustrative; real-world CCAR involves 50+ historical and hypothetical scenarios—unclear how SHARC generalizes.
4. **Linear decomposition assumption**: Decomposing SVaR into mean vs. volatility components assumes additive separability; nonlinear interactions (e.g., mean-variance correlation shifts) may not be fully captured.
5. **Regulator acceptance**: SHAP satisfies mathematical axioms, but individual regulators (Fed, OCC, FDIC) may have their own interpretability standards—this paper doesn't guarantee approval, only addresses the governance barrier.

## Open Questions
1. How does SHARC perform under extreme tail events or unprecedented scenarios (e.g., post-pandemic tail risk)? Do explanations break down or remain stable?
2. Can SHARC be extended to **dynamic** capital models where weights and correlations shift with market regime, or is it limited to static stress snapshots?
3. What is the trade-off between SHAP's fairness axioms and faster approximation methods (SHAP TreeExplainer, KernelSHAP variants)? Which approximation is "regulator-acceptable"?
4. How do SHARC explanations interact with **model retraining cycles**? If the GPR model is updated monthly, do explanations remain stable, or do capital drivers shift unpredictably?
5. Can this framework be applied to **interconnected risks** (e.g., collateral haircuts linked to volatility spikes linked to geopolitical shocks)? The current decomposition is additive.

## Key Takeaway
By applying game-theoretic explainability (SHAP) to machine learning risk models, this paper removes a major regulatory barrier to adoption, allowing banks to use faster, more accurate ML for capital estimation while maintaining full auditability and compliance with supervisory requirements.
