# Co-Optimization of Analog Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks for Low-Power Function Approximation in Flexible Electronics

**Authors:** Paula Carolina Lozano Duarte, Georgios Zervakis, Mehdi Tahoori, Sani Nassif
**Published:** 2026-06-26 | **arXiv:** [2606.27892v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27892v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces Analog Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (AKANs), a hardware-software co-optimized approach for implementing complex nonlinear functions directly in analog circuits for wearable and IoT devices. By combining circuit-level error modeling with pruning strategies, the method achieves 30–55% reductions in area and power consumption while maintaining approximation accuracy on flexible electronics platforms where traditional digital processing is prohibitively expensive.

## Theoretical Background
Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs) are neural network architectures based on the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem, which states that any continuous multivariate function can be represented as a composition of univariate functions and addition. Unlike traditional MLPs that learn weights on fixed activation functions, KANs learn spline-based univariate functions directly, making them expressive yet potentially more interpretable. Flexible electronics are ultra-thin, mechanically compliant substrates (often plastic or rubber) that enable wearable sensors and IoT devices but impose severe constraints on circuit complexity and power dissipation. Analog function approximation leverages the physics of analog circuits to compute functions with minimal digital overhead—a critical advantage when power budgets are measured in microwatts. This work bridges these domains: using KAN's expressive function class in the analog domain, while incorporating hardware-aware constraints (transistor mismatches, supply noise) directly into the training loop.

## Core Contributions
1. **Analog KAN architecture**: Extends KANs to analog circuit implementations, not just digital networks—a novel application domain requiring circuit-level error modeling.
2. **Hardware-software co-optimization**: Integrates realistic analog circuit imperfections (component mismatches, nonlinearities) into the training process, ensuring learned spline parameters remain robust to fabrication variability.
3. **Dual-level pruning methodology**: Applies pruning at both software (network structure) and hardware (circuit elements) levels, demonstrating that pruning can act as a regularizer and improve generalization accuracy, not just reduce complexity.
4. **Validated on flexible electronics**: Demonstrates practical feasibility on actual constrained platforms, with benchmarks showing 30–55% area/power savings across multiple function approximation tasks.

## Practical Applications
1. **Wearable biosignal processing**: On-skin ECG/EMG sensors applying nonlinear feature extraction (logarithmic compression, RMS calculation) without power-hungry ADCs.
2. **Sensor calibration in IoT**: Temperature/humidity/pressure sensors implementing inverse mapping functions (raw ADC counts → physical units) directly in analog frontend, reducing calibration data memory.
3. **Flexible implantable devices**: Bioelectronic implants performing local signal preprocessing (noise filtering, power-law compression) with micropower budgets.
4. **Low-power edge AI**: Inference of activation functions (ReLU, sigmoid, tanh) for ultra-low-power neural networks on edge devices where even a small digital multiply-accumulate is expensive.
5. **Auditory processing in hearing aids**: Frequency-dependent gain and dynamic range compression for audio signals in form-fitting wearables.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability to high-dimensional functions**: KANs themselves struggle with high-dimensional inputs due to curse of dimensionality; the paper doesn't clearly bound dimensionality or discuss multi-layer compositions.
2. **Accuracy vs. power trade-offs not fully characterized**: The paper shows average 30% power savings but doesn't provide Pareto frontiers showing what accuracy is sacrificed per dB of power reduction.
3. **Process/temperature variability**: Hardware imperfections are modeled during training, but robustness to PVT (process, voltage, temperature) corners is only implicitly claimed. No Monte Carlo fabrication simulations shown.
4. **Limited to smooth functions**: Discontinuous or sharp-edge functions (hard thresholds, clipping) may not be well-suited to spline-based approximation.
5. **Comparison baseline weak**: No direct head-to-head against classical analog circuits (op-amp comparators, transconductance stages) or other recent analog ML approaches; the baseline appears to be pure digital.
6. **Flexible electronics maturity**: Thin-film transistors and printed circuits have lower yield and worse matching than bulk CMOS; scaling to production volumes is unproven.

## Open Questions
1. How does approximation error scale with function dimensionality? Can hierarchical or modular KANs handle 10+dimensional sensor fusion tasks?
2. What is the robustness margin under process variation? Require PVT-corner simulations or measured silicon data from actual fabrication.
3. Can online learning or in-situ tuning recalibrate the analog KAN post-deployment as devices age?
4. How do mixed-signal hybrid approaches (partial analog, partial digital) compare—when is pure analog better?
5. What is the fundamental energy limit for a given approximation error tolerance? Can the paper provide information-theoretic bounds?
6. Does the regularization benefit of pruning generalize to other domains (computer vision, NLP), or is it specific to smooth function approximation?

## Key Takeaway
Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks, when co-optimized with analog circuit physics during training, can deliver 30–55% power savings for nonlinear function approximation in power-constrained wearables and IoT, turning a fundamentally digital architecture into a hardware-aware analog primitive.
