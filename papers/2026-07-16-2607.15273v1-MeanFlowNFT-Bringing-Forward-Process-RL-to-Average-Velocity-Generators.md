# MeanFlowNFT: Bringing Forward-Process RL to Average-Velocity Generators

**Authors:** Yushi Huang, Xiangxin Zhou, Jun Zhang, Liefeng Bo, Tianyu Pang
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.15273v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15273v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces MeanFlowNFT, a method that combines reinforcement learning with fast image and video generators called MeanFlow models. The key innovation is making it possible to use RL (which typically requires fine-grained velocity predictions) with MeanFlow generators (which use average velocities to sample quickly). The result: better-quality images and videos in just a few generation steps, outperforming existing few-step methods while being much faster than traditional diffusion models.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two main foundations:

1. **MeanFlow generators** — a recent class of fast generative models that predict *average* velocities over time intervals rather than instantaneous velocities, enabling efficient few-step sampling. These are related to flow matching and diffusion models, which generate images by gradually transforming noise into coherent outputs.

2. **DiffusionNFT** — a forward-process reinforcement learning framework that directly optimizes diffusion/flow models using human reward signals (e.g., aesthetic quality, alignment with text prompts) without needing to estimate likelihoods or simulate reverse trajectories. DiffusionNFT works by treating sampling trajectories as RL policies and applying policy-gradient updates.

The core tension: DiffusionNFT optimizes *instantaneous* velocities, but MeanFlow generators operate on *average* velocities over time intervals. These are mathematically different, making it unclear how to apply DiffusionNFT rewards to MeanFlow.

## Core Contributions
1. **MeanFlowNFT algorithm**: A principled way to apply forward-process RL to average-velocity generators by constructing an induced instantaneous-velocity predictor that bridges the gap between average and instantaneous velocities (inspired by the MeanFlow identity).

2. **Theoretical guarantee**: Proof that MeanFlowNFT inherits DiffusionNFT's strict policy-improvement guarantee—meaning the RL optimization is provably monotonically improving, not just heuristic.

3. **Empirical superiority**: Demonstrations that MeanFlowNFT outperforms prior RL-tuned few-step generators on standard benchmarks (6 of 8 metrics on SD3.5-M), and notably achieves better video quality in 4 steps than 50-step diffusion RL baselines.

## Practical Applications
1. **Fast image-to-image editing** — Interactive design tools that refine images in 4–8 steps while respecting user preferences (text or reward signals).

2. **Real-time video generation** — Streaming video applications where full diffusion sampling is too slow; MeanFlowNFT enables quality video in milliseconds.

3. **Mobile/edge generation** — Deploying generative models on phones or low-power devices where few-step generation is essential.

4. **Personalized content creation** — Game engines, advertising platforms, or creative tools that generate images/videos tuned to specific quality metrics or brand guidelines via reward signals.

5. **Adaptive generative systems** — APIs that learn from user feedback in production and improve their outputs without full retraining.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **FastGen API** — A cloud service offering fast-image and fast-video generation as a low-latency alternative to existing APIs, priced per inference step to undercut competitors on speed-adjusted cost.

2. **BrandAI** — A tool for enterprises to fine-tune image/video generation to match brand aesthetics and tone, using MeanFlowNFT's reward-learning for zero-shot alignment without manual dataset curation.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Reward signal dependency** — The method's quality depends entirely on the quality and alignment of the reward signal. Poor or biased rewards lead to poor outputs, and defining good rewards for complex tasks remains an open problem.

2. **Limited to average-velocity generators** — The approach is specifically designed for MeanFlow and similar average-velocity models; applicability to other fast-generation paradigms (e.g., consistency models, latent diffusion) is unexplored.

3. **Scalability to very long sequences** — While video results are shown, extending to long-form video (minutes) without compounding error is not addressed.

4. **Computational overhead of induced predictor** — Constructing and maintaining the induced instantaneous-velocity predictor adds computational cost during RL training, though not sampling.

5. **Evaluation metrics** — Relies on automatic metrics (VBench, etc.) which may not capture perceptual quality or task-specific requirements humans care about.

6. **Generalization across domains** — Most experiments are on image and short video; generalization to 3D generation, audio, or other modalities is unclear.

## Open Questions
1. How does MeanFlowNFT scale to truly long-horizon video generation (minutes to hours)? Do policy-improvement guarantees hold?

2. Can the induced predictor be made more efficient to reduce RL training time?

3. How sensitive is the method to reward model errors or distribution shift in the reward signal?

4. Can MeanFlowNFT be combined with other efficiency techniques (quantization, distillation) for sub-millisecond sampling?

5. Does the framework extend to conditional generation with multiple reward objectives (aesthetic quality *and* content accuracy *and* efficiency)?

6. Can the average-velocity bridge be applied to other fast-generation families beyond MeanFlow?

## Key Takeaway
RL can now efficiently improve fast few-step image and video generators by bridging average and instantaneous velocity predictions, beating much slower alternatives.
