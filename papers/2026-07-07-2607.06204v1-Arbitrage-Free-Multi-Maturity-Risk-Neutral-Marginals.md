# Arbitrage-Free Multi-Maturity Risk-Neutral Marginals

**Authors:** Hao Qin, Ruozhong Yang, Charlie Che, Liming Feng
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06204v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06204v1)

## Summary
This paper solves a practical problem in quantitative finance: converting market option prices into risk-neutral probability distributions that are mathematically consistent (arbitrage-free) and computationally tractable. The authors propose an explicit construction method that works directly from discrete option prices, guarantees no arbitrage opportunities, and produces distributions with closed-form solutions for density, quantiles, and sampling — enabling downstream applications like scenario analysis and tail-risk measurement without compromise.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on several classical ideas in quantitative finance. **Risk-neutral marginals** are probability distributions consistent with observed option prices under the assumption that markets are fairly priced (no arbitrage). Prior work formulated specific problems using these marginals — for example, **martingale optimal transport** (finding extremal probability laws respecting price constraints) and **Bass local-volatility calibration** (fitting volatility surfaces to option data). However, existing methods typically optimize only some desired properties (e.g., exact price reproduction *or* arbitrage-freeness *or* computational efficiency), not all simultaneously. The paper also references **SSVI** (stochastic Skew-Aware Volatility Index), a parametric volatility model used for synthetic testing. The foundational constraint is that marginals must be "butterfly-arbitrage-free" (no profitable bets on option spreads) and "calendar-arbitrage-free" (no profitable strategies exploiting time decay).

## Core Contributions
1. **Explicit construction algorithm**: A closed-form method that builds piecewise-constant probability mass on observed strikes to exactly reproduce input option prices, then extends outside the observed range using power-law tails satisfying boundary conditions.
2. **Guaranteed arbitrage-freeness by design**: Both butterfly and calendar arbitrage are eliminated structurally (not post-checked), making the approach fundamentally safer than optimization-based methods.
3. **Complete analytical solution**: The resulting marginal admits closed-form density, CDF, quantiles, and efficient Monte Carlo sampling — eliminating numerical approximation burden for downstream users.
4. **Computational efficiency and robustness**: Demonstrated on both synthetic (SSVI) and real (S&P 500) data, with practical scalability.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[derivatives-pricing]] and optimal transport theory. It relates to [[market-microstructure]] research on arbitrage detection, and connects to broader [[risk-management]] frameworks that depend on tail-risk estimates (which require accurate marginals). The construction strategy is complementary to [[optimization]]-based approaches like inverse-problem formulations in calibration — rather than solving a constrained optimization problem, it uses algebraic and geometric reasoning to guarantee properties upfront. The power-law tail closure is standard in financial risk modeling (e.g., extreme-value theory) but here tailored to the marginal-construction context.

## Practical Applications
1. **Scenario generation for risk assessment**: Portfolio managers can now sample realistic price paths for stress testing without solving inverse problems.
2. **Tail-risk measurement**: Insurance companies and pension funds can compute Value-at-Risk and Expected Shortfall directly from market option quotes with provable consistency.
3. **Exotic option valuation**: Quants can use the exact marginals as inputs to Monte Carlo pricing of path-dependent derivatives, eliminating intermediate approximations.
4. **Volatility smile analysis**: Traders and risk teams can decompose observed option surfaces into marginal and dependence components for cleaner interpretation of market regime.
5. **Regulatory capital calculations**: Banks need arbitrage-free marginals for stress-testing; this method produces them without ad-hoc tuning, improving auditability.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Discrete strike data assumption**: The method requires a full slice of liquid option quotes across a continuous range of strikes. Sparse or illiquid regions (far out-of-the-money) require extrapolation; power-law tails may misfit in those zones.
2. **Single-maturity at a time**: The paper focuses on constructing marginals at each maturity independently; cross-maturity consistency (joint pathwise absence of arbitrage across time) is not addressed — calendar-arbitrage freeness is checked post-hoc, not structural.
3. **No dynamic calibration**: Once constructed, the marginal is static; the method does not address how to update it as new market data arrives while preserving smooth transitions.
4. **Bid-ask spread handling**: Real markets have bid-ask spreads; the method assumes exact observed prices, so robustness to small price perturbations is not characterized.
5. **Tail model misspecification**: Power-law tails are a strong parametric assumption; if realized price distributions have different tail behavior (e.g., exponential), the marginal may overestimate tail mass.

## Open Questions
1. **Multi-maturity consistency**: Can the construction be extended to simultaneously handle multiple maturities, ensuring no arbitrage across time *by design* (not post-check)?
2. **Adaptive tail calibration**: Can the power-law exponent be estimated from bid-ask spreads or extreme-value statistics rather than fixed a priori?
3. **Model uncertainty quantification**: How sensitive are downstream applications (e.g., exotic option prices) to the choice of tail shape? Can we characterize a confidence band?
4. **Integration with stochastic volatility**: Can this marginal construction be embedded into a dynamic stochastic-volatility framework (e.g., rough volatility) for real-time updating?
5. **Empirical performance on edge cases**: How does the method perform during market dislocations, volatility regime shifts, or liquidity crises when the SSVI assumption breaks down?

## Key Takeaway
This paper closes a critical gap in quantitative finance by providing a provably arbitrage-free, analytically tractable way to extract probability distributions directly from option market prices — enabling downstream applications (scenario analysis, risk measurement, derivative valuation) without mathematical compromise.
