# ANGLE: Angular Neural Generative Learning via Engression

**Authors:** Rajdeep Pathak, Archi Roy, Tanujit Chakraborty
**Published:** 2026-07-14 | **arXiv:** [2607.12833v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12833v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces ANGLE, a deep generative framework for predicting circular data (angles, directions) and their full probability distributions rather than just point estimates. Traditional regression fails on circular data because the geometric mean of angles is often meaningless; ANGLE learns the complete conditional distribution using a novel loss function and proves it has desirable mathematical properties like rotational invariance, with applications to pose estimation and wind prediction.

## Theoretical Background
Circular statistics deals with data on a circle (angles from 0 to 360°), which has a fundamentally different geometry than Euclidean space — the mean of 10° and 350° should be 0°, not 180°. Prior work in distributional regression (learning full conditional distributions rather than point predictions) has mostly assumed Euclidean outputs. The paper builds on generalized energy scores — loss functions that measure how well a learned distribution matches observed data — and extends them to circular geometry. Key prior frameworks include generative models (neural networks that learn to sample from complex distributions) and the concept of proper scoring rules (losses that reward accurate probability estimates).

## Core Contributions
1. **GCES loss function**: A generalized circular energy score tailored for angular data, proven to be strictly proper (rewards honest probability estimates) and rotationally equivariant (rotating all angles by the same amount doesn't change model behavior).
2. **Distributional regression on circles**: A lightweight deep generative framework that learns the full conditional distribution p(angle | covariates) rather than just the mean.
3. **Unified toolkit**: First treatment of three previously scattered problems in circular statistics — extrapolation (predicting beyond observed angle ranges), sufficient dimension reduction (finding minimal summaries of high-dimensional circular data), and conditional distribution equality testing (comparing two circular distributions).
4. **Theoretical guarantees**: Strict propriety of the loss and rotational equivariance of estimators are formally established.
5. **Flexible noise handling**: Supports both pre- and post-additive noise models for circular responses.

## Connections to Other Work
ANGLE extends [[generative-models]] — particularly normalizing flows and score-based models — to the circular domain. It relates to [[distributional regression]] and [[conditional density estimation]] but handles the geometric constraints of circular data that Euclidean methods ignore. The proper scoring rule perspective connects to work in uncertainty quantification and probabilistic forecasting. The object pose estimation application bridges to [[computer-vision]] tasks like [[3D pose estimation]] where outputs are naturally circular/rotational.

## Practical Applications
1. **Object pose estimation**: Predicting 3D orientation of objects in images for robotics, surveillance, and augmented reality — where rotation angles have circular geometry.
2. **Wind direction prediction**: Forecasting wind direction for renewable energy systems, where east and north are cardinal directions, not linear quantities.
3. **Marine navigation**: Predicting ship or aircraft heading/bearing given sea state and weather covariates.
4. **Protein structure**: Predicting dihedral angles (phi/psi angles) in proteins given sequence context in structural biology.
5. **Geological strikes**: Modeling fault line or rock layer orientation in seismic or drilling applications.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Computational cost unclear**: Paper doesn't report wall-clock training time, inference speed, or memory requirements compared to baselines — critical for production systems.
2. **Limited baseline comparisons**: No direct comparison to other circular regression methods; most baselines appear to be Euclidean methods (unfair) rather than state-of-the-art circular statistics approaches.
3. **Scalability to high dimensions**: Generative models often struggle with very high-dimensional circular covariate spaces; unclear how well this scales beyond the experiments shown.
4. **Parametric assumption in noise models**: Even with "pre-additive noise," some parametric structure is assumed; robustness to model misspecification not thoroughly tested.
5. **Data efficiency**: No analysis of sample complexity — how many observations are needed for reliable distribution learning at different angular precision levels?
6. **Multimodal degeneracies**: When the angular distribution has many modes, generative models can suffer from mode collapse; this risk is not discussed.

## Open Questions
1. How does ANGLE perform with very sparse angular labels or extreme class imbalance (e.g., predicting rare wind directions)?
2. Can the framework extend to non-circular manifolds (tori, spheres, hyperbolic spaces) that arise in 3D pose estimation and protein folding?
3. What is the sample complexity as a function of the target angular precision and modality count?
4. How does ANGLE compare to recent diffusion-based generative models adapted for circular data?
5. Can the framework incorporate domain constraints (e.g., "wind never shifts faster than 30° per hour") into the generative process?
6. How does rotational equivariance property translate to equivariance under other symmetries (e.g., reflections)?

## Key Takeaway
Deep generative framework learns full probability distributions over angles, not just point predictions, enabling robust uncertainty quantification for circular data in pose estimation and weather forecasting.
