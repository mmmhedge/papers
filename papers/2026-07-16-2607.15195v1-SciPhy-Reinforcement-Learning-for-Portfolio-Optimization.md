# SciPhy Reinforcement Learning for Portfolio Optimization

**Authors:** Igor Halperin, Andrey Itkin
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.15195v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15195v1)

## Summary
This paper applies physics-informed reinforcement learning to portfolio optimization for large institutional investors, combining offline learning from historical data with microstructure-aware execution costs. The key innovation is solving the optimal control problem (expressed as a Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation) directly from data using neural networks, then converting continuous trading rates into discrete target holdings to match real-world execution constraints. The method shows measurable improvements in risk-adjusted returns (Sharpe ratio) on a real ETF portfolio while keeping turnover and volatility under control.

## Theoretical Background
Portfolio optimization has long relied on mean-variance frameworks and dynamic programming in continuous time. The Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation is the continuous-time analog of Bellman's equation in discrete reinforcement learning — it expresses the value function as a partial differential equation that optimal policies must satisfy. Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) are a recent technique that embeds differential equations as constraints into neural network loss functions, allowing you to solve PDEs directly from data without traditional numerical solvers. Market microstructure models capture how large trades move prices; a quadratic price impact model assumes execution costs grow with the square of trade size. The paper bridges these by learning policies that respect both the control-theoretic optimality conditions and the real costs of moving the market.

## Core Contributions
1. **HJB-to-pathwise reduction**: Reformulates the stochastic HJB equation as a pathwise Hamilton-Jacobi equation, making it solvable via PINNs on historical trajectories rather than requiring value iteration in a full state space.
2. **Single offline sweep**: Eliminates iterative value/policy updates entirely—the optimal policy is learned in one pass through historical data, reducing computational burden and avoiding exploration-exploitation tradeoffs.
3. **Discrete control reformulation**: Recasts the continuous trading rate into a discrete target position, ensuring immediate signal response while accurately accounting for execution costs under a microstructure-grounded quadratic impact model.
4. **Gibbs policy distribution**: Outputs a learned probabilistic policy over holdings, making the framework robust to signal noise and enabling principled risk control.
5. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates meaningful out-of-sample Sharpe improvements on a 14-asset ETF portfolio with controlled volatility and turnover.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[Reinforcement Learning|reinforcement-learning]] for finance and [[Market Microstructure|market-microstructure]]. It extends traditional dynamic programming approaches (Bellman's equation) and recent PINN methods in scientific computing into the portfolio domain. Related work includes [[Deep Reinforcement Learning for Trading]] and [[Model-Free Control in Finance]], but differs by using offline learning from fixed data rather than online or simulation-based training. The discrete control reformulation echoes practical concerns in [[Algorithmic Trading|algorithmic-trading]] about execution realism. The pathwise HJB formulation is novel in finance, bridging control theory with modern deep learning.

## Practical Applications
1. **Multi-period rebalancing for asset managers**: Funds managing $10M–$1B+ can use learned policies to rebalance equity/ETF portfolios in a cost-aware, signal-respecting manner without manual tuning of rebalance thresholds.
2. **Signal monetization**: Quant researchers with proprietary signals can immediately translate them into execution strategies that account for realistic market impact, rather than using ad-hoc heuristics.
3. **Risk-controlled allocation**: Institutional investors can set volatility and turnover budgets a priori, and the learned policy respects them while maximizing risk-adjusted returns.
4. **Backtesting alternative**: Funds can replace simulation-based backtests with offline RL policies trained on historical data, reducing look-ahead bias and model risk.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Cloud-based portfolio optimizer SaaS**: Subscription service for mid-sized asset managers—ingest signal data and historical returns, get back optimal rebalancing policies with Sharpe ratio forecasts and cost projections. Charge per-asset or per-AUM basis.
2. **Signal-to-execution pipeline (B2B)**: Sell a fully automated system to quant shops: ingest alpha signal → learn cost-aware policy → execute. White-label for brokers or RIAs.
3. **Backtesting and simulation platform**: Replace traditional tools (Portfolio Lab, Aladdin) with a neural-network-powered engine that fits HJB equations to historical data, enabling faster, more realistic scenario analysis.
4. **Risk dashboard for allocators**: Real-time allocation optimizer embedded in portfolio management platforms, showing what-if scenarios under different volatility or turnover constraints, powered by the learned policy.
5. **Consulting/research service**: Audit existing portfolios against learned optimal policies; identify hidden costs and signal leakage; recommend rebalancing schedules and target holdings.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Offline data dependency**: The method only learns from historical state-action trajectories; if market regimes shift or new assets appear, retraining is required. No online adaptation.
2. **Quadratic impact assumption**: The quadratic price impact model is a simplification; real markets exhibit nonlinear, state-dependent, and time-varying impact. Liquidity crunches or thin order books break the model.
3. **Signal quality assumption**: Results rely on a well-engineered oracle signal; the framework does not generate signals, only optimizes execution. Poor signal input = poor policy output.
4. **Scalability to many assets**: Tested on 14 ETFs; PINNs may struggle with high-dimensional state spaces (100+ assets) due to curse of dimensionality.
5. **Transaction cost modeling**: Does not explicitly model bid-ask spreads, commission, or market-impact parameter uncertainty. These are lumped into the quadratic term.
6. **Concentration and constraints**: The paper does not clearly address sector constraints, position limits, or concentration thresholds—critical for real institutional portfolios.

## Open Questions
1. How does the method perform in out-of-sample regime changes (e.g., crisis periods, new correlation structures, liquidity drought)?
2. Can the pathwise HJB approach be extended to multi-agent settings where many funds execute similar signals simultaneously?
3. What is the sensitivity of the Sharpe improvement to the choice of PINN architecture, loss function weights, and signal engineering?
4. How do learned policies compare to recently published [[Deep Reinforcement Learning for Trading|DRL]] baselines and [[Model Predictive Control|MPC]] approaches on the same data?
5. Can you incorporate explicit hard constraints (e.g., max position, sector limits) into the HJB formulation and still solve efficiently?
6. Does the Gibbs policy distribution provide meaningful uncertainty quantification for allocators, or is it primarily useful for regularization?

## Key Takeaway
Physics-informed learning solves portfolio optimization by fitting the control equation directly to data, avoiding iteration and respecting real market costs.
