# Will Scaling Improve Social Simulation with LLMs?

**Authors:** Caleb Ziems, William Held, Su Doga Karaca, David Grusky, Tatsunori Hashimoto et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-02 | **arXiv:** [2607.02464v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02464v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates whether larger language models will become better at simulating human social behavior—like predicting opinions, modeling how people act, and forecasting future trends. Using 85+ LLMs ranging from 0.5B to 70B parameters, the authors find that scale *does* improve social simulation in most cases, but with important exceptions: models fail to capture human cognitive biases (like risk aversion) and struggle with opinions from underrepresented groups in training data, suggesting that bigger models alone won't solve all social simulation problems.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on two foundational areas. First, **scaling laws** in deep learning—the empirical observation that model performance improves predictably with compute budget, parameterized by relationships like the Chinchilla scaling laws. Second, recent work using LLMs as social simulation engines, treating them as computational agents that can mimic human decision-making and opinion formation. The paper uses **transformers**—neural networks based on self-attention mechanisms that process sequences of tokens—as the underlying architecture, evaluated via standard capability benchmarks like MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding) that measure broad knowledge and reasoning.

The key assumption underlying the work is that social fidelity (how accurately a model simulates real human behavior) correlates with general capabilities. The paper tests whether this assumption holds across three domains: opinion modeling, behavioral simulation, and longitudinal forecasting.

## Core Contributions
1. **Systematic scaling law study for social simulation**: Quantifies how three distinct social tasks (opinion, behavior, forecasting) respond to compute scaling across two orders of magnitude (10^18–10^20 FLOPs), revealing domain-specific scaling curves.
2. **Identification of scaling-resistant phenomena**: Demonstrates that certain tasks—particularly modeling human cognitive biases and heuristics (risk aversion, correlated reward learning)—do *not* scale with model size, even with fine-tuning up to 8B parameters.
3. **Benchmarking toolkit**: Provides 85 controlled-budget models and evaluations of 35 larger models, enabling predictive loss-to-accuracy mappings for downstream social tasks.
4. **Population-conditional analysis**: Shows that scaling varies by demographic representation in training data—well-represented populations improve with scale, while underrepresented groups and low-resource opinion domains scale more slowly.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper extends [[Transformers]] research into the domain of behavioral modeling and relates to broader questions in [[Alignment]] about whether scaling improves value-alignment and human-like reasoning. It echoes findings from work on [[RLHF]] and human feedback—that model capability and human preference alignment are not automatically coupled. The work also connects to [[Multi-Agent Systems]] research that uses LLMs as agents in simulations, and touches on fairness/representational issues similar to those studied in bias and debiasing literature. The scaling laws methodology draws directly from precedent in large-language-models research (Kaplan et al., Hoffmann et al.).

## Practical Applications
1. **Policy simulation & forecasting**: Governments and NGOs could use well-scaling social simulations to predict policy impacts on opinion and behavior before rollout, especially in data-rich domains (public health, economics).
2. **Market research & product design**: Companies could simulate how different demographic segments will adopt or respond to products, with confidence that scale improves fidelity for mainstream populations.
3. **Misinformation & narrative spread modeling**: News organizations and platforms could forecast how narratives propagate through populations, though less reliably for minority viewpoints.
4. **Longitudinal study acceleration**: Researchers studying behavioral trends could use LLM-based simulations to prototype hypotheses before running expensive longitudinal experiments—though forecasting tasks scale slowly and require validation.
5. **Bias detection in training data**: Gaps in scaling (e.g., poor calibration on risk aversion) could serve as diagnostic tools to identify missing cognitive phenomena or underrepresented populations in web corpora.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Social Simulation SaaS for Policy Teams**: A platform that lets government agencies run counterfactual policy simulations (e.g., "How will opinion shift if we raise the minimum wage?"), with transparency about which populations/scenarios have high vs. low scaling confidence.
2. **Behavioral A/B Testing Pre-Flight**: A product that simulates how different user segments will respond to app/website designs or messaging before A/B testing, using scaling laws to estimate confidence intervals.
3. **Underrepresented-Group Modeling Consultancy**: A specialist service that fine-tunes and validates social simulations for niche populations (indigenous communities, rare professions, diaspora groups), compensating for poor scaling in low-data domains.
4. **Longitudinal Forecasting Augmentation Service**: A tool that combines LLM-based simulations with classical time-series models to predict social trends (e.g., profession popularity, migration patterns), acknowledging uncertainty from slow-scaling forecasting tasks.
5. **Bias Calibration & Debiasing Toolkit**: A software toolkit that detects where LLMs fail to reproduce human cognitive biases and heuristics, helping organizations either accept these gaps or commission domain-specific fine-tuning.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Cognitive biases not scale-solved**: The paper shows that models fail to internalize and reproduce human biases (risk aversion, heuristic learning) even at 8B parameters. This suggests a ceiling that pure scaling cannot overcome—these phenomena may require architectural or training-time changes.
2. **Representation bias in web corpora**: Underrepresented opinions and low-resource demographics scale poorly because training data (DCLM, a web corpus) inherently reflects internet demographics. Scaling alone cannot create knowledge the model was never exposed to.
3. **Longitudinal forecasting reliability**: Forecasting future social outcomes scales slowly and unreliably. The paper implies this task may require temporal reasoning capabilities that LLMs fundamentally lack or that don't improve with scale the same way static knowledge does.
4. **Lack of causal grounding**: LLMs simulate correlations in text; they don't model causal mechanisms of behavior. Scaling may improve pattern matching without improving mechanistic understanding, limiting generalization to novel contexts.
5. **Calibration uncertainty**: The paper predicts loss-to-accuracy mappings but doesn't deeply investigate confidence calibration. Models may scale in raw accuracy while remaining miscalibrated, leading to overconfident simulations.
6. **Validation against ground truth**: The paper evaluates on benchmarks, but true validation requires comparing simulations against real human behavior at scale—expensive and only partially done here.

## Open Questions
1. **What architectural changes would help model human biases?** Is the issue with transformer design, inductive bias in pre-training, or something more fundamental about how LLMs represent causality vs. statistics?
2. **How do we improve scaling for low-resource populations without retraining on new data?** Can adaptation techniques (prompt engineering, LoRA-style fine-tuning, retrieval augmentation) close gaps that web-scale training cannot?
3. **Does temporal grounding help longitudinal forecasting?** If you augment LLMs with time-series modules or causal inference tools, do forecasting scaling curves improve?
4. **What is the relationship between moral/value alignment and social simulation fidelity?** When models fail to capture human risk aversion, are they learning genuinely different values, or just failing to simulate human reasoning?
5. **Can we predict which tasks will scale from task properties alone?** Given a new social simulation task, can we characterize (e.g., via MMLU correlation, domain representation in web text) whether it will scale with compute?
6. **How do fine-tuning and in-context learning interact with scaling laws?** The paper shows fine-tuning doesn't help biases much; does this hold across all three domains and all parameter ranges?

## Key Takeaway
Scaling language models will improve most social simulations, but it will not fix their failure to capture human cognitive biases, heuristics, and low-resource opinions—these require orthogonal research beyond model size.
