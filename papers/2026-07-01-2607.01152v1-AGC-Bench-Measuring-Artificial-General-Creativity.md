# AGC-Bench: Measuring Artificial General Creativity

**Authors:** Roger Beaty, Vijeta Deshpande, Clin K. Y. Lai, Anna Attuch, Namrata Shivagunde et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-01 | **arXiv:** [2607.01152v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01152v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces AGC-Bench, a large-scale benchmark for measuring creativity in large language models across six domains (brainstorming, problem-solving, STEM, narrative, figurative language, and humor). The work combines a systematic literature review of 3,101 AI creativity papers with psychometric methods to address judge bias, ultimately discovering that LLMs exhibit a single underlying creativity factor analogous to general intelligence—but one that remains inferior to human creativity on matched tasks.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two foundational research traditions. First, decades of psychology research debating whether creativity is domain-specific or unified by a single latent factor (analogous to the 'g' factor in IQ research). Second, recent work in LLM evaluation that uses [[HELM]] (Holistic Evaluation of Language Models)—a standardized framework for benchmarking. The paper applies Judge Response Theory, a psychometric technique borrowed from educational testing, to calibrate systematic biases in how LLMs rate creativity (some judges are naturally lenient or harsh). This creates AGC-Judge, a specialized evaluator model fine-tuned on bias-corrected ratings to score unseen creativity tasks reliably.

## Core Contributions
1. **Comprehensive benchmark construction**: Systematic review of 3,101 papers identifying 497 creativity benchmarks, then curating 78 datasets across six domains—the first unified AI creativity benchmark at this scale.
2. **Bias-corrected judging pipeline**: Application of Judge Response Theory to quantify and remove judge leniency/severity bias, then fine-tuned Qwen3-30B to produce AGC-Judge—an open-weight evaluator that generalizes to unseen creativity benchmarks.
3. **Discovery of a creativity factor 'c'**: Factor analysis across 83 LLMs reveals a single latent creativity dimension explaining 81.5% of variance, separable from general knowledge/reasoning—empirically answering the psychology debate for AI systems.
4. **Prompt-based capability separation**: Evidence that "be creative" prompts boost performance far more than reasoning-enabling prompts, suggesting the benchmark measures genuine creativity rather than general ability.
5. **Open infrastructure**: Public leaderboard, AGC-Judge model, and human comparison data released as community resources.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of three fields:
- **LLM evaluation**: Extends [[HELM]] standardization to creativity; relates to broader work on [[RLHF]] and preference modeling in that both require robust human/model judgment.
- **Psychometrics in AI**: Similar to mechanistic work decomposing [[Transformers]] into capabilities, but applies classical psychometric theory (factor analysis, judge calibration) to understand LLM abilities.
- **Creativity science**: Directly addresses the domain-specificity vs. unitarity debate from psychology, now empirically in AI systems. Complements interpretability work on how models generate novel outputs.

## Practical Applications
1. **Creative AI product evaluation**: Startups building generative AI tools (design, marketing copy, game design) can use AGC-Bench to objectively compare models and detect domain-specific weaknesses.
2. **Hiring & skill testing**: Educational platforms or creative agencies could use AGC-Bench to assess whether LLM-assisted workers are actually improving creative output, not just writing speed.
3. **Model selection for creative tasks**: Organizations choosing between Claude, GPT, Llama, etc. for in-house creative work can benchmark on relevant AGC domains to avoid expensive post-hoc evaluation.
4. **Creativity augmentation research**: Researchers studying human-AI collaboration can measure whether AI suggestions actually improve human creative performance on AGC tasks.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **CreativeScore API**: Freemium SaaS wrapping AGC-Judge—startups building generative apps pay per evaluation to benchmark their outputs, with domain-specific leaderboards (e.g., "best humor generation," "best scientific ideation").
2. **Creativity-tuned model distillation service**: Fine-tune open models (Llama, Mistral) on high-performing LLM outputs from AGC-Bench, sell specialized models to agencies that need domain-specific creative strength.
3. **Human-AI creative studios**: Matchmaking platform that pairs freelance creatives (writers, designers) with the best-ranked LLMs for their domain (AGC scores), taking a commission on engagements.
4. **Talent selection for creative roles**: HR tech company offering AGC-based assessments to pre-screen or enhance applicant creativity profiles, differentiating human candidates by creative profile (narrative strength vs. STEM ideation, etc.).
5. **Competitive creativity leaderboard gaming**: Gamified platform where users prompt models to compete on AGC tasks, with prizes funded by model API partnerships and sponsorships (e.g., "Can your prompt out-create GPT on brainstorming?").

## Technical Limitations
1. **Human creativity complexity**: The top human still outperforms the top LLM; the 'c' factor explains 81.5% of variance, leaving ~18.5% unexplained. It's unclear whether this gap reflects fundamental model limitations or incomplete operationalization of creativity.
2. **Benchmark composition bias**: 78 datasets across 3,101 papers may still under-represent some creative domains (e.g., music, embodied design, improvisation), limiting generalization claims.
3. **Judge Response Theory assumptions**: Calibration assumes judges' leniency/severity is stable across domains, which may not hold if judges have domain-specific expertise (e.g., stricter on STEM vs. narrative).
4. **Domain-specificity not fully resolved**: While factor analysis finds a single 'c', the paper shows LLMs rank differently across domains. This suggests either domain-specific skills exist but are correlated, or evaluation methods capture different aspects of creativity per domain.
5. **Prompt sensitivity**: Results depend heavily on prompt phrasing ("be creative" vs. standard prompts). Real-world creativity often emerges from task framing, not explicit instructions—unclear how AGC handles that.
6. **Generalization to novel tasks**: AGC-Judge was fine-tuned on three frontier models; it's unknown whether it fairly scores future architectural innovations or models trained on different data.

## Open Questions
1. **Is the 'c' factor robust?** Does it replicate across different model families (vision-language models, multimodal), training procedures, or time (as new models emerge)?
2. **What causes domain variance?** Fine-grained analysis of why writing creativity ranks higher than scientific ideation—is it training data, tokenization, or something fundamental about how LLMs represent those domains?
3. **How does AGC relate to downstream utility?** Do higher AGC scores predict user satisfaction or commercial success for creative outputs (e.g., marketing copy, story generation)? Or do they measure a different construct?
4. **Can we isolate the creativity-vs.-knowledge boundary?** The paper shows separability from "general reasoning," but how do we operationally distinguish genuine novelty from recombination of training data?
5. **How do human-LLM pairs perform?** Do humans use AGC-ranked models as collaborators? Does working with a high-AGC model actually improve human creative output on downstream tasks?
6. **Adversarial robustness**: Can models game AGC-Judge through prompt injection or output manipulation? How does performance degrade under adversarial conditions?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs exhibit a unified, measurable creativity factor distinct from general reasoning ability—but remain inferior to humans—suggesting creativity in AI is a trainable capability separate from mere knowledge, opening pathways for specialized model development.
