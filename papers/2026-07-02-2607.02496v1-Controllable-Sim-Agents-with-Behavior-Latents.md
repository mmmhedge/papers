# Controllable Sim Agents with Behavior Latents

**Authors:** Juanwu Lu, Junyu Zhu, Ziran Wang
**Published:** 2026-07-02 | **arXiv:** [2607.02496v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02496v1)

## Summary
This paper presents CNeVA, a framework for creating realistic traffic simulation agents that can be precisely controlled along interpretable dimensions (speed, safety, map compliance). Rather than building agents that simply replay logged behaviors, the authors enable engineers to steer agent behavior smoothly—crucial for testing autonomous vehicles without real-world risk. The key innovation is using a learned latent space (Gaussian behavior codes) combined with a diffusion-like trajectory generator, allowing fine-grained control while maintaining realism.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on several established ideas. Variational inference (learning distributions over hidden variables via a lower bound on likelihood) provides the mathematical foundation for inferring per-agent behavior latents from observed data. Rectified flows are a recent generative modeling technique (simpler and faster than diffusion models) that learn to transform noise into realistic trajectories. The authors ground their approach in imitation learning—training agents to reproduce logged real-world behavior—and extend it with controllability via classifier-free guidance (a technique borrowed from text-to-image generation that conditions generation on control signals). Eligibility traces (a reinforcement learning concept) are repurposed here to smooth gradient signals during training.

## Core Contributions
1. **Closed-form conjugate variational update** for inferring behavior latents directly from per-channel returns, avoiding expensive iterative optimization during training.
2. **Soft eligibility gates** that replace hard thresholds with smooth exponential decay, preserving gradient signals for agents near decision boundaries—addressing a real numerical instability in reward-based steering.
3. **Channel-mask curriculum** for training the trajectory generator, enabling classifier-free guidance on individual behavior dimensions (speed, safety, map compliance) independently.
4. **Empirical demonstration** that steering must be validated against physical-plausibility constraints, not just reward metrics alone—exposing a pitfall in naive evaluation.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of imitation learning, controllable generation, and multi-agent simulation. It extends [[Diffusion|diffusion-based]] generation (and its successor, rectified flows) into the embodied agent domain, similar to how [[Diffusion Policy]] applied diffusion to robotic control. The use of behavior latents is related to [[VAE|variational autoencoders]] and [[BETA|behavior cloning with latent embeddings]]. The paper also engages with the broader challenge of [[Reward Hacking|reward hacking]]—where optimizing a metric can break real-world validity—a critical concern in [[Reinforcement Learning|RL]]-based sim-to-real transfer. Multi-agent simulation and [[Open-Ended Learning]] benefit directly from controllable agents.

## Practical Applications
- **Autonomous vehicle testing**: Engineers can isolate edge cases (e.g., "what happens when all surrounding vehicles drive conservatively?") without waiting for rare natural occurrences.
- **Scenario generation for AV validation**: Systematically sweep behavior space to stress-test autonomous driving stacks before road deployment.
- **Traffic engineering**: Simulate policy changes (e.g., stricter speed limits) by steering agent populations along behavior dimensions.
- **Insurance/risk modeling**: Generate diverse but physically plausible traffic scenarios to assess insurance products or liability.
- **Game/simulation engines**: Controllable NPC crowds in driving games or open-world simulations with realistic variety.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Autonomous Vehicle Testing-as-a-Service**: Sell access to a cloud platform where AV teams upload their perception/planning stacks and test them against controllable CNeVA scenarios (fog conditions, aggressive drivers, etc.) without simulation engines.
2. **Synthetic Data for AV Perception**: Automatically generate diverse, labeled traffic datasets by sweeping behavior latent dimensions; sell to companies training perception models.
3. **Insurance Risk Dashboard**: Real-time traffic simulation for underwriters—input local traffic patterns and policies, sweep safety/speed dimensions, visualize accident risk heatmaps.
4. **Driving Behavior Labeling Tool**: Use CNeVA as a foundation for interactive annotation—show engineers a realistic scenario, let them adjust agent behaviors via sliders, export labeled data for model training.
5. **Traffic Optimization Consultant Tool**: Simulate intersection designs or policy changes (e.g., lane restrictions) by steering simulated traffic; provide cost-benefit analysis before deployment.

## Technical Limitations
- **Scalability to dense traffic**: The paper focuses on medium-complexity scenarios; behavior must remain realistic under high-density constraints (behavior latents may not capture emergent interactions).
- **Multi-agent coordination**: Agents are steered independently; long-horizon multi-agent dependencies (platooning, negotiation) may not be well-captured by per-agent latents.
- **Domain shift**: Model trained on Waymo data; generalization to new cities, weather, or driving styles is untested.
- **Computational cost**: Inferring per-agent behavior latents and generating trajectories for thousands of agents simultaneously could be expensive.
- **Interpretability of latent dimensions**: While per-channel control (speed, safety) is exposed, the learned latent space may not align perfectly with human intuitions, risking misuse.
- **Evaluation circularity**: Paper acknowledges that steering metrics alone can deceive; requires manual validation against physics/collision constraints, limiting automation.

## Open Questions
- How do learned behavior latents generalize across different road geometries or traffic densities unseen during training?
- Can multi-agent coordination constraints (e.g., collision avoidance negotiations) be encoded into the latent space, or do they require explicit agent-agent messaging?
- What is the minimal amount of logged data needed to learn a faithful behavior model, and how does performance degrade with sparse data?
- Can the framework extend to continuous or hierarchical control (not just discrete channels like speed/safety)?
- How sensitive is the framework to misspecification of the return function for each channel? Can it recover gracefully?

## Key Takeaway
Controllable agents for simulation need interpretable, independent behavioral levers (speed, safety, compliance) grounded in gradient-friendly losses and validated against physical plausibility—not just reward metrics.
