# PolicyGuard: From Organizational Policies to Neuro-SymbolicCompliance Review Engines

**Authors:** Sameer Malik, Ayush Singh, Amar Prakash Azad
**Published:** 2026-06-30 | **arXiv:** [2606.32004v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.32004v1)

## Summary
PolicyGuard is a framework that converts organizational policies into executable compliance review engines by combining neural networks (LLMs) with symbolic logic rules. Instead of asking an LLM to make a yes/no compliance decision directly, it decomposes the problem: the LLM answers specific factual questions about a document, and formal logic rules then determine if those facts satisfy the policy. This matters because it makes compliance decisions transparent, auditable, and easier to update when policies change.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on two complementary traditions. First, neuro-symbolic AI — combining deep learning with formal logic to gain interpretability and reliability. Second, policy compliance and knowledge representation, where rules are formalized using relational logic (representing facts and constraints as structured data). The paper also draws on retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) — using document search to ground LLM answers in specific evidence — and structured information extraction, where neural models identify and label specific data from text. The key insight is that policy documents can be thought of as executable specifications, similar to formal methods in software verification.

## Core Contributions
1. **PolicyGuard framework**: A concrete pipeline that translates natural-language policy documents into (a) typed relational logic rules defining compliance constraints, and (b) atom-level extraction questions that decompose policy review into answerable subquestions.
2. **Separation of concerns**: Cleanly decouples three tasks — policy formalization (human + tool-assisted), document interpretation (LLM-driven extraction), and compliance checking (symbolic evaluation) — making each step independently testable and debuggable.
3. **NDA compliance instantiation and evaluation**: Demonstrates the approach on real contract review, showing how company-specific negotiation policies can be enforced systematically.
4. **Maintainability and testability**: Unlike end-to-end prompting, PolicyGuard policies can be version-controlled, updated without retraining, and tested against known examples independently.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to [[RLHF]] and alignment work in that both aim to make LLM behavior more predictable and controllable. It echoes [[Attention Is All You Need]] in relying on transformers for interpretation, but differs by not relying solely on LLM judgment. The work connects to knowledge graphs and semantic web frameworks (RDF, OWL) in its use of typed relations. It also parallels recent work on constitutional AI and rule-based prompting — attempting to encode values or constraints formally — but is more rigorous by separating the symbolic evaluation layer. Related to fact verification and natural language inference (NLI), which also ask whether documents support logical claims.

## Practical Applications
1. **Contract compliance**: Legal teams reviewing NDAs, MSAs, or SLAs against company policies without manually re-reading each clause.
2. **Regulatory document review**: Insurance, healthcare, or financial firms checking that customer-facing documents comply with regulatory requirements (e.g., GDPR, HIPAA).
3. **Policy audit trails**: Compliance systems that log *why* a document was flagged (which rule fired, which extracted fact triggered it), meeting audit and explainability requirements.
4. **Policy evolution**: When corporate policies change, administrators update the logic rules without retraining the LLM; old review decisions can be re-evaluated.
5. **Cross-org policy matching**: Assessing whether a vendor's privacy policy or terms of service align with a procurement policy.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **PolicyGuard-as-a-Service**: A SaaS platform where legal/compliance teams upload policies once (in structured form), then bulk-review contracts or documents against those policies with explainable verdicts and audit logs.
2. **Policy Template Marketplace**: Pre-built, vetted policy-to-logic templates for common document types (NDA, employment contract, vendor MSA) that enterprises can customize and deploy immediately.
3. **Automated Policy Maintenance Dashboard**: Tools that help in-house counsel translate new policy guidance into formal rules, versioning policies and tracking which past reviews are affected by changes.
4. **Compliance Intelligence Platform**: Integrated system combining PolicyGuard with entity/risk databases to not only flag non-compliance but suggest risk-mitigating edits to contracts in real time.
5. **Vertical Compliance Suite**: Industry-specific versions (e.g., for fintech, healthcare, e-commerce) with pre-loaded regulatory rules and policy templates, enabling smaller firms to deploy compliance automation without legal AI expertise.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Policy formalization bottleneck**: Translating natural-language policy into clean relational rules requires domain expertise and manual effort; the paper doesn't solve this automatically and it's the critical human step.
2. **LLM extraction errors**: The approach is only as good as the LLM's ability to answer factual questions accurately; hallucination or misinterpretation of ambiguous text still propagates to the symbolic evaluator.
3. **Coverage and edge cases**: Policies written in natural language often contain implicit exceptions, context-dependent rules, or judgment calls that don't map neatly to formal logic; the framework may oversimplify.
4. **Scalability of rule bases**: For very large or complex policies with many interdependencies, the rule base can become unwieldy and brittle.
5. **Retrieval quality**: Answers depend on finding relevant document evidence; if the retrieval module fails, the LLM may not have the context needed to answer correctly.

## Open Questions
1. **Automatic policy formalization**: Can we use neuro-symbolic techniques or few-shot learning to help convert natural-language policies to logic rules with minimal human intervention?
2. **Confidence and uncertainty**: How should the system quantify confidence in extracted facts (e.g., "50% confident this clause grants IP rights")? Should compliance decisions include a confidence margin?
3. **Counterfactual reasoning**: Can PolicyGuard suggest what would need to change in a document to make it compliant, beyond just flagging violations?
4. **Multi-policy reasoning**: How does the framework scale when policies interact or conflict? Can it detect and surface policy inconsistencies?
5. **Cross-lingual policies**: Does PolicyGuard work when policies and documents are in different languages, or when jargon/terminology varies?
6. **Human-in-the-loop updates**: What's the optimal workflow for legal experts to review, challenge, and refine the extracted facts and rule firings?

## Key Takeaway
PolicyGuard makes LLM-assisted compliance review auditable and maintainable by separating policy formalization, fact extraction, and symbolic rule evaluation — turning a black-box decision into an inspectable logical chain.
