# Discovery under Hypothesis Redundancy: A Geometric Theory of Discovery Bottlenecks

**Authors:** Li Xia, Baoxun Wang
**Published:** 2026-06-12 | **arXiv:** [2606.14386v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.14386v1)

## Summary
This paper identifies when hybrid discovery systems—combining local search with LLM-guided non-local exploration—actually improve scientific hypothesis discovery. The authors formalize "discovery bottlenecks" through a geometric theory showing that LLM proposals help only under specific conditions: spectral compression of explored space, orthogonal escape directions, and alignment with target signals. Testing across synthetic tasks, financial factor discovery, and symbolic regression reveals that novelty alone is insufficient; directed non-local exploration only yields gains when signal predictively aligns with unexplored directions.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on classical search and optimization theory, particularly the notion that discovery saturates when the hypothesis space becomes overcrowded or fully explored (related to [[curse-of-dimensionality]] in high-dimensional search). It leverages geometric analysis of vector spaces—specifically spectral decomposition and orthogonal projections—to formalize when hybrid strategies outperform either local search or pure random exploration. The work implicitly connects to [[active-learning]] and [[information-theory]] by treating hypothesis discovery as a signal-recovery problem where information gain depends on alignment with unknown targets.

## Core Contributions
1. **Search Compression Hypothesis**: Formalizes three necessary geometric conditions for hybrid advantage—spectral compression, orthogonal escape, and residual signal alignment—transforming LLM-guided discovery from heuristic novelty search into a diagnostic procedure.
2. **Theoretical Characterization**: Derives necessary conditions for when non-local proposals improve yield, with explicit failure modes (e.g., random orthogonal jumps expand coverage but not quality without alignment).
3. **Empirical Validation Across Domains**: Tests the framework on controlled synthetic environments, A-share financial factor discovery (real-world, high-dimensional), and symbolic regression, showing hybrid gains concentrate in weakly represented target-bearing directions.
4. **Operational Sanity Check**: Provides a public tabular test for practitioners to validate budget-allocation trade-offs between local and non-local exploration.

This work interfaces with:

## Practical Applications
1. **Quantitative Finance**: Accelerated discovery of predictive financial factors by detecting when spectral saturation occurs in feature exploration; saves compute budgets in multi-asset investing.
2. **Drug Discovery**: Identifying when ML-guided molecular hypothesis generation (e.g., via LLM-generated chemical structures) will yield novel compounds; avoiding redundant proposals in high-dimensional chemical space.
3. **Materials Science**: Detecting saturation in computational material screening; deciding when to deploy expensive LLM-guided non-local proposals versus exhaustive local search over crystal structures or alloy compositions.
4. **AutoML and Hyperparameter Optimization**: Diagnosing when Bayesian optimization plus LLM-guided suggestions outperform pure Bayesian search; operationalizing the budget trade-off.
5. **Biological Network Discovery**: Prioritizing which biological hypotheses (e.g., protein interactions, metabolic pathways) warrant LLM-assisted literature mining versus automated local search.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Assumption of Linear/Spectral Structure**: The framework assumes the hypothesis space has a clear spectral decomposition (e.g., via PCA or SVD). Non-linear or discrete hypothesis spaces (e.g., combinatorial program structures) may not admit clean spectral compression.
2. **Unknown Target Signal**: The theory requires residual signal alignment with an unknown target; in practice, if the true target direction is fully orthogonal to both the explored span and the LLM proposals, the framework cannot detect this failure.
3. **Scalability of Spectral Analysis**: Computing full spectral decomposition of very high-dimensional explored spaces (e.g., millions of evaluated hypotheses) becomes computationally prohibitive.
4. **LLM Quality Dependence**: The framework assumes LLM proposals are somewhat aligned with the task structure; if the LLM generates nonsensical or anti-correlated proposals, the method provides no guidance on recovery.
5. **Limited to Single-Agent Search**: The theory applies to hybrid sequential search; multi-agent or parallel discovery pipelines with feedback loops are not addressed.
6. **Empirical Validation Scope**: While SRBench and A-share experiments are strong, the results are concentrated on financial and symbolic-regression domains; generalization to wet-lab experiments or black-box simulators remains unclear.

## Open Questions
1. **Non-linear Hypothesis Spaces**: How should spectral compression, orthogonal escape, and signal alignment be generalized to manifold-structured or discrete hypothesis spaces? Can we develop spectral analogs for combinatorial or tree-structured search?
2. **Adaptive LLM Prompting**: Can the geometric conditions be used to dynamically adjust LLM prompts (e.g., emphasize orthogonality or signal themes)? Does feedback about spectral compression improve LLM proposal quality in a loop?
3. **Multi-objective Discovery**: The framework assumes a single target signal. How do the conditions change in multi-objective discovery (e.g., finding Pareto-optimal factors)?
4. **Human-in-the-Loop Integration**: When human experts are available, how should the geometric framework allocate budget between human judgment, local search, and LLM proposals?
5. **Theoretical Hardness**: Are there problem classes where spectral compression is mathematically unavoidable, rendering hybrid search futile? Can we characterize worst-case versus best-case geometries?
6. **Transductive vs. Inductive Generalization**: The framework diagnoses saturation in the explored hypothesis set; does it predict generalization to unseen test distributions? How does spectral compression of training factors correlate with out-of-sample performance in finance or symbolic regression?

## Key Takeaway
Discovery only benefits from LLM-guided non-local exploration when the explored hypothesis space exhibits spectral compression, proposals can escape orthogonally, and those escape directions align with the target signal—a diagnostic principle that transforms LLM guidance from generic novelty search into a mathematically grounded tool for deciding *when* to deploy it.
