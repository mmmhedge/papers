# Shapley in Context: Explaining Financial Language with Domain Expertise

**Authors:** Dangxing Chen, Pengzhan Guo
**Published:** 2026-07-01 | **arXiv:** [2607.00856v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.00856v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates whether Shapley values—a game-theoretic method for attributing model predictions to input features—can explain financial LLM decisions in ways that align with real financial reasoning. The authors show that Shapley-based explanations for financial text tasks are consistent with established domain knowledge, addressing a critical gap: most explainability methods are generic and don't account for finance's regulatory demands and high-stakes decision-making.

## Theoretical Background
Shapley values originate from cooperative game theory and assign each "player" (input feature or token) a fair contribution to an outcome by averaging their marginal contributions across all possible coalitions. In machine learning, they've become a standard tool for model-agnostic explainability (e.g., [[SHAP]]), providing principled attribution scores for individual features.

The paper situates this within financial NLP—the application of neural language models to financial texts (earnings calls, news, regulatory filings). Financial institutions increasingly use LLMs for sentiment analysis, risk assessment, and decision support, but regulatory bodies (SEC, ECB, etc.) require interpretability: institutions must explain *why* a model recommended a trade or flagged a risk.

Prior work on LLM explainability (attention visualization, gradient-based attribution) often fails to capture semantic understanding in financial contexts. The key insight here is that Shapley values, by their axiomatic properties (efficiency, symmetry, null player), may naturally respect financial reasoning—e.g., certain tokens (company names, risk keywords) should consistently drive model predictions in ways auditors expect.

## Core Contributions
1. **Alignment validation**: Demonstrates empirically that Shapley attributions in financial LLM tasks correlate with domain expert judgments—e.g., financial indicators, regulatory language, and company-specific signals are correctly weighted.
2. **Theoretical analysis**: Provides formal guarantees that Shapley-based explanations for LLM financial tasks satisfy financial reasoning principles (e.g., monotonicity: adding positive signals shouldn't decrease attribution to risk tokens).
3. **Methodological framework**: Introduces a systematic approach to evaluate Shapley explanations against domain knowledge, moving beyond generic explainability benchmarks.
4. **Empirical scope**: Tests across multiple financial tasks (sentiment, risk classification, anomaly detection in text) rather than a single application.

## Connections to Other Work
- **[[SHAP]] and LIME**: Standard model-agnostic explainability tools; this paper validates their applicability to financial domains, which prior work assumed but didn't rigorously verify.
- **[[Mechanistic interpretability]]**: Complements circuit-level analysis of LLMs by focusing on input-level attribution rather than internal activation patterns.
- **Financial NLP benchmarks** (e.g., FinBERT): This work bridges explainability and task-specific financial models.
- **Regulatory AI governance**: Addresses the emerging need for trustworthy AI in [[Risk management]] and compliance workflows.
- **Fairness and interpretability literature**: Shares concerns about black-box model behavior but in a high-stakes, regulated domain.

## Practical Applications
1. **Regulatory compliance & model cards**: Financial institutions can use Shapley attributions to document model decisions for regulatory filings and internal audits (required by Dodd-Frank Act, MiFID II).
2. **Trader/analyst decision support**: When an LLM flags a security as high-risk, explainability helps traders understand whether the model picked up on fundamental analysis (low P/E ratio) vs. sentiment shifts (analyst downgrade).
3. **Risk governance dashboards**: Portfolio managers monitor whether sentiment-driven LLM signals align with macroeconomic narratives; Shapley values help detect drift or gaming.
4. **Fraud detection in filings**: SEC and internal compliance teams use LLMs to detect misleading language; Shapley explanations show which phrases triggered alerts.
5. **Client-facing advisory**: Wealth management platforms explain investment recommendations to clients by showing which market signals or news items drove the model's thesis.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **FinExplain**: A SaaS platform that wraps financial LLMs with Shapley-based explainability dashboards, targeting compliance officers and risk teams. Generates regulatory-ready model cards and decision audit trails.
2. **RegAI Audit Engine**: Automated compliance tool that continuously tests whether financial LLMs' Shapley attributions align with institutional policies and regulatory frameworks; flags misalignments.
3. **Trader's Reasoning Lens**: Browser extension or terminal tool for traders that highlights which tokens in earnings calls or news drove LLM signals, enabling collaborative human-AI analysis.
4. **Financial LLM Benchmark Suite**: Proprietary benchmarks that evaluate explainability quality (Shapley vs. baselines) on domain-specific financial tasks, sold to enterprise risk departments.
5. **Explainable Portfolio Optimization**: A fund management tool that uses LLMs to construct portfolios and explains each holding via Shapley token attributions, marketed to ESG/impact investors seeking transparency.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Computational cost**: Shapley value estimation requires many forward passes (exponential in the number of tokens); the paper likely uses approximations (e.g., sampling, tree SHAP) that may lose fidelity on very long financial documents.
2. **Domain knowledge specification**: The paper validates alignment with "established financial domain knowledge," but this is somewhat circular—it assumes a gold standard of expert judgment, which can be noisy or outdated.
3. **Task-specific generalization**: Results on earnings calls or news sentiment may not transfer to derivatives pricing models or high-frequency trading signals, where the relationship between text and ground truth is more indirect.
4. **Temporal stability**: Financial markets and language evolve; Shapley attributions trained on 2020 data may not reflect current risk narratives.
5. **Causality ambiguity**: Shapley values show correlation, not causation; a token may be attributed importance because it's a proxy for unmeasured factors rather than because it directly drives the model's logic.
6. **Regulatory acceptance**: It's unclear whether regulatory bodies (SEC, Basel III enforcers) formally accept Shapley explanations as sufficient for model validation.

## Open Questions
1. **How do Shapley attributions for financial LLMs compare to human expert explanations** for the same predictions? Is there systematic divergence (e.g., experts cite macro factors, Shapley highlights specific words)?
2. **Can Shapley-based explanations detect adversarial or out-of-distribution inputs** in financial contexts (e.g., market manipulation, novel fraud patterns)?
3. **How stable are Shapley attributions across model architectures and training data?** Would fine-tuning on different financial corpora significantly change explanations?
4. **Do Shapley explanations improve end-user trust and decision quality** in real trading or risk workflows, or do they just *appear* trustworthy without changing behavior?
5. **Can we combine Shapley values with causal inference** to move beyond attribution to true causal effects of financial signals on model predictions?
6. **How do Shapley explanations scale to multimodal financial data** (text + price charts + news images)?

## Key Takeaway
Shapley values can produce interpretable, domain-consistent explanations for financial LLMs, bridging the gap between model-agnostic explainability and regulatory requirements—but deployment requires careful validation of temporal stability and formal acceptance by financial regulators.
