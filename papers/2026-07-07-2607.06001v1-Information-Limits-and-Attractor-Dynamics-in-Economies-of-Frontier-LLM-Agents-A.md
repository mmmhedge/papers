# Information Limits and Attractor Dynamics in Economies of Frontier LLM Agents: A Pre-Registered Test

**Authors:** Cheng Qian
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06001v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06001v1)

## Summary
This paper tests whether frontier LLM agents (Claude Opus 4.8) in simulated economies behave according to information-theoretic predictions about wealth growth and population coordination. Using pre-registered experiments with frozen predictions and cached model outputs, the authors confirm that relative wealth growth tracks relative information in parimutuel-coupled markets, but find that LLM populations fail to exhibit the smooth noise-stabilized diversity that mean-field theory predicts — instead showing sharp phase transitions and bistability near control boundaries.

## Theoretical Background
The work sits at the intersection of information theory, multi-agent economics, and alignment research. It builds on:

- **Information-theoretic capacity regions**: the idea that coupled systems (e.g., markets) have bounded channels for wealth redistribution, and that an agent's growth should equal its relative information advantage
- **Mean-field theory**: a classical framework that models large populations as smooth continuous systems, assuming random noise maintains diversity even under pressure
- **Parimutuel coupling**: a market structure where agents' payoffs depend on the relative composition of resources (like a betting pool)
- **Coalition value and submodularity**: game-theoretic concepts measuring whether joining a group adds or subtracts value; the paper tests whether this structure tracks conditional independence in information channels

The novelty is applying these abstract frameworks to frontier LLMs to see whether they self-organize as predicted or reveal empirical failures of the theory.

## Core Contributions

1. **Confirmation of the gap law** (G_a - G_b = I_a - I_b): across four market perception structures, relative wealth growth tracks relative information to near-perfect precision (46 millinats vs. 50 millinat pre-registered band).

2. **Coalition value-independence alignment**: demonstrated that coalitional value is submodular exactly when information channels are conditionally independent; a designed XOR synergy test flips this by design (0.62 ≥ ln2/2 nats).

3. **Structural mismatch with mean-field theory**: all 72 population runs showed goal dispersion collapse (V → 0) and sharp step-function responses to control levers rather than smooth scaling — revealing that LLM populations do *not* maintain the noise-driven diversity assumed by classical population models.

4. **Full reproducibility**: pre-registered protocol with frozen predictions, public git chain, cached API calls, and zero-cost re-runnable experiments.

## Connections to Other Work
- **[[Alignment]]**: this work probes whether multi-agent LLM systems exhibit controllable, predictable behavior or whether they exhibit phase transitions that resist smooth intervention
- **[[Multi-agent systems]]** and emergent coordination: connects to questions about whether neural agents converge to game-theoretic equilibria or exhibit non-smooth bifurcations
- **Information-theoretic approaches to agency**: echoes work on bounded rationality and information bottlenecks in decision-making
- **Mean-field methods in ML**: the paper's negative result challenges the assumption that large populations of diverse agents self-stabilize through noise; this is a direct empirical refutation of a common approximation used in population-level analysis

## Practical Applications

- **Multi-agent LLM marketplaces**: if LLM agents are to trade or cooperate in real markets, their phase-transition behavior needs to be mapped to avoid unstable regimes
- **Incentive design for AI systems**: the bistability near control boundaries suggests that incentive structures need explicit design to avoid flipping between qualitatively different population states
- **Resource allocation in federated AI systems**: understanding which agents can extract value from information advantages informs how to distribute compute or data among competing models
- **Robustness testing for autonomous agent swarms**: the collapse of goal dispersion under coupling could threaten coordination in robotics or distributed systems

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **LLM Market Simulation Platform**: A B2B tool for enterprises to test how teams of LLM agents will behave under different incentive structures, payment rules, and information regimes—using the cached-call reproducibility model to enable cheap, re-runnable scenario analysis.

2. **Bifurcation-Aware Incentive Designer**: Software that automatically detects phase transitions and bistability regions in multi-agent systems and recommends control parameter ranges that avoid sharp, unpredictable behavior flips.

3. **Information-Auditing Service**: Audit LLM agent economies to identify which agents are extracting disproportionate value via information asymmetry, and suggest market designs (like symmetric information revelation) to level the field.

4. **Emergent Coalition Formation Engine**: A platform for modeling and optimizing coalitions among LLM agents, using submodularity and conditional independence tests to predict which team structures will be stable vs. breakable.

5. **Agent Microeconomics Research-as-a-Service**: Offer pre-registered, mechanically reproducible experiments on frontier LLM agent behavior (piggy-backing on this paper's methodology) to AI labs and governance researchers needing empirical validation of theory.

## Technical Limitations

- **Sample size and seeds**: only 72 population runs, and outcomes appear seed-selected near boundaries; unclear whether this reflects fundamental brittleness or just insufficient sampling near phase transitions
- **Single model family**: experiments use only Claude Opus 4.8; generalization to other frontier models (GPT-4, Gemini, etc.) is untested
- **Small economy scale**: unclear whether observations hold as economies scale to hundreds or thousands of agents; mean-field theory is designed for large N limits
- **Information transparency**: agents see all market signals; in realistic settings, information is dispersed, noisy, or strategically withheld—this could stabilize diversity
- **Stateless agents**: LLMs are per-call stateless; they don't learn or update beliefs across rounds; real economies involve memory and adaptation
- **Linear payoff structures**: parimutuel coupling is simple; real markets have feedback loops, short-selling, bankruptcies, and exit—which could reshape the attractor landscape

## Open Questions

- **Does the collapse of goal dispersion persist with partial information?** If agents only observe noisy signals of others' wealth or goals, does the bistability wash out?
- **What happens with agent learning and memory?** If agents are given persistent memory (e.g., a transcript of past interactions), do they escape the bistability and approach predicted diversity?
- **Are there control levers that yield smooth, not sharp, transitions?** The paper tested two levers; is there a design that avoids the step-function response?
- **How do weaker models behave?** Does goal dispersion collapse for Claude 3.5 Sonnet, or does it only appear in frontier models?
- **Can coalition synergy be maintained at scale?** The XOR synergy test worked in tiny economies; does it survive in 100-agent or 1000-agent settings?
- **What is the mechanism of dispersion collapse?** Is it that agents converge to identical strategies, that they misalign their goals, or that they defect from the cooperative regime?

## Key Takeaway
Frontier LLM agents in coupled markets confirm information-theoretic predictions about wealth and value, but exhibit sharp phase transitions and bistability instead of smooth dispersion—revealing a fundamental mismatch between classical mean-field population theory and how LLMs actually self-organize.
