# The Dynamic Verifiable Multi-Agent Human Agentic Loyalty Loop (DVM-HALL) Model and the Net Human-Agent Score (NHAS) in Autonomous Commerce

**Authors:** Sai Srikanth Madugula, Peplluis Esteva de la Rosa, Daya Shankar
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.13998v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13998v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces the Dynamic Verifiable Multi-Agent Human Agentic Loyalty Loop (DVM-HALL), a new framework for understanding customer loyalty in an era where AI agents autonomously make purchasing decisions. Traditional loyalty models assume human consumers act alone; this work shows they fail when autonomous agents execute transactions on behalf of humans or in competition with them. The paper formalizes how trust, delegation, and execution risk jointly shape agent-brand relationships, and proposes the Net Human-Agent Score (NHAS) to measure human-agent alignment in commerce.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on three pillars: (1) **human-machine teaming** — research on how humans and machines collaborate toward shared goals; (2) **consumer decision theory** — classical models of brand choice and loyalty, traditionally based on emotional attachment and repeat purchasing; and (3) **algorithmic trust dynamics** — the study of how people delegate authority to automated systems and adjust trust based on outcomes.

A key insight is that autonomous agents exhibit **bounded rationality** — they optimize for metrics humans define, but not always the way humans expect. The paper also draws on **game theory** (multi-agent strategic interaction) and **mechanism design** from decentralized finance, where smart contracts execute transactions with measurable costs (gas fees, slippage, MEV exposure) that affect agent performance.

## Core Contributions

1. **DVM-HALL formalization**: A softmax probability model where brand choice emerges from five jointly-weighted factors: human emotional equity, agentic utility, calibrated trust, delegated authority, and verifiable execution outcomes. Critically, this *updates dynamically* — trust and delegation shift after each transaction.

2. **Execution risk as brand signal**: First to incorporate DeFi-specific execution hazards (gas costs, slippage, MEV frontrunning, smart-contract bugs) as *predictors* of agentic brand preference. This moves beyond behavioral economics into blockchain-native commerce.

3. **Net Human-Agent Score (NHAS)**: A verifiable, auditable metric combining human feedback, execution logs, benchmark performance, and cryptographic receipts to measure alignment between human intent and agent behavior.

4. **Three-stage validation roadmap**: Proposes controlled experiments (human shopping with agent assistance), multi-agent market simulations, and live DeFi testbeds — grounding theory in empirical evidence.

## Connections to Other Work

This research intersects several areas:

- **[[Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)]]**: NHAS is conceptually similar — using human signals to steer agent behavior. But NHAS is asynchronous and transaction-level, not training-time.
- **[[Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning]]**: The framework assumes agents compete/cooperate in markets; extends prior work to include human stakeholders with explicit trust models.
- **[[Alignment research]]**: Core concern is agent-human value alignment in autonomous commerce — a concrete instantiation of the alignment problem.
- **Behavioral economics & loyalty programs**: Critiques traditional models (Reichheld, Oliver) as agent-blind; proposes an agent-inclusive alternative.
- **Decentralized finance**: Treats DeFi execution risk as endogenous to brand choice, bridging a gap between fintech and consumer behavior.

## Practical Applications

1. **AI-native loyalty programs**: Brands building customer bases where agents hold assets or execute trades on behalf of humans. NHAS measures whether the agent is trustworthy.

2. **DeFi yield-farming partnerships**: Protocols could offer tokenized rewards; NHAS predicts which ones users' agents will prefer based on gas costs, slippage history, and audit trails.

3. **Autonomous portfolio management**: Wealth platforms using agents to rebalance or hedge. NHAS tracks alignment between client intent and agent execution, reducing disputes.

4. **Supply-chain procurement automation**: Enterprises deploy agents to negotiate with suppliers. DVM-HALL predicts which vendors agents will favor, based on execution reliability and trust history.

5. **Agent marketplaces**: Platforms where humans rent or subscribe to agents (e.g., trading bots, shopping assistants). NHAS becomes a rating system — like Uber's star system but with verifiable proof.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **"LoyaltyOS"** — A SaaS platform that applies DVM-HALL and NHAS to existing ecommerce brands. Ingests agent transaction logs and human feedback; outputs trust scores and brand-switching risk alerts. White-label for enterprise.

2. **"Agent Trustmark"** — A certification body and auditing service. Brands/protocols submit execution logs; third-party auditors compute NHAS and issue verifiable badges. Monetize via certification fees and ongoing audit subscriptions.

3. **"HumanAgent Hedge"** — A portfolio/fund manager that actively trades or allocates capital *only* to brands/protocols with high NHAS scores. Targets humans who want autonomy but with guardrails. Revenue via AUM fee.

4. **"Delegation Dashboard"** — A personal-finance app that helps humans visualize and adjust how much authority they delegate to agents (e.g., "I let my shopping bot spend up to $100 autonomously"). Uses DVM-HALL to forecast brand risk and optimize settings.

5. **"DeFi Agent Insurance"** — Underwrite execution risk (MEV slippage, smart-contract exploit losses). Premium based on NHAS of the protocol/agent. Claims paid in stablecoin or tokens.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Softmax weighting is opaque**: The model requires calibrating five weights (emotional equity, utility, trust, delegation, execution). How are these learned? Paper sketches "empirical validation" but doesn't detail the parameter-tuning regime. Risk of overfitting to training data.

2. **Trust dynamics under adversarial conditions**: Model assumes trust updates rationally after failures. In practice, humans may over-trust good-looking agents (performative trust) or abandon all agents after one high-profile hack (trust cliff). The recursive update rule needs robustness proofs.

3. **Verifiable execution is DeFi-only**: The framework shines when outcomes are blockchain-auditable (smart contracts, transaction hashes). For traditional ecommerce, "verifiable execution" is weaker — relies on merchant logs, which can be falsified. Limits applicability.

4. **No formal game-theoretic analysis**: Paper doesn't model strategic agents who might game NHAS (e.g., low-execution-risk strategies that hurt long-term value). If agents compete on NHAS, incentive misalignment could emerge.

5. **Human feedback bias**: NHAS incorporates human feedback as a signal. But humans are biased, inconsistent, and susceptible to framing. How robust is NHAS to adversarial feedback?

6. **Scalability of auditing**: Three-stage validation plan is labor-intensive. How does it scale to millions of agents and trillions in transaction volume?

## Open Questions

1. **How do emotional equity and agentic utility interact?** Does an agent with perfect execution but poor UX underperform one with mediocre execution but high brand charisma? Where's the empirical calibration?

2. **What is the minimal viable NHAS?** Can you compute meaningful trust scores with sparse feedback, or does every agent need dense transaction history? Crucial for bootstrapping.

3. **Does DVM-HALL predict real agent-brand loyalty?** The paper proposes validation but doesn't report results. Do real humans' agents actually follow the softmax formula? Or do they exhibit path-dependence, anchoring, or other behavioral anomalies?

4. **How do you prevent NHAS gaming?** If agents optimize for NHAS rather than human utility, does the score become Goodhart's Law in action? What safeguards are needed?

5. **Can trust be repaired?** Once an agent fails catastrophically, does dynamic recalibration ever rebuild trust, or is delegation permanently revoked? Temporal dynamics are underspecified.

6. **Multi-objective alignment**: What if human and agent goals diverge (e.g., agent prefers high-volatility trades for MEV, human wants stability)? How does NHAS weight conflicting objectives?

## Key Takeaway
AI agents acting autonomously in commerce need new loyalty frameworks; this paper formalizes trust, delegation, and execution risk to measure human-agent alignment.
