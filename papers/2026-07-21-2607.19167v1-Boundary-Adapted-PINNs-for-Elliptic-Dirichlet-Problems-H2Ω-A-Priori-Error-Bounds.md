# Boundary-Adapted PINNs for Elliptic Dirichlet Problems: $H^2(Ω)$ A Priori Error Bounds with Application to Mean Escape Time Computation

**Authors:** Nathanael Tepakbong, Jun Fan, Xiang Zhou, Ding-Xuan Zhou
**Published:** 2026-07-21 | **arXiv:** [2607.19167v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19167v1)

## Summary
This paper develops Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) — neural networks trained to solve differential equations — with theoretical guarantees for a specific class of boundary value problems. The key insight is that to get accurate solutions, the boundary condition enforcement method must satisfy a particular mathematical property (being "normalized to first order"), not just enforce the boundary exactly. This matters because it provides both practical guidance and theoretical understanding for using neural networks to solve differential equations, with application to computing mean escape times in stochastic processes.

## Theoretical Background
Physics-Informed Neural Networks combine neural network approximation with domain knowledge: they are trained on both the differential equation (PDE) itself and its boundary conditions, rather than on labeled data. The paper builds on approximation theory — the study of how well finite-dimensional models (like neural networks) can approximate infinite-dimensional function spaces — and learning theory, which bounds generalization error. Key prior work includes classical PINN methodology and approximation-theoretic results for shallow networks (small depth). The paper extends these by analyzing higher-order derivatives and establishing error bounds in $H^2(Ω)$, a Sobolev space of functions with two square-integrable derivatives. The motivation — computing Mean Escape Time for stochastic processes — requires solving an elliptic PDE with Dirichlet boundary conditions (values specified at the domain boundary).

## Core Contributions
1. **Boundary-adapted PINN framework**: Shows that naive exact boundary enforcement is insufficient; the distance-to-boundary function $ρ$ must satisfy a "first-order normalization" condition to achieve $H^2$ convergence. This is a concrete, verifiable design principle.
2. **A priori error bounds**: Provides explicit rates showing how approximation error depends on the choice of $ρ$, network architecture (ReQU and tanh), and problem regularity.
3. **VC-dimension bounds**: Derives new complexity measures for higher-order derivatives of ReLU-like networks (ReQU, tanh), extending classical learning theory.
4. **Shallow ReQU approximation theory**: Establishes new bounds for approximating functions in higher-order Sobolev norms using shallow networks.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper sits at the intersection of **numerical analysis** (solving PDEs) and **machine learning theory** (function approximation and statistical learning). It relates to broader PINN literature but differs by providing rigorous a priori analysis rather than empirical investigation. The work connects to [[approximation theory]] and [[Sobolev spaces]], classical tools in numerical PDE analysis. The boundary enforcement strategy echoes techniques in **constrained neural network training**, though the novelty here is showing why certain constraints are theoretically justified. The Mean Escape Time application links to stochastic processes and computational probability.

## Practical Applications
1. **Stochastic process simulation**: Computing mean escape times for particles diffusing through regions with barriers (drug delivery in biological tissues, ion transport in cells).
2. **Geometric PDEs**: Solving Laplace/Poisson equations in irregular domains where mesh-based methods are cumbersome (geophysics, fluid dynamics around complex geometries).
3. **Option pricing in finance**: PDEs govern option values; neural network solvers could handle high-dimensional problems where traditional methods fail.
4. **Uncertainty quantification**: Neural networks can encode both the solution and its gradient, enabling efficient uncertainty propagation.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **PDE solver as a cloud service**: Specialized neural network training platform for engineers/scientists to input custom PDEs and boundary conditions; auto-selects appropriate distance functions and architectures.
2. **Scientific ML IDE**: Visual tool for specifying domains, PDEs, and boundary conditions; automatically suggests network architectures based on smoothness requirements and generates error estimates.
3. **High-dimensional finance engine**: Dedicated software for solving pricing PDEs in 5+ dimensions (basket options, multi-asset derivatives) using boundary-adapted PINNs with embedded uncertainty bounds.
4. **Computational biology simulator**: Platform for solving diffusion/transport PDEs in cellular compartments (mitochondria, nucleus); automatically handles irregular boundaries from microscopy data.
5. **Parametric PDE solver**: Meta-learning system that solves families of PDEs (varying domain shape, coefficients) by learning distance-to-boundary representations once, then fine-tuning for variants.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Construction of $ρ$**: The theory requires a specific distance approximation function; the paper cites arXiv:2104.08426 for construction, but this is non-trivial and may not generalize to all domain shapes.
2. **Dimension scaling**: Approximation rates likely degrade exponentially in spatial dimension $d$ (curse of dimensionality), though the paper does not fully characterize this.
3. **High-order regularity**: The $H^2$ bounds assume the true solution is sufficiently smooth; PDEs with weak solutions or singularities violate this assumption.
4. **Network width**: Bounds are non-constructive — they do not specify how wide the network must be in practice to achieve a given error tolerance.
5. **Training dynamics**: Theory assumes optimal weights; in practice, gradient-based training may converge slowly or to local minima, especially for complex domains.

## Open Questions
1. How do error bounds extend to higher Sobolev norms ($H^3$, $H^4$)? Does the normalization condition still suffice?
2. Can the framework handle Neumann or mixed boundary conditions (gradient specified at boundary)?
3. What is the computational complexity trade-off between network width and training time compared to mesh-based solvers?
4. Do the VC-dimension bounds lead to practical sample complexity bounds, or are they too loose?
5. How does the approach perform on truly high-dimensional problems (e.g., $d \geq 10$)? Are there adaptive refinement strategies?

## Key Takeaway
Properly designed boundary enforcement in neural networks, not just exact boundary values, is theoretically necessary for accurate PDE solutions.
