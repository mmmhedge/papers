# Danus: Orchestrating Mathematical Reasoning Agents with Fact-Graph Memory

**Authors:** Jihao Liu, Guoxiong Gao, Zeming Sun, Bin Wu, Shurui Liu et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06447v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06447v1)

## Summary
This paper presents Danus, a system that orchestrates multiple AI agents to collaboratively solve research-level mathematics problems by sharing a unified "fact graph" — a structured memory of verified mathematical claims and their dependencies. Rather than having a single agent solve problems linearly, Danus coordinates parallel proof search across multiple worker agents while maintaining correctness through verification and organization. The work demonstrates that this approach can tackle genuine open problems in algebraic geometry and combinatorics, showing a practical path toward AI systems that can contribute to mathematical research.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on recent progress in [[large-language-models]] (LLMs) for mathematical reasoning — systems like GPT-4 and specialized math models that can generate proofs and formal reasoning. The key prior insight is that LLMs struggle with long-horizon, multi-step problems because they lose track of intermediate results and can accumulate errors. Danus applies ideas from multi-agent coordination (where multiple agents collaborate toward shared goals) and knowledge graphs (structured databases of facts and relationships) to mathematics. The verifier component draws on formal verification traditions — checking that claims are sound before accepting them. The fact graph itself is inspired by dependency tracking in software build systems and program synthesis work, adapted here to organize mathematical proofs as directed graphs where nodes are verified facts and edges represent logical dependencies.

## Core Contributions
1. **Fact-graph memory architecture**: A structured, persistent memory mechanism that stores mathematical facts alongside their proofs and logical dependencies, enabling incremental proof construction without losing context or accumulating unchecked claims.

2. **Orchestration framework**: A hierarchical agent design (main coordinator + parallel workers + stateless verifier) that decouples planning, proof search, and verification — allowing the system to scale worker agents without bottlenecks.

3. **Dynamic redirection and summarization**: The main agent periodically summarizes proof state and redirects workers toward promising directions, implementing adaptive search rather than rigid branching.

4. **Empirical validation on research problems**: Six case studies demonstrating that the system can construct long, detailed proofs in genuine research-level mathematics (algebraic geometry, singularity theory, combinatorics), not just textbook problems.

5. **Human-in-the-loop capability**: Progress reports and interaction points allow human mathematicians to guide the system, making it a tool for collaborative discovery rather than fully autonomous automation.

## Connections to Other Work
Danus relates to several research threads:

- **LLM reasoning systems** ([[Chain-of-Thought]], [[Tree-of-Thought]]): Danus generalizes the tree-search idea to a DAG (directed acyclic graph) of verified facts with explicit dependencies, improving on tree search's tendency to explore redundant branches.

- **Multi-agent systems**: Similar to systems like [[AutoGen]] and debate-based reasoning, but specialized for mathematics with formal verification as a core constraint.

- **Knowledge graphs in AI**: Draws on structured knowledge representation but applies it dynamically during reasoning rather than as static background knowledge.

- **Formal verification and proof assistants**: Adopts the discipline of verification-before-acceptance from systems like Coq and Lean, but uses LLMs as prover instead of human-written tactics.

- **Collaborative AI**: Related to work on human-AI collaboration in creative domains, adapted here to mathematical research where verification provides an objective correctness signal.

## Practical Applications

1. **Mathematical research acceleration**: Research teams in pure mathematics (algebraic geometry, topology, combinatorics) can use Danus as a "proof assistant partner" to explore conjectures, generate candidate proofs, and organize intermediate lemmas.

2. **Educational proof tutorials**: Mathematics instructors can use Danus to generate detailed, multi-step proofs with intermediate steps exposed via the fact graph, helping students understand proof structure and dependencies.

3. **Industrial applied mathematics**: Engineering and physics teams solving problems in optimization, signal processing, or materials science can collaboratively verify multi-step derivations with Danus.

4. **Automated literature synthesis**: Given a research problem and relevant papers, Danus can organize and verify cross-paper mathematical claims into a coherent proof structure.

5. **Conjecture-to-proof pipelines**: Combined with symbolic math systems (Mathematica, Singular), Danus can verify conjectures generated by search engines and organize families of related proofs.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Verifier soundness assumption**: The stateless verifier checks syntax and logical form but may not catch subtle mathematical errors (e.g., unstated assumptions, edge cases). It cannot verify deep mathematical claims without a formal proof assistant backend — Danus still relies on LLM confidence, which can be brittle.

2. **Scalability of fact graph**: As the graph grows, organizing and searching it efficiently becomes challenging. No mention of graph size limits or performance degradation. Very large proofs might create explosion of dependencies.

3. **Domain limitation**: Evaluation focuses on algebraic geometry, singularity theory, combinatorics — domains where symbolic manipulation and formal structure are clear. Likely less effective for probabilistic or applied mathematics with empirical components.

4. **Worker agent heterogeneity**: The system assumes all worker agents have similar reasoning capability. If workers diverge in quality, coordination overhead may outweigh parallelism benefits.

5. **Human bottleneck**: While the system supports human interaction, the paper doesn't quantify how often human guidance is needed. For fully autonomous research-level work, the system may still rely heavily on human redirection at scale.

6. **Generalization to new domains**: No evidence that Danus can self-adapt to new mathematical subfields; likely requires retuning the main agent's summarization and redirection heuristics per domain.

## Open Questions

1. **Can Danus integrate formal proof assistants?** Would coupling the verifier to Lean or Coq dramatically improve soundness? What's the performance trade-off?

2. **How does fact-graph size scale with problem complexity?** What's the largest proof Danus has tackled? At what graph size does performance degrade?

3. **How much human guidance is actually needed for the six case studies?** Quantify the feedback loop — how many redirections per worker agent, on average?

4. **Can the main agent learn redirection strategies?** Instead of hand-tuned heuristics, could reinforcement learning optimize which direction to steer workers based on past proof progress?

5. **How does Danus perform on problems with multiple valid proof strategies?** Does it prematurely converge to one strategy, or explore the proof-strategy landscape?

6. **Can the system compose proofs across distant mathematical domains?** E.g., if a proof in algebraic geometry uses techniques from number theory, can Danus discover and integrate cross-domain lemmas?

## Key Takeaway
Danus shows that orchestrating multiple reasoning agents around a shared, verified fact graph is an effective route to scaling AI-assisted mathematical research — enabling long-horizon proofs through parallelization, error-checking, and explicit proof organization.
