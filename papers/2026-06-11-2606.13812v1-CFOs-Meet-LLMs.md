# CFOs Meet LLMs

**Authors:** John R. Graham, Campbell R. Harvey, Manish Jha
**Published:** 2026-06-11 | **arXiv:** [2606.13812v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13812v1)

## Summary
This paper demonstrates that large language models can accurately reproduce CFO sentiment responses from the Duke-Federal Reserve CFO Survey by role-playing as specific company executives at specific dates. By prompting an LLM with firm-specific information and historical context, the authors show that predicted optimism scores significantly forecast actual CFO survey responses, offering a scalable alternative to expensive, slow traditional business sentiment surveys.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on two foundational literatures: (1) **business sentiment measurement** via the Duke-Federal Reserve CFO Survey (2002-2025), which is a gold-standard but expensive periodic survey of executive expectations; and (2) **prompt-based few-shot learning with LLMs**, where models can be conditioned on contextual information to make domain-specific predictions. The paper implicitly leverages research on [[prompt-engineering]] and [[in-context-learning]] to show that LLMs can encode meaningful economic knowledge about specific firms and time periods. It also connects to work on [[behavioral-economics]] insofar as CFO sentiment reflects both rational expectations and psychological biases that an LLM can apparently capture.

## Core Contributions
1. **Empirical validation of LLM-as-digital-twin**: First demonstration that an LLM can reliably predict individual CFO survey responses, not just aggregate sentiment.
2. **Causal design**: Uses firm and year-quarter fixed effects plus lagged responses to rule out simple time-series or firm-level confounds.
3. **Scaling law for information**: Shows that predictive accuracy improves systematically with (a) respondent history, (b) firm characteristics, and (c) information richness—suggesting the LLM genuinely leverages conditioning data.
4. **Quarterly aggregation**: Demonstrates robustness when aggregating predictions, supporting use as a high-frequency expectations data source.

## Connections to Other Work
- **[[Digital-twins]]**: This is a concrete financial application of the digital-twin concept—using an LLM to simulate a specific agent (CFO) with personalized, time-variant behavior.
- **[[Financial-modeling]] & expectations**: Related to rational expectations models in macroeconomics and behavioral finance; offers a new way to extract forward-looking expectations without survey costs.
- **[[Information-extraction]]**: The paper is fundamentally about extracting latent economic sentiment from unstructured firm data via LLM prompting.
- **[[Agent-based-modeling]]**: LLM-as-CFO mirrors agent-based computational models but with learned representations rather than explicit rules.
- **[[Time-series-forecasting]]**: CFO sentiment is used as a leading indicator for real economic outcomes; this work enables higher-frequency forecasting.

## Practical Applications
1. **Real-time business cycle monitoring**: Central banks and policy institutions can now access firm-level sentiment at quarterly or even monthly frequency, enabling faster macroeconomic signal detection vs. traditional surveys.
2. **Equity research & earnings forecasting**: Asset managers can use LLM-predicted CFO sentiment as an input to earnings models, firm valuation, and sector rotation strategies.
3. **Credit risk assessment**: Lenders can assess firm outlook risk by querying LLM-CFO twins with evolving firm conditions, improving credit models.
4. **M&A and corporate strategy**: Acquirers can simulate target-company CFO sentiment under post-deal scenarios to validate cultural and strategic fit.
5. **Regulatory stress-testing**: Regulators can generate synthetic CFO responses under adverse macroeconomic scenarios to supplement or replace costly, slow survey-based stress tests.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **"CFO Pulse" B2B SaaS**: Real-time subscription service querying LLM-CFO twins monthly for thousands of US firms, selling sentiment indices to asset managers, economists, and policy institutions. API-driven; compete on data freshness and firm coverage vs. Duke survey.
2. **AI-powered executive survey platform**: Replace costly manual surveys with LLM role-play interviews. Survey any population (C-suite, boards, investors) at scale; clients input firm/market data and get instant, bias-corrected sentiment responses.
3. **"Scenario Engine" for corporate planning**: SaaS tool allowing CFOs to simulate their own LLM twin under different macroeconomic scenarios (recession, rate shock, etc.), generating sentiment-conditioned forecasts of capex, hiring, debt refinancing.
4. **Hedge fund sentiment-alpha signal**: Proprietary real-time LLM-CFO sentiment data as a systematic trading signal. Publish monthly "Synthetic Sentiment Index" (SSI) for equities and credit markets; license to quant funds.
5. **Regulatory intelligence platform**: Automated quarterly "mood monitoring" of regulated firms (banks, insurers) to flag emerging capital or liquidity stress before disclosures; sell to regulators and compliance vendors.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Generalization to other surveys/geographies**: Paper uses only one survey (Duke CFO). Unclear if the approach transfers to other business sentiment surveys (PMI, Conference Board, international) or to non-executive populations.
2. **Data leakage risk**: LLM training data likely includes financial news and reports on many of these firms through 2024; cannot fully rule out that the model is pattern-matching on public news rather than truly "understanding" firm fundamentals.
3. **Temporal boundary**: Training cutoff (likely late 2024) means the model cannot incorporate very recent news; predictions for 2025+ may degrade.
4. **Heterogeneity in firm types**: Small private firms, startups, and non-US firms are not in the CFO survey; method's applicability to broader firm universe is untested.
5. **Sentiment vs. action**: CFO optimism may not translate into actual capex, hiring, or debt decisions; the paper doesn't validate that LLM sentiment correlates with real firm outcomes.
6. **Prompt engineering sensitivity**: Success likely depends heavily on prompt design, context window size, and model choice (GPT-4, etc.); reproducibility with smaller or open-source models unclear.

## Open Questions
1. **What information do LLMs actually use?**: Ablation study to identify which components of the conditioning data (recent news, earnings, macro data, history) are most predictive. Are there spurious correlations?
2. **Cross-survey validation**: Does the LLM-CFO sentiment predict other executive sentiment surveys (PMI, ISM, earnings call tone) or only the Duke survey it was trained on?
3. **Real economic outcomes**: Does LLM-predicted CFO optimism correlate with subsequent firm capex, hiring, debt issuance, or equity returns? This is essential for policy/investment use.
4. **Global applicability**: Train on non-US firm data and test on international CFO surveys or equivalent (e.g., European Central Bank, Bank of Japan quarterly executive surveys).
5. **Model interpretability**: Which firms/sectors does the LLM consistently get wrong? Are there systematic biases (e.g., tech firms vs. industrials, high-leverage vs. low-leverage)?
6. **Adversarial robustness**: Can adversaries or biased prompts easily manipulate LLM-CFO sentiment predictions? How stable are responses to small changes in prompt wording?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs can serve as scalable, real-time digital twins of corporate executives, enabling continuous monitoring of business sentiment at a fraction of the cost and latency of traditional surveys—potentially transforming how policymakers and investors track forward-looking economic expectations.
