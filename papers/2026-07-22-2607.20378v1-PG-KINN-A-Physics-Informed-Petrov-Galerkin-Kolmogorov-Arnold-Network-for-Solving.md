# PG-KINN: A Physics-Informed Petrov-Galerkin Kolmogorov-Arnold Network for Solving Forward and Inverse PDEs

**Authors:** Amirhossein Sadr, Nima Soltani, Vahideh Moghtadaiee, Aida Pakniyat, Dara Rahmati et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-22 | **arXiv:** [2607.20378v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20378v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces PG-KINN, a method that combines Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs — neural networks with learnable spline activations) with a Petrov-Galerkin weak formulation to solve partial differential equations (PDEs) both forward and inverse problems. The key insight is that by using a weak formulation (integration by parts) instead of strong-form residual minimization, the method achieves better numerical conditioning, lower differentiation requirements, and works for broader classes of PDEs including non-self-adjoint and nonlinear systems.

## Theoretical Background
Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) use neural networks to approximate PDE solutions by minimizing residuals of the governing equations. Traditional PINNs rely on multilayer perceptrons (MLPs), which suffer from spectral bias — they struggle to capture high-frequency components and require high-precision derivatives.

Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs) are a newer architecture where neuron activations are learnable spline functions rather than fixed nonlinearities. This structural alignment with polynomial bases used in classical numerical methods (finite elements) makes them more natural for PDE solving.

The Petrov-Galerkin method is a weak formulation of PDEs where both trial and test function spaces need not be identical. Integration by parts reduces differentiation requirements compared to strong-form residual minimization. Piecewise-polynomial test spaces with compact support (used here with Gauss-Legendre quadrature) are standard in finite element methods and provide excellent numerical conditioning through localized residuals.

## Core Contributions
1. **Petrov-Galerkin formulation for KANs**: First application of PG weak form to KAN-based PDE solving, pairing KAN trial spaces with independent piecewise-polynomial test spaces.

2. **Solves a collapse problem**: Demonstrates that standard energy (Bubnov-Galerkin) formulations fail for inverse parameter-identification problems by collapsing to trivial solutions; PG formulation avoids this.

3. **General applicability**: Method works for non-self-adjoint, nonlinear, and inverse PDEs, whereas energy formulations require self-adjoint operators and strong-form methods require careful loss weighting.

4. **Empirical validation across diverse benchmarks**: Crack singularities, stress concentration, Neo-Hookean hyperelasticity, inverse heterogeneous media, and complex geometries all show consistent improvements over MLP baselines and competing KAN variants (PIKAN).

## Connections to Other Work
This builds directly on [[Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)]] but replaces MLPs with KANs. It addresses known limitations of strong-form PINN formulations documented in prior work on spectral bias and derivative estimation error.

The weak formulation connection links to classical finite element method (FEM) theory, particularly Petrov-Galerkin projection. Unlike boundary integral formulations, PG-KINN doesn't require knowledge of fundamental solutions, making it more broadly applicable.

Relates to recent work on improving neural network interpretability through structural alignment with classical discretization schemes — KANs' spline basis naturally mirrors polynomial FEM bases.

## Practical Applications
1. **Structural mechanics and engineering simulation**: Solving elastic/plastic deformation, crack propagation in materials with singular stress fields.

2. **Inverse material property identification**: Inferring spatially-varying elastic moduli, permeability, or conductivity from noisy measurements without requiring explicit regularization tuning.

3. **Multi-physics and heterogeneous media**: Subsurface flow in geological formations with spatially-variable properties; thermal-mechanical coupling.

4. **Complex geometry handling**: Domains with intricate boundaries, holes, or stress concentrations where classical FEM meshing is expensive.

5. **Real-time surrogate models**: Once trained, evaluates PDE solutions instantly for design optimization or uncertainty quantification in engineering workflows.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Physics-informed ML platform for materials discovery**: Cloud service that takes experimental microscopy/measurement data and inverse-solves for material properties across heterogeneous samples in hours instead of days of FEM simulation.

2. **Structural health monitoring SaaS**: Embed trained PG-KINN models on edge devices to infer internal stress/strain/crack states in bridges, pipelines, or aircraft from sparse sensor measurements in real-time.

3. **Geophysical inversion for energy exploration**: Fast subsurface characterization (porosity, permeability) from seismic/electromagnetic surveys using inverse PDE solving — significantly faster than traditional inversion.

4. **Digital twins for manufacturing**: Real-time surrogate models of injection-molding, forging, or composite curing processes that run on factory controllers to optimize process parameters on-the-fly.

5. **Computational consulting tool**: Sell as a specialized solver competing with commercial FEM packages (ANSYS, COMSOL) for users who want faster, more interpretable solutions without mesh generation overhead.

## Technical Limitations
1. **KAN scalability**: Compared to MLPs, KANs have higher computational cost per forward pass (spline evaluations + basis computations). Unclear how method scales to very high-dimensional PDE systems (>100D).

2. **Test space design**: Requires manual selection of piecewise-polynomial test space (order, support size, quadrature nodes). No principled guidance given for choosing these hyperparameters in new problem domains.

3. **Singular geometries**: While crack singularities are handled, the method assumes smooth or piecewise-smooth geometry. Fractal boundaries or highly non-convex domains not tested.

4. **Inverse problem conditioning**: Though PG avoids collapse, ill-conditioning of inverse problems (parameter non-identifiability, measurement noise) is not fully addressed; appears to rely on data quality.

5. **Comparison fairness**: Benchmarks compare primarily to standard PINN and PIKAN; missing comparisons to hybrid approaches (e.g., neural networks + classical FEM coupling) or recent adaptive PINN variants.

6. **Theoretical guarantees**: No convergence rates or approximation error bounds provided; purely empirical validation.

## Open Questions
1. How do KAN spline knot distributions and polynomial test space resolution couple? Is there an adaptive refinement strategy?

2. Can the method handle parametric PDEs (PDEs with high-dimensional parameter spaces) efficiently, or does KAN scaling become prohibitive?

3. For inverse problems, what is the regime where PG-KINN's conditioning advantages translate to robustness under realistic measurement noise and model mismatch?

4. Does the method generalize to PDEs on manifolds or non-Euclidean domains?

5. Can automatic differentiation combined with local test function support enable sparse Jacobian/Hessian computations for large-scale inverse problems?

6. How does performance degrade when test space polynomial order is under-specified for the solution smoothness?

## Key Takeaway
Pairing learnable spline networks with weak-form PDE solvers yields more accurate, interpretable solutions for complex mechanics problems than standard neural PDE methods.
