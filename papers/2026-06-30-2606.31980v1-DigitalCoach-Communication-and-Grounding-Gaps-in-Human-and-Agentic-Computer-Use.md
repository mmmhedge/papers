# DigitalCoach: Communication and Grounding Gaps in Human and Agentic Computer Use Coaching

**Authors:** Meng Chen, Anya Ji, Tsung-Han Wu, Tobias Maringgele, David M. Chan et al.
**Published:** 2026-06-30 | **arXiv:** [2606.31980v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31980v1)

## Summary
DigitalCoach introduces a dataset of 72 real coaching sessions where human experts teach novices to use software, captured with screen recordings and dialogue transcripts. The paper shows that current AI models struggle to coach effectively—they give direct instructions but fail to explain *why*, diagnose errors, or ground their advice in what's actually visible on screen. This matters because as AI agents automate more software tasks, they need to bridge the gap between what they can do and what they can teach humans to do.

## Theoretical Background
This work sits at the intersection of two areas. First, **human-computer interaction (HCI)** has long studied how to design effective instruction and tutoring systems—the idea that good teaching involves not just stating steps but scaffolding understanding, checking comprehension, and adapting to learner misconceptions. Second, recent advances in **multimodal language models** (models that process both text and images) have enabled agents to "see" and interact with computer screens, but most work focuses on task completion, not instruction or grounding. The paper also draws on work in **visual question answering** and **screen understanding**, where models struggle to reliably connect language to UI elements.

## Core Contributions
1. **DigitalCoach dataset**: A novel multimodal dataset of 72 authentic coaching sessions (22,752 dialogue turns, 28.1 hours of screen/input recordings) across five real applications. This is the first large-scale resource pairing natural human coaching with fine-grained ground-truth screen and interaction data.
2. **Empirical analysis of model coaching failures**: Automated metrics show models produce fewer explanations, error diagnoses, and knowledge-check questions than humans—they default to imperative instruction instead of adaptive teaching.
3. **Visual grounding gap**: Even when models are constrained to match human coaching strategies, their utterances show poor grounding—they reference UI elements incorrectly or miss relevant visual context.
4. **Interactive human evaluation**: User studies demonstrate that learners paired with model coaches passively follow instructions and gain less understanding than those coached by humans.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to:
- **[[Instruction following and grounding]]** in language models—much work assumes models can ground language in visual context, but this paper shows coaching (where grounding is critical) exposes that weakness.
- **[[RLHF]]** and alignment—the paper implicitly highlights a mismatch between what models optimize for (generating plausible text) and what makes good tutoring (adaptive, explanation-rich, visually accurate guidance).
- **[[Screen understanding and UI automation]]** (e.g., recent work on agents like Gato or WebAgent)—those systems focus on *doing* tasks; this paper shows they can't yet *teach* those tasks.
- **Pedagogical AI and intelligent tutoring systems**—a classical area that emphasizes error diagnosis, metacognitive scaffolding, and formative assessment; modern LLMs haven't imported these principles into multimodal coaching settings.

## Practical Applications
1. **AI-powered software onboarding**: Tech companies (Figma, Slack, Salesforce, etc.) could embed DigitalCoach-trained models to guide new users through complex workflows in real time.
2. **Accessible computing for older adults or people with disabilities**: Personalized software coaching agents that adjust explanation depth and visual highlighting based on user engagement.
3. **Internal corporate training**: Large enterprises could use these agents to reduce the burden on human trainers for repetitive software training (e.g., ERP systems, compliance tools).
4. **Educational software platforms**: EdTech companies could integrate coaching agents into learning management systems to provide just-in-time help without human tutors.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **InteractiveCoach Pro**: A SaaS layer that sits on top of any software application, providing real-time AI coaching adapted to screen state and user behavior. Charges per-seat or per-company.
2. **Knowledge Capture for Enterprise**: Record expert users at work, automatically synthesize coaching sessions, and deploy a fine-tuned model as the "always-on trainer" for that company's bespoke software.
3. **Visual Grounding as a Service (VGaaS)**: An API that takes a screenshot and user question, returns visually grounded explanations and element-level pointers. Sell to app developers and edtech platforms.
4. **Adaptive Onboarding Agent**: A white-label coaching agent for B2B SaaS platforms (CRM, design tools, analytics dashboards) that learns from customer cohort behavior and personalizes coaching depth and style.
5. **Accessibility-First Coaching**: Specialized coaching agents tuned for screen readers and high-contrast interfaces, serving as a bridge between complex software and users with visual impairments.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scale and domain specificity**: The dataset covers only five applications. Generalization to unseen software is unproven—coaching advice is highly UI-specific, so models may not transfer well.
2. **Visual grounding at scale**: Current vision-language models struggle with precise localization on dense, complex screens. The paper doesn't propose a solution, only documents the problem.
3. **Lack of real-time interaction loop**: The dataset is offline; models don't actually interact with the software, adjust instructions based on live user actions, or see results of their coaching in real time.
4. **No error recovery**: The paper doesn't address how models should diagnose *why* a user's action failed or recover from coaching mistakes—critical for adaptive tutoring.
5. **Human annotation cost**: Creating new coaching datasets requires expert human coaches and deep recording setup, limiting scalability.
6. **Evaluation metrics**: Automated metrics are coarse (instruction vs. explanation). Deeper pedagogical quality (Does this actually help learning?) relies on small user studies, not scalable metrics.

## Open Questions
1. **Can models learn to diagnose errors?** The paper shows models produce fewer diagnoses; can we fine-tune or prompt them to infer and explain user mistakes from video evidence?
2. **How do we measure grounding formally?** Is there a scalable metric (beyond user studies) to evaluate whether a generated utterance is visually faithful to the screen state?
3. **Multi-turn adaptation**: Does coaching quality improve if models see the learner's actual success/failure on prior instructions and adapt the next step's explanation?
4. **Cross-application transfer**: Can a model trained on DigitalCoach coaching sessions for one app (e.g., Word) transfer to another (e.g., Photoshop)? What's the generalization boundary?
5. **Human-in-the-loop coaching**: Would a hybrid system (human coach + AI agent as scribe or explanation generator) outperform either alone?
6. **Longitudinal learning outcomes**: Do learners taught by (improved) model coaches retain knowledge and become independent, or do they remain dependent on step-by-step guidance?

## Key Takeaway
Current AI models can automate software tasks but fail to teach humans to use them—they prioritize instruction over explanation, miss visual context, and produce passive rather than engaged learning.
