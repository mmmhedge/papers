# LKValues: Aligning Large Language Models with Sri Lankan Societal Values

**Authors:** Nethmi Muthugala,  Supryadi, Surangika Ranathunga, Nisansa de Silva, Ruijie Tao et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-22 | **arXiv:** [2607.20410v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20410v1)

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical gap in LLM value alignment: most existing approaches embed Western cultural norms, leaving underrepresented languages and non-Western societies poorly served. The authors create LKValues, a comprehensive resource suite grounded in Sri Lankan societal values, including a dataset of 150k instruction-tuning examples and a 1,000-instance benchmark in Sinhala and English. They show that even state-of-the-art models exhibit significant cultural and cross-lingual alignment gaps, and that fine-tuning on culturally-grounded data can meaningfully reduce these disparities.

## Theoretical Background

Value alignment in LLMs typically builds on [[RLHF]] (reinforcement learning from human feedback) and instruction-tuning paradigms, where models learn to produce outputs consistent with specified values or preferences. Most alignment work relies on Western datasets and frameworks—e.g., standard safety benchmarks assume English-speaking, globally-minded raters. The paper adapts established frameworks (like Schwartz's value theory) and uses survey-based elicitation to identify culturally-specific values. The core insight is that alignment is not culturally neutral: values around family, spirituality, hierarchy, and community differ significantly across societies, yet LLMs trained on Western-dominant corpora encode Western priorities by default.

## Core Contributions

1. **LKValues survey**: First systematic collection of 40 majority-endorsed societal values for Sri Lanka derived from 205 respondents, integrating both global frameworks and LLM-elicited local constructs (not imposed from outside).

2. **LKvaluesIT dataset**: 150k scenario-based instruction-tuning examples in Sinhala and English, derived from news corpora and human curation—a reusable resource for country-specific alignment.

3. **LKvaluesBench**: 1,000-instance evaluation benchmark explicitly designed for Sinhala-English value sensitivity, enabling rigorous measurement of alignment gaps.

4. **Empirical findings**: Concrete evidence that newer, larger models still fail on low-resource cultural alignment; fine-tuning on LKValues data reduces invalid outputs and cross-lingual disparities, though improvements are model-family dependent.

5. **Replicable pipeline**: The methodology (survey → value extraction → dataset construction → evaluation) is generalizable to other low-resource, pluralist contexts.

## Connections to Other Work

This work sits at the intersection of [[RLHF]], multilingual NLP, and fairness/bias mitigation. It extends prior work on:
- **Multilingual alignment**: Papers addressing non-English LLM performance (e.g., work on Aya, Qwen) but without explicit cultural value grounding.
- **Fairness and cultural bias**: Related to broader efforts in fairness in NLP, but more principled—grounded in actual surveyed values rather than abstract notions of harm.
- **Low-resource language alignment**: Complements work on Sinhala and other underserved languages, but adds a value-centric lens.
- **Instruction tuning**: Builds on standard fine-tuning practices but with cultural scaffolding (scenario design, value relevance).

The work implicitly critiques the "one-size-fits-all" alignment paradigm and advocates for pluralist, context-aware approaches.

## Practical Applications

1. **Sinhala customer support and public services**: Government chatbots, healthcare systems, and financial services in Sri Lanka can embed locally-appropriate values (respect for hierarchy, community welfare) in their responses.

2. **Culturally sensitive content moderation**: Social media platforms operating in Sri Lanka can tune moderation policies to reflect local sensitivities around religion, family honor, and cultural heritage.

3. **Educational applications**: LLM-powered tutoring and homework systems for Sinhala-speaking students can adapt to local pedagogical values and cultural narratives.

4. **News and media**: Automated content generation and fact-checking tools can maintain alignment with Sri Lankan editorial values and community concerns.

5. **Psychological and counseling services**: Mental health chatbots can be tuned to reflect Sinhala cultural attitudes toward family, collectivism, and emotional expression.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **LKValues-as-a-Service (SaaS)**: A platform offering fine-tuned LLM endpoints and evaluation APIs for Sri Lankan enterprises. Charge per-API call or per-model deployment; bundle with cultural training data and consulting.

2. **Country-specific value alignment consultancy**: Help governments and large enterprises in underrepresented regions (South Asia, Africa, Southeast Asia) build their own LKValues equivalents. Sell methodology, training, and licensing.

3. **Multilingual cultural chatbot marketplace**: Pre-fine-tuned, value-aligned models for common use cases (support, education, health) in Sinhala, Tamil, and English. Sell licenses or freemium access with premium customization.

4. **Value-aware content moderation SaaS**: For social platforms, news outlets, and e-commerce, offer moderation APIs that respect local cultural and societal values while flagging genuinely harmful content.

5. **Sinhala LLM training startup**: Build and sell open-source, culturally-aligned Sinhala foundation models. Monetize via commercial licensing, fine-tuning services, and partnerships with local government and enterprises.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Small survey sample (205 respondents)**: May not fully capture diversity within Sri Lanka; geographical, religious, and class-based value differences could be underrepresented.

2. **News-derived corpus bias**: Using news text may over-represent media outlets' values rather than grassroots sentiment; could skew toward urban, literate perspectives.

3. **Model-family dependency**: Fine-tuning improvements vary significantly across Qwen, Aya, and other families; no clear guidance on which base models are optimal for this task.

4. **Evaluation-only for Sinhala-English**: Cross-lingual transfer to Tamil (widely spoken in Sri Lanka) is not addressed; scalability to other language pairs unclear.

5. **Static value snapshot**: Cultural values evolve; dataset becomes outdated quickly. No clear mechanism for continuous re-alignment.

6. **Benchmark size (1,000 instances)**: Relatively small; may not catch long-tail edge cases or emerging cultural conflicts.

7. **Assumes monolithic "Sri Lankan values"**: In reality, values differ by region, religion, caste, and generation; the 40-value set is a simplification.

## Open Questions

1. **Cross-lingual transfer**: How well do Sinhala-tuned models transfer to Tamil, Malay, and other South/Southeast Asian languages? Is there a shared value alignment benefit?

2. **Value conflict resolution**: When surveyed values conflict (e.g., individual autonomy vs. family duty), how should LLMs navigate trade-offs? Can the benchmark and dataset handle this?

3. **Temporal stability**: Do fine-tuned models maintain alignment over time as user interactions accumulate? Do they drift toward Western-aligned outputs if exposed to English data post-deployment?

4. **Scaling survey methodology**: What's the minimum survey sample for reliable value extraction in a low-resource setting? Can LLM-based value elicitation replace human survey entirely?

5. **Negative transfer**: Does fine-tuning on Sri Lankan values degrade performance on global benchmarks (MMLU, etc.)? Is there an Pareto frontier here?

6. **Generalization to other underrepresented regions**: How replicable is this pipeline for, say, Nigeria, Vietnam, or Morocco? What contextual factors matter most?

7. **Human evaluation**: The benchmark uses automatic metrics; how do human raters (especially those from Sri Lanka) score the same outputs? Are there annotator-model alignment mismatches?

## Key Takeaway

Culturally-grounded fine-tuning on surveyed local values can align LLMs with non-Western societies, but requires context-specific datasets and careful model selection.
