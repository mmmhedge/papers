# Multi-Turn On-Policy Distillation with Prefix Replay

**Authors:** Baohao Liao, Hanze Dong, Christof Monz, Xinxing Xu, Li Dong et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-06 | **arXiv:** [2607.04763v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.04763v1)

## Summary
This paper tackles the problem of training smaller language models (students) to imitate larger ones (teachers) as they interact with environments over multiple steps — a task called on-policy distillation. The key insight is that standard approaches require expensive repeated queries to both the teacher and the environment; instead, the authors propose ReOPD, which reuses pre-collected teacher trajectories and has the student practice on selected steps, making training 4× faster while matching or improving performance.

## Theoretical Background
On-policy distillation builds on two traditions: (1) **behavioral cloning** — training a model to mimic expert actions — and (2) **on-policy reinforcement learning** — where an agent learns from its own live experience. In multi-turn agentic settings, the tension arises because the student's behavior gradually diverges from the teacher's, making old trajectories less relevant but also cheaper to reuse. The paper identifies a "prefix trap": improving student-on-policyness (making histories match what the student would see) paradoxically degrades teacher reliability on those same histories, creating a two-sided distribution shift that prior work hasn't explicitly modeled.

## Core Contributions
1. **Formalization of the prefix trap**: Explicit characterization of why naive multi-turn OPD fails — student occupancy and teacher target reliability pull in opposite directions.
2. **ReOPD method**: A practical off-environment algorithm that reuses teacher trajectories as "replayed prefixes," letting students act at selected checkpoints while receiving dense supervision without new environment calls.
3. **Step-decaying sampling schedule**: Simple heuristic that prioritizes early (low-distribution-shift) prefixes, treating multi-turn OPD as a reliability-aware prefix distribution design problem.
4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstration across mathematical reasoning (Python execution) and search environments showing 4× speedup per training step, zero tool calls during training, and performance preservation or improvement over full online OPD.

## Connections to Other Work
This work bridges [[reinforcement-learning]] (on-policy learning, distribution shift) and [[large-language-models]] distillation (mimicking teacher behavior). It relates to:
- **Behavioral cloning and covariate shift**: Extends foundational ideas about how student behavior drift breaks training.
- **Offline RL**: Similar motivation (reusing pre-collected data) but applied to the imitation/distillation setting rather than reward optimization.
- **Multi-step reasoning in LLMs**: Complements recent work on chain-of-thought and tool-use agents by making their training cheaper.
- **RLHF-style supervision**: Conceptually similar to dense per-step feedback, but applied to trajectory segments rather than whole episodes.

## Practical Applications
1. **Cost-efficient tool-use agent training**: Companies building coding agents, math solvers, or web automation can reuse expensive teacher rollouts across many student model sizes without re-querying external tools (APIs, code execution, search engines).
2. **Multi-model deployment**: Train specialized students for latency-sensitive or on-device scenarios by distilling from a larger teacher, amortizing the teacher cost across the student training pipeline.
3. **Cross-environment transfer**: Collect teacher demonstrations once in a complex environment (e.g., scientific simulation), then train multiple students on different aspects using the same offline dataset.
4. **Curriculum learning at scale**: Use the prefix-sampling schedule to automatically prioritize easier (early) parts of reasoning chains, enabling more efficient curriculum design without manual annotation.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Offline assumption**: Requires pre-collected teacher trajectories; does not improve performance on *new* environments or out-of-distribution user queries not covered by the original teacher rollouts.
2. **Prefix truncation bias**: By sampling early prefixes more heavily, the method may under-train on later reasoning steps where students most differ from teachers — potentially hurting long-horizon or iterative reasoning tasks.
3. **Teacher trajectory quality**: If the teacher rollouts are suboptimal or contain errors, the reuse amplifies those mistakes across student training. No mechanism to filter or adapt bad trajectories.
4. **Limited scalability analysis**: Unclear how the approach scales to very long trajectories (100+ steps) or to environments where step-level costs vary dramatically (e.g., expensive early simulation, cheap later refinement).
5. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: The step-decay schedule appears heuristic; the paper doesn't systematically study sensitivity to decay rate or other design choices.

## Open Questions
1. **Adaptive prefix sampling**: Can we learn the prefix distribution online during training, rather than using a fixed decay schedule? Could meta-learning or bandit approaches improve on the hand-tuned schedule?
2. **Trajectory filtering and weighting**: How should we weight or filter teacher trajectories by quality, uncertainty, or environment cost? Could importance weighting recover some of the lost on-policy benefit?
3. **Generalization to new tasks**: How much do learned students transfer when the environment, task, or tool set changes? Does the distillation quality degrade gracefully?
4. **Long-horizon reasoning**: Does the method work well on very long chains of thought (100+ steps)? Do later steps suffer from insufficient training?
5. **Combination with other distillation methods**: Can ReOPD be combined with on-the-fly distillation (mixing offline replay with occasional fresh rollouts) to balance cost and accuracy?

## Key Takeaway
By reusing teacher trajectories as "replayed prefixes" and sampling them carefully to avoid the prefix trap — the tension between student relevance and teacher reliability — ReOPD makes multi-turn agent distillation 4× cheaper while preserving performance, turning expensive environment interactions into a reusable offline asset.
