# FreyaTTS Technical Report

**Authors:** Ahmet Erdem Pamuk, Ömer Yentür, Ahmet Tunga Bayrak, Yavuz Alp Sencer Öztürk, Mustafa Yavuz
**Published:** 2026-07-10 | **arXiv:** [2607.09530v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09530v1)

## Summary
Freya-TTS is a compact 183M-parameter text-to-speech model optimized for Turkish, using a diffusion-based approach that works directly from text without requiring phoneme conversion or discrete speech tokenizers. It achieves high-quality 48 kHz audio synthesis while running faster than real-time on consumer CPUs, making it practical for edge deployment.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on several converging ideas: diffusion models (neural networks trained to gradually denoise random noise into structured data), flow-matching (a technique for training diffusion models more efficiently), and Diffusion Transformers or DiT (combining transformer architectures with diffusion). The paper uses AudioVAE2, a variational autoencoder trained to compress speech into a continuous latent space — a frozen learned representation that captures audio information compactly. Unlike earlier TTS systems that rely on phonemizers (rule-based or learned systems converting text to phonemes) or discrete speech tokenizers (converting audio to a fixed vocabulary of sound units), Freya-TTS operates end-to-end from raw character sequences, leveraging transformer self-attention mechanisms to directly model text-to-speech relationships.

## Core Contributions
1. **Rule-free end-to-end architecture**: Eliminates phonemizers and grapheme-to-phoneme (G2P) frontends by learning directly from a 92-character Turkish vocabulary, reducing engineering complexity and potential error propagation.
2. **Non-autoregressive parallel denoising**: Predicts the entire audio latent sequence simultaneously using duration prediction rather than generating audio sequentially, dramatically speeding up inference.
3. **Production-oriented post-training recipe**: Two-stage fine-tuning combining voice-locking (improving speaker consistency) and short-utterance robustness to handle real conversational use cases.
4. **Efficient parameter count**: 183M parameters on frozen continuous latents, achieving WER 8.0% / CER 3.0% while running at 0.11 real-time factor on consumer GPUs and sub-real-time on CPU.

## Connections to Other Work
Freya-TTS represents a shift away from discrete tokenization approaches (related to [[VQ-VAE]], [[Hubert]]) and phoneme-based pipelines (traditional G2P systems) toward end-to-end continuous latent modeling. It advances [[Diffusion Transformers (DiT)]] applied to speech, extending ideas from [[Latent Diffusion Models]] into the audio domain. The non-autoregressive approach is conceptually related to [[parallel decoding]] strategies in language modeling but applied to speech synthesis. This contrasts with autoregressive baselines like Glow-TTS or traditional attention-based Tacotron variants.

## Practical Applications
1. **Edge-deployed voice assistants**: Running TTS on mobile devices or embedded systems (IoT, smartwatches, embedded robots) without cloud connectivity.
2. **Turkish-language accessibility tools**: Screen readers, reading applications, and accessibility software for Turkish speakers with low computational requirements.
3. **Real-time customer service**: Multilingual chatbots and IVR systems requiring fast, natural Turkish speech synthesis in resource-limited data centers.
4. **Offline conversational AI**: Lightweight conversational agents on laptops or local servers in low-bandwidth environments.
5. **Game and media localization**: Real-time character voice synthesis for games, animations, or video dubbing in Turkish without streaming latency.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Turkish-first edge TTS API**: A serverless service billing by character synthesized, targeting businesses deploying voice features on mobile or embedded devices with latency/privacy constraints.
2. **Accessibility middleware**: A browser extension or OS-level service automatically adding natural Turkish TTS to websites, PDFs, and applications with minimal overhead.
3. **Conversational AI platform for Turkish**: A low-cost chatbot/voice assistant platform for small businesses (e.g., customer support, scheduling) leveraging Freya-TTS for cost-effective synthesis.
4. **Gaming and metaverse voice creation**: A game engine plugin (Unity, Unreal) enabling real-time Turkish character voice generation without prerecording or per-character voice actors.
5. **Enterprise speech analytics + synthesis**: A platform combining Turkish speech-to-text with Freya-TTS for conversational recording, analysis, and automated call summarization with on-device processing.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Turkish-specific scope**: Model trained on Turkish; generalization to other languages is unexplored and would require retraining.
2. **Continuous latent space dependency**: Relies entirely on AudioVAE2; if the VAE's reconstruction quality degrades for certain speech characteristics (e.g., extreme emotions, singing), the TTS inherits those failures.
3. **Frozen latent space assumption**: Does not fine-tune the VAE; this reduces flexibility but also prevents catastrophic latent space corruption — trade-off not fully explored.
4. **Limited prosody control**: No explicit mention of pitch, pace, or emotion control; user control over speech characteristics may be limited.
5. **Evaluation on Freya-TR-Eval only**: Benchmark appears proprietary; WER/CER numbers not compared on standard multilingual benchmarks, making cross-system comparison difficult.
6. **Short-utterance focus**: Post-training emphasizes short utterances; performance on long-form synthesis (e.g., audiobook chapters) is unclear.

## Open Questions
1. Does the model generalize to out-of-domain Turkish text (e.g., technical jargon, code-mixed text, colloquial social media language)?
2. How does performance scale with reduced model size (e.g., 100M, 50M parameters) for even more constrained edge devices?
3. Can the continuous latent approach support multi-speaker synthesis or voice cloning with minimal fine-tuning?
4. How does the non-autoregressive duration prediction perform on highly variable speaking styles or emotional speech?
5. Could this approach be adapted to other morphologically rich or low-resource languages with similar latent-space efficiency?
6. What is the subjective quality gap (MOS scores) between Freya-TTS and larger commercial systems like Google TTS or Microsoft Edge TTS?

## Key Takeaway
Freya-TTS demonstrates that compact, rule-free, continuous-latent text-to-speech models can rival much larger systems while enabling real-time synthesis on consumer hardware — a practical blueprint for resource-constrained TTS deployment.
