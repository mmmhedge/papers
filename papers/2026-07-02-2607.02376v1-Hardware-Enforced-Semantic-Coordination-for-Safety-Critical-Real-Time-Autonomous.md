# Hardware-Enforced Semantic Coordination for Safety-Critical Real-Time Autonomous Systems

**Authors:** Uwe M. Borghoff, Paolo Bottoni, Remo Pareschi
**Published:** 2026-07-02 | **arXiv:** [2607.02376v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02376v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes a hardware-enforced coordination layer for safety-critical autonomous systems that combine large language models, world models, and multiple specialized components. Rather than relying on software to manage timing, synchronization, and safety constraints, the authors implement selected coordination semantics directly on field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs) to guarantee deterministic, bounded behavior in real time. The key insight is that semantic reasoning can remain flexible and software-driven while coordination rules become ironclad and verifiable at the hardware level.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on the Topic-Based Communication Space Petri Net (TB-CSPN) framework, which is a formal model for managing interactions among concurrent components. Petri Nets are a classical mathematical tool for modeling concurrent systems, representing states, transitions, and rules governing state changes. TB-CSPN extends this by adding topic-based communication semantics — components publish and subscribe to topics, creating a structured interaction space. The paper assumes readers understand that autonomous systems increasingly integrate heterogeneous subsystems (LLMs, optimization engines, specialized neural networks) that must coordinate under real-time and safety constraints, where software-only solutions can suffer from unpredictable latency and difficulty proving safety properties.

## Core Contributions
1. **Hardware-enforced semantic coordination layer**: Maps TB-CSPN coordination primitives onto FPGA-native logic, moving coordination enforcement from software to hardware. This is not about computational speedup but about achieving *determinism* and *verifiability* for safety-critical constraints.

2. **Separation of semantic reasoning from interaction management**: Allows LLMs and adaptive reasoning to remain in software while hardening only the coordination layer, avoiding the need to make all reasoning deterministic.

3. **Enforced temporal synchronization, semantic gating, and authorization constraints at hardware level**: Provides hard guarantees on timing, access control, and coordination behavior that software implementations cannot match.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[multi-agent-systems]] coordination theory and real-time systems safety. It relates to broader efforts in [[alignment]] and safety for autonomous systems by asking: *how do we guarantee safe coordination behavior under concurrency?* Rather than focusing on value alignment or objective specification (the usual lens), it tackles the mechanical layer: enforcing coordination rules at the hardware level. It complements research on [[agents]] and agentic AI by addressing the "last mile" of deployment — moving from high-level reasoning to low-latency, verifiable execution. The approach echoes time-triggered architectures and safety-critical real-time systems (e.g., AUTOSAR in automotive), but applies them to modern heterogeneous AI systems.

## Practical Applications
- **Autonomous vehicles**: Coordinating perception, planning, and control subsystems where timing violations can cause accidents. Hardware-enforced semantic gating ensures that actuator commands cannot execute before safety checks complete.
- **Collaborative robotics**: Multi-robot teams where one robot's action must be gated by another's authorization; hardware enforcement prevents race conditions.
- **Aerospace and space systems**: Where software patch cycles are impractical and coordination logic must be immutable post-deployment.
- **Industrial process control**: Mixing adaptive AI-driven optimization with hard real-time safety interlocks (e.g., emergency shutdown must trigger within microseconds regardless of LLM latency).
- **Medical robotics**: Surgical assistant systems where coordination between vision, planning, and actuation must meet deterministic latency and safety bounds.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **FPGA-as-a-Service Coordination Layer**: Cloud service offering pre-synthesized TB-CSPN FPGA overlays for teams building autonomous systems. Customers upload coordination rules; service generates and deploys hardware cores.

2. **Safety-Critical AI Deployment Platform**: Enterprise tooling that automatically partitions agentic AI systems into hardware-enforced coordination and software reasoning, with formal verification of partition boundaries and safety contracts.

3. **Real-Time Orchestration Middleware for Edge AI**: Embedded hardware module (USB-C FPGA dongle or system-on-chip) that sits between multiple AI models and actuators, enforcing temporal and semantic coordination without adding latency.

4. **Autonomous System Certification Suite**: Testing and verification framework that certifies multi-agent systems against coordination-safety properties, with FPGA-based monitors that record and prove compliance at hardware level.

5. **Robotics Control Stack with Hardened Coordination**: Drop-in replacement for ROS or similar frameworks, where ROS-like messaging is augmented with FPGA-synthesized semantic gating, targeting research labs and robotics companies building safety-critical platforms.

## Technical Limitations
- **Limited scope of FPGA mapping**: Only certain coordination semantics can be efficiently mapped to FPGA primitives; overly complex or dynamic coordination rules may not benefit from hardware enforcement.
- **Design-time vs. runtime flexibility**: Once synthesized to FPGA, coordination rules become immutable; systems requiring frequent coordination rule changes are poorly served.
- **FPGA development expertise**: Requires hardware design skill; a significant barrier for software-focused AI teams.
- **Scalability of semantic expressiveness**: As systems grow and coordination logic becomes richer, representing it at the hardware level may become unwieldy. The paper doesn't clearly show how to scale beyond moderate complexity.
- **Integration with heterogeneous components**: Unclear how to coordinate LLMs and world models operating at wildly different timescales while enforcing fine-grained hardware semantics.
- **Vendor lock-in and portability**: FPGA designs are typically vendor-specific, limiting portability and long-term maintainability.

## Open Questions
- **How do you formally verify that the hardware-enforced coordination semantics correctly implement the high-level safety requirements?** The paper doesn't discuss formal methods for bridging the gap between TB-CSPN models and FPGA synthesis.
- **What is the performance/latency overhead of the hardware coordination layer, and how does it scale with system size and coordination rule complexity?** Concrete benchmarks against software baselines are absent.
- **Can this approach handle dynamic coordination rules (e.g., reordering priorities at runtime) or rule updates without re-synthesizing the FPGA?**
- **How do you debug and test coordination behavior when rules are baked into hardware?** What observability and monitoring mechanisms are in place?
- **How does this extend to distributed systems where coordination hardware is not co-located?** The paper assumes tight coupling; decentralized systems are unclear.

## Key Takeaway
Hardware-enforcing coordination semantics for autonomous systems trades flexibility for determinism and verifiability, moving the coordination layer below software where timing and safety guarantees cannot be violated.
