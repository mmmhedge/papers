# Bridging Ab Initio Symmetries and Global Nuclear Masses with Interpretable Neural Networks

**Authors:** Phong Dang, Evander Espinoza, Xiaoliang Wan, Michela Negro, Jerry P. Draayer et al.
**Published:** 2026-06-26 | **arXiv:** [2606.28287v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28287v1)

## Summary
This paper demonstrates that fundamental symmetries from nuclear physics (Wigner's SU(4) and Elliott's SU(3)) can be embedded into interpretable neural network models to predict nuclear binding energies across the entire nuclear chart. The authors show that these symmetry-based models achieve competitive predictive performance while providing physical insight into emergent nuclear structure — revealing symmetry restoration near the neutron dripline and unexpected operator contributions in superheavy nuclei.

## Theoretical Background
Nuclear binding energy — the energy needed to disassemble a nucleus — depends on complex many-body interactions. Historically, empirical mass formulas (like the liquid-drop model) capture bulk effects through a few parameters. Ab initio nuclear theory has revealed that certain symmetry groups — particularly Wigner's SU(4) (supermultiplet symmetry combining spin and isospin) and Elliott's SU(3) (spatial deformation symmetry) — emerge as dominant organizing principles in light and intermediate-mass nuclei. Casimir operators are invariant mathematical objects associated with symmetry groups that characterize the strength of symmetry in a given nucleus. The authors ask whether these symmetries generalize across the entire nuclear chart by constructing neural networks informed by the Casimir operators themselves rather than learning from scratch.

## Core Contributions
1. **Symmetry-informed architecture**: Three novel NN variants (FINN, GINN, WINN) that explicitly incorporate SU(3) and SU(4) Casimir operators as features or basis functions, rather than treating them as black-box inputs.
2. **Quantitative validation of symmetry universality**: Demonstrates that SU(4) operators alone reduce RMSE by ~50% versus a liquid-drop baseline on training/test data and ~20% on extrapolation, empirically supporting the hypothesis that Wigner symmetry governs the entire nuclear chart.
3. **Discovery of physical phenomena**: Reveals an enhancement of the quadratic SU(4) Casimir near the neutron dripline (suggesting symmetry restoration in extreme regimes) and identifies a quartic operator contribution in superheavy nuclei — both unexpected findings not previously prominent in literature.
4. **Competitive yet interpretable performance**: WINN achieves 0.430 MeV RMSE — matching state-of-the-art empirical mass models — while remaining compact and physically transparent.

## Connections to Other Work
This work bridges **ab initio nuclear theory** (shell-model symmetries) with **modern machine learning**, similar in spirit to how [[mechanistic-interpretability]] aims to extract human-understandable rules from neural networks. The approach parallels recent trends in physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) and inductive biases in deep learning — encoding domain knowledge (symmetries) into architecture rather than learning everything from data. It relates to efforts in other domains to use symmetry principles (e.g., equivariant neural networks) to improve both interpretability and generalization. The extrapolation test (AME2016 → AME2020) is analogous to out-of-distribution evaluation in ML and tests whether the learned symmetries have true predictive power beyond memorization.

## Practical Applications
1. **Nuclear mass table prediction**: Extending to unmeasured or difficult-to-measure isotopes (e.g., short-lived exotics), reducing experimental burden.
2. **Astrophysics**: Accurate binding energies feed into nucleosynthesis and neutron-star equation-of-state calculations, improving models of stellar explosions and mergers.
3. **Nuclear waste transmutation**: Understanding binding energy across a wide isotopic range aids design of reaction pathways for radioactive waste reduction.
4. **Fundamental physics searches**: Ultra-precise mass measurements constrain exotic decay modes and tests of fundamental symmetries.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Limited to binding energy**: The model predicts a single scalar (total binding energy); it does not capture decay modes, transition probabilities, or individual nucleon distributions — restricting its scope.
2. **Casimir operators as sufficient**: Assumes the quadratic and quartic Casimirs capture essential physics; higher-order operators or non-polynomial dependencies may be important in some regimes.
3. **Training data bias**: AME2016/2020 emphasizes stable and near-stable nuclei; the model may overfit to this distribution and extrapolate poorly to extremely exotic nuclei far from stability.
4. **No explicit many-body dynamics**: The model is static; it cannot predict excited states, lifetimes, or time-dependent processes.
5. **Uncertainty quantification caveats**: GINN's Gaussian uncertainties assume normally distributed errors — a simplification that may break down in poorly-constrained regions (e.g., very heavy or very neutron-rich nuclei).
6. **Theoretical justification gap**: While empirical validation is strong, the paper does not rigorously prove *why* these symmetries should dominate globally — the mechanism remains partly phenomenological.

## Open Questions
1. **Symmetry beyond SU(3) and SU(4)**: Are there other symmetry groups or operators (e.g., octupole, pairing correlations) that contribute meaningfully in superheavy or very asymmetric nuclei?
2. **Error bars and confidence:** How do predictions degrade as nuclei move further into unmeasured territory? Is there a quantitative "confidence horizon"?
3. **Microscopic origin**: Can the emergent global symmetry be derived from first principles (e.g., from the nucleon-nucleon interaction) rather than observed empirically?
4. **Dynamic predictions**: Can symmetry-informed NNs predict decay rates, excitation spectra, or reaction cross-sections — moving beyond a single scalar?
5. **Generalization to neutron stars**: Do these symmetries hold in the ultra-high-density, zero-temperature regime of neutron-star interiors?
6. **Ablation and interpretability**: Which specific terms in WINN are most predictive in different regions of the chart? A detailed saliency or sensitivity analysis could reveal regional physics.

## Key Takeaway
Fundamental nuclear symmetries (Wigner's SU(4) and Elliott's SU(3)) can be encoded directly into neural networks as interpretable basis functions, yielding both competitive accuracy and physical insight into how symmetry governs binding energy across the entire nuclear landscape.
