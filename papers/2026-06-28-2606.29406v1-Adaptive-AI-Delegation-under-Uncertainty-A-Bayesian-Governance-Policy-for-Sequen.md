# Adaptive AI Delegation under Uncertainty: A Bayesian Governance Policy for Sequential Decision Authority

**Authors:** Matthew Francis Dixon
**Published:** 2026-06-28 | **arXiv:** [2606.29406v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29406v1)

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical organizational problem: how much decision-making authority should be delegated to AI systems when their reliability and evidence quality are uncertain and evolving? The authors formulate adaptive AI delegation as a sequential decision problem (a Governance-Aware POMDP) solved via Bayesian inference and optimization, then validate it against synthetic stress tests and alternative governance heuristics, showing that sequential Bayesian policies outperform static rules across varying AI-quality regimes.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on three intellectual foundations:

1. **Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs)** — sequential decision frameworks where the true state is unobserved; agents maintain a belief distribution and optimize expected long-term value. Standard POMDPs are well-established in control theory and reinforcement learning.

2. **Bayesian inference under model uncertainty** — using observed LLM outputs and accuracy signals to iteratively refine beliefs about true AI competence; this is classical sequential estimation.

3. **Governance and organizational risk management** — the paper acknowledges that AI governance frameworks (compliance, transparency, audit trails) exist but lack quantitative guidance for *dynamic* authority allocation. This is where the gap lies: most governance is static.

The novelty is combining these: treating delegation authority as a state-dependent control variable optimized under uncertainty about AI quality.

## Core Contributions

1. **Governance-Aware POMDP formulation**: Explicitly models organizational decision authority as a continuous control variable (what fraction of decisions to delegate) rather than a binary accept/reject choice. The state space includes both AI-quality beliefs and organizational risk appetite.

2. **Validation and stress-testing framework**: Goes beyond typical ML metrics (accuracy, calibration) to test:
   - Graceful degradation when AI quality declines
   - Robustness to confidence-score manipulation (LLM overconfidence)
   - Adaptive response to improving evidence quality
   - Interpretable calibration of organizational conservatism

3. **Empirical benchmarking**: Compares the Bayesian sequential policy against five representative governance heuristics (likely including rules like "delegate only when confidence > 80%" or "static 50% delegation") under identical information and objectives—showing the adaptive approach wins across heterogeneous AI-quality regimes.

4. **Quantitative governance guidance**: Moves from *qualitative* governance principles to a principled, optimized decision rule that organizations can implement and monitor.

## Connections to Other Work

This work sits at the intersection of:

- **[[Reinforcement Learning]]** for sequential decision-making under partial observability, though applied to an organizational governance problem rather than robotics or games.
- **[[Risk Management]]** — specifically, how to manage model risk when delegating to AI systems; related to existing literature on algorithmic auditing and AI risk quantification.
- **[[Alignment]]** in a broader sense: ensuring AI systems' recommendations are used appropriately given uncertainty about their true reliability, though this is more about *use* governance than training alignment.
- **Agency and delegation theory** in economics/organizational behavior — but here formalized quantitatively via optimal control.
- **Uncertainty quantification in ML** — the paper depends on LLM confidence scores and forecast accuracy measures; connection to calibration and epistemic uncertainty estimation.

The paper explicitly contrasts with static governance frameworks (compliance-focused) and suggests sequential Bayesian methods are missing from current AI governance toolkits.

## Practical Applications

1. **Financial risk management**: Banks deciding when to delegate AML (anti-money laundering) flagging, credit decisions, or trade recommendations to AI systems; authority scales with observed AI accuracy on holdout test sets.

2. **Clinical decision support**: Hospitals using AI for diagnostic screening or treatment recommendations; the POMDP framework determines what fraction of cases should be autonomously triaged vs. escalated to clinicians, adapting as model performance is monitored.

3. **Regulatory/compliance automation**: Legal and compliance teams using LLMs to draft regulatory filings or monitor policy violations; delegation authority increases if the model's outputs pass expert review with high accuracy.

4. **Supply chain and logistics optimization**: AI systems proposing procurement, inventory, or routing decisions; authority scales dynamically based on forecast accuracy during changing market conditions.

5. **Content moderation at scale**: Platforms using AI to flag harmful content; delegation authority adjusts based on false-positive/negative rates observed in human review samples.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **AI Governance Dashboard SaaS**: A monitoring and decision-authority management platform for enterprises using multiple AI systems. Ingests LLM confidence scores, human validation feedback, and organizational risk appetite; recommends and tracks optimal delegation authority per system/domain. Targets compliance and ops teams.

2. **Risk-Aware Delegation Engine**: A plug-and-play service layer that sits between LLMs and downstream decision systems (APIs, databases), dynamically filtering/escalating AI recommendations based on a learned POMDP. Sells to financial services and healthcare.

3. **AI Quality Certification & Benchmarking**: A third-party service that stress-tests client AI systems (internal or vendor-provided) using the paper's validation framework, producing a certified "delegation capacity" score across different uncertainty regimes. Helps enterprises assess which AI vendors/models to trust at what authority levels.

4. **Adaptive Insurance for AI Decisions**: An insurance product priced on real-time delegation risk scores derived from this framework. As an organization's AI system degrades, premium or deductible adjusts automatically; incentivizes good monitoring and conservative delegation practices.

5. **AI Governance Consulting Platform**: Use the POMDP framework to audit and optimize delegation policies for large enterprises; provide before/after ROI (cost of errors prevented vs. decision velocity lost). Targets C-suite risk and strategy teams.

## Technical Limitations

1. **POMDP computational complexity**: Solving a POMDP exactly scales exponentially with state/action/observation space size. The paper doesn't detail scalability—real organizations may have dozens of AI systems with correlated failure modes; exact POMDP solutions may be intractable.

2. **Belief accuracy assumption**: The approach assumes Bayesian beliefs about AI quality are *well-calibrated*. If the prior is wrong or the likelihood model is misspecified (e.g., LLM confidence doesn't correlate with actual accuracy, or accuracy changes non-stationarily), the whole framework degrades. Limited discussion of model misspecification.

3. **Stationary environment assumption**: The paper likely assumes AI quality evolves slowly or predictably. In practice, LLM behavior can shift abruptly with prompt changes, fine-tuning, or system updates; the POMDP may lag behind reality.

4. **Synthetic validation only**: Stress tests are "synthetic"—not on real organizational decisions with real consequences. Benchmarking against heuristics is useful but may not transfer to messy real-world data (missing observations, feedback delay, strategic behavior by decision-makers).

5. **Single-agent framing**: The paper treats delegation as a unilateral organizational decision. Real organizations have multiple stakeholders (risk, ops, legal); multi-objective optimization and distributional concerns (who bears the cost of AI errors?) are not addressed.

6. **Reward signal definition**: How does the organization define success (cost of errors vs. speed of decisions)? The POMDP needs an explicit utility function; the paper doesn't discuss how to elicit this from stakeholders or how sensitive results are to specification.

## Open Questions

1. **How does this scale to portfolios of AI systems?** Real organizations use multiple LLMs for different tasks, with potential correlations in failure (e.g., all fail during adversarial attacks). Can the POMDP be extended to multi-system delegation with dependence structure?

2. **What happens under distributional shift?** If the test set (on which AI accuracy is measured) differs from the operational distribution, does the policy remain robust? Can the framework detect and adapt to this?

3. **How should feedback delays be incorporated?** In practice, ground truth labels arrive slowly (e.g., a loan decision's outcome is known 12 months later). Does the POMDP framework handle asynchronous, delayed feedback?

4. **Can we learn the reward function (organizational utility) from observed delegation choices?** Rather than asking stakeholders to specify it directly, inverse POMDP methods might infer organizational risk appetite from historical data.

5. **How does human behavior interact with changing AI authority?** If delegation authority drops abruptly, does operator trust degrade? Is there a behavioral economics aspect (automation bias, deskilling) that should be modeled?
