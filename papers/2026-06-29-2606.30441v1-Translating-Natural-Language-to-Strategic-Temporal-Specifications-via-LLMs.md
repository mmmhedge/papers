# Translating Natural Language to Strategic Temporal Specifications via LLMs

**Authors:** Marco Aruta, Francesco Improta, Vadim Malvone, Aniello Murano, Vladana Perlic
**Published:** 2026-06-29 | **arXiv:** [2606.30441v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30441v1)

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of formally specifying Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) by building an LLM-based translator that converts natural language descriptions into ATL/ATL* formulas—a formal logic for expressing strategic abilities and temporal objectives in agent systems. The authors create a novel expert-validated dataset and demonstrate that fine-tuned small models (3–7B parameters) can match few-shot proprietary baselines at 0.84 semantic accuracy, enabling non-expert users to specify complex agent behaviors without expertise in formal logic.

## Theoretical Background
Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) require formal specifications that capture not just what happens, but what agents can strategically achieve—this is where Alternating-Time Temporal Logic (ATL) and its extension ATL* come in. ATL/ATL* are modal logics designed to reason about strategic abilities: they let you express properties like "agent A can guarantee outcome X regardless of what agent B does." Traditional approaches require experts to manually write these formulas, which is error-prone and slow. Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown success in code generation and formal language translation tasks through fine-tuning on domain-specific datasets. The key insight here is that natural language and formal specifications share enough semantic structure that a trained LLM can learn the mapping—but only if you have labeled training data, which didn't exist for NL-to-ATL/ATL* before.

## Core Contributions
1. **First NL-to-ATL/ATL* translation framework**: No prior work systematized this translation task; this paper establishes both the problem formulation and a working solution.
2. **Expert-curated dataset**: Created and validated a novel dataset of natural language specifications paired with correct ATL/ATL* formulas, manually checked by domain experts.
3. **Fine-tuning analysis for small models**: Demonstrates that modest 3–7B parameter open-weight models (Llama-series), when fine-tuned in-domain, match proprietary few-shot APIs (e.g., GPT-4-level performance) at 0.84 vs. 0.86 semantic accuracy.
4. **Judge reliability paradox**: Reveals that stronger LLMs are often worse at evaluating candidate specifications—they over-reject semantically correct paraphrases—whereas mid-tier open-weight models (Llama-3.3-70B) most reliably track expert ground truth.
5. **Integration with model checker**: Embedded the translator into an existing strategic logics model checker, making formal verification accessible to non-experts.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[Large Language Models|LLMs]] for code/formal language synthesis (e.g., [[Program Synthesis]], neural theorem proving), [[Multi-Agent Systems]] verification (classical model checking, strategic logics), and [[Natural Language Processing|NLP]] semantic parsing. It extends prior work on LLM-to-formal-specification translation (used in software engineering, e.g., translating to SMT-LIB or temporal logics like LTL) into the less-explored domain of strategic and multi-agent reasoning. The dataset creation methodology mirrors recent work in domain-specific NL-to-code benchmarking. The finding about judge reliability relates to broader questions about LLM evaluation and hallucination in formal domains.

## Practical Applications
1. **Specification for autonomous multi-robot teams**: Factory or warehouse automation where non-specialist engineers can describe desired coordination strategies ("Robot A must always be able to reach the goal while Robot B avoids obstacles") in English, auto-translated to verifiable logic.
2. **Game AI and esports simulation**: Game designers specify NPC team tactics and guarantees without learning formal logic.
3. **Smart contract specification in blockchain**: Non-technical stakeholders express multi-party protocol guarantees (e.g., "Party A can always exit within 10 blocks") that are verified before deployment.
4. **Cyber-physical systems safety**: Safety engineers in aerospace or automotive describe what agents/subsystems must guarantee under adversarial conditions, then verify automatically.
5. **Enterprise workflow automation**: Business analysts specify multi-tenant system requirements (e.g., "Actor X can always trigger approval without waiting for Actor Y") without formal training.

2. **MAS-as-a-Service platform**: SaaS offering for specifying, verifying, and monitoring multi-agent systems (robots, drones, game NPCs) via natural language; charges per verification or per deployment hour.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Dataset scale and domain bias**: The curated dataset is likely modest in size and may over-represent certain MAS patterns (e.g., simple two-agent games); generalization to highly novel agent configurations is uncertain.
2. **Semantic accuracy vs. practical correctness**: 0.84 semantic accuracy sounds good, but in formal verification, even small errors in logic (e.g., universal vs. existential quantification) break correctness entirely. The paper doesn't report false-positive/false-negative rates for actual model-checking failures.
3. **Scalability to complex specifications**: ATL/ATL* formulas can grow rapidly in complexity. The paper doesn't stress-test on large formulas or deeply nested strategic quantifiers.
4. **Judge reliability problem cuts both ways**: While the paper identifies that strong models are unreliable judges, this undermines confidence in the evaluation itself. Disagreement between proprietary and open-weight judges (0.86 vs. 0.84) may reflect judge noise rather than true model quality.
5. **Limited to English and ATL/ATL***: No evidence the approach transfers to other languages or other strategic logics (e.g., Strategy Logic, QCTL).
6. **Real-world specification validation**: The paper integrates with a model checker but doesn't show case studies where non-experts actually used it end-to-end; user error and over-specification remain unchecked.

## Open Questions
1. **How does translation quality degrade with more complex agent specifications?** E.g., 10+ agents, nested temporal operators, probabilistic guarantees—does semantic accuracy plateau or collapse?
2. **Can you detect and correct specification errors post-hoc?** If the LLM generates a plausible but incorrect formula, could a second pass (e.g., "does this formula match your intent?" + refinement loop) improve reliability?
3. **How do fine-tuned models compare on out-of-domain MAS problems?** Does a model fine-tuned on the curated dataset generalize to agent specifications it's never seen, or does it memorize patterns?
4. **Why exactly are stronger models worse judges?** Is it overconfidence, reward model misalignment, or something fundamental about scale? Can you train a better judge?
5. **Can this integrate with probabilistic or stochastic extensions of ATL?** Many real systems are stochastic; does the approach extend to PATL or other probabilistic logics?
6. **How do users perceive specification errors?** If the generated formula is wrong but looks plausible, will users catch it, or will they trust the model? What are failure modes in practice?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs fine-tuned on expert-curated domain data can reliably translate informal strategic requirements into formal multi-agent logic, making rigorous verification accessible to non-experts—but evaluation reliability remains a challenge, and scalability to realistic complex specifications is still unproven.
