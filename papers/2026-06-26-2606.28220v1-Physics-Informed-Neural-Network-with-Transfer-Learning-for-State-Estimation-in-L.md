# Physics-Informed Neural Network with Transfer Learning for State Estimation in Lithium-Ion Batteries using the Single Particle Model with Electrolyte

**Authors:** Gift Modekwe, Qiugang Lu
**Published:** 2026-06-26 | **arXiv:** [2606.28220v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28220v1)

## Summary
This paper applies physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) with transfer learning to estimate state variables in lithium-ion batteries modeled via the single particle model with electrolyte (SPMe). The key contribution is demonstrating that pretraining a PINN on general electrochemical dynamics, then fine-tuning it for specific battery chemistries, significantly reduces training time while maintaining physical consistency and improving generalization across different batteries.

## Theoretical Background
Physics-informed neural networks embed conservation laws and governing equations directly into the loss function, allowing neural networks to solve nonlinear partial differential equations (PDEs) while respecting underlying physics. The single particle model with electrolyte (SPMe) is a reduced-order electrochemical model that describes lithium-ion battery behavior through coupled equations: lithium diffusion in particle phases, electrolyte transport, electrochemical reaction kinetics, and voltage equations. Traditional numerical solvers (finite difference, finite element methods) discretize space and time, which becomes computationally expensive for nonlinear coupled systems. Transfer learning — training a model on a source task, then adapting it to a target task by reusing learned weights — has proven effective across domains (computer vision, NLP) by reducing data requirements and convergence time. This paper combines these concepts: PINNs encode physics constraints, transfer learning accelerates adaptation to new batteries.

## Core Contributions
1. **Transfer learning framework for SPMe-PINNs**: A two-stage training strategy where the model is pretrained on diverse electrochemical dynamics, then fine-tuned to specific battery chemistries by freezing selected layers and updating remaining parameters.
2. **Demonstrated efficiency gains**: Shows that transfer learning reduces training time and improves convergence compared to training SPMe-PINNs from scratch.
3. **Validation against PyBaMM**: Demonstrates accurate voltage prediction while preserving electrochemical consistency, confirming the approach maintains physics fidelity.
4. **Generalization across chemistries**: Evidence that learned representations transfer across different battery compositions and operating conditions.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of physics-informed machine learning (PINNs, a well-established paradigm for solving PDEs) and battery modeling. It extends prior PINN work on battery systems by addressing a practical bottleneck: retraining for new chemistries. The transfer learning strategy parallels approaches in [[Transformers]] and domain adaptation literature, but applied to electrochemical PDEs rather than sequences or images. Related to broader [[optimization]] efforts in battery management systems (BMS) that balance accuracy, speed, and physical plausibility. Complements traditional model-order reduction techniques by using learned representations rather than hand-crafted basis functions.

## Practical Applications
1. **Battery management systems (BMS)**: Real-time state-of-charge (SOC) and state-of-health (SOH) estimation on embedded hardware with reduced computational burden.
2. **Battery pack design optimization**: Rapidly predicting thermal and electrochemical behavior across new chemistries or cell geometries during design iterations.
3. **Second-life and recycled battery assessment**: Quickly adapting models to degraded or alternative chemistry packs without expensive calibration.
4. **Multi-chemistry fleet management**: EV manufacturers managing vehicles with different battery suppliers; one pretrained model adapts to each supplier's chemistry.
5. **Manufacturing quality control**: Fast validation of cell performance during production by transferring a pretrained model to individual cells.

## Product & Startup Ideas

## Technical Limitations
1. **Limited scope of pretraining**: The paper trains on "general electrochemical dynamics," but doesn't clearly specify the range of chemistries, temperatures, and operating conditions in the pretraining dataset. Transfer learning breaks down if the target battery lies far outside this distribution.
2. **Validation on synthetic data**: PyBaMM is a simulator; validation on real cell data (actual voltage, current, temperature traces) would be needed to confirm practical robustness.
3. **SPMe assumptions**: The single particle model neglects side reactions, electrolyte inhomogeneities, and thermal coupling beyond surface effects. Real batteries may violate these assumptions significantly.
4. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: Transfer learning requires choosing which layers to freeze and learning rates for fine-tuning. The paper doesn't explore sensitivity to these choices or provide principled selection methods.
5. **Computational cost not quantified**: While the paper claims "improved scalability," absolute training times and inference latency on embedded hardware are not reported.
6. **Generalization limits unclear**: No systematic ablation showing how far transfer generalizes (e.g., does a model trained on NMC transfer to LFP, or only to variants of NMC?).

## Open Questions
1. **Pretraining curriculum**: What composition of chemistries, thermal conditions, and degradation states in the pretraining set maximizes transfer to novel batteries? Is there an optimal curriculum?
2. **Few-shot fine-tuning**: How much target-battery data is actually needed for effective fine-tuning? Can you adapt with only a handful of charge-discharge cycles?
3. **Failure mode detection**: When does transfer fail? Can you detect distribution shift (e.g., target battery is outside pretraining space) and trigger retraining or flag uncertainty?
4. **Coupled physics**: How does the approach scale to fuller models that include thermal coupling, side reactions, or mechanical stress? Do physics constraints in the loss function remain sufficient?
5. **Uncertainty quantification**: Bayesian variants of PINNs quantify epistemic uncertainty; can transfer learning preserve or improve calibrated uncertainty estimates?
6. **Real-world validation**: Does this work on field data from in-service batteries with measurement noise, aging, and non-ideal operating profiles?

## Key Takeaway
Transfer learning dramatically reduces the cost of adapting physics-informed neural networks to new battery chemistries, enabling rapid deployment of electrochemically consistent state estimators across diverse battery types.
