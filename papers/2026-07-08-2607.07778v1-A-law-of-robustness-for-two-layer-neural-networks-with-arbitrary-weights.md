# A law of robustness for two-layer neural networks with arbitrary weights

**Authors:** Yitzchak Shmalo
**Published:** 2026-07-08 | **arXiv:** [2607.07778v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07778v1)

## Summary

This paper proves a fundamental law about neural network robustness: any two-layer neural network that memorizes noisy data must have a large Lipschitz constant (a measure of how sensitive the network is to input changes). The result holds for networks with arbitrary weight magnitudes, closing a gap left by prior work that required bounded weights. This matters because it reveals a deep trade-off between fitting data and maintaining stability — you cannot do both simultaneously without penalty.

## Theoretical Background

The paper builds on work by Bubeck, Li, Nagaraj, and Sellke on understanding fundamental limits of neural network interpolation. The key concept is the **Lipschitz constant** — the smallest value L such that |f(x) − f(y)| ≤ L·||x − y|| for all inputs. A small Lipschitz constant means small input perturbations cause small output changes; large constants indicate instability.

Prior work (Bubeck and Sellke) proved a "law of robustness" for **bounded-parameter models** — roughly, fitting n noisy labels requires Lipschitz constant at least √(n/m) where m is the network width. This boundedness assumption was essential at depth three, but unknown for two-layer networks with unbounded weights. The paper studies **piecewise-linear activations** (like ReLU) and generic data distributions (uniform on spheres or Gaussian).

## Core Contributions

1. **Proves the conjectured unbounded-weight law for two-layer networks**: For ReLU and other continuous piecewise-linear activations, fitting n labels below noise level σ² forces Lipschitz constant at least ~√(n/m) (up to logarithmic factors), with zero restriction on weight magnitudes.

2. **Function-space covering argument**: Instead of covering parameter space (impossible with unbounded weights), the proof covers the space of realized functions. This is a genuinely novel technique.

3. **Rigidity lemma**: The core technical result shows that on generic data (high-dimensional spheres or Gaussian distributions), coefficients of each "kink" (where ReLU activations switch) are controlled by the network's Lipschitz constant because kinks on different hyperplanes cannot cancel. This fails in 2D and the paper exhibits a matching counterexample.

4. **Realized-kink-count formulation**: The bound holds in terms of actual number of distinct kink hyperplanes k(f) realized by the function, independent of how many redundant hidden units parameterize it.

## Connections to Other Work

This extends the robustness program initiated by [[Bubeck et al.]] on interpolation-stability trade-offs. It sits within the broader mechanistic understanding of neural networks — understanding what functions networks *must* compute if they fit data. The work relates to [[double descent]] and generalization phenomena, though from a robustness angle rather than sample complexity.

The piecewise-linear function-space perspective connects to work on neural network expressiveness and the geometry of ReLU networks. The result has implications for understanding why overparameterization doesn't eliminate robustness costs — width alone doesn't help if you oversfit.

## Practical Applications

1. **Architecture selection for safety-critical systems**: The law quantifies robustness degradation with memorization. Engineering teams can estimate required network width for tasks demanding both accuracy and input stability (e.g., medical imaging, autonomous driving).

2. **Adversarial robustness analysis**: Understanding inherent Lipschitz-fitting trade-offs informs bounds on adversarial vulnerability. Networks that fit noise tightly will necessarily be sensitive to small perturbations.

3. **Regularization parameter tuning**: The law suggests principled ways to set noise-fitting thresholds (ε) to achieve desired Lipschitz constants without grid search.

4. **Audit and verification**: For deployed two-layer networks, the bound provides checkable post-hoc guarantees on worst-case robustness given observed fit quality.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **Neural network robustness certification tool**: SaaS platform that, given a trained two-layer network and its training error, computes rigorous worst-case Lipschitz bounds and adversarial perturbation budgets for regulatory compliance (medical AI, finance).

2. **Automated architecture recommender for robust learning**: System that, given a task and robustness requirement, suggests minimum network width and regularization needed using this law plus empirical tuning.

3. **Interpretability-as-a-service for piecewise-linear models**: Tool that decomposes two-layer ReLU networks into their realized kinks and analyzes which data points drive instability, enabling human auditing of model robustness.

4. **Adversarial training cost estimator**: Commercial software predicting computational and statistical costs of achieving adversarial robustness levels, helping teams budget ML projects.

5. **Safety envelope calculator for autonomous systems**: Real-time system monitoring Lipschitz constants of deployed neural controllers and alerting when overfitting risk exceeds safety thresholds.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Two-layer restriction**: Results do not extend to deeper networks (depth ≥ 3 without boundedness). Practitioners using modern deep architectures cannot directly apply this.

2. **Logarithmic gap**: The bound holds only up to logarithmic factors (log(Cmnd/ε)). This gap is tight for d=2 but unknown whether it's tight for d≥3.

3. **Generic data assumption**: Results require data uniformly on high-dimensional spheres or standard Gaussian. Real-world data with structure, correlations, or lower effective dimension may behave differently.

4. **Piecewise-linear activations only**: Smooth activations (sigmoid, tanh) are not covered, limiting applicability to modern architectures using GELUs, Swish, etc.

5. **No label structure**: Labels are arbitrary in [−1,1]. Real classification tasks have discrete structure; continuous relaxations may not capture actual memorization costs.

6. **Dimension dependence unclear**: Bounds scale with dimension d implicitly through the covering argument; dependence on d is not fully explicit.

## Open Questions

1. **Depth three and beyond**: Can the function-space covering extend to three-layer networks? Is boundedness truly necessary for deeper networks, or is it an artifact of the proof technique?

2. **Tight logarithmic factors**: Is the log(Cmnd/ε) factor necessary, or can it be removed entirely? The d=2 counterexample suggests the law itself is tight but doesn't rule out tightness at higher d.

3. **Smooth activations**: What changes for Lipschitz-smooth activations like GELU or Swish? Is the law still √(n/m) or does smoothness buy robustness?

4. **Lower effective dimension**: On real datasets with intrinsic dimension d_eff << d, does the bound improve? Can data geometry be exploited?

5. **Dynamic fitting**: As training progresses, how does the Lipschitz constant evolve? Can the law inform early stopping heuristics?

6. **Weight magnitude effects**: While unbounded weights are allowed, do empirical constants c depend on realized weight scale? Is there a latent trade-off between parameterization and robustness cost?

## Key Takeaway

Any two-layer neural network that overfits to noisy data must pay a fundamental robustness cost (large Lipschitz constant) proportional to √(n/m), regardless of weight magnitudes — a universal law of neural networks.
