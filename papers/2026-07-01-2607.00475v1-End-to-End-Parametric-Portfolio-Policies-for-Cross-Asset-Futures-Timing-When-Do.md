# End-to-End Parametric Portfolio Policies for Cross-Asset Futures Timing: When Do AI Models Beat Simple Rules?

**Authors:** Austin Pollok, Kevin Robik
**Published:** 2026-07-01 | **arXiv:** [2607.00475v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.00475v1)

## Summary
This paper asks whether machine learning models can beat simple rule-based strategies for timing cross-asset futures portfolios. The researchers train neural networks (LSTMs and transformers) to map market conditions directly to portfolio weights using a differentiable Sharpe ratio loss, then compare them against equal weighting, risk parity, and momentum rules on CME futures data. They find that learned policies can outperform rules on average, but the advantage shrinks significantly once trading costs are factored in—and transformers win out by trading far less than LSTMs.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two distinct traditions. First, classical portfolio optimization — the idea that you forecast returns, estimate risk, and solve for optimal weights (the "two-step" approach). Second, it draws on the growing use of deep learning for time-series forecasting and control problems. The authors use a differentiable Sharpe ratio as a loss function, which lets them optimize directly for the metric practitioners care about rather than prediction accuracy. The architectures they test — LSTMs (long short-term memory networks that process sequences and remember long-range patterns) and transformers (neural networks using attention mechanisms to weight different parts of a time series) — are standard tools from deep learning. The benchmark strategies (equal weighting, risk parity, and time-series momentum) are well-established heuristics in quantitative finance.

## Core Contributions
1. **End-to-end policy learning**: Rather than the traditional two-step pipeline (forecast → optimize), the paper trains a single neural network to map market state directly to portfolio weights, eliminating an intermediate step and its associated errors.
2. **Sharpe ratio optimization**: Use of a differentiable Sharpe ratio objective allows the model to optimize for a real trading metric, not just prediction accuracy.
3. **Empirical comparison on liquid futures**: Testing on the sixteen most liquid CME futures reduces the risk that results are driven by liquidity or market microstructure effects.
4. **Architecture comparison with transaction costs**: The finding that transformers and LSTMs diverge once costs are included is practical and non-obvious—it highlights that lower trading frequency (a transformer property) can matter more than raw performance.
5. **Honest baseline comparison**: The paper compares against strong, simple rules rather than strawmen, showing nuance about when ML actually helps.

## Practical Applications
1. **Systematic asset allocation funds**: Hedge funds and asset managers can use these policies to automate cross-asset tactical rebalancing with lower costs.
2. **Risk parity alternatives**: For institutional investors wanting to move beyond mechanical risk parity, learned policies could improve Sharpe ratios on diversified portfolios.
3. **Futures trading boutiques**: Quant shops specializing in futures can deploy transformer-based timing models as a low-turnover alternative to rules-based systems.
4. **Robo-advisor enhancements**: Platforms managing broad-based portfolios could use these to reduce drag from naive rebalancing schedules.
5. **CTA-style strategies**: Commodity trading advisors can augment or replace momentum rules with learned policies that adapt to changing market regimes.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Lookback period and regime stability**: The paper trains on historical data; it's unclear how long the learned patterns persist if market regimes shift (e.g., inflation spike, structural asset correlation changes).
2. **Data leakage and overfitting**: End-to-end learning on 16 highly correlated futures could suffer from subtle overfitting; the paper's out-of-sample test is important but doesn't guarantee forward performance.
3. **Causality and spurious correlations**: Neural networks can exploit spurious correlations; there's no guarantee the learned policies have causal economic meaning.
4. **Liquidity and market impact**: Even CME futures have bid-ask spreads and market impact at scale; the analysis may not account for large institutional trade execution costs.
5. **Transaction cost modeling**: Real costs (commissions, slippage, market impact) vary by broker, size, and time of day; the paper's cost assumptions may not generalize.
6. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: No discussion of how sensitive the results are to learning rate, regularization, architecture depth, etc.
7. **Statistical significance**: No significance tests or confidence intervals; it's unclear which outperformance claims are robust vs. noise.

## Open Questions
1. **Why does the transformer trade less?** A mechanistic analysis of what patterns the transformer learns (and how it differs from the LSTM) would illuminate whether it's finding true regime features or just regularizing differently.
2. **Out-of-sample stability**: How do policy weights and performance degrade over longer forward periods (1-5 years)? Do retraining frequencies matter?
3. **Generalization across universes**: Can policies trained on CME futures transfer to other asset classes (equities, cryptocurrencies, OTC derivatives)?
4. **Robustness to market shocks**: Do the policies break during crisis periods (e.g., March 2020, flash crashes)? Can they be constrained to avoid catastrophic losses?
5. **Interpretability**: What features (implied vols, yields, spreads, momentum, correlations) does each model actually use? Can we extract simple rules?
6. **Interaction with execution**: How should the policy adapt if execution is not instantaneous? Can it be jointly optimized with an execution algorithm?
7. **Multi-period planning**: Does optimizing one-step (map state to weights) miss gains from planning ahead or explicitly modeling the cost of rebalancing?

## Key Takeaway
Neural networks can beat simple portfolio rules on cross-asset futures, but transformers' practical advantage comes mostly from trading less frequently, not from superior return forecasting—a reminder that in finance, low turnover and transaction costs often trump raw model performance.
