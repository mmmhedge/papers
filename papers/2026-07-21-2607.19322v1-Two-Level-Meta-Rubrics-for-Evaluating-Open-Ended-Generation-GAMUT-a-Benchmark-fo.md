# Two-Level Meta-Rubrics for Evaluating Open-Ended Generation: GAMUT, a Benchmark for Factual Completeness

**Authors:** Xilun Chen, Zhaleh Feizollahi, Ross Goodwin, Seungwhan Moon, Scott Yih et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-21 | **arXiv:** [2607.19322v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19322v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces GAMUT, a benchmark for evaluating whether long-form text generated by AI models contains all the facts it should — what researchers call "factual completeness." Most existing evaluation methods only check if claims are correct (precision), missing the equally important question of whether responses are complete. The authors propose a two-level framework that captures hierarchical relationships between facts, making it possible to automatically grade completeness at scale.

## Theoretical Background
Evaluating factuality in generated text has traditionally relied on the decompose-search-verify pipeline: breaking claims into atomic facts, searching for evidence, and verifying each fact's correctness. This approach works well for precision — catching false claims — but ignores recall: whether the response covers all necessary information. Prior work on [[FEVER]] and similar benchmarks focused on fact verification (is this claim true?), not completeness (are all important facts present?). The challenge with completeness is structural: facts don't always form flat lists. They can form hierarchies, describe processes with inherent ordering, or form networks where relationships matter. Traditional boolean checklists miss these dependencies.

## Core Contributions
1. **Two-level meta-rubric framework**: A novel representation where a high-level structured rubric captures the organization and importance relationships of required content, then mechanically compiles into flat, machine-gradable binary checks. This bridges hierarchical reasoning with LLM evaluation.
2. **GAMUT benchmark**: 1,813 open-ended questions grounded in real wearable imagery (multimodal), spanning 10 diverse domains, each with expert-verified rubrics. A text-only variant also released.
3. **Empirical findings**: Evaluation of 14 models (frontier and open-weight), showing the benchmark is genuinely hard (best score 58.7%), highly discriminative, and robust to judge choice.

## Connections to Other Work
GAMUT addresses a recognized gap in factuality evaluation alongside precision-focused work like [[FEVER]] and [[BoolQ]]. It relates to broader problems in open-ended generation evaluation (like [[SQuALITY]] for long-form summaries) but adds a structured content representation layer. The work also connects to multimodal evaluation challenges, where grounding in visual evidence adds authenticity. The meta-rubric compilation approach is novel and could generalize to other structured evaluation tasks beyond completeness.

## Practical Applications
- **AI assistant evaluation**: Assessing whether customer service bots or technical support systems provide complete answers to user questions.
- **Educational content generation**: Checking if AI-generated textbook chapters or study guides cover all learning objectives.
- **Medical/legal document generation**: Ensuring generated reports or briefs include all required sections and topics.
- **Question-answering systems**: Measuring whether QA models answer comprehensively or miss important context.
- **Content moderation and quality assurance**: Auditing whether generated content marketing or product descriptions hit all key selling points.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Completeness-as-a-Service (CaaS)**: A hosted API for evaluating factual completeness of any generated text, using GAMUT's framework to compute scores and generate improvement suggestions.
2. **AI Quality Dashboard**: Enterprise dashboard for teams fine-tuning or deploying LLMs, showing not just accuracy but completeness metrics across use cases (support tickets, documentation, customer comms).
3. **Rubric Builder for Enterprises**: Low-code tool that helps companies define their own domain-specific meta-rubrics (e.g., for insurance claims, medical notes) and auto-compile them into evaluation checklists.
4. **Fact-Complete Training Data**: A data labeling service that uses GAMUT-style rubrics to mark incomplete responses during training data collection, enabling models to be fine-tuned specifically for completeness.
5. **Compliance & Audit Platform**: For regulated industries (finance, healthcare), auto-evaluate whether AI-generated documents meet completeness requirements (e.g., disclosure requirements, informed consent checklists).

## Technical Limitations
- **Rubric design burden**: Creating good meta-rubrics still requires domain expertise and manual effort; no automation for rubric generation from scratch.
- **Wearable imagery bias**: Core benchmark is grounded in visual data; generalization to non-visual domains unclear (text-only variant released but not deeply evaluated).
- **LLM judge reliability**: Framework depends on an LLM judge scoring binary rubrics; quality degrades with weaker models or adversarial inputs.
- **Scalability to very long-form**: Benchmark focuses on question-answer pairs; unclear how well this scales to full documents or conversations with hundreds of facts.
- **Incomplete ground truth**: In open-ended domains, the "full set of required facts" is inherently fuzzy; expert annotation may miss valid complete answers that differ from the rubric.
- **Modality interaction**: While labeled modality-agnostic, the framework was developed with multimodal grounding; behavior in purely text-only settings may differ.

## Open Questions
- Can meta-rubrics be **automatically generated** from question-answer pairs or domain specifications, reducing human labeling cost?
- How does performance vary across **non-English languages** and **diverse cultural contexts** where completeness expectations may differ?
- Can this framework extend to **multi-turn conversations** where completeness is cumulative across exchanges?
- Do models trained with completeness feedback (via RLHF or fine-tuning using GAMUT labels) actually improve in real-world deployment?
- How sensitive is the framework to **rubric annotation disagreement** when multiple experts define completeness differently?
- Does the two-level compilation preserve **interpretability** — can users understand why the system flagged a response as incomplete?

## Key Takeaway
A benchmark and framework for automatically measuring whether AI-generated text covers all necessary facts, not just whether its claims are true.
