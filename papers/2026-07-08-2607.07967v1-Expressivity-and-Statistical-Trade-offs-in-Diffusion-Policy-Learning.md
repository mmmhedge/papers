# Expressivity and Statistical Trade-offs in Diffusion Policy Learning

**Authors:** Viet Vu, Renyuan Xu, Jiacheng Zhang, Yufei Zhang
**Published:** 2026-07-08 | **arXiv:** [2607.07967v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07967v1)

## Summary
This paper analyzes diffusion-based policies—a method for learning action distributions in reinforcement learning by modeling them as solutions to diffusion processes. The key finding is that a single parameter (the drift Lipschitz constant K) governs a fundamental trade-off: larger K makes the policy more expressive (better approximation of optimal actions), but requires more data to learn reliably. The paper provides theoretical bounds and practical guidance on balancing this trade-off for finite-sample learning.

## Theoretical Background
Diffusion policies represent an action distribution as the terminal state of a stochastic differential equation with a learned drift term. Prior work established that diffusion-based models are flexible function approximators (see [[generative-models]]), but lacked formal understanding of *why* they work and *when* they fail. This paper builds on classical approximation theory and statistical learning bounds (via Rademacher complexity and metric entropy arguments for neural networks), connecting them to the Lipschitz continuity of the drift function—a standard regularity condition in differential equations.

## Core Contributions
1. **Identifies drift Lipschitz constant K as the expressivity lever**: Proves that diffusion policies with K-bounded drifts achieve value approximation error ~1/K, with a matching lower bound.
2. **Quantifies the expressivity-sample complexity trade-off**: Shows finite-sample error scales as Õ(n^{-2/(m+6)}) for generic neural-network drifts, and Õ(n^{-2/(m+4)}) for special dissipative structures, where n is sample size and m is state dimension.
3. **Provides a design principle**: Recommends selecting K and neural-network architecture jointly based on available sample size—a concrete algorithmic takeaway.
4. **Proves non-matching lower bounds are tight**: Demonstrates the upper bounds are information-theoretically necessary (modulo log factors).

## Connections to Other Work
This work bridges [[reinforcement-learning]] policy learning and approximation theory. It relates to:
- Prior empirical success of diffusion policies (Diffusion-based RL literature) but adds the missing theory
- Classical statistical learning bounds for neural networks, extended to the diffusion setting
- The broader landscape of policy parameterizations ([[Actor-Critic methods]], [[Policy Gradient]]) by providing formal expressivity guarantees
- Optimal transport and generative modeling theory (diffusion models share mathematical structure with score-based generative models)

## Practical Applications
1. **Robotics control**: Choosing network capacity and K for real robot tasks with limited demonstration data (e.g., 1000 trajectories vs 10k).
2. **Imitation learning**: Balancing model complexity against finite expert demonstrations in high-dimensional action spaces.
3. **Offline RL**: Designing diffusion-based critics and actors for batch RL where data is fixed and expensive.
4. **Multimodal behavior learning**: Learning diverse manipulation strategies (e.g., pick-and-place with multiple grasp modes) from limited rollouts.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **AutoML for diffusion policies**: Tool that automatically selects K and architecture given data size, state dimensionality, and computational budget—sell to robotics/RL teams.
2. **Data-efficient imitation learning platform**: SaaS for learning robot skills from small demo sets, using this theory to recommend model capacity automatically.
3. **Diffusion policy validator**: Software that certifies whether a learned policy approximates the optimal value within a given error bound, given sample statistics.
4. **RL benchmarking service**: Benchmark suite comparing diffusion vs. other policy classes on standard tasks, with recommendations for when to use diffusion (based on data regime and dimensionality).
5. **Adaptive policy learner**: Interactive system that suggests how many more samples are needed to hit a performance target, using the convergence rates derived here.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Curse of dimensionality**: The rate Õ(n^{-2/(m+6)}) degrades sharply in state dimension m, making high-dimensional problems harder (typical for neural network learning).
2. **Nondegenerate noise assumption**: Lower bounds assume noise is always present; results may not extend to deterministic limits.
3. **K selection in practice**: While the theory prescribes K in terms of n, practitioners don't know optimal n in advance and must tune K empirically anyway.
4. **Limited to policy representation**: Focuses on action distribution expressivity; doesn't address value function approximation or exploration.
5. **Assumes data i.i.d. from fixed behavior policy**: Results may not apply in online RL or distribution shift settings.
6. **One-sided dissipative class**: The improved rate requires special structure (dissipative drifts); applicability to general problems unclear.

## Open Questions
1. How do these bounds extend to **online RL** or **bandit feedback** settings, where data distribution changes with learning?
2. Can we achieve the Õ(n^{-2/(m+4)}) rate for broader drift classes beyond one-sided dissipative functions?
3. How do value function approximation errors interact with policy approximation errors in the full RL pipeline?
4. What is the computational complexity of choosing K optimally? Does it require solving an auxiliary optimization problem?
5. Do the bounds hold under **function approximation** errors in the drift network (current analysis assumes exact optimization)?
6. How do partial observability or state estimation errors affect the trade-off?

## Key Takeaway
The expressivity of diffusion policies is controlled by a single Lipschitz parameter K, which creates an unavoidable bias-variance trade-off: practitioners must scale K with data size to avoid overfitting or underfitting.
