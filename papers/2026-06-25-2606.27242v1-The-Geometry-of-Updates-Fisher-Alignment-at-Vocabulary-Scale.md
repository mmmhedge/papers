# The Geometry of Updates: Fisher Alignment at Vocabulary Scale

**Authors:** John Sweeney
**Published:** 2026-06-25 | **arXiv:** [2606.27242v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27242v1)

## Summary

This paper introduces FisherSketch, a computationally efficient method for selecting training data sources for language model families that share tokenizers but differ in prediction targets (e.g., SMILES, protein, genomic sequences). By reformulating Fisher alignment as a cosine similarity in joint activation-error space rather than requiring expensive Fisher matrix computation, the method enables practical source selection at vocabulary scale with minimal memory overhead (16–192 KB signatures per task).

## Theoretical Background

The paper builds on several foundational concepts:

- **Classical transfer learning**: Representation similarity metrics (e.g., CKA, HSIC) that assume shared representations enable positive transfer—a common assumption in domain adaptation literature
- **Fisher information geometry**: Fisher matrices capture the curvature of the loss landscape and have been used to measure task similarity and model relatedness, but are prohibitively expensive for large models
- **Kernel methods**: The insight that Fisher alignment can be expressed as cosine similarity between kernel mean embeddings in activation-error space draws from kernel theory and enables efficient approximation
- **Vocabulary-scale LLM analysis**: Prior work on multi-task and multi-domain LLM training, where shared vocabularies mask heterogeneous prediction targets

The key theoretical contribution is the non-identifiability result: identical representations and orthogonal head updates can coexist, breaking the common assumption that representation similarity implies transfer compatibility.

## Core Contributions

1. **Non-identifiability theorem**: Proves that representation metrics (CKA, etc.) are non-identifiable for transfer in shared-output-head settings—two models can share identical representations yet have completely orthogonal gradient updates.

2. **Fisher-kernel embedding identity**: Shows that head Fisher alignment equals a cosine similarity in the joint activation-error space, decomposing transfer into activation, error, and coupling factors without materializing the full Fisher matrix.

3. **FisherSketch algorithm**: A streaming algorithm that estimates this cosine in a single pass with O(m) memory (m=4096 dimensions), reducing per-task storage to 16 KB (task signature) + 192 KB (streaming state).

4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates on Llama-3.1-8B that FisherSketch remains informative for source selection and task-similarity diagnosis even when CKA cannot distinguish tasks (e.g., verbalizer-shift experiments).

## Connections to Other Work

This work sits at the intersection of [[transfer-learning]], [[representation-learning]], and [[mechanistic-interpretability]]:

- **Representation similarity metrics** ([[CKA]], HSIC): The paper directly challenges the sufficiency of these metrics, showing they can be uninformative without assumptions about error geometry.
- **Domain adaptation**: Relevant to scientific domain adaptation (SMILES, proteins, genomics) where tokenizers are shared but semantic targets diverge.
- **Fisher-based transfer metrics** (Fisher pruning, Fisher merging): Extends Fisher geometry to the regime where direct computation is infeasible.
- **Task similarity and meta-learning**: Related to work on learning-to-learn and understanding which tasks should be trained together.
- **Mechanistic interpretability**: The decomposition into activation, error, and coupling factors contributes to understanding what drives transfer between LLM tasks.

## Practical Applications

1. **Scientific-domain source selection**: For SMILES (molecular), protein sequences, genomic data: automatically select which pre-trained model or corpus best serves a new fine-tuning task without training.

2. **Curriculum learning in multi-task settings**: Use FisherSketch signatures to order tasks by transfer-relevance, improving sample efficiency in continual learning.

3. **Model composition and merging**: Identify which models in a family (fine-tuned variants, different architectures with shared vocabulary) are most compatible for merging or ensemble.

4. **Data-efficient adaptation for specialized vocabularies**: In biology, chemistry, clinical NLP—where labeled data is scarce—FisherSketch allows rapid transfer diagnosis before expensive training.

5. **Task diagnostics in LLM evaluation**: Understand whether poor transfer is due to activation mismatch, label-shift (error), or their interaction, informing data collection and augmentation strategies.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **Transfer-Ready Model Selector (B2B SaaS)**: A cloud service that accepts a new scientific domain task and corpus, computes FisherSketch signatures for a library of pre-trained LLM checkpoints, and recommends the best base model—targeting biotech, drug discovery, and genomics startups reducing time-to-deployment.

2. **Adaptive LLM Fine-tuning Pipeline**: Integrate FisherSketch into MLOps platforms (Weights & Biases, Hugging Face Inference) to automatically select training data sources and rank corpora by transfer value, reducing compute waste in multi-domain enterprises.

3. **Scientific Model Zoo with Transfer Diagnostics**: Build a marketplace of domain-specific LLM checkpoints (proteins, molecules, genes) with pre-computed FisherSketch signatures, enabling researchers to instantly determine compatibility—like a GitHub for scientific models.

4. **Curriculum Learning Engine for Multi-task Training**: A platform that uses FisherSketch to order training tasks by transfer-relevance, helping companies train efficient multi-task models with minimal manual task curation.

5. **Efficient Model Merging & Composition as a Service**: Enable users to merge multiple fine-tuned LLMs without retraining by using FisherSketch to verify alignment before fusion, reducing inference latency and infrastructure costs.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Shared-output head assumption**: The method assumes all models in the family use the same output head; it is unclear how to extend to models with different head dimensions or architectures.

2. **Streaming state overhead**: While 192 KB per task is small, scaling to thousands of tasks or models still requires central storage and coordination; distributed diagnostics are not addressed.

3. **Activation-regime dependence**: The method works in "activation-dark" regimes but may be less informative when representation similarity is already strong and predictive—no clear guidance on when to use FisherSketch vs. simpler metrics.

4. **Error geometry assumptions**: The decomposition into activation, error, and coupling factors assumes label quality and well-behaved loss landscapes; failure modes on noisy or mislabeled datasets are not discussed.

5. **Vocabulary-scale vocabulary**: Limited to models sharing tokenizers; extending to cross-lingual or cross-architecture settings with different vocabularies requires new theory.

6. **Empirical scope**: Validation is primarily on Llama-3.1-8B and scientific strings; generalization to diverse architectures (GPT, Mistral, encoder-decoder) and general-domain tasks is unclear.

## Open Questions

1. **Non-shared head settings**: How can FisherSketch be adapted for models with different output heads or classifiers? Is a more expensive approximation needed?

2. **Temporal dynamics**: Does FisherSketch stability change during training? Can it be used for online source selection as training progresses?

3. **Cross-tokenizer transfer**: Can kernel embeddings be aligned or translated across different vocabularies to enable transfer diagnosis without shared tokenizers?

4. **Negative transfer prediction**: Can FisherSketch predict when source selection will harm performance (negative transfer)? What thresholds matter?

5. **Interaction with fine-tuning hyperparameters**: How sensitive is transfer prediction to learning rate, batch size, and regularization? Does FisherSketch remain predictive under hyperparameter variation?

6. **Scaling to billions of tasks**: For enterprises with hundreds of task variants, can FisherSketch signatures be hierarchically compressed or hashed further while preserving discriminative power?

## Key Takeaway

By reformulating Fisher alignment as cosine similarity in activation-error space, FisherSketch enables practical, training-free transfer assessment at vocabulary scale with minuscule memory footprint, revealing that representation similarity alone cannot predict transfer in shared-output settings.
