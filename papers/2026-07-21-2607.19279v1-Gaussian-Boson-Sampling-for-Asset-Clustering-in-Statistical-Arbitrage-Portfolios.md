# Gaussian Boson Sampling for Asset Clustering in Statistical Arbitrage Portfolios

**Authors:** Dayne Marcus Lopena, Daniel Buguks, Zhenghao Li, Ewan Mer, Shana H. Winston et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-21 | **arXiv:** [2607.19279v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19279v1)

## Summary
This paper applies Gaussian Boson Sampling (GBS)—a quantum computing method for finding dense subgraphs—to identify clusters of correlated stocks for building statistical arbitrage portfolios. The authors show that quantum clustering algorithms outperform classical methods in constructing market-neutral portfolios, especially during high-volatility periods and in large stock universes, potentially opening a practical quantum advantage in portfolio construction.

## Theoretical Background
Statistical arbitrage (StatArb) strategies profit from temporary mispricings by identifying groups of co-moving assets and constructing market-neutral positions. Classical approaches use correlation matrices and clustering algorithms like Spectral Clustering and SPONGE to partition stocks. Gaussian Boson Sampling is a photonic quantum computing approach that samples from bosonic systems to efficiently search dense subgraphs in adjacency matrices—a combinatorial problem NP-hard for classical computers. The paper bridges quantum physics and quantitative finance by mapping stock correlation structures into GBS-compatible graph representations.

## Core Contributions
1. **Novel mapping**: Converts S&P 500 residual correlation matrices into GBS-compatible adjacency matrices suitable for quantum sampling.
2. **GBS Roots algorithm**: Introduces a new quantum clustering algorithm alongside GBS Boost, tailored for portfolio construction.
3. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates superior alpha generation compared to Spectral and SPONGE clustering across multiple macroeconomic regimes, particularly in high-volatility periods and large universes.
4. **Loss resilience**: Shows the quantum advantage persists under realistic photonic loss conditions using coherent displacement techniques.
5. **Rolling window backtests**: Tests dynamic, market-neutral portfolio construction over rolling one-year windows—realistic operational conditions.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[quantitative-finance]], quantum computing, and graph clustering. It extends classical correlation-based clustering methods (Spectral, SPONGE) into the quantum domain. ## Practical Applications
1. **Statistical arbitrage funds**: Real-time construction of dynamic market-neutral baskets in equities, identifying structural mispricings at scale.
2. **Multi-asset correlation trading**: Extended to bonds, commodities, or forex pairs where correlation structure is predictive.
3. **Portfolio rebalancing**: Adaptive clustering during regime shifts to maintain diversification within StatArb sleeves.
4. **Cross-asset pairs trading**: Identifying synthetic pairs or triplets for pairs-trading strategies across asset classes.
5. **Risk factor rotation**: Using quantum clustering to dynamically partition factor exposures and optimize risk-parity allocations.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability bottleneck**: GBS hardware remains limited (current photonic systems handle dozens to hundreds of modes); S&P 500 is 500 assets, requiring dimensionality reduction or staged clustering.
2. **Photonic loss**: Real devices suffer 20-50% photon losses; coherent displacement mitigation is tested only in simulation, not on hardware.
3. **Latency**: Quantum sampling and classical post-processing introduce delays unsuitable for high-frequency strategies; best suited for medium-frequency (daily to weekly) rebalancing.
4. **Correlation stationarity**: Residual correlations are time-varying; the rolling window approach assumes one-year stationarity, which may break under extreme market shocks.
5. **Out-of-sample overfitting**: Backtests on historical S&P 500; unclear if quantum advantage generalizes to other universes, shorter rebalance horizons, or transaction cost-inclusive scenarios.
6. **Hardware access**: GBS hardware is nascent and geographically concentrated; practical deployment requires latency-tolerant architectures or on-premises quantum systems (capital-intensive).

## Open Questions
1. How does GBS clustering scale to full factor models (>1,000 assets) or truly high-dimensional universes? Is hierarchical quantum clustering viable?
2. Can the quantum advantage be demonstrated on actual photonic hardware under realistic loss, noise, and latency conditions?
3. Does the superior alpha hold out-of-sample on post-2026 data or across non-US equity markets?
4. How sensitive is the approach to hyperparameter choices (GBS squeezing, adjacency matrix thresholding) and can these be learned adaptively?
5. What is the transaction cost impact? Does alpha survive after commissions, market impact, and borrowing costs for the short leg?
6. Can GBS be combined with other quantum algorithms (QAOA, VQE) for end-to-end portfolio optimization rather than just clustering?

## Key Takeaway
Quantum photonic sampling outperforms classical clustering for identifying profitable stock groups in high-volatility markets, suggesting a practical quantum edge in portfolio construction.
