# Dialogue Summarization with Emotion Dynamics Using Topic- and Participant-Centric Decomposition

**Authors:** Linyun Xiang, Mark Neerincx, Stephanie Tan
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.14769v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14769v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes a new framework for summarizing dialogues (conversations between multiple people) that captures not just *what* was said, but *how people felt* throughout the exchange. Unlike existing summarization methods built for single-author documents like news articles, this approach decomposes conversations by topic and by individual speaker, then combines these perspectives to generate summaries that preserve both semantic content and emotional trajectories—a first step toward making dialogue summaries emotionally faithful to the original conversation.

## Theoretical Background
Dialogue summarization extends classical text summarization (which traditionally handles monologic sources like articles or reports) to multi-party conversations where meaning emerges from turn-taking and interaction. The paper builds on hierarchical summarization approaches—methods that first break down long texts into smaller, summable chunks before combining them. The authors adapt a Chain-of-Agents framework, which uses multiple specialized language model agents (each handling a specific subtask) to collaborate in solving complex problems. The work also draws on emotion recognition in NLP—computational methods that detect emotional content in text—and recognizes that emotional dynamics (how feelings shift across a conversation) matter for faithful summarization, even though most prior work ignores them.

## Core Contributions
1. **Multi-perspective dialogue decomposition**: Decomposes conversations along two orthogonal dimensions—by topic (across all speakers) and by participant (speaker-specific segments)—rather than treating dialogue as a flat sequence.
2. **Emotion-aware summarization**: Automatically infers emotions during summarization and explicitly incorporates emotional information into both topic-level and participant-level summaries, then preserves emotional trajectories in final output.
3. **New evaluation metrics**: Introduces emotion trajectory metrics that measure how well summaries preserve the *flow* of emotional content, going beyond standard content-accuracy metrics (ROUGE, BERTScore).
4. **Multimodal dialogue pipeline**: Demonstrates the approach on multimodal dialogue data (speech + text) using smaller language models, showing practical feasibility without requiring massive models.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of dialogue understanding, emotion detection, and multi-agent reasoning. It extends [[RLHF]]-style collaborative language model approaches by having agents decompose a problem (dialogue) rather than optimize a single objective. It relates to broader work on [[transformers]]-based summarization (which this likely builds on) but adds emotion as an explicit dimension. The participant-centric view connects to speaker-aware dialogue systems and turn-taking analysis. The emotion trajectory idea parallels narrative arc detection in storytelling and sentiment dynamics in social media analysis—both areas where *how feelings change* matters as much as *what happens*.

## Practical Applications
1. **Customer service transcript analysis**: Automatically summarize support call conversations while flagging escalating frustration or satisfaction trends—useful for quality assurance and training.
2. **Meeting notes generation**: Create summaries of team meetings that capture both decisions (semantic) and team morale or conflict (emotional), helping managers understand meeting dynamics.
3. **Mental health & counseling**: Generate session summaries for therapists that preserve emotional arcs, supporting case documentation and progress tracking.
4. **Mediation & conflict resolution**: Summarize disputed conversations with emotion awareness, helping mediators understand escalation points and emotional triggers.
5. **Podcast/interview transcription**: Auto-generate summaries that capture interviewer-interviewee rapport and emotional engagement, useful for content creators and discovery.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Emotion inference bottleneck**: The framework relies on automatically inferred emotions, which are notoriously error-prone in NLP, especially across diverse cultural contexts and sarcasm. Wrong emotion labels propagate to final summaries.
2. **Scalability of decomposition**: The two-dimensional (topic + participant) decomposition may become intractable in multi-party conversations with many speakers or highly topic-fragmented dialogues; no discussion of computational complexity.
3. **Small language model constraints**: Experiments use small LLMs; unclear whether the approach generalizes to larger models or whether smaller models actually capture subtle emotional nuance reliably.
4. **Multimodal dependency**: Relies on multimodal inputs (speech + text); many real-world dialogue logs lack speech data, limiting applicability.
5. **Emotion trajectory metrics not validated**: The newly proposed emotion trajectory metrics lack comparison to human judgments of emotional faithfulness; unclear if they actually measure what practitioners care about.
6. **Limited scope of evaluation**: Only tested on unspecified "multimodal dialogue datasets"—no comparison to existing dialogue summarization baselines or human evaluation of emotional accuracy.

## Open Questions
1. How does automatic emotion inference error impact downstream summary quality? (Ablation on emotion label noise would be valuable.)
2. Can the framework scale to long, multi-party conversations with many topic shifts and speakers?
3. Do larger language models (e.g., GPT-4 scale) actually need this explicit two-perspective decomposition, or do they handle it implicitly?
4. How do emotion trajectory metrics correlate with human judgments of "emotionally faithful" summaries? (Human evaluation is critical.)
5. Can the approach handle sarcasm, mixed emotions, and cultural differences in emotional expression?
6. What's the performance on dialogue types not seen during development (e.g., domain-specific jargon in legal negotiations)?

## Key Takeaway
Multi-speaker conversation summaries can preserve emotional flow by decomposing dialogue by topic and speaker, then aggregating emotion-aware partial summaries.
