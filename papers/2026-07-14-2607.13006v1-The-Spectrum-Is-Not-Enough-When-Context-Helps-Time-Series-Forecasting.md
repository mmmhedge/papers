# The Spectrum Is Not Enough: When Context Helps Time-Series Forecasting

**Authors:** Mert Onur Cakiroglu, Mehmet Dalkilic, Hasan Kurban
**Published:** 2026-07-14 | **arXiv:** [2607.13006v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13006v1)

## Summary
This paper challenges the common assumption that spectral indices (mathematical measures of a time series' predictability based on frequency content) can tell us whether adding context—like longer lookback windows or retrieval systems—will improve forecasting. The authors prove this is mathematically impossible and introduce a new diagnostic called "coverage deficit" to determine when context actually helps. Their work matters because practitioners often misuse spectral indices to make deployment decisions, potentially wasting resources on methods that won't work for their data.

## Theoretical Background
Time-series forecasting relies on understanding what makes a series predictable. Spectral methods analyze a signal's frequency components (its "power spectrum"), which are invariant under phase randomization—meaning rearranging the order of observations while keeping their magnitudes. The paper builds on decades of work in signal processing and information theory showing that second-order statistics (variance, autocorrelation) can be extracted from the spectrum alone. Modern forecasting increasingly uses context—longer historical windows, retrieval-augmented systems, or foundation models (pretrained neural networks). The key insight is that while the spectrum is phase-invariant, the *value* of context depends on higher-order statistical structure (beyond variance and correlations) that disappears under phase randomization. The paper formalizes this gap using surrogate pairs: synthetic datasets designed to have identical spectra and marginal distributions but different phase structure.

## Core Contributions
1. **Impossibility result**: Proves formally that any index computed solely from the power spectrum cannot predict whether context (retrieval, longer windows, pretrained models) will improve forecasting.
2. **Surrogate pair methodology**: Constructs controlled datasets that fix spectrum and marginal distribution while varying phase structure, enabling causal comparison of what helps.
3. **Coverage deficit diagnostic**: Introduces a label-free, configuration-level metric that decomposes forecasting value into a surviving second-order part and a beyond-linear margin, predicting when each mechanism (spectrum vs. context) will help.
4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates on seven benchmarks that spectral indices fail to predict context value (e.g., retrieval's value swings from +33% to −35% across surrogate pairs, *p* < 10⁻⁴⁰), while coverage deficit succeeds.

## Connections to Other Work
This work clarifies a fundamental confusion in the forecasting literature around [[attention mechanisms]] and [[transformers]] applied to time series. It relates to broader questions about when deep learning helps versus simpler linear models—a theme in recent "why does scaling work?" research. The phase-randomization argument echoes classical signal processing (Wiener filtering, spectral estimation) but applies it to a modern question: when do foundation models and retrieval genuinely add value? The diagnostic resembles [[mechanistic-interpretability]] efforts to decompose model behavior into interpretable components. The paper also connects to literature on forecasting benchmarking (e.g., criticism of datasets that favor specific architectures) by showing that spectral properties alone are insufficient for algorithm selection.

## Practical Applications
1. **Forecasting system deployment**: Teams building production forecasters can use coverage deficit to decide whether to invest in expensive retrieval or foundation models versus simpler baselines.
2. **Dataset assessment**: Data scientists can diagnose whether a time series has "learnable" structure beyond linear patterns before committing to complex architectures.
3. **Algorithm selection**: Organizations can avoid wasting compute on context-based methods for inherently low-structure series (e.g., random-walk-like prices), redirecting resources to series where beyond-spectrum structure exists.
4. **Model pruning**: When serving multiple forecasts, teams can identify which series benefit from lightweight linear windows vs. which need heavy lifting.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Forecasting diagnostics SaaS**: A tool that ingests time-series data and returns a "forecasting readiness report"—which methods will actually help, estimated cost-benefit of upgrading to foundation models, and optimal lookback window size.
2. **Smart algorithm selector**: A meta-learner that recommends retrieval depth, model size, and window length by computing coverage deficit in real time, reducing trial-and-error for production teams.
3. **Time-series data quality audit**: Consulting service that analyzes enterprise data pipelines to identify which forecasting challenges are actually solvable (vs. noisy) and allocates ML budgets accordingly.
4. **AutoML for forecasting**: An AutoML platform that uses coverage deficit as a fast filter before expensive hyperparameter tuning, cutting search time 10–100×.
5. **Foundation model selection for enterprises**: API that tests proprietary time-series data against multiple pretrained models and returns ROI projections, helping teams avoid vendor lock-in and misaligned tool choices.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Surrogate construction**: Creating phase-randomized surrogates is computationally tractable but may not exhaust all forms of beyond-spectrum structure; the method is most powerful for Gaussian approximations.
2. **Analog vs. linear prediction gap**: Coverage deficit uses analog (nearest-neighbor) prediction to isolate structure, but analog methods themselves are unstable in high dimensions and sparse regimes.
3. **Scope**: The paper focuses on univariate or low-dimensional forecasting; multivariate or highly correlated systems may have phase-coupled structure that the diagnostic misses.
4. **Foundation model heterogeneity**: The diagnosis assumes foundation models primarily capture beyond-spectrum structure; if they also exploit second-order patterns more efficiently than linear methods, the decomposition conflates gains.
5. **Computational cost**: Coverage deficit requires fitting analog predictors on surrogate pairs, adding overhead—unclear how quickly it scales to very long series or high-dimensional feature spaces.

## Open Questions
1. How does coverage deficit extend to multivariate forecasting, where phase coupling between series may carry predictive value?
2. Can the diagnostic work in streaming/online settings, or does it require full historical context?
3. Do other beyond-spectrum structures (e.g., nonlinear state-space dynamics) appear in the "small margin" coverage deficit leaves unexplained?
4. How does coverage deficit interact with data augmentation, synthetic generation, or transfer learning from related domains?
5. Can the insight be inverted: given a forecast error, can we infer what structure the forecaster exploited and diagnose model misfit?

## Key Takeaway
Spectral indices cannot predict whether context helps forecasting; use phase structure diagnostics instead to decide deployment investments wisely.
