# The Count Is There, but Misaligned: Understanding and Correcting Counting Failures in VLMs

**Authors:** Ahmed Oumar El-Shangiti, Abzal Nurgazy, Hilal AlQuabeh, Nikolai Rozanov, Kentaro Inui
**Published:** 2026-07-10 | **arXiv:** [2607.09544v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09544v1)

## Summary
Vision-language models (VLMs) struggle with basic object counting despite strong performance on other tasks. This paper discovers that VLMs often internally represent the correct count but output wrong answers — a misalignment between internal knowledge and external behavior. The authors propose a simple fix: an error detector that identifies when the model is likely to fail, then triggers a re-prompt, improving counting accuracy by up to 15.6 percentage points with no retraining.

## Theoretical Background
Vision-language models combine image understanding (computer vision) with text generation capabilities to answer questions about images. Prior work has documented their limitations on counting tasks, but it was unclear whether this reflected missing knowledge or a gap between what the model "knows" internally and what it says.

This paper builds on mechanistic interpretability — the approach of probing a model's internal activations (the numerical values flowing through its neural network) to understand what information it encodes. The authors use two key techniques: (1) training simple linear and nonlinear probes on activation patterns to decode whether a model "knows" the correct count, and (2) SVCCA (Singular Vector Canonical Correlation Analysis), a method for comparing the geometry of learned representations across different probe training objectives. Causal steering interventions — techniques that directly manipulate internal activations to change model behavior — validate that strengthening count-related activation patterns improves output accuracy.

## Core Contributions
1. **Empirical discovery**: Through probing four VLMs across five counting datasets, the authors show that nonlinear probes trained on ground-truth counts reliably detect errors, suggesting the correct count is encoded internally even when the model outputs wrong answers.

2. **Mechanistic insight via SVCCA**: They demonstrate that probes trained on ground-truth counts and probes trained on actual model outputs occupy partially shared but misaligned subspaces of activations — revealing a specific form of knowledge-to-output misalignment.

3. **Causal validation**: Steering interventions prove causality: strengthening the activation directions associated with correct counts improves counting performance.

4. **Practical inference-time method**: A detector-guided self-correction approach uses an error detector to selectively re-prompt only when the model is predicted to fail, achieving 15.6 pp improvements without any parameter updates.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to broader work on [[mechanistic-interpretability]] of large language and vision models, which aims to understand how models solve tasks internally. It complements research on VLM failures and [[alignment]] concerns — not alignment in the safety sense, but literal alignment between internal representations and outputs.

The work also connects to activation-based interventions like representation steering and probing for hidden knowledge (e.g., in the LLM interpretability community). The self-correction idea echoes approaches like [[chain-of-thought prompting]] and [[verification]], but adds a learned error detector as a gating mechanism.

## Practical Applications
1. **Improved image annotation systems**: E-commerce and inventory management systems could use VLMs with built-in counting correction to accurately count items in photos (e.g., stock levels, damaged goods).

2. **Medical image analysis**: Counting cells, lesions, or anatomical structures in medical scans, where accuracy is critical.

3. **Autonomous systems**: Robots or drones performing scene understanding tasks that require accurate object counts (e.g., counting people for crowd management).

4. **Quality assurance**: Manufacturing and logistics systems that verify counts of parts, packages, or products in images.

5. **Educational tools**: Tutoring systems that use VLMs to analyze student work and count objects for feedback.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **CountAI**: A cloud service wrapper around VLMs specifically optimized for counting in photos. API users submit images; the service returns accurate object counts with confidence scores. Market: supply chain, retail inventory, insurance claims.

2. **Visual Auditing Platform**: SaaS tool for compliance teams that auto-detects and corrects counting errors in warehouse, dock, and shelf-monitoring camera feeds in real time.

3. **Medical Imaging Co-Pilot**: Desktop software for pathologists and radiologists that uses corrected VLM counting as a first-pass screening tool for cell/lesion counts, flagging cases for manual review.

4. **Robotics Counting Module**: SDK for robotics companies integrating corrected counting into manipulation and inspection workflows (e.g., parts-picking robots that verify counts before shipping).

5. **AI Reliability Layer**: Standalone inference middleware that sits between any VLM API and applications, using learned error detectors to improve accuracy on task-specific benchmarks (counting, reading, spatial reasoning) with zero model retraining.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Limited scope**: The method is validated only on counting tasks; generalization to other failure modes (hallucination, reasoning, localization) is unclear.

2. **Probe training overhead**: Requires task-specific ground-truth labels to train error detectors and counting probes — limits applicability to new domains without labeled data.

3. **Scalability of SVCCA**: The mechanistic analysis (SVCCA) is computationally expensive and doesn't scale easily to very large models or datasets.

4. **Re-prompting cost**: The self-correction method requires additional forward passes (re-prompting), increasing inference latency and cost compared to single-pass baselines.

5. **Probe generalization**: It's unclear how well probes trained on one VLM architecture transfer to others, or how robust they are to model updates and fine-tuning.

6. **Limited dataset diversity**: Evaluation on five counting datasets — further validation on diverse real-world images (different scales, cluttered scenes, transparent/occluded objects) is needed.

## Open Questions
1. **Generalization across tasks**: Can similar detector-guided self-correction approaches work for other failure modes in VLMs (hallucinations, spatial reasoning, arithmetic)?

2. **Transfer of probes**: Do probes trained on one VLM transfer to another model, or must they be retrained per architecture?

3. **Why is alignment broken?**: What architectural or training properties of VLMs cause this misalignment between internal counts and outputs? Can training objectives be modified to prevent it?

4. **Scaling**: How do results hold for very large models (GPT-4V, newer architectures) and extremely large counts?

5. **Probe interpretability**: Can we understand *why* certain activations encode counts? Do they correspond to interpretable features (spatial attention, object detection heads)?

6. **Comparison to other corrections**: How does detector-guided re-prompting compare to other inference-time interventions (ensemble methods, chain-of-thought, in-context examples)?

## Key Takeaway
Vision-language models often know the correct count internally but fail to output it — and a simple error detector can identify these failures and trigger correction, substantially improving accuracy without any retraining.
