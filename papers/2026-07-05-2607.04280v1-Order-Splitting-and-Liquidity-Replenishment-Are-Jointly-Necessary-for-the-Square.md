# Order Splitting and Liquidity Replenishment Are Jointly Necessary for the Square-Root Law of Market Impact:

**Authors:** Yang Zhou, Jianwen Chen, Ruipeng Wei
**Published:** 2026-07-05 | **arXiv:** [2607.04280v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.04280v1)

## Summary
This paper explains why market impact follows a square-root law — a universal empirical pattern where large trades move prices by roughly the square root of their size. The authors use a simulated limit-order-book market with heterogeneous trading agents to show that two specific mechanisms — order splitting (breaking large trades into smaller pieces) and liquidity replenishment by market makers — are jointly necessary and sufficient to produce this law. Testing three competing theoretical models, they find all three fail in isolation, but their agent-based model matches real market data from the Tokyo Stock Exchange when both mechanisms are present.

## Theoretical Background
The square-root law (SRL) of market impact is an empirical regularity observed across many markets: the price impact $I$ of a trade scales as $I/\sigma_D = c(Q/V_D)^{\delta}$ with $\delta \approx 0.5$, where $Q$ is trade size, $V_D$ is daily volume, and $\sigma_D$ is daily volatility. Three competing theoretical explanations exist: GGPS derives the exponent from information asymmetry ($\delta = \beta - 1$); FGLW derives it from volatility-dependence ($\delta = \alpha - 1$); and LOB walking posits the exponent comes from limit-order-book shape ($\delta = 1/(1+\gamma)$). This paper tests these against agent-based simulation using a minimal limit-order-book model — a computational market where heterogeneous agents place buy/sell orders and transact against an order book (the posted prices at which you can trade).

## Core Contributions
1. **Empirical falsification**: Direct comparison of three leading SRL theories on identical simulated data reveals all three fail quantitatively — GGPS and FGLW over-predict $\delta$ by factors of 2–4, while LOB walking under-predicts.

2. **Causal mechanism identification**: Counterfactual ablation (selectively removing components) directly identifies order splitting and liquidity replenishment as jointly necessary. Removing either alone causes $\delta$ to drop from ~0.55 to ~0.32–0.39; other factors (momentum trading, price limits, splitting rule details, background liquidity) have <10% effect.

3. **Robustness across parameter space**: The model reproduces $\delta = 0.539 \pm 0.048$ across 2000 independently parameterized stocks, showing the SRL is robust to heterogeneity in model parameters.

4. **Negative results on tail and book shape**: Contrary to prior intuition, the SRL depends on neither the distribution of metaorder sizes (size tail) nor visible book shape in isolation — suggesting these are red herrings in the mechanism.

## Practical Applications
1. **Optimal execution**: Traders executing large orders need to predict impact; this work explains which market conditions (when splitting/replenishment are active) will produce predictable square-root scaling vs. worse impact.

2. **Market design**: Exchanges considering changes to tick sizes, order types, or maker rebates can now predict how these affect impact via their effect on splitting incentives and maker liquidity provision.

3. **Risk management**: Quant funds predicting transaction costs for portfolio rebalancing or risk hedges can now use the SRL with confidence in markets where order splitting and market-maker liquidity are present.

4. **Algorithmic trading**: Smart order routing can exploit the identified mechanisms — routes that encourage liquidity replenishment or that split orders more efficiently will reduce their own impact.

5. **Regulatory impact assessment**: Regulators considering market structure changes can model knock-on effects on market impact via changes to splitting behavior and maker participation.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Market impact prediction SaaS**: A tool that inputs stock/market characteristics, detects whether order splitting and maker liquidity are active, and outputs expected impact curves for execution algorithms. Sell to hedge funds and algo trading desks.

2. **Optimal order splitting optimizer**: Real-time software that given a target order size, market conditions, and execution horizon, recommends order split sizes and timing to minimize impact via the SRL mechanism. License to brokers.

3. **Market microstructure simulator**: A SaaS platform (inspired by this paper's model) that lets market-makers and exchanges simulate the effect of regulatory changes, tick size changes, or rebate structures on market impact and liquidity. Sell to exchanges and large market-makers.

4. **Maker rebate optimizer**: For exchanges, a tool that tunes maker rebates dynamically to maximize liquidity replenishment (a key mechanism identified here), thereby reducing overall market impact and attracting more volume. Revenue share with the exchange.

5. **Impact-aware portfolio construction**: A fund management platform that incorporates predicted SRL-based impact into portfolio optimization, allowing large asset managers to construct positions that minimize total transaction costs. Charge a fee to asset managers.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Model simplicity**: The minimal limit-order-book model may miss real-world complexities — dark pools, latency, information asymmetry, tick size constraints, and intraday volatility clustering are all absent. Results may not generalize to markets where these are important.

2. **Single calibration point**: Calibration is done against Tokyo Stock Exchange data. Generalization to other markets (US equities, bonds, crypto) is assumed but not demonstrated.

3. **No endogenous agent learning**: Agents follow fixed strategies; there is no adaptation or learning. Real market participants adjust splitting strategies and liquidity provision over time in response to market conditions.

4. **Order splitting model**: The paper assumes agents split orders, but does not explain *why* they do so beyond algorithmic convenience. In reality, splitting is driven by information leakage concerns, volatility timing, and execution logistics — none of which is modeled endogenously.

5. **Causality via ablation**: While counterfactual ablation is informative, it assumes linear separability of mechanisms. If order splitting and liquidity replenishment interact nonlinearly, removing one may not reveal the true causal effect of the other.

6. **Limited to equity markets**: All empirical validation is on equities. Applicability to FX, commodities, or fixed income (where market structure and agent behavior differ) is unclear.

## Open Questions
1. **Endogenous order splitting**: Can you build a model where agents *choose* to split orders based on economic incentives (information leakage, volatility control, execution risk) rather than imposing it exogenously? Does the SRL still emerge?

2. **Mechanism interaction**: Are order splitting and liquidity replenishment truly independent, or do they interact? Does the effect of removing one depend on the state of the other?

3. **Generalization across markets**: Does the same pair of mechanisms (splitting + replenishment) explain the SRL in other markets — US equities, FX, bonds, crypto? Or are market-specific mechanisms at play?

4. **Micro-level behavior**: Which types of agents do most of the splitting and replenishment? Can you trace SRL to specific trader archetypes or behavior?

5. **Tail behavior**: The paper shows the SRL is robust to metaorder size distribution in the model, but what about extreme events — does the SRL hold for very large metaorders (e.g., >5% of daily volume)?

6. **Dynamic stability**: If you shock the market (e.g., crash in maker liquidity), how quickly does the SRL re-establish itself? Is there hysteresis?

## Key Takeaway
Order splitting and liquidity replenishment are the two mechanisms jointly necessary and sufficient to produce the square-root law of market impact; all three leading competing theories are incomplete without an agent-based model that captures these dynamics.
