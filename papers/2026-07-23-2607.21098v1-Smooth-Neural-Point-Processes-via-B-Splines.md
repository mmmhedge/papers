# Smooth Neural Point Processes via B-Splines

**Authors:** Michele Bellomo, Riccardo Ramaschi, Alberto Dolara, Tomaso Aste
**Published:** 2026-07-23 | **arXiv:** [2607.21098v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21098v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes a new way to model temporal point processes (sequences of events in continuous time) using neural networks combined with B-splines — smooth mathematical curves. The key insight is that by directly modeling the conditional intensity function (the instantaneous rate of events) as a weighted combination of B-spline basis functions, the method achieves exact likelihood calculations, faster training through parallelization, and better predictive accuracy than existing neural approaches.

## Theoretical Background
Temporal point processes (TPPs) are mathematical frameworks for modeling sequences of irregularly-timed events — think of earthquakes, financial trades, or social media posts. The fundamental quantity is the conditional intensity function (CIF), which describes the instantaneous probability of an event occurring given the history so far. Neural TPPs use deep learning to flexibly learn this CIF from data, typically trained via Maximum Likelihood Estimation by minimizing negative log-likelihood (NLL). The NLL depends on both the CIF and its integral over time (the compensator). B-splines are piecewise polynomial curves that smoothly interpolate through data; they are well-established in approximation theory for their computational efficiency and ability to enforce smoothness constraints.

## Core Contributions
1. **Direct CIF parameterization**: Rather than modeling the compensator (integral of intensity) like recent methods, this work directly parametrizes the CIF itself as a non-negative linear combination of B-spline basis functions, with coefficients predicted by a neural network.
2. **Exact likelihood without constraints**: Achieves exact NLL evaluation (no numerical integration needed) while preserving full architectural flexibility — prior exact methods impose specific neural network constraints.
3. **Parallelizable training**: Event contributions to the NLL can be computed in parallel (rather than sequentially), enabling significant computational speedups during training.
4. **Built-in smoothness regularization**: The B-spline structure naturally enables CIF smoothness regularization via the integrated squared second derivative penalty, improving generalization.

## Connections to Other Work
This work extends the neural TPP literature, which has grown to include recurrent and transformer-based architectures for event sequence modeling. It addresses known limitations of prior "exact" methods (e.g., [[Neural Hawkes Process]] variants) that trade flexibility for computational exactness. The use of B-splines as a parametric skeleton over a neural backbone parallels hybrid neural-symbolic approaches in other domains. The smoothness regularization concept connects to broader work on inductive biases in neural networks and [[regularization]] for generalization.

## Practical Applications
- **Financial markets**: Modeling high-frequency trading events, order arrivals, or price jumps to improve market microstructure understanding and risk assessment.
- **Seismology**: Predicting earthquake aftershock sequences with smoother, more interpretable intensity estimates.
- **Social networks**: Modeling user activity (posts, likes, comments) to detect engagement trends or anomalies.
- **Healthcare**: Modeling clinical event sequences (diagnoses, treatments, lab results) for patient trajectory prediction and intervention planning.
- **Cybersecurity**: Detecting attack patterns in network event logs by learning anomalous temporal point processes.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Real-time market anomaly detection platform**: Use the fast, parallelized model to detect unusual trading patterns or flash crashes by learning conditional intensity of order flow in real time.
2. **Earthquake early warning system**: Deploy the smooth CIF model to improve aftershock forecasting and provide faster, more confident alerts to emergency services.
3. **Social media engagement optimization API**: Offer creators a tool that predicts optimal posting times by learning the conditional intensity of audience activity.
4. **Clinical risk prediction SaaS**: Help hospitals predict patient deterioration (readmission, adverse events) using electronic health records as event sequences.
5. **Cybersecurity threat detection**: Develop a managed security service that learns network event intensity patterns to detect intrusions faster than rule-based systems.

## Technical Limitations
- **B-spline basis selection**: The choice of knot placement, spline degree, and number of basis functions is a hyperparameter that requires tuning; no principled automatic selection method is proposed.
- **Scalability to very high dimensions**: While training is parallelized, the method still requires computing B-spline bases, which may become expensive if event type or context dimensionality is very large.
- **Non-negativity constraint**: Enforcing non-negative B-spline coefficients (to ensure valid CIF) may limit expressiveness in some regimes; the paper doesn't discuss how tightly this constraint binds.
- **Limited theoretical analysis**: No formal convergence guarantees, approximation error bounds, or sample complexity analysis are provided.
- **Evaluation limited to standard benchmarks**: Experiments are on relatively small-scale synthetic and standard real-world datasets; scaling and robustness to distribution shift are not explored.

## Open Questions
- How does B-spline basis selection (knot placement, degree, count) affect performance, and can this be automated (e.g., via cross-validation or structure learning)?
- Does the method extend naturally to marked point processes (where events carry continuous or discrete attributes)?
- How does computational performance scale with event sequence length and context dimensionality in realistic high-frequency settings?
- Can interpretability of the learned CIF be improved by regularizing B-spline coefficients directly, or by learning sparse coefficient structures?
- How robust is the method to misspecification (e.g., when the true CIF is highly non-smooth or multi-modal)?

## Key Takeaway
B-spline-based neural point processes enable exact likelihood computation and fast parallel training while naturally enforcing smoothness.
