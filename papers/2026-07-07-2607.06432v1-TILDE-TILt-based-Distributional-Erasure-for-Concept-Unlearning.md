# TILDE: TILt-based Distributional Erasure for Concept Unlearning

**Authors:** Naveen George, Naoki Murata, Yuhta Takida, Konda Reddy Mopuri, Yuki Mitsufuji
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06432v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06432v1)

## Summary
This paper addresses concept unlearning in text-to-image diffusion models—the ability to remove unwanted concepts (copyrighted styles, trademarked characters, etc.) after training while maintaining the model's quality on other tasks. The key insight is framing unlearning as a distributional alignment problem rather than just erasing a concept: the goal is to match the distribution of a reference model trained from scratch without the unwanted data, not just suppress target images. The paper proposes TILDE, which uses energy-tilted distributions and gradient flow learning to achieve both strong forgetting and strong retention.

## Theoretical Background
Text-to-image diffusion models generate images by iteratively denoising random noise, guided by text prompts. They are conditioned on text embeddings and learn to reverse a noise diffusion process. Prior unlearning approaches typically use simple objectives—either pushing target concepts toward low probability or directly erasing model weights—but lack a principled specification of what the *post-unlearning distribution* should look like.

This paper builds on:
- **Energy-based models** and the concept of tilting distributions by an energy function (giving higher or lower probability to certain outcomes)
- **GFlowNets** (generative flow networks), which learn to sample from complex distributions by training a flow that balances probability across modes
- **Conditional distribution alignment**, treating the unlearned model as approximating a reference conditional distribution given a forget constraint

The core theoretical novelty is formulating the target post-unlearning distribution as the minimum-deviation conditional distribution under a forgetting constraint—essentially, "how much must I change the pretrained model to forget concept X while staying as close as possible to the original?"

## Core Contributions
1. **Problem formulation**: Concept unlearning as explicit distributional alignment, with the reference being a hypothetical retain-only model. This replaces implicit retention (hoping the model keeps quality) with an explicit distributional target.

2. **TILDE algorithm**: Uses an energy-tilted, anchor-free target distribution defined via a forget energy function. The model learns via residual ∇-GFlowNet training—training a score correction (gradient) relative to the pretrained diffusion model, rather than retraining from scratch.

3. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates that across objects, artistic styles, and character concepts, TILDE achieves both strong forgetting (low concept reactivation) and strong retention (semantic coverage and diversity on benign prompts), outperforming prior baselines on both metrics simultaneously.

4. **Distributional fidelity**: Shows that the learned post-unlearning distribution more closely matches the reference retain-only distribution than prior methods.

## Connections to Other Work
This connects to:
- **[[Concept Unlearning]]** in vision and language models—prior work (e.g., SISA, machine unlearning) typically uses simpler objectives without explicit distributional targets
- **[[RLHF]]** and alignment literature: similar desire to steer model behavior post-hoc without full retraining
- **Energy-based models** and variational inference: the tilting mechanism recalls Boltzmann machines and exponential family distributions
- **Score-based diffusion** and score matching: the gradient-correction framing aligns with recent work on adapting pretrained diffusion models
- **GFlowNet training**: extends GFlowNets from sampling discrete combinatorial objects to learning score corrections in continuous diffusion spaces

## Practical Applications
1. **Copyright protection**: Remove copyrighted artistic styles or character likenesses from deployed generative models without full retraining, enabling compliance with copyright holders' requests.

2. **Privacy-preserving deployment**: Remove facial likenesses or personal data concepts from public models after users request deletion, without retraining.

3. **Safety compliance**: Suppress generation of trademark-infringing content, weapons, or other regulated imagery in commercial deployments post-launch.

4. **Model customization**: Quickly specialize a pretrained model by forgetting irrelevant concept clusters while retaining quality on relevant tasks.

5. **Regulatory adaptation**: Update models to comply with new regulations (e.g., GDPR, local content restrictions) by forgetting specific concepts without model replacement.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **"UnlearnAI" API service**: A SaaS offering that takes a deployed diffusion model and a list of concepts to forget, applies TILDE-like techniques, and returns an updated model—enabling on-demand copyright/privacy compliance without retraining infrastructure.

2. **Concept auditing & erasure tools**: A tool for model owners to audit which concepts are present, visualize their impact, and selectively erase them with confidence scores—sold as a compliance/safety suite.

3. **Federated unlearning platform**: Infrastructure for content creators and rights holders to request concept removal from multiple deployed models simultaneously, with verification and logging (blockchain-backed for tamper-proof audit trails).

4. **Customized image generation platform**: A consumer app that lets users create a "personalized" version of a diffusion model by removing disliked art styles, artists, or visual concepts—"your own custom Stable Diffusion."

5. **Enterprise model governance dashboard**: B2B tool for enterprises deploying diffusion models internally, with UI to manage unlearning workflows, test retention metrics, and generate compliance reports for regulators.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Reference model assumption**: The method assumes a "retain-only" reference model is available (trained from scratch without unwanted data). In practice, obtaining this is expensive or infeasible; the paper doesn't deeply explore robustness if the reference is approximate or biased.

2. **Scalability of GFlowNet training**: Residual ∇-GFlowNet training adds computational cost beyond the pretrained model. For very large models or many concepts, amortized cost may be prohibitive.

3. **Concept specificity**: The method requires a clear *definition* of the concept to forget (e.g., training set of images of concept X). Vague concepts (e.g., "overly anime-like") may be harder to specify, and the concept definition quality directly impacts forgetting quality.

4. **Retention-forgetting tradeoff**: While the paper shows improvements over baselines, it doesn't fully characterize the hard tradeoff: how much retention quality *must* be sacrificed to achieve strong forgetting? Is there a fundamental limit?

5. **Prompt-level control**: The method forgets a concept across all prompts. Fine-grained control (e.g., "forget style X only for portraits") is not addressed.

6. **Evaluation of "benign"**: Defining what counts as "benign" generation is subjective. The paper measures retention via diversity and semantic coverage, but doesn't address potential biases in what the reference model generates.

## Open Questions
1. **Robustness to approximate references**: What happens if the reference model is trained on slightly different data or has different architecture? How sensitive is TILDE to reference model quality?

2. **Multi-concept unlearning**: How does TILDE scale when forgetting multiple concepts simultaneously? Do they interfere, or can the distributional alignment handle disjoint forget sets?

3. **Concept reactivation and backdoors**: Can forgotten concepts be reactivated via adversarial prompts or fine-tuning? Is there a formal guarantee of irreversibility?

4. **Efficiency improvements**: Can the GFlowNet training be amortized across multiple concepts, or can it be distilled into a lightweight adapter module?

5. **Theoretical analysis**: What is the sample complexity of ∇-GFlowNet training for score corrections? When does the learned distribution converge to the tilted target?

6. **Human evaluation**: Beyond metrics, do humans perceive the retained generation quality as equivalent to the reference? Are there subtle mode collapses or artifacts?

## Key Takeaway
TILDE reframes concept unlearning as matching a reference distribution under a forgetting constraint, achieving both strong erasure and strong retention by training score corrections via energy-tilted GFlowNets—a principled alternative to implicit retention in prior methods.
