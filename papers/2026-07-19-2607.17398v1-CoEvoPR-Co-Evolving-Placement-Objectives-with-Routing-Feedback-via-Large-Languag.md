# CoEvoP&R: Co-Evolving Placement Objectives with Routing Feedback via Large Language Models

**Authors:** Ruogu Chen, Weihua Xiao, Ramesh Karri, Jie Han
**Published:** 2026-07-19 | **arXiv:** [2607.17398v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.17398v1)

## Summary
This paper uses large language models to automatically design better objective functions for chip placement — a critical step in circuit design where components must be positioned on a silicon die. Instead of manually tuning placement objectives (which is time-consuming and requires expert knowledge), the authors let an LLM iteratively propose new objective functions, test them against real routing outcomes, and use that feedback to propose better ones. The result: significantly smaller, less congested chip layouts that route more successfully.

## Theoretical Background
Chip placement is traditionally solved as an optimization problem: minimize some differentiable objective (like half-perimeter wirelength — a rough estimate of wire length between connected components) subject to density constraints. Analytical placers use gradient-based optimization to move cells around the die to minimize these objectives. The core challenge is that placement-stage objectives don't perfectly align with downstream routing and timing goals — a placement that looks good by wirelength metrics may be hard to route or cause timing violations. Prior work addresses this gap either by hand-crafting additional penalty terms (labor-intensive, requires circuit design expertise) or learning black-box surrogate models (opaque, hard to integrate into existing flows). This paper sits at the intersection of [[large-language-models]] as optimization tools and circuit design automation.

## Core Contributions
1. **LLM-based objective co-evolution**: A framework where an LLM proposes human-readable, differentiable placement objectives at each generation, conditioned on routing feedback, timing metrics, and prior candidate objectives.
2. **Tight integration with real tools**: Proposed objectives are embedded in DREAMPlace (a state-of-the-art analytical placer), then evaluated against a timing proxy and actual router, closing the placement–routing feedback loop.
3. **Significant empirical gains**: 16.9% wirelength reduction, 36.7% congestion reduction, and 0.70 ns worst-case timing improvement on Nangate45 benchmarks; 5.4%–23.2% gains on ICCAD 2015 designs.
4. **Interpretability by design**: Unlike black-box surrogates, the evolved objectives remain readable and debuggable — practitioners can understand why placement changed.

## Connections to Other Work
This work bridges [[large-language-models]] with [[optimization]] in EDA (Electronic Design Automation), extending prior work on learning surrogate objectives for placement. It relates to broader trends in using LLMs for code generation and algorithm design (e.g., [[AlphaCode]]-style approaches), but grounded in a specific, high-stakes domain. The co-evolution loop (propose → test → feedback → refine) echoes reinforcement learning and evolutionary algorithms, but outsources the proposal mechanism to an LLM, treating it as an intelligent proposal generator rather than a search strategy.

## Practical Applications
1. **Automated EDA tool tuning**: Chip design teams can run CoEvoP&R once per design or process node to generate optimized placement objectives, eliminating weeks of manual parameter tuning.
2. **Design for manufacturability (DFM)**: Extended objectives could incorporate yield, power, or thermal constraints—the framework naturally supports multi-objective feedback.
3. **Cross-generational design reuse**: Archived objective candidates and their feedback could be leveraged across similar designs (e.g., within a product family), accelerating placement optimization for new chips.
4. **Hybrid human–AI workflows**: Circuit designers can review and tweak LLM-proposed objectives, using them as starting points for further refinement.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Placement-as-a-Service**: A cloud platform where chip design teams upload netlists and get back optimized placement objectives tailored to their process node and design style, reducing time-to-tape-out.
2. **LLM-powered EDA plugin**: A commercial plugin for industry tools (Cadence, Synopsys) that runs CoEvoP&R-style optimization in the background, continuously refining placement objectives as designs evolve.
3. **Design automation benchmarking service**: Sell validated, LLM-evolved objectives for public benchmarks (Nangate, OpenROAD) to academic and corporate teams, with versioning and comparative performance dashboards.
4. **Yield & power co-optimization**: Extend the framework to jointly optimize for routing, timing, yield, and power, marketed to high-volume consumer chip (mobile, IoT) teams where margin is tight.
5. **Multi-die chiplet optimizer**: Offer CoEvoP&R for advanced packaging (chiplets, interposers), where placement objectives must balance intra-die routing with inter-die signaling and thermal constraints.

## Technical Limitations
1. **LLM proposal quality depends on prompt engineering**: The framework's success relies on how well the prompt encodes the "restricted objective interface" and prior context. Poor prompts may lead to divergence rather than improvement.
2. **Scalability to huge designs**: Evaluated on Nangate45 (hundreds of thousands of cells) and Superblue (tens of thousands). It's unclear how the feedback loop scales to billion-transistor designs or whether gradient-based optimization remains tractable.
3. **Generalization across process nodes**: Models evolved for 45 nm may not transfer to 3 nm without retraining. The paper doesn't explore transfer learning or few-shot adaptation.
4. **Computational overhead**: Running the router and timing tool at every generation is expensive. The paper doesn't report total wall-clock time or compare against human tuning time.
5. **Black-box LLM dependency**: Results depend on a proprietary LLM's behavior; reproducibility and long-term maintainability depend on API stability.

## Open Questions
1. **Does the evolved objective generalize to unseen designs?** Can objectives trained on one design set transfer to a different design class (e.g., processor vs. ASIC)?
2. **What is the LLM actually learning?** Mechanistic analysis of the evolved objectives (e.g., which terms dominate, do they align with human intuition?) would strengthen interpretability claims.
3. **How does performance scale with design size and complexity?** What is the actual end-to-end runtime, and is it practical for industrial flows?
4. **Can this approach extend to multi-objective Pareto-frontiers?** Current work optimizes a single objective; real design involves timing, power, area, and yield trade-offs.
5. **How sensitive is the method to routing/timing tool accuracy?** If the proxy or router gives poor feedback, does the loop diverge? What's the robustness to noisy feedback?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs can evolve human-readable placement objectives that beat hand-tuned baselines by learning from real routing feedback.
