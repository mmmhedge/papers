# How network perturbations distort agreement trajectories in LTI multi-agent systems

**Authors:** Gal Barkai, Irinel-Constantin Morărescu
**Published:** 2026-07-21 | **arXiv:** [2607.18913v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18913v1)

## Summary
This paper studies how network disruptions (like communication delays) distort the trajectories that multi-agent systems are supposed to synchronize to. The key finding: even tiny transmission delays can completely derail a system's ability to follow periodic reference signals—a vulnerability not previously recognized in distributed coordination theory. This matters because many real systems (power grids, robotic swarms, autonomous vehicles) rely on these protocols to stay synchronized.

## Theoretical Background
Multi-agent systems use cooperative protocols where agents exchange information over a communication network to reach agreement. The classical framework assumes agents are Linear Time-Invariant (LTI) dynamical systems—meaning their behavior follows fixed linear difference or differential equations. The paper builds on consensus theory and output regulation (the ability of a system to track a prescribed reference signal asymptotically). Prior work established that static consensus is robust to heterogeneous transmission delays, but the paper challenges this by showing periodic trajectory tracking is far more fragile. The analysis uses Laplace-domain methods (frequency-domain tools for analyzing linear systems) and Laplacian matrices (graph-theoretic objects that encode network connectivity).

## Core Contributions
1. **Identification of a critical fragility**: Proves that arbitrarily small transmission delays destroy synchronization to periodic trajectories, even though static consensus remains robust. This contradicts the intuition from classical robustness results.

2. **Laplace-domain characterization**: Develops a new criterion using poles in the frequency domain to exactly predict which agreement trajectories emerge under dynamic coupling uncertainties.

3. **Distinction between perturbation types**: Formally separates structure-preserving dynamics (perturbations that preserve the null space of the network Laplacian) from transmission-only dynamics (affecting only adjacency weights). Shows they have qualitatively different robustness properties.

4. **Frequency-shift vulnerability**: Demonstrates that for regular network topologies, uniform transmission delays can shift the system to synchronize at an entirely different frequency—a qualitatively wrong outcome.

5. **Design implications**: Argues that reference generators in networked systems require fundamental structural redesign to tolerate transmission dynamics.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper extends classical [[consensus]] and [[cooperative control]] theory by exposing a gap between static agreement robustness and dynamic tracking robustness. It relates to [[robust control]] and uncertainty quantification in networked systems, but shifts focus from perturbation magnitude to *trajectory distortion*. The work connects to recent interest in fragility of distributed algorithms—similar vulnerabilities have been noted in learning and optimization but not previously in synchronization. ## Practical Applications
1. **Power grid synchronization**: Ensuring distributed generators synchronize to the 50/60 Hz grid frequency when communication links have delays; small errors cascade into blackouts.

2. **Autonomous vehicle platoons**: Maintaining synchronized motion (constant spacing, velocity) when vehicles communicate with latency; periodic reference signals are essential here.

3. **Robotic swarms**: Coordinating rhythmic behaviors (flocking, formation control with oscillatory patterns) over wireless networks with jitter and transmission delays.

4. **Satellite constellations**: Keeping orbiting satellites in synchronized orbits or periodic patterns despite radio communication delays in the control loop.

5. **Industrial process control**: Distributed control of manufacturing or chemical processes that must follow time-varying setpoints across geographically separated nodes.

2. **Adaptive reference generator hardware**: A plug-and-play device that redesigns the reference signal generation in real time based on detected network delays, maintaining intended synchronization. License to robotics and autonomous systems companies.

## Technical Limitations
1. **LTI assumption**: Real agents often have nonlinear dynamics, saturation, or time-varying parameters. Results may not extend to nonlinear systems.

2. **Continuous-time focus**: The paper primarily treats continuous dynamics; discrete-time implementations (common in networked systems) may exhibit different fragility profiles.

3. **Symmetric network structure**: Analysis often assumes regular or symmetric topologies. Highly heterogeneous or time-varying network structures are not addressed.

4. **No proposed fix**: The paper identifies vulnerabilities but does not provide a complete synthesis method for robust reference generators. Design guidance is qualitative.

5. **Measurement noise and quantization**: Assumes perfect state measurements and communication. Real systems have sensor noise and quantized data rates.

6. **Scalability**: Computational complexity of the Laplace-domain criterion for large networks is not discussed; feasibility for 1000+ agents is unclear.

## Open Questions
1. **Nonlinear extensions**: Do similar fragilities appear in nonlinear multi-agent systems or systems with consensus-based learning?

2. **Adaptive protocols**: Can agents detect and compensate for trajectory distortion in real time, or is redesign the only option?

3. **Necessary and sufficient conditions**: What network topologies or perturbation structures are *guaranteed* to preserve tracking robustness? A complete characterization is missing.

4. **Distributed redesign**: How can reference generators be synthesized in a distributed manner (without central coordination) to tolerate transmission dynamics?

5. **Trade-offs**: Is there a fundamental trade-off between robustness to static consensus, robustness to periodic tracking, and communication bandwidth? Quantify it.

6. **Time-varying networks**: How do results change if the network topology or delays themselves change over time?

## Key Takeaway
Tiny transmission delays can wreck periodic synchronization in multi-agent systems, requiring fundamental protocol redesign—not just robustness margins.
