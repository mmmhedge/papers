# Dynamic neural manifolds for flexible closed-loop control on neuromorphic hardware

**Authors:** Oskar von Seeler, Christian Tetzlaff, Andrew Lehr
**Published:** 2026-07-08 | **arXiv:** [2607.07373v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07373v1)

## Summary
This paper demonstrates how spiking neural networks (brain-inspired neural circuits that communicate via discrete spike events) can implement flexible control behaviors by organizing neural activity along dynamic, low-dimensional manifolds — geometric structures that compress high-dimensional neural activity into simpler patterns. The authors implement this on neuromorphic hardware (the SpiNNaker 2 chip), showing a robot can use sensory feedback to rapidly switch between different behaviors and navigate a maze, bridging neuroscience insights with real-time robotic control.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on two main foundations. First, neuroscience has observed that biological neural circuits achieve flexible behavior by organizing sequential activity along low-dimensional manifolds — the idea that despite having thousands of neurons, meaningful activity patterns live in a much lower-dimensional space. Second, recent work in computational neuroscience has shown that specific circuit mechanisms (particularly heterogeneous inhibition and gain modulation) can create and control these manifolds. The paper extends this to neuromorphic hardware — chips designed to mimic biological neural computation using spiking neurons (neurons that communicate via discrete time pulses) rather than the continuous-valued neurons in standard deep learning. Neuromorphic hardware offers energy efficiency and biological plausibility but is harder to program and control than conventional neural networks.

## Core Contributions
1. **Parameterizable manifold control**: The authors show how to explicitly design heterogeneous inhibition, gain, and transient currents in spiking networks to create controllable dynamic manifolds — not just observing them in trained networks, but engineering them by design.
2. **Real-time closed-loop implementation**: This is the first demonstration of dynamic manifold control running on actual neuromorphic hardware (SpiNNaker 2) with real sensory feedback, not just simulation.
3. **Behavioral switching mechanism**: They demonstrate that sensory inputs can modulate the geometry of the manifold to rapidly rotate between different behavioral subspaces, enabling both behavior switching and fine-grained trajectory control within a behavior.
4. **Explainability framework**: Unlike black-box deep learning approaches, the manifold geometry directly corresponds to interpretable circuit mechanisms, offering a more transparent model of the computation.

## Connections to Other Work
This extends prior work on [[manifold hypothesis]] in neuroscience and links to the broader field of computational neuroscience modeling. It relates to work on [[reinforcement-learning]] for control (though this is more biologically grounded), and to mechanistic interpretability efforts that seek to understand neural computation through circuit mechanisms rather than black-box learning. The use of sensory modulation for behavioral switching connects to gating and modulation in biological neural circuits. Unlike conventional deep learning approaches to robotics control, this maintains a direct correspondence to neural circuit structure, offering contrast to end-to-end trained policies.

## Practical Applications
1. **Energy-efficient robotic control**: Neuromorphic hardware consumes orders of magnitude less power than GPUs; deploying this on field robots, autonomous vehicles, or swarms could enable real-time learning in power-constrained environments.
2. **Edge inference for mobile agents**: Robotic systems that must process sensory feedback and adjust behavior without cloud connectivity (e.g., disaster response robots, autonomous drones).
3. **Biomedical implants**: Neural stimulation devices that must adapt control policies in real time based on sensory feedback while consuming minimal power.
4. **Adaptive prosthetics**: Prosthetic limbs that can switch between walking, running, and stair-climbing by dynamically reconfiguring their control manifold based on sensory context.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Neuromorphic Robot Middleware**: A software framework that lets roboticists design manifold-based controllers for neuromorphic chips without deep expertise in spiking networks — offering explainability and energy efficiency over standard RL.
2. **Energy-Efficient Edge AI for IoT**: Deploy low-power, adaptive control on resource-constrained IoT devices (e.g., agricultural drones, environmental sensors) that need to switch behaviors based on sensory input.
3. **Biologically-Faithful Neural Simulation Platform**: A commercial tool for neuroscience labs to rapidly prototype and deploy neural circuit models on hardware, bridging the gap between simulation and wetware.
4. **Adaptive Prosthetics/Exoskeleton Controller**: Hardware + firmware for prosthetics that dynamically reconfigure their control strategy based on user intent and environmental feedback, marketed to orthopedic device manufacturers.
5. **Neuromorphic AI Chips for Autonomous Systems**: Partner with chip manufacturers to produce optimized neuromorphic processors for autonomous vehicles, packaged with pre-trained manifold-based controllers for common driving behaviors.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability**: The paper demonstrates maze navigation; it's unclear how this scales to high-dimensional sensory inputs (raw images, LiDAR) or complex, multi-objective control tasks.
2. **Manifold design**: The approach requires hand-designing which circuit parameters (inhibition, gain, transients) to modulate; there's no automated method for manifold design from task specifications — it's more engineering than learning.
3. **Hardware specificity**: The implementation is tied to SpiNNaker 2; generalizing to other neuromorphic platforms (e.g., Intel Loihi, BrainScaleS) is not addressed.
4. **Learning from scratch**: The paper doesn't show how to *learn* manifold parameters end-to-end; the control gains and circuit parameters appear hand-tuned or pre-trained, limiting adaptability.
5. **Stability and robustness**: Robustness to sensory noise, unseen environments, and perturbations is not thoroughly characterized — the validation is a single simulated maze task.
6. **Comparison baselines**: No direct comparison to standard RL or other neuromorphic approaches on the same task, making it hard to assess computational advantage.

## Open Questions
1. Can manifold geometry be *learned* automatically from task specifications, or does it require hand-design for each behavior?
2. How does this scale to high-dimensional sensory inputs (vision, audio) and complex multi-objective tasks?
3. What is the actual energy consumption and latency compared to GPU-based RL on realistic robotic platforms?
4. Can this approach be combined with learning to adapt manifold parameters online during deployment?
5. How robust is the approach to sensory noise, perturbations, and distribution shift to new environments?
6. Can the same framework handle hierarchical or compositional behaviors (e.g., multiple levels of behavior switching)?

## Key Takeaway
Dynamic neural manifolds on neuromorphic hardware offer a path to explainable, energy-efficient robot control by explicitly encoding behaviors as geometric structures in spiking networks rather than learning black-box policies.
