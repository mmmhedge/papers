# Minimax Theory of Likelihood-Based Deep Learning for Speckle Regression

**Authors:** Soham Jana
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.14064v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14064v1)

## Summary
This paper studies the theoretical statistical limits of deep neural networks for removing speckle noise — a type of multiplicative noise common in radar and medical imaging. The authors prove that likelihood-based deep learning methods can achieve optimal estimation rates, and surprisingly, the difficulty of this problem is essentially the same as standard regression with only additive noise, despite multiplicative noise being fundamentally harder to work with.

## Theoretical Background
Speckle noise arises in coherent imaging systems (synthetic aperture radar, optical coherence tomography, digital holography) where light or waves interfere to create grainy patterns. Unlike additive Gaussian noise, speckle multiplies the true signal — making standard least-squares regression problematic because the true signal cannot be recovered from the conditional mean alone. Minimax theory provides a framework for determining the fundamental limits of any estimator: it finds the fastest rate at which estimation error must shrink as sample size grows, and shows when particular algorithms (like DNNs) achieve this limit. Prior work established minimax rates for additive-noise regression and developed deep learning methods that achieve them, but the theory for multiplicative noise remained open.

## Core Contributions
1. **Likelihood-based DNN framework**: Proposes using maximum likelihood estimation with DNNs rather than least-squares, which is essential for identifiability under multiplicative noise.
2. **Finite-sample upper bounds**: Proves convergence rates for the DNN estimator with explicit dependence on sample size, dimension, and smoothness.
3. **Matching minimax lower bounds**: Derives fundamental limits showing any estimator must be at least this slow, and proves the upper and lower bounds match (up to log factors).
4. **Surprising theoretical result**: Demonstrates that multiplicative speckle noise does not increase minimax difficulty compared to additive-only noise, resolving a gap between intuition and theory.
5. **Sparse high-dimensional setting**: Extends results to sparse features where dimension can exceed sample size.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper sits at the intersection of **statistical learning theory** and **deep learning**. It extends classical nonparametric statistics (minimax estimation under smoothness assumptions) to modern DNNs, similar to recent work proving that [[empirical risk minimization]] with neural networks achieves optimal rates for simpler models. The use of likelihood-based objectives connects to [[maximum likelihood estimation]] in statistics and relates to information-theoretic lower bounds. The result that multiplicative noise does not worsen rates parallels findings in robust statistics where certain noise structures do not fundamentally increase problem difficulty.

## Practical Applications
1. **Synthetic aperture radar (SAR) image denoising**: Removes speckle from radar imagery for improved land-use classification, geological surveying, and disaster monitoring.
2. **Optical coherence tomography (OCT) enhancement**: Cleans up medical imaging for ophthalmology and cardiology applications.
3. **Digital holography reconstruction**: Improves quality of phase-recovered holograms in microscopy and 3D imaging.
4. **Ultrasound image processing**: Many ultrasound systems exhibit multiplicative speckle; this theory guides denoising algorithm design.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Smoothness assumption**: Theory assumes the true regression function belongs to a Hölder-smooth function class; real signals may have sharper edges or discontinuities.
2. **DNN architecture not fully specified**: Paper does not fully optimize over architecture choices (depth, width, activation); optimal rates may depend on these details.
3. **Logarithmic gaps**: Upper and lower bounds match only up to log factors, leaving a small gap; the exact constant factors are unknown.
4. **Finite-sample vs. asymptotic**: Theory describes behavior as sample size grows; finite regimes with practical sizes may not exhibit these rates.
5. **Model assumptions**: Assumes knowledge of noise distribution (multiplicative + additive Gaussian); real speckle may deviate from this model.
6. **Computational cost not addressed**: Paper proves rates but does not analyze training time, memory, or computational complexity.

## Open Questions
1. Can the logarithmic gaps in minimax bounds be closed? Are there architectural choices that eliminate them?
2. How do these results extend to non-smooth functions, edges, or piecewise-constant signals common in real imaging?
3. What happens when the noise model is misspecified — e.g., speckle deviates from the assumed distribution?
4. Can adaptive methods that learn the smoothness level achieve the same rates without knowing it a priori?
5. How do these bounds change for very high-dimensional or infinite-dimensional settings (e.g., functional data)?
6. Can you combine this theory with [[RLHF]] or other learning paradigms to handle perceptual losses where human feedback guides denoising?

## Key Takeaway
Deep learning can denoise speckled images optimally—multiplicative noise is statistically no harder than additive noise alone.
