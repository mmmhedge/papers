# Scaling the Horizon, Not the Parameters: Reaching Trillion-Parameter Performance with a 35B Agent

**Authors:** Lei Bai, Zongsheng Cao, Yang Chen, Zhiyao Cui, Shangheng Du et al.
**Published:** 2026-06-29 | **arXiv:** [2606.30616v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30616v1)

## Summary
Agents-A1 is a 35B Mixture-of-Experts agentic model that achieves trillion-parameter-level performance by scaling the agent's decision-making horizon rather than model size. The key innovation is using long-horizon trajectories (averaging 45K tokens) with heterogeneous agent abilities, trained via multi-teacher domain-routed distillation, to match or exceed the performance of trillion-parameter models like Kimi-K2.6 and DeepSeek-V4-pro on complex long-horizon reasoning and scientific tasks.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on several streams of research:

**Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** models, which route different inputs to specialized subnetworks, enabling parameter-efficient scaling without proportional compute increases.

**Agentic reasoning** — the paradigm where language models operate as autonomous agents that iteratively perceive environments, take actions, receive observations, and refine plans over extended sequences. This extends beyond single-turn generation to multi-step problem-solving.

**Knowledge-action trajectories** — structured sequences that interleave external knowledge retrieval, executable actions, environment observations, and verification signals. This is related to [[Chain-of-Thought]] prompting but substantially more structured.

**Horizon scaling** — the observation that agent performance can improve dramatically by extending the depth of reasoning and decision steps, rather than increasing raw parameter count. This contrasts with traditional "model scaling" (more weights).

**On-policy distillation** — a training technique where student models learn from teacher models trained on the same distribution, often more efficient than offline distillation.

## Core Contributions

1. **Long-horizon infrastructure**: A systematic framework for constructing agentic trajectories that integrate external knowledge, actions, observations, and verifications, achieving 45K-token average sequences — substantially longer than typical LLM reasoning contexts.

2. **Horizon-scaling thesis**: Empirical demonstration that scaling trajectory depth/complexity yields trillion-parameter-equivalent performance from a 35B model, challenging the assumption that parameter count is the primary scaling axis for agentic tasks.

3. **Multi-teacher domain-routed distillation**: A novel training recipe that:
   - Trains specialized domain-level teachers (capturing expertise in, e.g., scientific reasoning, code generation, web search)
   - Routes training examples to appropriate teachers
   - Uses "salient vocabulary alignment" (presumably aligning embeddings for domain-specific terms) to improve cross-domain knowledge transfer
   - Consolidates six heterogeneous domains into a single deployable model

4. **Three-stage training recipe**: Full-domain supervised fine-tuning → domain-level teacher training → multi-teacher domain-routed on-policy distillation, providing a reproducible path for others.

5. **Benchmarking across 8 diverse long-horizon tasks**: Demonstrating state-of-the-art or competitive results on SEAL-0, IFBench, HiPhO, FrontierScience-Olympiad, MolBench-Bind, SciCode, HLE, and BrowseComp.

## Connections to Other Work

This paper relates to:

- **Parameter-efficient scaling**: Like [[LoRA]] and [[QLoRA]], it challenges the need for massive parameter counts, but via a different axis (horizon/trajectory depth rather than low-rank adaptation).
- **Mixture-of-Experts scaling**: Extends MoE beyond simple load-balancing to domain-aware routing with teacher-student dynamics.
- **Agentic LLMs**: Follows in the tradition of [[ReAct]], [[Toolformer]], and similar work that gave models the ability to use tools and reason iteratively, but operates at much longer horizons.
- **Distillation and knowledge transfer**: Related to [[DistilBERT]] and modern distillation work, but applied to heterogeneous domains with salient-vocabulary alignment.
- **Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)** and [[on-policy training]]: The on-policy distillation step shares conceptual roots with RL-based alignment, though the details differ.
- **Scientific reasoning benchmarks**: Competes with models like AlphaProof and other systems designed for formal reasoning, code, and scientific tasks.

## Practical Applications

1. **Scientific research acceleration**: Agents-A1 can autonomously execute experimental design, literature search, hypothesis generation, and validation — reducing researcher iteration time on problems like molecular binding prediction (MolBench-Bind) or formal proof verification (FrontierScience-Olympiad).

2. **Autonomous web search and information synthesis**: On BrowseComp and similar tasks, the agent can navigate websites, aggregate information, and produce structured summaries without human direction.

3. **Code generation and debugging**: Long-horizon reasoning enables multi-step debugging and refactoring; deploying a 35B model is far cheaper than trillion-parameter alternatives for enterprise development tools.

4. **Dynamic knowledge retrieval systems**: The knowledge-action infrastructure supports real-time fact-checking and grounding, critical for applications requiring up-to-date information (news generation, compliance systems).

5. **Domain-specific vertical AI applications**: The multi-teacher domain-routed approach enables building specialized agents (medical reasoning, legal discovery, manufacturing control) without training monolithic trillion-parameter models.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **Autonomous research assistant SaaS**: Sell a fine-tuned instance of Agents-A1 (or the recipe) to biotech and materials science labs; it autonomously designs experiments, runs simulations, and writes lab reports. Revenue: per-user subscription + API calls.

2. **Edge-deployed agentic reasoning**: A 35B model fits on a single GPU or edge cluster; offer on-premises agentic AI to enterprise clients (hospitals, law firms, manufacturing) who need long-horizon reasoning without cloud latency or data exfiltration.

3. **Domain-specialized agent marketplace**: Productize the multi-teacher distillation recipe; allow customers to train domain-specific agents (insurance claims processing, HR automation, customer support) and host them as microservices.

4. **Real-time code co-pilot**: Integrate Agents-A1 into IDEs; leverage long-horizon context to suggest multi-file refactorings and architectural changes (vs. line-by-line suggestions from smaller models).

5. **Autonomous data analysis platform**: Users upload datasets and questions; the agent performs EDA, hypothesis testing, visualization, and causal inference — all via long-horizon reasoning. Target: business intelligence and academic research teams.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Trajectory length stability**: 45K-token sequences are near the limits of many hardware implementations (attention complexity, memory). Inference latency and cost may be prohibitive in latency-sensitive applications.

2. **Domain assumptions**: The multi-teacher approach assumes you can cleanly partition the problem space into 6 domains. Real-world tasks often have messy boundaries; generalization to new, unlabeled domains is unclear.

3. **Verification signal dependency**: The infrastructure relies on "verifier outcomes" (e.g., checking if code runs, if a proof is valid). For ill-defined tasks (creative writing, open-ended brainstorming), this breaks down.

4. **Comparison fairness**: Comparison with 1T models (Kimi-K2.6, DeepSeek-V4-pro) may not be apples-to-apples if those models aren't fine-tuned for long-horizon tasks or released with known architectural differences. Relative performance on standard benchmarks (not agentic) would clarify.

5. **Salient vocabulary alignment**: The paper mentions this technique but does not deeply explain how it works or how sensitive results are to vocabulary selection. This is a potential bottleneck in transfer across domains.

6. **Scalability of on-policy distillation**: Collecting 45K-token on-policy trajectories is expensive. How does performance degrade if you scale down trajectory length or data volume?

## Open Questions

1. **Does horizon scaling continue beyond 45K tokens?** What is the saturation point? Is there a principled way to allocate a "compute budget" between trajectory length and model size?

2. **How much of the performance gain comes from each stage?** Ablations on the three-stage recipe (especially the contribution of domain-level teachers vs. salient vocabulary alignment) would clarify where to invest engineering effort.

3. **Cross-domain generalization**: How does the agent perform on tasks that don't fit cleanly into the six domains? Can the learned routing mechanism transfer to novel domains?
