# The Large Cancer Assistant (LCA): A Model-Agnostic Orchestration Framework for Scalable Clinical Decision Support in Oncology

**Authors:** Ghassen Marrakchi, Basarab Matei
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06531v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06531v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces the Large Cancer Assistant (LCA), a framework that orchestrates multiple AI models for clinical oncology decision support without being tightly coupled to any specific model. The key innovation is architectural: by decoupling data intake, routing logic, and AI inference, the system can swap out models, handle incomplete data gracefully, and integrate with hospital IT systems more flexibly than traditional monolithic approaches.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on several foundational concepts. **Geometric Deep Learning (GDL)** — a framework that applies deep learning principles to non-Euclidean data structures — is used to standardize heterogeneous patient data. The paper introduces **Algorithmic Impermeability**, a design principle ensuring that orchestration logic (the "traffic controller") remains independent of the black-box AI models it manages, analogous to middleware in software architecture. The framework is formalized as a 7-tuple mathematical structure, drawing on systems theory and formal specifications common in safety-critical systems engineering. The approach also implicitly addresses the **interpretability problem** in clinical AI: by isolating core inference in a "Standardized Intermediate Payload (SIP)", the system creates architectural transparency even when underlying models remain opaque.

## Core Contributions
1. **Model-agnostic orchestration architecture**: Unlike systems built around specific models (e.g., a particular transformer), the LCA can swap models without rewriting routing or data ingestion logic.
2. **Entry Theory via GDL**: A principled way to normalize multimodal oncology data (imaging, pathology reports, lab results, genetics) into a unified representation space.
3. **Failure-safety mechanisms**: The Cancer Switching Module actively detects data anomalies and generates Supplementary Data Requests (SDRs) with 100% recall in the PoC, rather than silently degrading.
4. **Standardized Intermediate Payload (SIP)**: A deliberate architectural boundary that decouples inference outputs from downstream EMR systems, enabling future interoperability.
5. **Empirical validation of impermeability**: The PoC demonstrated that routing projections remain invariant when swapping AI models.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to broader trends in clinical AI: the shift from monolithic end-to-end models toward **modular, composable systems** (similar to microservices in software engineering). It echoes concerns about [[mechanistic-interpretability]] — if you can't open the black box, design the system around it rather than within it. The work also connects to **multi-model ensemble orchestration** in [[large-language-models]] and [[agents]], where routing decisions must be decoupled from individual model capabilities. From a healthcare angle, it addresses long-standing interoperability challenges with Electronic Medical Records (EMRs), a classic pain point in digital health.

## Practical Applications
1. **Multi-institutional oncology networks**: A hospital system can deploy LCA as a hub, allowing different sites to plug in their preferred AI models (radiology AI from vendor A, pathology AI from vendor B) without rebuilding the orchestration layer.
2. **Model upgrades without workflow disruption**: When a better cancer detection model becomes available, swap it in via the SIP interface; clinicians' workflows remain unchanged.
3. **Regulatory compliance and audit trails**: The SIP creates a clear, standardized record of what data entered the AI system and in what form, simplifying regulatory review.
4. **Hybrid human-AI workflows**: The SDR mechanism can flag incomplete or ambiguous cases for manual review, enabling safe handoffs between AI and oncologists.
5. **Multi-protocol clinical trials**: Different trial arms can run different AI backends while sharing the same data orchestration layer, reducing trial complexity.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Oncology Integration Platform (OIP)**: A SaaS product that hospitals license to connect their existing AI vendors (radiology, pathology, genetics) and EMRs without custom integration work. Charge per institution + per model integration.
2. **AI Model Marketplace for Oncology**: An app store where certified AI models (from startups, academic centers, or vendors) can be listed and plugged into LCA-compatible systems. Take a 20-30% revenue share.
3. **Clinical Decision Support as a Service**: A managed cloud service where LCA runs orchestration, hospitals send patient data, and they get structured clinical recommendations. Charge per consultation or per patient.
4. **Compliance & Audit SaaS**: Build on the SIP's standardization to offer hospitals automated regulatory compliance reporting (FDA, HIPAA, state-level regulations). Position as "AI governance for healthcare."
5. **Multi-Modal Data Standardization Toolkit**: Sell Entry Theory (GDL-based normalization) as a standalone library for healthcare AI teams to ingest diverse oncology data sources. Freemium model with premium support.

## Technical Limitations
1. **PoC vs. production**: The validation is a proof-of-concept; real-world performance under high load, network failures, or adversarial inputs remains untested. Oncology systems demand 99.99%+ uptime.
2. **Model-agnostic abstraction cost**: Creating a routing layer that works with any model may introduce latency overhead or require lossy data compression in the SIP, potentially degrading model performance.
3. **Generalization beyond oncology unclear**: The framework is tailored to oncology (cancer-specific data modalities, clinical workflows). Applicability to cardiology, neurology, etc. is not established.
4. **Data quality assumptions**: The system assumes incoming data can be meaningfully normalized via GDL. Severely corrupted or proprietary data formats may violate this assumption.
5. **No comparison to alternatives**: The paper does not benchmark against simpler orchestration approaches (e.g., traditional ensemble voting, rule-based routing) or other clinical decision support frameworks.
6. **Downstream EMR integration speculative**: The paper positions SIP as enabling EMR interoperability but does not demonstrate actual integration; this remains future work.

## Open Questions
1. How does orchestration latency scale with the number of AI models and data modalities? Is there a practical ceiling?
2. Can Entry Theory generalize to non-oncology medical domains, or is it cancer-specific?
3. What happens when two AI models in the orchestration produce contradictory recommendations? How should the system adjudicate?
4. How does performance degrade with realistic hospital data (noisy, incomplete, inconsistent labeling) vs. the clean PoC scenarios?
5. Can you formally prove algorithmic impermeability, or is it only empirically validated?
6. What is the human cognitive load for clinicians interpreting an orchestrated multi-model output vs. a single model's output?

## Key Takeaway
By decoupling orchestration logic from AI inference via a standardized intermediate layer, LCA enables oncology systems to swap and compose models flexibly while maintaining safety and auditability — solving a critical bottleneck in clinical AI deployment.
