# When Summaries Distort Decisions: Information Fidelity in LLM-Compressed Financial Analysis

**Authors:** Hoyoung Lee, Suhwan Park, Seunghan Lee, Jun Seo, Jaehoon Lee et al.
**Published:** 2026-06-28 | **arXiv:** [2606.29251v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29251v1)

## Summary
This paper identifies a critical problem in financial decision-making: when LLMs compress financial documents (earnings calls, SEC filings), they can produce fluent summaries that are factually correct but nevertheless alter the investment decisions those documents should support. The authors propose Agentic Context Compression, which generates multiple compressions and cross-validates them against the original source to preserve decision-relevant information.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two threads: (1) **information fidelity** — the idea that compression is only useful if it preserves the decision-relevant signal from the source; and (2) **context window limitations** in LLMs — transformers (neural networks using attention mechanisms to process sequences) have finite context, forcing summarization when analyzing long documents. Traditional compression metrics like ROUGE or factual accuracy don't capture whether a summary preserves the original's *decision implications*. The work also engages with **agentic systems** — multi-step reasoning pipelines where an LLM makes decisions, passes results to another step, and so on — where small fidelity losses can cascade and compound.

## Core Contributions
1. **Fidelity Loss Framework**: Operationalizes information fidelity as "does this compression preserve the investment decision the source would induce?" — distinct from factual correctness or readability.
2. **Two Diagnostic Patterns**: Identifies decontextualization (salient evidence retained but separated from caveats) and model dependency (different LLM compressors yield different decision-relevant insights from the same source).
3. **Agentic Context Compression Algorithm**: A multi-candidate approach that generates k compressions, evaluates disagreements against the original, and selectively retains decision-preserving context rather than blindly truncating.
4. **Empirical Validation**: Demonstrates fidelity loss across real financial documents and shows the method recovers decision alignment better than single-pass compression.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to [[Attention Is All You Need]] and the transformer context-window problem. It extends debates in [[RLHF]] and [[alignment]] by showing that even factually-aligned summaries can misalign with downstream human decisions. The multi-agent auditing component echoes work on [[multi-agent-systems]] and debate-based verification. It also touches [[mechanistic-interpretability]] implicitly — understanding *why* a compressor makes decisions differently than the source would help debug fidelity loss.

## Practical Applications
1. **Robo-advisor platforms**: Compress earnings transcripts for automated stock recommendations; fidelity loss directly impacts portfolio returns and regulatory liability.
2. **Institutional research automation**: Sell research summaries that preserve buy/sell signals from original 10-K filings; clients pay premium for decision-preserving compression.
3. **Compliance monitoring**: Regulatory bodies use LLM summaries of disclosure documents; fidelity loss could hide material risks (e.g., risk factor warnings dropped in summarization).
4. **Credit analysis at banks**: Compress loan application documents; fidelity loss leads to bad credit decisions and loan losses.
5. **Hedge fund data pipelines**: Real-time compression of news, filings, and earnings calls for factor exposure signals; fidelity directly affects alpha decay.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **FidelityAI**: A compression-as-a-service platform specifically for financial institutions. Generates multiple candidate compressions and audits them, returning a "fidelity score" and explanation of how each compression might shift investment decisions. Charge per-document or per-decision-audit.
2. **FinDocs Validator**: Browser extension or API that highlights segments of an LLM-generated investment summary that diverge from the original source. Targets retail investors and small funds skeptical of AI summaries.
3. **RiskPreserver**: Specialized for regulatory documents (10-K, 10-Q). Ensures that risk disclosures, litigation warnings, and material adverse clauses survive summarization. Sell to compliance departments and credit rating agencies.
4. **AuditChain for Financial AI**: General audit layer for financial decision systems. Monitors whether LLM-compressed inputs to buy/sell algorithms preserve the original information. Includes explainability reports for regulators.
5. **FidelityBench**: Benchmark suite and leaderboard for evaluating financial compression models on decision-preservation rather than just factuality or efficiency. Monetize via licensing, consulting, and model-builder partnerships.

## Technical Limitations
1. **"Decision" is implicit**: The paper operationalizes decision through human annotation (presumably buy/hold/sell labels), but in the real world, different investors weight evidence differently. What counts as fidelity-preserving for a value investor may differ from a momentum trader.
2. **Scalability of multi-candidate generation**: Agentic Context Compression requires k separate compressions. As k grows, cost and latency grow; the paper doesn't discuss computational trade-offs or optimal k.
3. **Limited evaluation scope**: Tested on earnings calls and filings, but financial decisions also depend on macroeconomic context, competitor actions, and sentiment. Single-document compression may not capture these interactions.
4. **Baseline comparisons**: The paper doesn't clearly compare against simpler alternatives (e.g., extractive summarization, domain-specific templates) or against human-written summaries of the same documents.
5. **No temporal analysis**: Financial decisions depend on timeliness. If compression introduces delays, even perfect fidelity may not preserve value in fast-moving markets.

## Open Questions
1. How does fidelity loss compound across chained agentic steps? Can you quantify the error propagation and recommend maximum chain depth?
2. Can we build a parametric model of when/why a compressor will introduce fidelity loss, so we can predict it *before* generation rather than auditing after?
3. Does fine-tuning an LLM explicitly on decision-fidelity (e.g., RLHF where reward is "does compressed → original agree on investment action?") outperform post-hoc auditing?
4. How do fidelity losses differ across asset classes (equities vs. bonds vs. derivatives) and document types?
5. Can a smaller, specialized model (e.g., a distilled BERT) compress financial documents with higher fidelity than larger general-purpose LLMs, and at what inference cost?

## Key Takeaway
LLM-compressed financial summaries can be fluent and factually correct while still altering the investment decisions they should support, and multi-candidate auditing can recover lost decision-relevant context.
