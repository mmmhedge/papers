# Unified Complex-valued Neural Network: A Magnitude-Phase Computational Model for Event-Driven Neuromorphic Learning

**Authors:** Reza Ahmadvand, Sarah Safura Sharif, Yaser Mike Banad
**Published:** 2026-06-27 | **arXiv:** [2606.29099v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29099v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes a new type of artificial neuron called the Unified Complex-valued Neuron (UCN) that combines two complementary neural computing approaches: the accuracy of traditional neural networks and the efficiency of event-driven spiking neural networks. The key innovation is using complex numbers (with magnitude and phase components) to simultaneously encode signal strength and timing information, then train both pathways together. This matters because current systems must choose between accuracy or energy efficiency—this work aims to get both.

## Theoretical Background
The paper bridges two established neural computing paradigms. Traditional artificial neural networks (ANNs) use continuous activation functions and backpropagation for supervised learning, providing high accuracy but requiring constant computation. Spiking neural networks (SNNs) process information as discrete temporal events (spikes), mimicking biological brains and enabling sparse, energy-efficient computation on neuromorphic hardware—but they're harder to train and often less accurate.

Complex-valued neural networks extend this further by representing neuron states as complex numbers rather than real numbers, allowing separate representation of magnitude (amplitude) and phase (angle). The paper's innovation is using this representation to let magnitude handle signal encoding while phase controls the timing of when a neuron "fires" or generates an event. The training combines backpropagation (BP)—the standard algorithm for updating weights in ANNs—with backpropagation through time (BPTT), which extends BP to handle sequential/temporal dependencies in recurrent networks.

## Core Contributions
1. **Unified Complex-valued Neuron (UCN) architecture**: A novel neuron model where magnitude encodes continuous information and phase governs both intrinsic temporal dynamics and spike generation, creating a mathematically unified structure.

2. **Dual training framework**: First, a complete backpropagation + BPTT approach that jointly optimizes magnitude and phase pathways, then a computationally lighter Event-driven Adaptive Phase Learning (EAPL) rule that reduces complexity while maintaining performance.

3. **Interpretability and stability**: Demonstrates that the approach yields interpretable spatiotemporal representations with empirically verified stability properties—useful for safety-critical applications.

4. **Sparse neuromorphic efficiency**: Preserves the event-driven, low-power computation benefits of SNNs while recovering the accuracy and learnability of ANNs.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of several active research areas:

- **Spiking neural networks**: Extends the biological realism and energy efficiency narrative of SNNs, but addresses a core challenge—training accuracy—through a hybrid approach rather than relying solely on SNN-specific learning rules.

- **Complex-valued networks**: Builds on prior work using complex numbers in neural networks for phase encoding; the novel contribution is grounding this in event-driven computation.

- **Neuromorphic computing**: Directly targets the edge-AI and hardware-efficient deployment problem that motivates neuromorphic hardware (e.g., Intel Loihi, IBM TrueNorth).

- **Temporal learning and RNNs**: Related to LSTM and GRU architectures that also try to learn long-range temporal patterns, but with an explicit phase-driven event mechanism.

- **Phase encoding in neural systems**: Connects to neuroscience literature on oscillatory dynamics and theta-gamma coupling in biological neural circuits.

## Practical Applications
1. **Mobile/embedded vision**: Real-time object tracking on edge devices (phones, drones, robots) where power consumption is critical and latency matters.

2. **Neuromorphic hardware deployment**: Efficient inference on specialized chips (Intel Loihi, SpiNNaker) that natively support event-based processing.

3. **Time-series anomaly detection**: Detecting faults or anomalies in industrial IoT sensors or financial market data where both accuracy and computational efficiency are essential.

4. **Autonomous vehicles**: Low-power, real-time processing of event camera data (which natively output asynchronous events rather than frames).

5. **Bio-signal processing**: ECG, EEG, or EMG classification on wearable devices where power budgets are tight and interpretability is valuable.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Training complexity and convergence**: While EAPL is more efficient than full BP+BPTT, the paper does not provide detailed analysis of convergence rates, local minima, or sensitivity to initialization—critical for practitioners.

2. **Limited experimental validation**: Only tested on two tasks (object tracking and Lorenz attractor learning). Generalization to other domains (language, large-scale vision, control) is undemonstrated.

3. **Phase interpretation**: The claim that phase "governs intrinsic temporal evolution" lacks rigorous theoretical justification. What ensures phase remains stable and interpretable across training?

4. **Hardware deployment gap**: The paper claims neuromorphic efficiency but doesn't benchmark actual power/latency on real hardware—only computational complexity in theory.

5. **Scalability questions**: No discussion of how UCNNs scale to deep networks or large problem sizes. Recurrent complex-valued networks can be numerically unstable.

6. **Comparison with baselines**: Limited head-to-head comparison with state-of-the-art SNN training methods (e.g., surrogate gradient methods) or hybrid approaches.

## Open Questions
1. How does UCNN performance scale with network depth and width? Are there depth-dependent stability issues?

2. Can UCNNs outperform standard SNNs and ANNs on standard benchmarks (ImageNet, CIFAR, DVS-CIFAR) with fair computational budgets?

3. What is the theoretical foundation for phase stability? Can you prove bounds on phase drift under learning dynamics?

4. How does EAPL compare empirically to other efficient SNN learning rules (e.g., spike-timing-dependent plasticity, threshold-adaptive learning)?

5. Can UCNNs be trained end-to-end on larger-scale temporal tasks (speech, video, reinforcement learning)?

6. Does the interpretability claim hold quantitatively? How do you measure "interpretability" of phase and magnitude across layers?

## Key Takeaway
Complex-valued neurons can unify continuous signal encoding with event-driven timing by using magnitude and phase as separate computational pathways, enabling accurate yet energy-efficient learning for neuromorphic systems.
