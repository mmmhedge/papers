# Signed-Permutation Coordinate Transport for RMSNorm Transformers

**Authors:** John Sweeney
**Published:** 2026-06-30 | **arXiv:** [2606.31963v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31963v1)

## Summary

This paper solves a critical technical problem in modern LLM workflows: how to correctly move interpretability tools (like steering vectors and sparse autoencoders) between different model checkpoints. The authors show that the mathematical symmetry group governing this transport depends on the model's normalization layer — RMSNorm models have a larger symmetry group than LayerNorm models — and permutation-only alignment misses half the structure, leading to broken steering effects and interpretability tools. By tracking both permutations *and* per-channel sign flips, they recover 91% of coordinates across fine-tuning runs versus 60% with existing methods, and restore the functionality of tools that currently fail under standard alignment.

## Theoretical Background

Modern LLMs process data through a sequence of layers, each producing a "residual stream" — a high-dimensional vector that carries information forward. When researchers want to interpret or steer these models, they extract objects from checkpoints (saved model states) and try to apply them to other checkpoints. This requires a consistent coordinate system, or "gauge" — analogous to choosing a coordinate frame in physics.

The paper builds on [[Attention Is All You Need]] transformers and recent mechanistic interpretability work using [[sparse autoencoders]] and steering vectors. It imports gauge theory from physics: the residual stream has redundant symmetries — you can permute coordinates without changing the model's input-output function. LayerNorm (a normalization technique that standardizes activations) preserves only permutations; RMSNorm (a simpler normalization using root-mean-square scaling) *also* preserves independent sign flips per channel because its gain parameters are multiplicative. The authors formalize this as a symmetry group: $S_d$ (permutations) for LayerNorm, and $B_d = S_d \ltimes \{\pm 1\}^d$ (signed permutations) for RMSNorm.

## Core Contributions

1. **Formal characterization of RMSNorm gauge structure**: Proof that RMSNorm residual streams have signed-permutation symmetry ($B_d$), not just permutation symmetry ($S_d$), making standard Hungarian matching incomplete.

2. **Sign-marginalized Hungarian matching algorithm**: A principled way to compute optimal coordinate alignment that respects both permutations and signs. Shows that raw signed-correlation matching has a theoretical ceiling (permutation accuracy ≤ fraction of positive signs in true gauge) that sign-marginalization removes.

3. **Coordinate-preserving transport along fine-tuning trajectories**: Rather than matching endpoints, compose local $B_d$ gauges along the optimization path. Recovers 91.1% of coordinates at 1500 steps versus 60.3% for endpoint matching.

4. **Empirical validation of tool portability**: Demonstrates that [[sparse autoencoders]] reconstruction, sentiment steering, and refusal steering all break under permutation-only alignment but work under signed-permutation alignment. Shows AdamW optimizer state must also be transported via signed-permutation to preserve training trajectories.

5. **Gauge-dependent interpretability audits**: Proves that index-level interpretability claims (e.g., "neuron X encodes sentiment") are only reproducible relative to an explicit gauge choice — a methodological insight for the field.

## Connections to Other Work

This work directly extends [[mechanistic interpretability]] methods like circuit analysis and [[sparse autoencoders]]. It critiques and improves upon permutation-matching approaches used in model merging (e.g., Git Re-Basin) and checkpoint alignment. The gauge-theoretic framing relates to symmetries in neural network loss landscapes and recent work on permutation-invariance in model merging.

The coordinate transport problem also connects to [[RLHF]] and fine-tuning: when you continue training a model, you're implicitly changing the gauge; the paper shows this has measurable consequences for optimizer state and tool behavior. The work sits at the intersection of mechanistic interpretability, model editing, and geometric deep learning.

## Practical Applications

1. **Reliable model steering**: Practitioners building steering vectors for factuality, safety, or style can now port them across checkpoints without loss — critical for production systems fine-tuning on proprietary data.

2. **Sparse autoencoder feature transfer**: SAEs trained on one checkpoint can be reliably used on derivatives (quantized versions, fine-tuned variants), enabling cost-effective interpretability tools.

3. **Safe model merging**: Multi-model merging (e.g., combining specialized LoRAs) currently risks misalignment. Signed-permutation alignment ensures merged weights behave consistently.

4. **Reproducible mechanistic analysis**: Teams auditing model internals (e.g., to verify refusal behavior) can now document and audit their gauge choice, making findings reproducible across labs.

5. **Efficient checkpoint interpolation**: Fine-tuning transparency and continuous model updates benefit from correct coordinate alignment along the trajectory.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **Steering-as-a-Service platform**: Hosted API that accepts a user's steering vector and fine-tuned model checkpoint, automatically discovers the correct gauge via sign-marginalized matching, and applies the steering. Charge per checkpoint or per steering operation.

2. **Interpretability audit software**: SaaS tool for AI safety teams to verify that mechanistic claims (e.g., "this neuron fires on harmful content") hold across model versions and gauges. Includes gauge-sweep reports showing robustness.

3. **Model merge optimizer**: Commercial tool for enterprises merging multiple fine-tuned models (different domains, languages, tasks). Uses signed-permutation alignment to guarantee functional equivalence and reduced inference costs.

4. **Continuous steering debugger**: IDE-like tool for LLM engineers: load a model, probe its steering vectors, see live gauge diagnostics, run gauge-aware sweeps to test interpretability claims. Real-time feedback on steering effectiveness.

5. **Fine-tuning trajectory analyst**: Post-training analytics platform that tracks how coordinate structure evolves during fine-tuning, predicting which steering vectors will transfer and flagging gauge incompatibilities before they cause production issues.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Computational cost**: Sign-marginalized Hungarian matching is O(d³) in residual dimension (typically 4k–32k). Scaling to very large models or real-time gauge discovery may be infeasible.

2. **Trajectory composition assumptions**: The method assumes checkpoints share a "same-base" fine-tuning history. Merging models from independent training runs requires endpoint matching, which this work does not improve.

3. **Sparse, noisy coordinates**: In practice, steering vectors and SAE features are sparse and noisy. The covariance-based alignment assumes enough signal; breaks down with low-rank or adversarial perturbations.

4. **Other normalization layers**: The work focuses on LayerNorm and RMSNorm. Other normalizations (GroupNorm, InstanceNorm in vision transformers, etc.) may have different symmetries requiring case-by-case analysis.

5. **Interpretability claims remain task-dependent**: Even with correct gauge, whether a "neuron" is truly interpretable depends on the task, dataset, and definition of interpretability. The gauge is necessary but not sufficient.

6. **No guidance on gauge *selection***: The paper describes how to align gauges but doesn't address which gauge is "most interpretable" or "most useful" — a subjective choice.

## Open Questions

1. **Online gauge tracking during training**: Can you maintain a running gauge estimate as the model trains, rather than aligning checkpoints post-hoc? Would this improve interpretability or steering?

2. **Gauge structure in other architectures**: What are the full symmetry groups for MQA/GQA attention, mixture-of-experts, or multimodal transformers? Do they introduce new gauge constraints?

3. **Steering robustness across gauges**: Is the 95.8% preservation of steering effect universal, or does it depend on the steering method (activation addition, SAE clamping, LoRA, etc.)?

4. **Gauge-aware mechanistic discovery**: Can we design circuit-analysis or SAE-training algorithms that are gauge-aware, rather than retrofitting alignment afterward?

5. **Formal characterization of "best" gauge**: Is there an information-theoretic or statistical criterion for choosing one gauge over another (e.g., maximum sparsity, minimum cross-entropy)?

6. **Multi-checkpoint merging**: How do you align three or more checkpoints simultaneously? Is there a notion of consensus gauge, or are pairwise alignments transitive?
