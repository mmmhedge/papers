# Learning Probabilistic Filters with Strictly Proper Scoring Rules

**Authors:** Eviatar Bach, Ricardo Baptista, Jochen Bröcker, Bohan Chen, Andrew Stuart
**Published:** 2026-06-25 | **arXiv:** [2606.26497v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26497v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces the Proper Scoring Ensemble Filter (PSEF), a machine-learning approach to Bayesian filtering that trains a transformer-based neural network to approximate filtering distributions using only synthetic trajectories and strictly proper scoring rules. Unlike traditional ensemble Kalman filters or MSE-based learning methods, PSEF directly optimizes probabilistic accuracy across the entire posterior distribution, achieving superior performance on nonlinear, non-Gaussian, and multi-modal filtering problems.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on classical **Bayesian filtering** theory (e.g., Kalman filtering, particle filters) and ensemble data assimilation methods like the **Ensemble Kalman Filter (EnKF)**. It leverages **strictly proper scoring rules** — loss functions that incentivize honest probabilistic forecasting — particularly the energy score. The theoretical framework relies on **mean-field consistency arguments** to connect finite-ensemble empirical objectives to population-level objectives, drawing from statistical learning theory and optimal transport concepts. The use of permutation-invariant architectures (transformers) connects to [[representation-learning]] and [[invariant-architectures]].

## Core Contributions
1. **Novel training objective**: Uses strictly proper scoring rules (energy score) instead of MSE to directly optimize probabilistic calibration during filter training, theoretically grounded by a realizability assumption proving the Bayesian filter minimizes the population objective.
2. **Architecture design**: Proposes a permutation-invariant transformer-based analysis map that takes forecast ensembles and observations as input, naturally handling variable ensemble sizes.
3. **Theoretical rigor**: Derives the finite-ensemble empirical objective and proves its consistency with the population objective via mean-field analysis.
4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates superior performance on highly non-Gaussian and multi-modal posteriors compared to classical EnKF and MSE-trained baselines, while also identifying when EnKF corrections remain optimal.

## Practical Applications
1. **Weather forecasting & climate modeling**: Real-time probabilistic prediction of atmospheric states from sparse, noisy observations; ensemble forecast calibration.
2. **Geophysical inversion**: Inferring subsurface properties (oil/gas exploration, carbon sequestration) from seismic or electromagnetic data with nonlinear, non-Gaussian uncertainty.
3. **Financial risk estimation**: Online posterior inference for latent volatility, credit spreads, or portfolio returns in high-dimensional, non-linear markets.
4. **Robotics & autonomous systems**: Real-time state estimation (e.g., localization, attitude) under model uncertainty and nonlinearity.
5. **Epidemiological forecasting**: Inferring true infection counts from biased observations in disease spread models with complex, multi-modal posteriors.

## Product & Startup Ideas

2. **Quantified supply-chain analytics**: B2B software predicting demand and inventory levels with honest uncertainty bands, using PSEF on partially observed warehouse/logistics networks.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Realizability assumption**: The theoretical guarantee assumes the true filtering distribution lies in the model class — if the transformer architecture cannot represent it, guarantees evaporate.
2. **Synthetic trajectory dependency**: Requires ability to generate large amounts of synthetic state--observation pairs; impractical when the forward model is unavailable, expensive, or poorly calibrated.
3. **Ensemble size scaling**: Permutation invariance handles variable ensemble sizes in principle, but computational cost and approximation quality may degrade with very large or very small ensembles.
4. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: No principled guidance on choosing ensemble size, energy score variant, or training dynamics; empirical tuning likely required.
5. **Limited to offline learning**: The method trains on synthetic data; adapting to real observational data drift or model mismatch is not addressed.
6. **Comparison scope**: Experiments focus on relatively low-dimensional systems; scalability to high-dimensional, complex spatiotemporal systems (e.g., full weather models) is unclear.

## Open Questions
1. **When does end-to-end learning outperform EnKF corrections?** The paper notes this empirically but lacks a theoretical characterization of problem class / non-Gaussianity thresholds.
2. **Can online adaptation be incorporated?** How would the filter behave if real-world observations systematically diverge from the synthetic training distribution?
3. **How does performance scale with state dimensionality?** Do current transformer architectures maintain efficiency in truly high-dimensional (e.g., >1000-D) filtering tasks?
4. **Can this generalize across ensemble sizes?** Does a filter trained on, say, 50 particles transfer well to 100 or 10?
5. **What is the role of model error?** How robust is PSEF to mild model misspecification (unlike the clean synthetic setting)?
6. **Scalability of proper scoring rules**: Can alternatives to the energy score (which scales as O(N²) in ensemble size) enable practical scaling?

## Key Takeaway
Learning Bayesian filters via strictly proper scoring rules on synthetic data directly incentivizes probabilistic calibration, outperforming classical and MSE-based methods on nonlinear, non-Gaussian posteriors—but realizability assumptions and synthetic data requirements are critical limitations.
