# Quantitative Gaussian-Process limits of Tensor Programs

**Authors:** Andrea Agazzi, Eloy Mosig García, Dario Trevisan
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06290v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06290v1)

## Summary
This paper proves quantitative bounds on how closely finite neural networks approximate their infinite-width Gaussian-process limits using tensor programs. The authors show the approximation error shrinks at a rate of roughly 1/√width—meaning doubling network width cuts the error by ~30%—and their framework applies to feed-forward, recurrent, and transformer architectures without requiring architecture-specific proofs.

## Theoretical Background
Neural network theory has long studied what happens as network width grows infinitely. A key insight—proven rigorously in recent years—is that infinitely wide networks converge to Gaussian processes (probability distributions over functions). Tensor programs are a unifying mathematical formalism that describes neural network computations as traces of random tensor operations, making it easier to analyze different architectures uniformly. Wasserstein distance is a standard metric for measuring how far apart two probability distributions are. This paper builds on the tensor program framework and applies quantitative probability theory (specifically concentration inequalities and coupling arguments) to pin down exactly how fast finite networks approach the GP limit.

## Core Contributions
1. **Explicit finite-width error bounds**: Derives concrete convergence rates (O(1/√width)) rather than just proving limits exist. Previous work often showed limits without quantifying approximation error.
2. **Architecture-agnostic framework**: Single proof technique covers feed-forward nets, RNNs, and transformers—avoiding repetitive architecture-specific analyses.
3. **Rigorous Wasserstein convergence theory**: Proves convergence in Wasserstein distance (a strong metric), not just in distribution or in expectation.
4. **Practical width recommendations**: Error bounds are explicit enough to estimate required widths for desired accuracy.

## Connections to Other Work
This extends classical neural network GP theory—initiated by work on infinite-width limits—by adding quantitative precision. It complements [[Neural Tangent Kernel]] theory (which studies another infinite-width regime) and relates to recent work on tensor programs as a universal language for neural network analysis. The √width convergence rate is standard in random matrix theory and concentration bounds, but making it rigorous here required careful handling of tensor program traces.

## Practical Applications
- **Architecture search & design**: Predict whether a finite network will exhibit desired properties by computing requirements from theoretical bounds.
- **Transfer learning initialization**: Estimate how width affects the gap between target GP behavior and actual finite-width network—guide width selection for fine-tuning.
- **Uncertainty quantification in neural nets**: Use GP behavior (and finite-width corrections) to construct confidence intervals on predictions.
- **Hyperparameter selection**: Jointly optimize width and learning rate by understanding how width affects the optimization landscape relative to GP limits.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Neural Architecture Advisor**: A tool that takes architecture specs and returns required widths/depths to guarantee within-ε distance of desired GP behavior, with cost estimates.
2. **Finite-Width Correction Library**: Off-the-shelf module that wraps trained networks and computes confidence bands on predictions using theoretical finite-width correction terms.
3. **Automated Model Sizing Service**: SaaS that analyzes a proposed neural net architecture, computes Wasserstein bounds, and recommends optimal width/cost trade-offs for production inference.
4. **Theoretical Performance Predictor**: Enterprise tool for ML teams to forecast how network width affects generalization gaps and convergence speed before training.
5. **Federated Learning Validator**: Check whether distributed training across heterogeneous device widths stays within provable convergence guarantees.

## Technical Limitations
- **Assumption: random initialization & no training**: Bounds apply at initialization; once networks are trained, the GP approximation may break down because weights drift far from random.
- **Requires smoothness**: Works best for smooth activations (ReLU behavior at kinks not fully captured).
- **Width must be large**: Rates scale as 1/√width, so very narrow networks (width < 100) may have loose bounds in practice.
- **Composition depth**: Bounds may degrade with very deep networks; no explicit depth-dependent analysis provided.
- **Weight sharing (RNNs/Transformers)**: Coverage is claimed but may require careful treatment of sequence length effects not fully explored.

## Open Questions
- How do these bounds change *after* training? Does the finite-width error grow during SGD, or do trained networks stay close to GP predictions?
- Can the √width rate be improved for specific architectures (e.g., is 1/width possible with more structure)?
- How do depth and width jointly affect the convergence rate—is there a depth × width trade-off?
- Do these bounds extend to non-smooth activations (ReLU, GeLU) with rigorous proofs, or only approximately?
- Can this framework quantify the error incurred by discretization (finite-precision arithmetic in practice)?

## Key Takeaway
Finite neural networks provably stay within O(1/√width) Wasserstein distance of their infinite-width Gaussian-process limits across architectures, providing the first quantitative convergence theory with explicit error bounds.
