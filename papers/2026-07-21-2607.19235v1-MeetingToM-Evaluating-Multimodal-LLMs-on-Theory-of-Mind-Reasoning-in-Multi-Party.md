# MeetingToM: Evaluating Multimodal LLMs on Theory-of-Mind Reasoning in Multi-Party Meetings

**Authors:** Ziyi Wang, Yuhang Wu, Dongxu Piao, Xingyu Liu, Tianhui Zhou et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-21 | **arXiv:** [2607.19235v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19235v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces MeetingToM, a benchmark that tests whether multimodal language models (AI systems that process both text and visual information) can understand hidden social dynamics in group meetings. The researchers reveal that current models struggle to read between the lines — they miss non-verbal cues, fail to detect when people privately disagree while publicly appearing to agree, and can't distinguish genuine consensus from fake agreement driven by social pressure.

## Theoretical Background
Theory of Mind (ToM) — the ability to infer what others believe, intend, and know — is foundational to human social cognition. Prior work has tested ToM in simplified visual scenarios (e.g., false-belief tasks, video QA benchmarks like Social IQa and MovieToM), but these typically focus on *overt, externally verifiable* signals and miss the layered complexity of real group interaction. Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) — systems like GPT-4V and Gemini that combine language understanding with vision — have shown promise but remain largely unevaluated on *latent social states* (hidden attitudes, unstated intentions) and *group dynamics*. The paper builds on prior ToM research but expands the scope to naturalistic, multi-party settings where social cues are distributed across speech, body language, and interaction patterns.

## Core Contributions
1. **MeetingToM benchmark**: A hierarchically structured evaluation set grounded in real multi-party meetings, organized across three levels of social granularity — individual mental states, dyadic interactions (addressee understanding), and group-level phenomena.

2. **Pseudo-consensus concept**: Introduces and operationalizes a meeting-specific phenomenon where apparent group agreement masks private dissent under social pressure — a failure mode not well-captured by existing benchmarks.

3. **Systematic analysis of MLLMs**: Conducts controlled experiments on representative multimodal models, diagnosing specific failure modes: poor integration of non-verbal cues, weak inference of hidden attitudes, and inability to distinguish genuine from fake consensus.

4. **Unified evaluation protocol**: Establishes a standardized methodology for measuring multi-level ToM reasoning in meetings.

## Connections to Other Work
This work extends prior multimodal ToM benchmarks (MovieToM, Social IQa) by shifting from short clips and isolated scenarios to naturalistic, long-form group interactions. It relates to [[Alignment|alignment research]] in that inferring hidden beliefs and intentions is crucial for safe AI systems that must model human preferences accurately. The group dynamics aspect connects to [[multi-agent-systems]] literature, though here the "agents" are human participants. The focus on non-verbal cues and latent state inference also resonates with [[mechanistic-interpretability]] — understanding what a system "knows" beneath surface outputs.

## Practical Applications
1. **Meeting assistants**: AI tools that alert participants when group decisions may be falsely unanimous or when dissenting views aren't being heard.

2. **Negotiation support**: Systems that flag pseudo-consensus in contract talks, board meetings, or diplomatic negotiations, helping parties surface true positions.

3. **Mental health and team coaching**: Tools that analyze group therapy sessions or team retrospectives to identify unspoken tension or disengagement.

4. **HR and conflict resolution**: Automated analysis of recorded meetings to surface interpersonal dynamics and potential conflicts before they escalate.

5. **Interview and hiring evaluation**: Assessment of candidates' ability to read social cues and navigate group dynamics during panel interviews.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **MeetingMind**: A real-time meeting companion that flags pseudo-consensus and alerts facilitators when group agreement may be superficial; generates prompts to surface hidden dissent.

2. **ToM Analytics for Enterprise**: Post-meeting analysis tool that tags psychological states, power dynamics, and consensus quality; targets corporate strategy and M&A teams.

3. **Negotiation Coach**: AI-powered analysis of negotiation recordings that identifies when counterparties' body language contradicts their stated positions; used by sales, legal, and business development teams.

4. **Team Health Monitor**: Continuous analysis of recurring meetings to track changes in engagement, conflict, and psychological safety over time; dashboard for people ops.

5. **Therapy Assistant**: Tools for therapists and counselors that flag group dynamics in family or team sessions, helping facilitators intervene before problems crystallize.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Benchmark grain size**: The evaluation is anchored in a specific corpus of meetings; generalization to other cultural contexts, languages, or meeting types (virtual vs. in-person) is untested.

2. **Non-verbal cue availability**: The model's performance depends on video/audio quality and camera angles; meetings with poor framing or acoustic issues will degrade signal.

3. **Lack of ground truth for latent states**: "Hidden attitudes" and "private dissent" are inherently subjective; the benchmark likely relies on post-hoc self-report or annotator consensus, both imperfect.

4. **Static vs. dynamic reasoning**: The paper doesn't clarify whether models are evaluated on snapshots or temporal reasoning; real social dynamics evolve throughout a meeting.

5. **Computational cost**: Multimodal processing of long meetings (potentially hours) is computationally expensive; deployment at scale faces practical constraints.

6. **Causality and counterfactuals**: The benchmark tests correlation (reading cues) but not causal reasoning (if X had happened differently, would Y's belief change?).

## Open Questions
1. Can models trained on meeting-grounded ToM tasks transfer to other naturalistic social settings (classrooms, courtrooms, etc.)?

2. How do different architectures for fusing speech, text, and visual information perform? Is cross-attention better than early/late fusion?

3. Can models be fine-tuned or prompted to actively *probe* for hidden disagreement (e.g., by asking clarifying questions) rather than passively inferring?

4. Does explicit training on pseudo-consensus reduce the failure rate, or is this phenomenon fundamentally harder?

5. What is the human ceiling? Do annotators reliably agree on latent mental states, and do humans in real meetings agree with post-hoc ToM judgments?

6. Can models predict *when* pseudo-consensus will form (early warning), or only detect it after the fact?

## Key Takeaway
Multimodal AI models fail to detect hidden disagreement masked by false group consensus in real meetings.
