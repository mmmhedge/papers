# Agon: Competitive Cross-Model RL with Implicit Rival Grading of Reasoning

**Authors:** Vladislav Beliaev
**Published:** 2026-07-08 | **arXiv:** [2607.07690v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07690v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces Agon, a competitive training method where two language models grade each other's reasoning on difficult problems. Instead of relying on a single external reward signal (like checking only final answers), the models compete by alternately solving problems and critiquing each other's work — forcing both to develop genuine reasoning skills rather than just longer outputs. On hard math problems, this approach roughly doubles the performance of standard RL methods.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on recent work in reinforcement learning for language models, particularly [[GRPO]] (Group Relative Policy Optimization) and similar verifiable-reward approaches that train reasoning models by providing scalar feedback on solution quality. Traditional RL from human feedback (RLHF) and process-supervised approaches have explored whether grading intermediate reasoning steps matters more than final answers; Agon proposes that competitive dynamics between models can implicitly surface good reasoning without explicit process labels. The work sits at the intersection of multi-agent RL (where agents learn against each other) and LLM alignment — the challenge of making models behave in desirable ways during training.

## Core Contributions
1. **Competitive mutual grading**: Two models serve as implicit reward models for each other, eliminating the need for an external verifier or process-supervision labels.
2. **Asymmetric information structure**: One model drafts a solution (which the opponent sees), the other solves after reading — forcing the reader to out-reason someone with informational advantage.
3. **Empirical validation at scale**: Demonstrates 2x improvement over GRPO on DeepMath's hard split (Qwen3), and consistent gains across model families and domains (code, reasoning tasks).
4. **Training=Inference parity**: The two-stage cascade used at inference is identical to the training procedure, avoiding the usual train-test mismatch.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to [[self-play]] in game-playing RL (AlphaGo, AlphaZero) but applied to open-ended reasoning rather than fully observable games. It extends beyond [[RLHF]] and [[process supervision]] by avoiding the need for ground-truth labels on intermediate steps — reasoning quality emerges from competition. The approach differs from [[mixture of experts]] or [[ensemble methods]] in that both agents are simultaneously optimized against a moving target (the rival), creating a richer training signal than fixed ensembles. It also echoes ideas from debate-based alignment where agents argue to convince a judge, but here the "judge" is implicit in who solves the problem better.

## Practical Applications
1. **Training more capable reasoning models**: Critical for mathematics, science, and planning tasks where current models struggle on competition-level problems.
2. **Code generation and verification**: Competitive pairing of models could improve code correctness by having one model write and another review/fix after seeing the attempt.
3. **Scientific hypothesis evaluation**: Models could jointly generate and critique research hypotheses or proofs without human-provided ground truth.
4. **Reduced labeling cost**: Eliminates the need for expensive process-supervision labels or reward model training, lowering deployment cost for reasoning-heavy applications.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Competitive math-tutoring platform**: Pair specialized reasoning models (one for setup/intuition, one for verification) to generate high-quality worked solutions and catch common errors.
2. **Code audit and generation service**: Deploy Agon-trained pairs to write and mutually verify production code, replacing some human code review with aligned AI review.
3. **Research paper co-author assistant**: Two models jointly draft and critique research arguments, hypotheses, and proofs, letting researchers focus on novelty rather than exposition.
4. **Competitive exam prep**: Agon pairs trained on past competitions in math, physics, or programming—one drafts solutions, the other critiques—to generate better practice materials than single-model baselines.
5. **Reasoning API for enterprises**: License Agon-trained model pairs as an API for complex decision-making (logistics, finance, legal document review) where reasoning quality justifies the 2x compute cost.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Computational cost**: Requires running two models per problem, doubling inference cost—a hard tradeoff for latency-sensitive applications.
2. **Model pairing constraints**: Requires two "comparably strong and behaviorally different" models; unclear how to ensure diversity or what happens if one model is significantly weaker.
3. **Coordination overhead**: Text-based communication between models is slow; latent-space reasoning (mentioned as future work) is speculative and may not preserve interpretability.
4. **Generalization to verification-hard domains**: Works on problems with verifiable answers (math, code). Fails for open-ended tasks (creative writing, subjective reasoning) where "out-solving" is undefined.
5. **Scalability of pairing**: As models grow in size, the 2x compute cost becomes prohibitive; unclear if one large model + one smaller model works or if both must be similar scale.
6. **Training stability**: Alternating optimization of two models against each other could lead to cycles or collapse if one model finds an exploitable pattern; no analysis of convergence or mode collapse.

## Open Questions
1. **What is the minimum capability gap?** At what performance difference does competitive training break down? Is there a "Pareto frontier" where pairing stops helping?
2. **Does behavioral diversity matter mechanically?** How much of the gain comes from diversity vs. simply having two training signals? Can you engineer diversity (e.g., different architectures, initialization)?
3. **Can one model be much smaller?** Would pairing a large reasoning model with a smaller verifier achieve the same gains at lower cost?
4. **Latent-space communication**: Can models learn to collaborate in embedding space rather than text? Does this preserve the competitive incentive structure?
5. **Domain generalization**: Does an Agon pair trained on math transfer to code? To long-horizon planning? What's the sample efficiency cost?
6. **Theoretical convergence**: What are the convergence guarantees? Under what conditions does this avoid mode collapse or exploitative equilibria?

## Key Takeaway
Competitive pairing of language models implicitly rewards good reasoning without process labels, doubling performance on hard reasoning tasks by forcing each model to out-think a rival that has seen its work.
