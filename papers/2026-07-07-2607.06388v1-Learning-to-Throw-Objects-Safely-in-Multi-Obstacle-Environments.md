# Learning to Throw Objects Safely in Multi-Obstacle Environments

**Authors:** Mohammadreza Kasaei, Klemen Voncina, Hamidreza Kasaei
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06388v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06388v1)

## Summary
This paper addresses robotic throwing in cluttered environments—a challenging task that requires a robot to toss objects into a target basket while navigating around randomly placed obstacles. The authors introduce a potential field representation that encodes both target attraction and obstacle repulsion on a compact grid, enabling reinforcement learning policies to generalize across different obstacle configurations. Real-robot experiments achieve up to 90% success in cluttered scenes, demonstrating practical sim-to-real transfer.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on prior robot manipulation research, particularly TossingBot, which showed that throwing (rather than grasping) can be an efficient strategy for object placement in robotics. The paper leverages standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms (SAC, DDPG, TD3 — policy gradient and actor-critic methods that learn from visual or state-based observations). The key conceptual foundation is the potential field representation, borrowed from motion planning and navigation, which uses grid-based encoding of attractive and repulsive forces to guide robot behavior. Kinesthetic demonstrations (learning from human-guided movement) provide initialization to bootstrap the RL training process, reducing sample inefficiency.

## Core Contributions
1. **Potential Field Representation (PFR)**: A novel fixed-size grid encoding that combines goal attraction and obstacle repulsion, enabling generalization to unseen obstacle numbers and configurations without explicit obstacle enumeration.
2. **Systematic RL comparison**: Empirical evaluation of SAC, DDPG, and TD3 for robotic throwing, demonstrating SAC's superior consistency.
3. **Sim-to-real validation**: Demonstration that policies trained in simulation transfer reliably to real robots on unseen objects and cluttered scenes (90% success).
4. **Scalability proof**: Empirical evidence that PFR outperforms explicit state encodings when generalization to variable obstacle counts is required.

## Connections to Other Work
This extends [[TossingBot]] (visual throwing without obstacles) to obstacle-rich domains. It relates to broader work in [[reinforcement-learning]] for manipulation, sim-to-real transfer in robotics, and potential field methods from classical motion planning (traditionally used for path planning). The kinesthetic initialization strategy connects to learning from demonstrations and imitation learning pipelines. The work also touches on generalization in [[deep-learning]] — how representations affect transfer to unseen configurations.

## Practical Applications
- **Warehouse automation**: Sorting and bin-packing in cluttered fulfillment centers where fast object placement (throwing vs. grasping) accelerates throughput.
- **Manufacturing assembly lines**: Rapid object placement in congested workspaces with fixed or moving obstacles.
- **Recycling and waste sorting**: Automated sorting of objects into bins with obstacles and debris in the scene.
- **Robotic pick-and-place in constrained spaces**: Any setting where grasping is inefficient or physically impossible due to clutter.
- **Space robotics**: Micro-gravity environments where throwing may be more energy-efficient than traditional manipulation.

## Technical Limitations
- **Limited object diversity**: Training data likely covers a narrow range of object masses, shapes, and aerodynamic properties; generalization to radically different throwables is unvalidated.
- **Fixed potential field grid resolution**: The fixed-size grid encoding may lose information in highly dense obstacles or very large workspaces; scalability to extreme clutter is unclear.
- **Sim-to-real gap for physics**: Throwing involves complex contact dynamics, air resistance, and spin; mismatches between simulation and reality could degrade performance on new environments.
- **Basket proximity assumption**: The method assumes the basket remains static and visible; dynamic targets or occluded baskets are not addressed.
- **Sample efficiency**: No analysis of how many real-robot throws were needed to validate transfer; RL is typically sample-hungry.
- **Obstacle representation**: The potential field assumes convex or simple obstacle shapes; complex or deformable obstacles might not be well-represented on a grid.

## Open Questions
- How does PFR perform when the number of obstacles far exceeds training distribution, or when obstacles are dynamic (moving)?
- Can the approach generalize to different basket geometries, sizes, or locations without retraining?
- What is the minimum visual feedback needed? Could the method work with lower-resolution or event-based cameras?
- How sensitive is performance to errors in obstacle detection or localization from vision?
- Can hierarchical or multi-scale potential fields improve performance in very dense scenarios?
- Does the method scale to heterogeneous multi-robot systems (e.g., coordinating throws to avoid collisions)?

## Key Takeaway
Potential field representations on fixed grids provide a compact, generalizable way to encode obstacles in robotic throwing, enabling RL policies to achieve robust sim-to-real transfer in cluttered real-world environments.
