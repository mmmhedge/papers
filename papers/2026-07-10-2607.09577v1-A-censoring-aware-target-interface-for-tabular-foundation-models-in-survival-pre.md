# A censoring-aware target interface for tabular foundation models in survival prediction

**Authors:** Yue Lyu, Steven H. Lin, Xuelin Huang, Ziyi Li
**Published:** 2026-07-10 | **arXiv:** [2607.09577v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09577v1)

## Summary
This paper solves a fundamental problem in medical AI: how to use tabular foundation models (reusable neural networks trained on diverse datasets) for survival prediction when patient follow-up data is incomplete. The key innovation is converting censored survival outcomes into pseudo-observations based on restricted mean survival time (RMST), allowing standard regression models to handle incomplete data without survival-specific training. The approach is tested on 36 real medical datasets and shows competitive performance with traditional survival methods.

## Theoretical Background
Survival analysis traditionally models time-to-event data where some patients are "censored" — we know they haven't experienced the event by a given date but don't know when (or if) it will happen. Restricted mean survival time (RMST) is a clinically interpretable summary: the average event-free time within a fixed time window. The paper builds on the concept of pseudo-observations — a statistical technique that converts incomplete event data into complete targets suitable for standard regression. Tabular foundation models are deep neural networks pre-trained on diverse tabular datasets, similar in spirit to language model pre-training but for structured data. The core tension is that these foundation models assume fully observed outcomes, but survival data is inherently incomplete due to censoring.

## Core Contributions
1. **SurvFM-RMST framework**: A censoring-aware target interface that transforms right-censored survival outcomes into jackknife pseudo-observation targets for RMST. This conversion allows any tabular backbone (pre-trained or not) to perform horizon-specific RMST regression without survival-specific modifications.
2. **Empirical validation of pseudo-targets**: Controlled simulation experiments demonstrate that RMST pseudo-observations outperform naive baselines (restricted observed time, event-only labels) at recovering true conditional RMST.
3. **Scalable evaluation**: Systematic evaluation across 36 SurvSet datasets showing that foundation-model backbones are competitive with specialized survival methods and classical RMST regression, with performance varying by clinical endpoint and prediction horizon.
4. **Stratification capability**: Demonstration that predicted RMST can meaningfully segment held-out patients into risk groups with ordered observed event-free time and event enrichment.

## Connections to Other Work
This work bridges [[survival analysis]] (a classical biostatistics domain) and [[tabular foundation models]] (emerging deep learning infrastructure). It relates to broader efforts in [[domain adaptation]] — adapting general-purpose models to specialized problems without retraining. The pseudo-observation approach is a statistical wrapper, similar in spirit to how [[transfer learning]] uses pre-trained weights. The paper implicitly addresses the problem of handling missing data and distribution shift in medical AI, concerns also central to [[calibration]] and [[uncertainty quantification]]. It complements recent work on foundation models for tabular data by adding explicit support for right-censored outcomes, a major constraint in clinical prediction.

## Practical Applications
- **Hospital prognostic systems**: Predicting patient survival or time to adverse events (readmission, disease progression) to guide resource allocation and intervention timing.
- **Oncology trial planning**: Estimating restricted mean survival time to power clinical trials and determine follow-up duration.
- **Insurance and risk stratification**: Predicting time-to-claim events for health insurance underwriting and premium setting.
- **Biomarker discovery**: Using foundation-model interpretability to identify which patient features most strongly predict event-free time, guiding treatment personalization.
- **Multi-disease prediction pipelines**: Leveraging pre-trained foundation models to quickly adapt survival predictions to new patient cohorts with minimal labeled data.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Clinical risk API**: A cloud service that ingests patient tabular data (labs, demographics, comorbidities) and returns personalized RMST predictions with confidence intervals, marketable to hospitals and EHR vendors as a plug-and-play prognosis module.
2. **Foundation-model-as-a-service for biotech**: A platform offering pre-trained tabular backbones fine-tuned on survival tasks, allowing pharma and biotech firms to rapidly predict outcomes in their own cohorts without ML expertise.
3. **Insurance underwriting engine**: A product that predicts time-to-claim for life, disability, and health insurance applicants by applying SurvFM to underwriting data, reducing claims reserve volatility.
4. **Clinical trial simulator**: Software that uses predicted RMST to simulate trial enrollment, dropout, and event rates, helping sponsors design more efficient trials and negotiate with regulators.
5. **Personalized patient coaching app**: A consumer-facing app that combines RMST predictions with behavioral interventions, showing patients their predicted event-free time under different lifestyle/medication scenarios to drive engagement.

## Technical Limitations
- **Censoring mechanism assumption**: The method assumes missing data is missing completely at random or at random; if censoring depends on unobserved prognostic factors, bias can occur.
- **Computational overhead**: Jackknife pseudo-observations require multiple passes through the data, adding computational cost relative to direct regression; scalability to very large datasets is unclear.
- **Limited validation on extreme horizons**: RMST is sensitive to the choice of time window; performance may degrade outside the range where training data has dense follow-up.
- **Foundation model dependence**: The approach is only as good as the pre-trained backbone; poor pre-training (e.g., on non-clinical tabular data) could hurt performance.
- **Modest dataset sizes**: SurvSet datasets are relatively small (median ~500 patients); unclear whether the pseudo-observation approach scales to millions of records where foundation models shine.
- **Handling of competing risks**: The paper does not explicitly address competing risks (e.g., death from causes other than the event of interest), common in clinical settings.

## Open Questions
- How does SurvFM-RMST perform on very large datasets (100k+ patients) where foundation models typically excel, and does the pseudo-observation computation remain practical?
- Can the framework naturally extend to competing risks, where multiple mutually exclusive events can occur?
- What is the sensitivity of RMST predictions to the choice of time window? Should the window be learned from data?
- How do predictions perform in external validation (different hospital, different population) versus internal hold-out? Is transfer learning truly helping or just adding noise?
- Can pseudo-observation uncertainty be calibrated and propagated to downstream clinical decisions?
- How does SurvFM compare to survival-specific foundation models (if any are developed), or is the gain from ignoring censoring structure offset by the loss of pre-training?

## Key Takeaway
A simple statistical wrapper (jackknife pseudo-observations) can unlock pre-trained tabular foundation models for survival prediction without survival-specific fine-tuning, offering a portable interface between censored clinical data and general-purpose neural networks.
