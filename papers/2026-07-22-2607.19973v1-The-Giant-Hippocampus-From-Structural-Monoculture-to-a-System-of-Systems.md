# The Giant Hippocampus: From Structural Monoculture to a System of Systems

**Authors:** Jaeho Seol
**Published:** 2026-07-22 | **arXiv:** [2607.19973v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19973v1)

## Summary
This paper argues that modern AI has made a structural mistake by applying identical Transformer architectures to all tasks, when neuroscience shows that different cognitive functions require qualitatively different neural structures. The author proposes a "System of Systems" design — heterogeneous modules with specialized inductive biases communicating through standardized interfaces — as a principled alternative to monolithic, scaled-up models.

## Theoretical Background
The paper grounds itself in a century of neuroscience, from Brodmann's cytoarchitecture (early 1900s categorization of distinct cortical regions by structure) through modern single-cell recording techniques. It contrasts this with deep learning's convergence on Transformers — neural networks using attention mechanisms to process sequences — as a universal substrate. The argument draws on the "Hardware Lottery" concept: architectural choices often succeed due to engineering convenience rather than theoretical optimality. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are cited as a counterexample: they encoded spatial locality and hierarchical structure directly, achieving strong performance with less data. The paper frames Transformers functionally as similar to the hippocampus — specialized for associative memory and relational learning — rather than as a general-purpose cortex.

## Core Contributions
1. **Structural critique of standardization**: Demonstrates that applying identical Transformer templates across vision, language, and audio is mechanistically misaligned with how biology solves different problems.
2. **Functionalist analysis**: Maps Transformers to hippocampal computation, not cortical computation, exposing a conceptual category error in the field.
3. **Heterogeneous Topological Network (HTN) design**: Proposes a concrete alternative architecture where distinct modules retain task-specific inductive biases and communicate via standardized interfaces — a design discipline, not a post-hoc interpretation.
4. **Critique of Mixture-of-Experts**: Shows that MoE, often cited as architectural diversity, merely partitions parameters among identical experts rather than creating structural heterogeneity.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to [[mechanistic-interpretability]] by arguing that understanding *why* models work requires examining structure, not just behavior. It echoes critiques of the "scaling laws" paradigm — the assumption that bigger versions of the same architecture solve harder problems. The paper implicitly challenges [[inductive bias]] assumptions in modern deep learning and connects to neuroscience-inspired AI (e.g., work on sparse, modular networks). It also touches on the broader question of whether [[attention mechanisms]] are truly universal or whether task-specific architectures (convolution for vision, recurrence for temporal tasks) remain preferable.

## Practical Applications
1. **Specialized vision models**: Architectures that encode 2D locality directly (not through learned attention), reducing data requirements for visual tasks.
2. **Audio/speech systems**: Dedicated temporal integration modules instead of generic Transformers, improving efficiency for sequential acoustic data.
3. **Reasoning and planning**: Separate executive/gating modules (inspired by prefrontal cortex) for tasks requiring working memory and structured computation.
4. **Embodied AI / robotics**: Modular systems where perception, proprioception, and motor control maintain domain-specific structure rather than being merged into a single learned representation.
5. **Low-data regimes**: By encoding inductive biases structurally, models could learn more efficiently in domains with limited training examples (medical imaging, rare languages).

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **AutoArch for domain-specific AI**: A design tool that takes task specifications (vision, audio, reasoning, control) and automatically composes heterogeneous modules with pre-tuned inductive biases, then trains end-to-end. Sell to companies building custom AI stacks.
2. **Efficient edge-AI platform**: Modular neural networks that decompose into specialized sub-networks, allowing selective deployment and pruning on resource-constrained devices (phones, IoT, autonomous vehicles).
3. **Neuroscience-guided model interpretability service**: Analyze trained models by mapping their learned structure to known cognitive functions (hippocampus, prefrontal cortex, sensory hierarchies), providing explanability-as-a-service for regulated industries (healthcare, finance).
4. **Data-efficient transfer learning framework**: Leverage structural modularity to pre-train task-specific components separately, enabling few-shot transfer with minimal labeled data. Target pharma and biotech.
5. **Modular foundation model marketplace**: Offer a library of pre-trained, structurally distinct modules (vision encoders with convolutional biases, recurrent temporal modules, attention-based semantic modules) that teams can compose and fine-tune, reducing training costs.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Training dynamics unclear**: The paper does not provide empirical evidence that end-to-end training of heterogeneous modules converges as reliably as homogeneous Transformers. Optimization landscapes may be harder.
2. **Interface design under-specified**: What do "standardized interfaces" actually look like? The paper lacks concrete specifications for how modules communicate and coordinate.
3. **No direct comparison**: The paper is largely theoretical. Empirical benchmarks comparing HTN to Transformers on vision, audio, and language tasks are absent.
4. **Biological plausibility as design principle unclear**: Biology is not always optimal for computation (metabolic constraints, evolutionary history). Copying structure may not translate to better algorithms.
5. **Scalability questions**: Does modularity impose communication bottlenecks? How does performance scale compared to dense, homogeneous models?
6. **Task definition dependency**: Modularity requires specifying task boundaries and module roles *before* training. This is harder for ill-defined or multi-faceted problems where no ground truth decomposition exists.

## Open Questions
1. **Empirical validation**: Can you train an HTN on ImageNet, LibriSpeech, and language modeling benchmarks and beat or match Transformers while using less data?
2. **Module discovery**: Can you learn the *structure* of modularity (where to place boundaries, what inductive biases each module needs) rather than hand-specifying it?
3. **Generalization**: Do structurally heterogeneous models transfer better across tasks? Do they require less fine-tuning?
4. **Interface learning**: What is the optimal design for communication channels between modules? Should they be learned, fixed, or hybrid?
5. **Composition at scale**: Can you compose 50+ specialized modules without exponential blowup in complexity? How do you manage interdependencies?
6. **Neuroscience validation**: Do trained heterogeneous models' learned representations actually map to known cognitive structures (layers, functional columns, neurotransmitter systems)?

## Key Takeaway
Modern AI treats diverse tasks with one template; biology uses specialized structures — this paper argues the field should too.
