# LMs as Task-Specific Knowledge Bases: An Interpretability Analysis

**Authors:** Amit Elhelo, Amir Globerson, Mor Geva
**Published:** 2026-06-25 | **arXiv:** [2606.27237v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27237v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates whether language models function as true knowledge bases by examining whether the same factual knowledge produces consistent outputs across different tasks. Through behavioral and mechanistic analyses, the authors demonstrate that LMs actually encode knowledge in task-specific ways, with different parameter subsets encoding the same facts for different tasks. This finding challenges the intuitive "knowledge base" analogy and raises important questions about the reliability and controllability of factual reasoning in LMs.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on the mechanistic interpretability framework that seeks to understand how neural networks implement specific computations at the parameter level. It engages with two key theoretical assumptions:

1. **The knowledge base hypothesis**: The intuition that neural network parameters function analogously to traditional knowledge bases, where facts are stored in a unified, query-independent manner
2. **Parameter localization**: Prior work showing that specific facts and capabilities can be traced to localized parameter subsets within networks (related to [[Sparse Autoencoders]] and circuit-based interpretability)

The paper also draws on transfer learning theory, which typically assumes that knowledge learned on one task should be broadly applicable to related tasks—an assumption this paper problematizes.

## Core Contributions
1. **Behavioral evidence of task-specificity**: Demonstrates empirically that facts learned during training on one task frequently fail to manifest on other tasks, even when presented with identical factual queries
2. **Mechanistic explanation via parameter localization**: Shows through probing and parameter analysis that different task-specific parameter subsets encode the same underlying facts, suggesting knowledge is not stored in a unified way
3. **Chain-of-thought mechanism clarification**: Reveals that chain-of-thought reasoning's effectiveness partly derives from engaging task-specific parameters beyond those directly tied to the evaluation task, suggesting intermediate reasoning engages different knowledge encoding pathways
4. **Systematic mapping of task-knowledge coupling**: Provides concrete evidence that "what the model knows" and "how it is asked" are fundamentally entangled in parameter space rather than orthogonal

## Connections to Other Work
This research directly critiques the simplistic "knowledge base" view often used to explain LM behavior and relates to several adjacent literatures:

- **[[Mechanistic Interpretability]]**: Extends circuit-based analysis to understanding how facts are encoded across task contexts
- **[[Sparse Autoencoders]]**: The parameter localization methodology resonates with recent work using SAEs to identify interpretable features
- **[[Transfer Learning]]**: Challenges optimistic assumptions about zero-shot and few-shot generalization by showing task-specificity operates at the parameter level
- **Knowledge graphs and entity extraction**: Questions whether LM knowledge aligns with the unified, structured assumptions underlying traditional knowledge representation
- **Chain-of-thought reasoning**: Connects to recent work on mechanistic explanations for why intermediate reasoning steps improve model outputs

## Practical Applications
1. **Fact verification and hallucination detection**: Systems could identify when a model's "knowledge" of a fact is actually task-specific and unreliable in new contexts, enabling confidence scoring based on parameter engagement patterns
2. **Task-specific knowledge auditing**: Organizations deploying LMs could map which facts are reliably encoded across their intended task distribution vs. which are brittle and task-dependent
3. **Adversarial robustness**: Understanding task-specific encoding enables detection of queries that exploit narrow parameter subsets, improving safety in adversarial settings
4. **Targeted knowledge injection**: Fine-tuning procedures could be designed to create unified, task-general encodings of critical facts rather than task-specific ones
5. **Multi-task reasoning systems**: Applications requiring consistent reasoning across varied contexts could be redesigned to explicitly manage task-specificity rather than assuming unified knowledge

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **LM Factuality Auditor**: A tool that systematically probes LMs across task variants to identify brittle vs. robust knowledge encodings, generating reports for enterprises using LMs in high-stakes applications (finance, healthcare, legal)
2. **Knowledge Unification Service**: A fine-tuning/adaptation service that takes a pre-trained LM and specific critical facts, then optimizes to create task-independent encodings of those facts while preserving task performance
3. **Behavioral Testing Platform**: Automated red-teaming tool that generates task variants of factual queries to expose task-specific knowledge gaps before deployment, with visualization dashboards showing parameter-level explanations
4. **LM Knowledge Alignment Consultant**: Professional service helping organizations verify that their LM deployments encode mission-critical facts reliably across all intended use cases, with remediation recommendations
5. **Mechanistic Debugging IDE**: Developer tool for LM engineers that visualizes parameter engagement patterns when querying facts across tasks, helping identify why models fail on apparently simple transfer tasks

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability of parameter localization**: The mechanistic analyses likely become intractable at larger model scales; unclear if results generalize to 70B+ parameter models where parameter localization is harder
2. **Task selection bias**: Results depend heavily on which tasks are chosen for comparison—cherry-picked task pairs might show more or less task-specificity than random distributions
3. **Definition ambiguity**: The paper may conflate genuine task-specificity (different facts for different tasks) with legitimate context-dependence (same fact requiring different reasoning paths)
4. **Training dynamics confound**: Facts that "fail to co-emerge" might reflect optimization quirks during training rather than fundamental parameter organization, and single-run results lack robustness quantification
5. **Limited to factual knowledge**: Results may not generalize to procedural knowledge, skills, or abstract reasoning patterns that might be encoded more uniformly
6. **Probing reliability**: Parameter localization experiments depend on probe quality; adversarial or poorly-calibrated probes could mislocalize knowledge, affecting mechanistic conclusions

## Open Questions
1. **How universal is task-specificity?** Does this pattern hold across model scales, architectures (MoE, mixture-of-heads), and different types of knowledge (common sense, numerical, temporal reasoning)?
2. **Can we engineer unified encodings?** Can explicit training objectives (e.g., contrastive losses across task variants) force models to learn task-general fact encodings, and what is the performance cost?
3. **What drives task-specificity?** Is it an unavoidable consequence of gradient-based learning on task-diverse datasets, or an artifact of standard architectures and training procedures?
4. **Relationship to in-context learning?** How does task-specificity in parameters interact with in-context learning and prompting? Do prompt instructions help mitigate parameter task-specificity?
5. **Scaling behavior**: Does the degree of task-specificity increase or decrease with model scale? Do larger models achieve more unified fact encoding?
6. **Interaction with continual learning**: In continual learning settings, does task-specific encoding lead to catastrophic forgetting, and would unified encodings help?

## Key Takeaway
Language models encode factual knowledge in task-specific parameter subsets rather than unified representations, meaning the same fact may be reliably retrievable on one task but inaccessible on another—a finding that fundamentally challenges the knowledge-base analogy and has serious implications for reliability and controllability in LM applications.
