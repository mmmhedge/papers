# RTS Smoother-Guided Learning of Physics-Based Neural Differential Models

**Authors:** Ahmet Demirkaya, Georgios Stratis, Tales Imbiriba, Zachary D. Danziger, Deniz Erdogmus
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.15180v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15180v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes a hybrid approach that combines known physics equations with neural networks to model dynamical systems when only partial measurements are available. The method alternates between inferring hidden system states (using a classical statistical smoother) and learning the neural network parameters, allowing it to discover missing physics while maintaining interpretability. This matters because many real systems have well-understood components alongside unknown dynamics—and current pure neural approaches often miss the structure that makes models trustworthy and generalizable.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on several established ideas. **Ordinary differential equations (ODEs)** are classical tools for modeling systems that evolve over time. The **Rauch–Tung–Striebel (RTS) smoother** is a Bayesian state estimation algorithm that infers unobserved system states from noisy measurements by running a forward-backward pass. **Neural differential equations** (e.g., Neural ODEs) are recent models that embed neural networks inside ODE solvers to learn dynamics end-to-end. The key insight here is that instead of replacing physics entirely with a neural network, the authors keep known equations explicit and use a neural network only for the unknown residual—a form of **inductive bias** that improves learning and interpretability.

## Core Contributions
1. **Explicit hybrid architecture**: A principled framework that partitions dynamics into known (physics-based) and unknown (neural) components, rather than learning everything end-to-end.
2. **RTS-guided parameter learning**: A two-stage iterative algorithm that alternates between (a) state smoothing with known parameters and (b) neural network training on smoothed trajectories, forming a structured EM-like loop.
3. **Robustness to partial observations**: Demonstrates learning under incomplete state measurements and stiff dynamics (systems with vastly different timescales), which are common in practice but challenging for naive neural approaches.
4. **Empirical validation**: Benchmarks on linear, nonlinear, and stiff ODE systems showing better latent-state reconstruction and long-horizon prediction compared to baselines.

## Connections to Other Work
This extends ideas from [[Physics-Informed Neural Networks]] (PINNs) and [[Neural ODEs]], which embed structure into neural models. However, it differs by explicitly separating known and unknown dynamics rather than learning everything jointly. The iterative state-parameter estimation loop resembles [[EM algorithms]] and [[variational inference]] approaches. Related work includes latent ODE models and hybrid symbolic-neural systems, but this paper's use of the RTS smoother as an explicit training component is novel and connects classical control theory (Kalman filtering lineage) with modern deep learning.

## Practical Applications
- **Systems biology**: Learning gene regulatory networks where some feedback loops are known but others are hidden; measurements are typically sparse (e.g., a few genes out of thousands).
- **Climate modeling**: Incorporating known atmospheric physics while learning unresolved sub-grid turbulence or cloud dynamics from satellite/station data.
- **Neuroscience**: Reconstructing neural population dynamics when only a subset of neurons are recorded; retaining known connectivity constraints.
- **Industrial process control**: Inferring missing states in chemical reactors or manufacturing systems where some physics is well-understood but others (e.g., surface reactions) are poorly characterized.
- **Pharmacokinetics**: Modeling drug metabolism where absorption and elimination rates are partially known but individual variability is learned.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Scientific ML SaaS platform**: A web service for researchers to upload time-series data and specify which physics components are known (as symbolic equations); automatically learns missing components and returns interpretable, validated models—target: biotech, pharma, climate labs.
2. **Hybrid model discovery tool**: Software that automatically partitions a learned system into interpretable rules (for stakeholders) and neural residuals (for predictive power); used by chemical engineers and materials scientists for process optimization.
3. **Real-time state reconstruction service**: APIs for industrial IoT that fuse known dynamical models with neural components to infer full system state from sparse sensor networks; sell to manufacturing and energy companies.
4. **Uncertainty-quantified forecasting**: A product offering physics-informed digital twins with built-in confidence intervals; target: financial derivatives pricing on commodity futures (oil, agricultural) where some physics is known but markets add noise.
5. **Medical device modeling**: Software for medical device companies to validate hybrid models in regulatory submissions; combines FDA-trusted physics simulators with learned corrections from clinical trial data.

## Technical Limitations
- **Convergence guarantees**: No theoretical proof that the two-stage alternating procedure converges to a good solution or what that optimum is; may get stuck in local minima.
- **Partial observability assumption**: Requires that the system is sufficiently excited in the observed dimensions; if critical unobserved states are decoupled, the smoother cannot infer them.
- **Known physics must be accurate**: If the "known" ODE component is misspecified, the neural network may learn incorrect compensations rather than true missing physics.
- **Computational cost**: RTS smoothing is O(T) in sequence length T but requires storing and differentiating through entire trajectories; scales poorly to very long time series.
- **Stiffness handling**: While the paper claims robustness to stiff dynamics, the actual ODE solver (implicit vs. explicit) and step-size adaptation are not detailed; failure modes under extreme stiffness not explored.
- **Generalization beyond training regime**: No evidence that learned dynamics generalize to significantly different initial conditions or parameter regimes not seen during training.

## Open Questions
- Does the method work when the "known" physics is itself approximate or noisy? How sensitive is it to physics misspecification?
- Can you provide convergence rates or at least guarantees that the alternating procedure improves a well-defined objective?
- How does performance scale with the dimension of the latent state and the fraction of observed variables?
- Can you compare against recent end-to-end neural ODE baselines with explicit physics constraints (e.g., Lagrangian or Hamiltonian structure)?
- For truly stiff systems, does this outperform implicit solvers or structure-preserving schemes like symplectic integrators?
- Can you learn *which components* are unknown (structure learning) rather than assuming the split is known a priori?
- How robust is the method to measurement noise and outliers in the training data?

## Key Takeaway
Hybrid neural-physics models learn missing dynamics while preserving interpretability by smoothing states and training only the unknown neural components.
