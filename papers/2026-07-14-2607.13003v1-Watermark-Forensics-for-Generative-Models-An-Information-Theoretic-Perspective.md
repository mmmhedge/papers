# Watermark Forensics for Generative Models: An Information-Theoretic Perspective

**Authors:** Xiaoyu Li, Zheng Gao, Xiaoyan Feng, Jiaojiao Jiang, Yulei Sui et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-14 | **arXiv:** [2607.13003v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13003v1)

## Summary
This paper analyzes watermarks in generative model outputs through an information-theoretic lens, asking what it costs (in tokens) to detect whether text is machine-made, attribute it to a specific user, extract hidden payloads, or localize edited portions. The key finding is a tight asymptotic law: attributing text to one of N users requires Θ(log N / h) tokens on average, where h is the source entropy rate — a fundamental limit that applies universally to stationary sources and holds for major language models like GPT-2 and Qwen2.5.

## Theoretical Background
Watermarking in generative models typically embeds a signal into generated text that can be detected later — either as proof of machine generation or as evidence of tampering. The paper builds on information theory (particularly mutual information and surprisal), viewing a watermark as carrying a secret S (user identity or payload) and analyzing how much each token's entropy reveals about that secret.

Prior work treated watermarks as either *subtle* (a small signal on every token) or *loud* (sharp changes on a few tokens), but without principled sample complexity bounds. This paper unifies these through the *information profile* ν(t) = I(S; X_t | X_{<t}), which measures how much the t-th token leaks about the secret conditioned on earlier context. The framework connects to classical channel coding theory and statistical hypothesis testing — specifically, the idea that detection requires distinguishing marked from unmarked distributions, while attribution requires reliably sorting a sample among many hypotheses.

## Core Contributions

1. **Tight entropy-rate law for multi-user attribution**: For distortion-free watermarks and any stationary-ergodic source with entropy rate h, attributing a text to one of N users costs exactly Θ(log N / h) tokens (up to a 1+o(1) factor). This is the first sharp, universal bound for this problem.

2. **Decoder design that achieves the rate**: Shows that naive collision-counting (ranking candidates by likelihood ratio) can overcharge by unbounded factors; the correct decoder thresholds each candidate's surprisal individually, balancing false positives and false negatives optimally.

3. **Two-sided characterization**: Provides both upper bounds (achievable schemes) and matching lower bounds (converse theorems), making the law fully tight.

4. **Payload extraction bound**: Extracting an ℓ-bit hidden payload costs Θ(ℓ / h) tokens.

5. **Fundamental gaps and trade-offs**: Identifies a Θ(log N)-token window in which text provably contains a watermark yet cannot be reliably attributed to any user, and proves an uncertainty principle between footprint resolution (locating edited portions) and payload capacity.

6. **Empirical validation**: Confirms theoretical constants on GPT-2, Pythia-410M, and Qwen2.5.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[watermarking]], [[information theory]], and generative model security. It relates to:

- **Detection-only watermarking** (prior work asking "is this machine-made?"): Shows detection is fundamentally easier because it exploits *presence* (distributional distance) rather than information leakage.
- **Robustness and imperceptibility trade-offs** in traditional signal watermarking: Parallels the choice between subtle vs. loud encoding, but derives rigorous sample complexity costs.
- **Statistical hypothesis testing and channel coding**: The information profile mirrors achievable rates in channel coding; the decoder design echoes optimal sequential hypothesis testing.
- **Adversarial robustness**: The localization problem (identifying edited tokens) echoes adversarial perturbation localization in vision, though applied here to text.
- **[[Alignment]]** and safety: Watermarks are a tool for ensuring generated content is traceable and verifiable, relevant to model accountability and misuse prevention.

## Practical Applications

1. **Content provenance & anti-deepfake**: Publishers or AI labs embed user/source identifiers into generated text; receivers can prove who created it and detect forgery, scaling to millions of users.

2. **Copyright infringement detection**: Authors watermark their generated content; automated systems later identify which user's model leaked proprietary text without explicit attribution.

3. **Model output auditing in regulated domains**: Financial, medical, or legal AI systems watermark outputs so regulators can trace decisions back to specific model versions and users, satisfying audit trails.

4. **Bot detection and platform integrity**: Social media platforms watermark official LLM-generated content; detecting unmarked AI-generated posts (or misattributed ones) prevents impersonation and coordinated disinformation.

5. **Interactive content tagging**: Generative UI systems (image, text, code) watermark outputs; users can later ask "which tool made this?" and trace edits, useful for collaborative creative workflows.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **Watermark-as-a-Service SaaS**: Hosted API that takes a user ID and text, embeds a watermark, and provides a companion detector/attributor. Targets content creators, publishers, and enterprises needing legal proof of AI origin. Revenue: per-query licensing.

2. **Model provenance blockchain**: Embed watermarks into fine-tuned model weights and generated outputs; maintain an immutable ledger of who created what, when, and with what model. For AI supply chains and IP protection.

3. **Forensic analysis tool for lawyers**: Specialized software that, given disputed text, computes attribution probabilities across known model/user pairs and reports confidence intervals (based on theoretical bounds). Sells to law firms handling AI-generated content disputes.

4. **Real-time deepfake detector**: Browser extension + backend that watermark-verifies text on social media, news sites, and chat; surfaces confidence scores for whether content is authentic or machine-generated. B2C + B2B platform licensing.

5. **Generative model insurance & compliance**: Consultancy/auditing service that designs and validates watermarking schemes for companies deploying LLMs, ensuring regulatory compliance (traceability, robustness to editing) and mitigating liability. Charges per deployment engagement.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Distortion-free assumption**: The main theorem assumes watermarks impose zero statistical distortion (marked and unmarked distributions have identical entropy rate). In practice, even "subtle" watermarks add noise; the theory doesn't quantify the cost of this relaxation.

2. **Stationarity and ergodicity**: Bounds apply to stationary-ergodic sources; real language has non-stationary topics, style shifts, and domain dependence. The constants h may vary significantly across text types, weakening universality.

3. **Adversarial robustness not addressed**: The paper assumes honest senders and decoders. An adversary who knows the watermark scheme could strategically edit or paraphrase to evade detection or false-attribute; the theory provides no defense.

4. **Computational complexity ignored**: Decoding requires comparing a text against N user hypotheses (each thresholded independently); for millions of users, this scales poorly. No algorithmic complexity analysis.

5. **Localization trade-off under-explored**: The "footprint-resolution uncertainty principle" is stated but not fully characterized; unclear how practical systems should balance precision (which tokens are edited) vs. robustness.

6. **Empirical validation limited**: Experiments on three models (GPT-2, Pythia-410M, Qwen2.5) confirm trends but don't stress-test on adversarial edits, cross-domain generalization, or very large N (many users).

## Open Questions

1. **What happens with non-distortion-free watermarks?** Can you derive a rate-distortion trade-off showing how much information leakage *versus* imperceptibility costs in tokens?

2. **Can you break the Θ(log N) unattributable window?** Is there a scheme that makes every machine-generated token attributable, or is this gap fundamental?

3. **How does adversarial editing degrade attribution?** If an attacker knows the watermark and paraphrases to minimize information leakage, what is the robust sample complexity?

4. **Do the constants scale with model size?** The experiments use relatively small models; do h and the watermarking constants change qualitatively as models grow to 70B+ parameters?

5. **Can you achieve extraction *and* attribution simultaneously without doubling sample cost?** Or is there an inherent separation between the two capabilities?
