# ECGLight: Compute-Light Framework For Paper ECG Digitization and Myocardial Infarction Screening

**Authors:** Shreyasvi Natraj, Cyrus Achtari, Felice Gragnano, Andrea Milzi, Marco Valgimigli et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-08 | **arXiv:** [2607.07683v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07683v1)

## Summary
This paper presents ECGLight, a lightweight software pipeline that converts smartphone photos of paper ECGs into digital signals and screens for myocardial infarction (heart attack) in under 30 seconds on CPU-only devices. The system addresses a critical gap: millions of paper ECGs in remote clinics worldwide cannot access modern AI diagnostics due to poor connectivity and limited computing power, causing dangerous delays in detecting life-threatening heart conditions.

## Theoretical Background
ECG digitization requires two separate technical challenges: (1) **image-to-signal reconstruction** — converting a photograph of a printed ECG into calibrated electrical waveforms, a computer vision problem; and (2) **diagnostic classification** — identifying pathological patterns in the reconstructed signal, a time-series classification task. Prior work tackled these problems independently using resource-heavy models (large deep neural networks, cloud-based APIs). The paper builds on standard **convolutional neural networks (CNNs)** for feature extraction and **temporal convolution networks** for sequence modeling, but optimizes them for extreme computational constraints. **SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)** is a model-agnostic technique that explains individual predictions by quantifying each input feature's contribution to the final diagnosis.

## Core Contributions
1. **End-to-end compute-light pipeline** — first integrated system combining paper ECG digitization + MI screening that runs on CPU in <30s, not just individual components.
2. **High-fidelity signal reconstruction** — achieves 95.51% MI detection accuracy on PTB-XL despite starting from degraded smartphone/scanned images (a non-trivial image quality problem).
3. **Clinical validation on real hospital data** — tested on ECG-Matrix dataset from actual clinical settings, not just research benchmarks, with 88.89% accuracy for old myocardial infarction (OMI) detection.
4. **Interpretability for low-resource settings** — integrated SHAP explanations so clinicians in remote areas understand *why* the system flagged a case, critical for adoption and trust.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[Computer Vision|computer vision]] (document digitization, image-to-signal reconstruction) and [[Deep Learning|deep learning]] for medical diagnostics. It relates to broader trends in:
- **On-device ML** — similar spirit to mobile-first models (e.g., MobileNets, TensorFlow Lite) that prioritize inference speed over absolute accuracy.
- **Medical AI interpretability** — part of the push for explainable AI in clinical settings, relevant to [[Alignment|alignment]] concerns about deploying AI in high-stakes domains.
- **Digital health equity** — addresses the broader problem that advanced diagnostics are concentrated in wealthy healthcare systems; connects to work on low-resource ML.

Differs from prior ECG work by *not* requiring cloud connectivity, high-speed internet, or specialized hardware — a pragmatic constraint-driven design rather than accuracy-first optimization.

## Practical Applications
1. **Rural clinic networks in low-income countries** — a clinic in Uganda or Bangladesh can photograph a paper ECG on a phone, get instant MI screening, and make reperfusion therapy decisions without waiting for remote expert review.
2. **Emergency response in areas with poor connectivity** — paramedics in remote regions use the system to triage acute coronary syndrome cases before transport, reducing treatment delays.
3. **Legacy record digitization** — hospitals can systematically convert historical paper ECG archives into searchable digital databases for retrospective research or patient follow-up, without expensive scanning infrastructure.
4. **Offline diagnostic apps** — deployed as a smartphone app that works entirely offline, useful in flight (for airborne medical emergencies) or disaster zones where connectivity is disrupted.
5. **Primary care screening in underserved populations** — small clinics without cardiologists use the system as a first-line filter to identify high-risk patients who need specialist referral.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **"ECGHub"** — SaaS platform for hospitals/clinics to upload paper ECG images; backend runs ECGLight, stores results, and provides real-time analytics dashboards for population-level MI burden by region (monetize via per-scan fees or institutional subscriptions).
2. **Offline smartphone app** — branded as a first-aid tool (e.g., "HeartCheck") available on iOS/Android; users photograph paper ECGs or import scans, get instant risk scores with explanations; monetize via freemium (basic screening free, detailed reports/history $0.99–$4.99/month).
3. **Workplace health screening service** — partner with NGOs and employers in low-income countries to offer subsidized ECG campaigns; use ECGLight for rapid triage, flag positives for nurse follow-up; revenue from employers or health microinsurance partners.
4. **Telemedicine integration layer** — white-label ECGLight as a backend for existing telehealth platforms (Teladoc, 98point6, etc.) to enable remote screening without expensive digital ECG hardware; charge licensing fees per patient screened.
5. **Hospital legacy digitization service** — sell as a turnkey service (software + data pipeline) to digitize hospital archives; charge per-record or per-hospital pricing; target radiology/cardiology departments in developing nations.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Image quality dependency** — performance assumes reasonably clear smartphone photos or scans; severely degraded, water-damaged, or hand-written-on ECGs may fail; no explicit handling of extreme noise or occlusion.
2. **Single pathology focus** — trained on MI detection; cannot diagnose other important conditions (arrhythmias, hypertrophy, conduction blocks) despite ECG's broad clinical utility.
3. **Population generalization** — validated on PTB-XL (mostly European patients) and ECG-Matrix; unknown performance on non-Western populations with different baseline ECG morphology or disease epidemiology.
4. **Calibration uncertainty** — reconstructs 12-lead signals from 2D images; assumes printed ECG uses standard paper speed (25 mm/s) and voltage conventions; non-standard printouts may yield incorrect diagnoses.
5. **No time-series context** — processes single ECGs in isolation; cannot leverage serial ECGs (comparing changes over weeks/months) which clinicians routinely use to detect evolving MI.
6. **Interpretability depth** — SHAP explains feature importance, but doesn't validate that explanations match actual clinical reasoning; "black-box" neural networks may highlight spurious correlations that happen to work on training data.

## Open Questions
1. **How robust is signal reconstruction to extreme image degradation?** — Test on deliberately blurred, tilted, or partially occluded ECG photos; quantify failure modes.
2. **Can the system generalize to non-MI pathologies with minimal retraining?** — Explore multi-task learning or transfer learning to add arrhythmia, hypertrophy, or bundle-branch-block detection without 10x increase in compute.
3. **Does SHAP interpretability actually improve clinical adoption and decision-making?** — Run prospective usability studies with remote clinicians; measure whether explanations change their behavior or confidence.
4. **What is the cost-benefit of offline-only vs. hybrid (cached results + occasional cloud sync)?** — Analyze if occasional cloud refinement (when connectivity briefly available) improves accuracy significantly, justifying added infrastructure.
5. **How do serial ECGs improve diagnostic accuracy?** — If a clinic stores historical paper ECGs, can comparing new to old ECG changes boost MI detection sensitivity?
6. **Can this scale to other medical imaging modalities** (e.g., chest X-rays, fundus photos) with similar compute constraints?

## Key Takeaway
ECGLight democratizes AI-powered cardiac screening for the 2+ billion people without reliable internet or computing infrastructure by converting paper ECGs into diagnostic-grade digital signals on smartphones in under 30 seconds.
