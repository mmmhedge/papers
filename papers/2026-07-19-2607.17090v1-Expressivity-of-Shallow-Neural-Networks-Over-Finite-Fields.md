# Expressivity of Shallow Neural Networks Over Finite Fields

**Authors:** Maksym Zubkov, Carol Wu, Shiwei Yang, Param Mody, Yifei Chen
**Published:** 2026-07-19 | **arXiv:** [2607.17090v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.17090v1)

## Summary
This paper studies how expressive shallow neural networks with polynomial activations can be when operating over finite fields (number systems with limited elements) rather than real numbers. The authors measure expressivity by counting how many different functions a network can compute, connecting this problem to classical algebraic geometry and number theory—specifically, the famous Weil conjectures about counting solutions to polynomial equations. They show that the choice between finite and infinite fields dramatically changes what functions a network can learn.

## Theoretical Background
Neural network expressivity typically asks: what functions can a network architecture compute? Historically, this has been studied over real or complex numbers using approximation theory and topological arguments. This paper shifts the lens to *finite fields*—algebraic structures like ℤ/pℤ (integers modulo a prime p)—where intuitions from continuous mathematics break down. A key tool is the concept of a *neuromanifold*, the geometric object formed by all possible outputs of a network as weights vary. The paper connects this to counting *rational points on algebraic varieties over finite fields*, a problem with deep roots in algebraic geometry, notably through the Weil conjectures, which bound the number of solutions to polynomial equations over finite fields.

## Core Contributions
- **Neuromanifold cardinality as an expressivity measure**: Rather than using traditional approximation-theoretic notions, the authors quantify expressivity by the size of the image of the weight-to-output map—a discrete, combinatorial notion suited to finite fields.
- **Explicit bounds**: They derive both lower and upper bounds on neuromanifold cardinality for given architectures, making the expressivity question concrete and computable.
- **Field-characteristic separation**: The paper demonstrates an architecture where the neuromanifold has fundamentally different structure over finite versus characteristic-zero fields, proving that field choice is not merely a technical detail but central to expressivity.
- **Connection to Weil conjectures**: By framing the counting problem within algebraic geometry, the work bridges neural network theory and deep classical results in mathematics.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to classical work on [[neural network expressivity]] and [[universal approximation]], but sidesteps continuous approximation in favor of algebraic and combinatorial methods. The finite-field setting echoes recent interest in neural networks on discrete structures (e.g., [[graph neural networks]]) and cryptographic applications of neural nets. The paper's use of algebraic geometry is novel in the ML context but mirrors how mechanistic interpretability researchers study neural network circuits as algebraic objects. The shift in expressivity based on field characteristic parallels findings in [[mechanistic-interpretability]] where architectural features have qualitatively different behaviors under different conditions.

## Practical Applications
- **Cryptography and secure computation**: Finite-field neural networks are natural for applications in homomorphic encryption and secure multi-party computation, where one cannot work over reals.
- **Hardware efficiency**: Computing over finite fields can reduce numerical precision requirements and error accumulation, useful for edge devices and quantum computing contexts.
- **Error-correcting codes**: Finite-field networks may inform the design of neural decoders for communications systems operating in modular arithmetic.
- **Boolean circuit learning**: Networks over $\mathbb{F}_2$ (binary field) directly learn Boolean functions, relevant to logic synthesis and program verification.

## Technical Limitations
- **Scalability**: The paper focuses on *shallow* networks (one or two hidden layers); it is unclear whether expressivity bounds remain tight or computable for deeper architectures.
- **Practical expressivity vs. theoretical cardinality**: Counting the neuromanifold size does not directly tell us how *easy* it is for SGD or other learners to find useful functions—learnability and expressivity are distinct.
- **Small field sizes**: Finite fields used in practice may be very large (e.g., characteristic > 2^256 for cryptography), and the bounds may be loose or vacuous.
- **Monomial activations only**: The restriction to polynomial networks with monomial activations is narrow; ReLU, sigmoid, and modern activations over finite fields remain unexplored.
- **No learning theory**: The paper does not address sample complexity, generalization, or convergence guarantees for training these networks.

## Open Questions
- How does network depth affect expressivity and the tightness of bounds? Can the results extend to deep networks?
- What is the learnability landscape? How hard is it to optimize these networks in practice, and do standard gradient-based methods work?
- Can we extend the analysis to other activation functions beyond monomials (e.g., polynomial splines or other algebraic functions)?
- How do the bounds change as field size varies, and what is the phase transition behavior?
- Can these results inform the design of neural networks for cryptographic applications (e.g., learning with errors)?
- Do finite-field neural networks exhibit phase transitions analogous to those in random graph theory or statistical physics?

## Key Takeaway
Shallow neural networks over finite fields have expressivity determined by algebraic geometry, revealing how field structure fundamentally shapes what networks can compute.
