# When Does Muon Help Agentic Reinforcement Learning?

**Authors:** Kai Ruan, Jinghao Lin, Zihe Huang, Ziqi Zhou, Qianshan Wei et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-17 | **arXiv:** [2607.16169v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16169v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates whether Muon, an optimizer competitive with AdamW in large-scale pre-training, can improve reinforcement-learning post-training for language-model agents. Testing on ALFWorld (a benchmark for embodied agents that interact with text-based environments), the authors find that Muon applied selectively to hidden weights can substantially boost task success rates—sometimes doubling performance—but the benefit depends critically on which advantage estimator and learning rate are used. The work suggests optimizers, advantage estimators, and learning rates should be studied jointly rather than in isolation.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two separate optimization traditions. First, [[AdamW]] — the widely-adopted adaptive optimizer that maintains per-parameter learning rates and momentum — has dominated both pre-training and RL post-training. Second, [[Muon]] is a second-order optimizer designed for large-scale training that operates on Householder reflections of weight matrices. The paper also references **Group-in-Group Policy Optimization (GiGPO)** and **GraphGPO**, which are recent policy optimization algorithms for RL that use different advantage estimators (ways of measuring how good an action is relative to a baseline). The core tension: does Muon's inductive bias—which treats hidden layers differently from parameters—translate to RL, where trajectory-based learning has fundamentally different structure than next-token prediction?

## Core Contributions
1. **First systematic study** of Muon for agentic RL: prior work did not compare Muon to AdamW in sparse-reward RL settings with language models.
2. **Selective application insight**: Muon applied only to hidden weight matrices (not all parameters) achieves +88% success rate improvement under GiGPO on ALFWorld, whereas standard AdamW with higher learning rates fails.
3. **Hyperparameter interaction**: demonstrates that the benefit of Muon is not universal—it depends jointly on the choice of advantage estimator (GRPO vs. GraphGPO) and learning rate (3e-5 vs. 1e-5), suggesting prior optimizer comparisons that ignore these interactions may be misleading.
4. **Convergence acceleration**: Muon reaches success thresholds of 0.5 and 0.75 significantly earlier (30–60 updates) than baselines, not just higher final performance.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of three communities that rarely talk to each other:
- **Optimizer research** ([[AdamW]], [[Muon]]): mostly focused on pre-training efficiency; post-training is understudied.
- **Policy optimization for LLMs** ([[PPO]], GRPO, GraphGPO): typically fixes the optimizer (AdamW) and tunes reward models and learning rates; optimizer choice is rarely examined.
- **Embodied RL** (ALFWorld, interactive environments): benefits from better optimizers but research often conflates multiple hyperparameters.

The paper hints at but does not fully explore connections to [[RLHF]] (reinforcement learning from human feedback), where optimizer choice similarly remains largely fixed by convention.

## Practical Applications
1. **Language-model agent training**: any system deploying RL-trained agents in web navigation, tool use, or text-based game playing could see 2× speedup in convergence and higher final performance by swapping to Muon.
2. **Fine-tuning small language models**: the experiments use Qwen2.5-0.5B, a small model; Muon could reduce training cost for on-device or edge-deployed agents where compute is constrained.
3. **Sparse-reward RL systems**: ALFWorld is notoriously difficult (success rates near 0.3); Muon appears to help most when rewards are sparse and learning signal is weak, a common real-world scenario.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Optimizer-as-a-service for RL post-training**: A platform that automatically selects and tunes optimizers (Muon, AdamW, etc.) for a user's policy optimization setup; charges per training run.
2. **Agent training toolkit**: A framework that jointly searches over optimizer, advantage estimator, and learning rate for language-model agents; benchmarks against standard setups to sell performance gains.
3. **Consulting for RL fine-tuning**: Advise teams deploying language models on which optimizer + algorithm combos to use; package Muon + optimal hyperparameters as a "post-training recipe."
4. **Automated hyperparameter tuning for agentic RL**: Build a tool that runs parallel trials of different (Muon / AdamW, estimator, LR) triples and reports Pareto-optimal configurations; license to enterprise teams.
5. **Open-source Muon implementation for transformers**: Publish an optimized, drop-in Muon implementation for popular RL libraries (Hugging Face TRL, Ray RLlib); monetize via enterprise support contracts.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Single-seed results**: The experiments are exploratory and use a single random seed per condition. This is standard for early research but means small differences may not be statistically significant; multi-seed validation is acknowledged as future work.
2. **Single task and model size**: ALFWorld is one benchmark; effects may not generalize to other RL domains (robotics, game-playing, dialogue). Qwen2.5-0.5B is small; unclear if Muon helps at 7B or 70B scale where pre-training gains are largest.
3. **Selective application is ad-hoc**: Applying Muon only to hidden layers and not embeddings/output layers is empirically motivated but lacks theoretical justification; the paper does not explain *why* this decomposition works.
4. **Confounded hyperparameter search**: The paper does not exhaustively tune AdamW learning rates for each (estimator, seed) pair, so the +88% gain may partly reflect under-tuned baselines rather than true optimizer superiority.
5. **Advantage estimator interactions remain unexplained**: The paper observes that Muon helps GRPO at 3e-5 but GraphGPO at 1e-5, but does not mechanistically explain *why* these estimators respond differently to Muon's second-order structure.

## Open Questions
1. **Does Muon generalize across tasks and domains?** Test on web navigation (WebShop, VisualWebBrowser), robotics (simulated or real), dialogue reward modeling. Is ALFWorld special?
2. **Why does selective Muon (hidden layers only) work?** Ablate: apply Muon to embeddings, output layers, attention, etc. separately. Is this a properties-of-ALFWorld artifact or a general principle?
3. **Can we theoretically characterize when Muon helps?** The paper is empirical; a follow-up should derive or empirically model the conditions (advantage estimator structure, reward sparsity, trajectory length, etc.) under which Muon's geometry is beneficial for policy learning.
4. **What is the sample efficiency / wall-clock time cost?** Muon is computationally more expensive than AdamW; report actual training time, not just update counts. Is the +30-update convergence gain worth the compute overhead?
5. **Does Muon help in mixed pre-training + post-training?** Train a model from scratch with Muon throughout, then compare end-to-end to Muon-for-RL-only. Does pre-training with Muon interact with RL post-training improvements?
6. **Multi-task meta-learning**: Can we learn *when* to use Muon as a function of task properties (reward sparsity, episode length, action space size)?

## Key Takeaway
Muon can double language-model agent training performance on text-based tasks, but only when paired with the right advantage estimator and learning rate.
