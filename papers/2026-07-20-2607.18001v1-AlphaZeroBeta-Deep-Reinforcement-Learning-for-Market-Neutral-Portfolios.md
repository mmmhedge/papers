# AlphaZeroBeta: Deep Reinforcement Learning for Market-Neutral Portfolios

**Authors:** Boris Belyakov
**Published:** 2026-07-20 | **arXiv:** [2607.18001v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18001v1)

## Summary
This paper applies deep reinforcement learning to build market-neutral investment portfolios that generate excess returns (alpha) while staying uncorrelated with the broader market (near-zero beta). Traditional portfolio optimization breaks down during market shifts; AlphaZeroBeta learns adaptive strategies using neural networks trained on 10 years of stock data, achieving better risk-adjusted returns than conventional methods while maintaining the desired market-neutral properties.

## Theoretical Background
Market-neutral investing seeks to profit from relative mispricings while eliminating exposure to overall market movements—a goal typically pursued through [[factor models]] (statistical representations of return drivers) or [[convex optimization]] (mathematically guaranteed solutions to investment allocation problems). These classical approaches assume stable market structure and linear relationships between assets, which breaks under regime shifts.

Deep reinforcement learning (a family of machine-learning techniques where an agent learns by trial-and-error to maximize cumulative rewards) has recently shown promise in trading. The paper builds on [[Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)]] — a popular RL algorithm that balances exploration and exploitation — combined with recurrent neural networks (RNNs that maintain memory across time steps) and convolutional layers (neural building blocks that extract local patterns). The core insight is that RL can learn nonlinear, adaptive decision rules without explicitly assuming market structure.

## Core Contributions
1. **Composite reward design**: A multi-objective reward function that jointly optimizes excess return (alpha), benchmark correlation (pushing beta toward zero), and transaction costs — avoiding the common pitfall of optimizing a single metric that breaks in deployment.

2. **CNN-GRU policy architecture**: A hybrid neural network combining convolutional layers (to capture cross-sectional patterns across assets) with gated recurrent units (to maintain temporal state), rather than standard fully-connected or transformer architectures.

3. **Recurrent PPO training**: Adaptation of PPO to work with stateful RNN policies, addressing technical challenges in applying RL to sequential portfolio problems.

4. **Walk-forward evaluation (2014–2024)**: Rigorous backtesting protocol that rolls the training window forward over a decade, simulating realistic out-of-sample performance and avoiding lookahead bias.

5. **Empirical validation across seven equity indices**: Demonstrates generalization beyond a single market or asset class, with reported Sharpe ratio improvements over baselines and consistent near-zero correlation to benchmarks.

## Connections to Other Work
Related to [[RLHF]] (reinforcement learning from human feedback) in the sense that both inject domain-specific objectives into learned policies, though here the objectives are quantitative metrics rather than human preferences. Connects to broader work on [[agents]] in finance that learn adaptive trading rules, and shares methodological DNA with [[deep-learning]] applications to market microstructure and [[risk-management]].

Differs from traditional [[algorithmic-trading]] by avoiding hard-coded signals (moving averages, mean reversion rules) in favor of learned representations. Complements [[derivatives-pricing]] work by focusing on portfolio allocation rather than option valuation.

The walk-forward protocol addresses a known pitfall in quantitative finance: overfitting to historical data. This design choice reflects lessons from the broader [[optimization]] literature on cross-validation for time-series forecasting.

## Practical Applications
1. **Hedge fund alpha strategies**: Institutional investors seeking consistent returns uncorrelated with public markets can use AlphaZeroBeta as a core engine, reducing portfolio drag during bear markets.

2. **Long-short equity funds**: Pairs of long and short positions in complementary assets, where the model learns which pairs to weight to maintain zero beta while capturing mispricings.

3. **Factor-neutral overlays**: Institutions holding conventional portfolios can layer AlphaZeroBeta on top to generate additional returns while hedging broad market exposure.

4. **Retail robo-advisor risk management**: Wealth management platforms can embed market-neutral substrategies to reduce client portfolio volatility without reducing expected returns.

5. **Systematic commodity or currency trading**: Generalize the approach to other asset classes (bonds, FX, commodities) where market-neutral structures are valuable (e.g., hedging inflation without currency risk).

## Technical Limitations
1. **Data requirements and overfitting risk**: The model trains on 10 years of historical equity data. Sharpe ratios and beta statistics can appear better in backtests than in live trading due to subtle look-ahead bias, slippage, and regime breaks not captured in training data.

2. **Transaction cost modeling**: The reward function includes transaction costs, but real costs vary by market conditions (liquidity, time of day, position size). The simplified model may underestimate costs during stressed markets.

3. **Beta target may not be achievable**: Pushing beta toward zero requires short selling and frequent rebalancing. In practice, short constraints (borrow availability, regulatory limits) and market impact from large positions can prevent the model from achieving target correlations.

4. **Reward hacking**: A composite reward function can create unintended behaviors — e.g., the policy might reduce transaction costs by freezing positions, inadvertently reducing returns. The paper does not discuss safeguards against this.

5. **Scalability and liquidity**: Backtests assume frictionless execution; actual deployment on institutional-scale capital (billions) would face slippage and impact that degrade performance.

6. **Non-stationarity**: Financial markets change structurally (new regulations, volatility regimes, correlations shift). The model is trained on past data; generalization to future unseen regimes is uncertain and untested.

## Open Questions
1. **Regime adaptation**: How does AlphaZeroBeta perform during unprecedented market shocks (e.g., March 2020, SVB collapse)? Does the model degrade gracefully or catastrophically?

2. **Interpretability**: What patterns does the CNN-GRU learn? Are they economically intuitive (e.g., momentum, mean reversion) or opaque? Can we understand why the model deviates from the benchmark?

3. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: How sensitive are results to the relative weights in the composite reward (alpha vs. beta vs. transaction cost)? Can the model learn these weights from data instead of hand-tuning?

4. **Multi-asset generalization**: The paper tests seven equity indices; would the same architecture work for bonds, commodities, or FX? What modifications are needed?

5. **Explainability for regulators**: How would an asset manager or regulator audit and validate this model's decisions? What reporting would be required under MiFID II or similar frameworks?

6. **Out-of-distribution testing**: Backtest on data from other countries, time periods, or asset universes that the model has never seen to quantify true robustness.

## Key Takeaway
Reinforcement learning can build market-neutral portfolios that beat traditional optimization by learning adaptive strategies from market data over 10 years.
