# SPyCE: Skill-Policy Co-evolution for Multimodal Agents

**Authors:** Ru Zhang, Weijie Qiu
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.13854v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13854v1)

## Summary
This paper presents SPyCE, a framework that trains multimodal agents (systems that reason about images and use tools) by evolving a reusable skill library alongside the policy during learning. Rather than treating trajectories as scalar rewards or static memory, SPyCE distills them into hierarchical skills that improve over time, creating a feedback loop where better policies generate better skills, which in turn guide better policies. The approach substantially outperforms existing reinforcement learning and memory-based methods across eight benchmarks.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on reinforcement learning (RL) for sequential decision-making in complex environments. Traditional RL methods reduce rich trajectories to scalar rewards, losing intermediate reasoning structure. Memory-augmented approaches (retrieval-based methods) retain past experience but fail to consolidate reusable patterns into the policy itself — they retrieve at test time without integration. The paper is also informed by hierarchical RL concepts, where high-level skills abstract lower-level operations, and skill discovery literature that seeks to identify reusable behavioral primitives. Multimodal reasoning combines vision (visual grounding, scene understanding) with language and tool invocation, requiring agents to maintain coherent reasoning across modalities.

## Core Contributions
1. **Hierarchical skill distillation**: Trajectories are decomposed into two skill types — execution skills (local visual operations) and workflow skills (high-level tool orchestration) — rather than collapsed to scalars or stored statically.
2. **Co-evolution mechanism**: A closed-loop training process where the policy conditions on retrieved skills to improve rollouts, while the skill library evolves using the best rollouts generated by the improved policy.
3. **Empirical validation**: Demonstration across eight benchmarks that co-evolution substantially outperforms RL and memory-based baselines, with ablations confirming both the hierarchical design and co-evolution are essential.

## Connections to Other Work
This work relates to [[hierarchical reinforcement learning]] (decomposing tasks into subtasks) and [[skill discovery]] in RL. It contrasts with pure [[RLHF]]-style approaches that treat trajectories as binary feedback. The memory-augmented framing connects to retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) concepts in language models, but extends them by integrating retrieval feedback into policy learning. The paper also implicitly engages with curriculum learning — the evolving skill library acts as an implicit curriculum. Multi-task learning and meta-learning frameworks (learning to learn) share the goal of reusability across tasks.

## Practical Applications
1. **Autonomous visual reasoning systems**: Web automation agents that learn to navigate, extract, and manipulate visual information on unfamiliar websites by reusing learned visual and workflow skills.
2. **Robotic manipulation**: Robots learning manipulation skills (e.g., grasping, pushing) that transfer across objects and scenes through hierarchical skill abstraction.
3. **Scientific discovery agents**: Systems that design and execute multi-step experiments (e.g., chemistry labs), where workflow skills capture experimental protocols and execution skills handle instrument control.
4. **Document understanding and processing**: Multi-step document intelligence pipelines where agents learn reusable skills for table extraction, entity linking, and form completion.
5. **Game-playing AI**: Agents in partially observable visual environments (e.g., Minecraft, complex video games) that compose learned execution and strategy skills.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Enterprise RPA + Vision Platform**: A robotic process automation (RPA) tool that learns visual and workflow skills from human demonstrations, progressively improving its ability to automate cross-application workflows without hard-coded rules.
2. **Self-improving Data Labeling Service**: A platform where a multimodal agent learns labeling workflows by co-evolving with human-corrected rollouts, reducing reliance on manual annotation over time.
3. **AI Research Assistant SaaS**: A subscription service for researchers that executes multi-step experimental protocols (literature search, data retrieval, analysis) by learning domain-specific skills from user interactions.
4. **Autonomous Quality Assurance**: A testing framework for visual UIs that learns visual and interaction skills, catching regressions and maintaining test coverage as products evolve.
5. **Smart Document Routing**: An AI system for enterprises that learns to classify, extract, and route complex documents by acquiring reusable extraction and classification skills, improving with each processed batch.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability of skill library**: As the skill library grows, retrieval and integration costs may become prohibitive; the paper doesn't discuss how to prune or organize skills in long-running systems.
2. **Skill generalization**: Learned skills are task-specific; it's unclear how well skills transfer across domains with different visual or tool distributions.
3. **Reward signal dependency**: The framework still requires meaningful RL rewards; noisy or sparse reward signals may prevent both policy and skill improvement.
4. **Computational cost**: Co-evolution requires multiple forward passes (policy rollouts + skill updates); wall-clock training time comparisons and sample efficiency are not discussed.
5. **Multimodal grounding assumptions**: The method assumes reliable vision encoders and tool APIs; degradation under adversarial images, domain shift, or unreliable tools is not addressed.
6. **Skill interpretability**: While hierarchical, the learned skills may not be human-interpretable, limiting debugging and safety auditing.

## Open Questions
1. How does SPyCE scale to very large skill libraries, and what are principled strategies for skill pruning or hierarchical organization?
2. Can skills learned in one domain transfer to structurally similar tasks in different visual domains (e.g., web UIs → mobile apps)?
3. What is the sample efficiency gain over standard RL per benchmark, and is there a sweet spot for skill library size?
4. How robust is the approach to partial policy failures or corrupted skill updates early in training?
5. Can skill interpretability be improved (e.g., via natural language annotations) without hurting performance?
6. How does SPyCE perform with very sparse rewards or in fully observation-only settings without direct task labels?

## Key Takeaway
Multimodal agents learn faster by evolving reusable skills alongside their policy in a closed feedback loop.
