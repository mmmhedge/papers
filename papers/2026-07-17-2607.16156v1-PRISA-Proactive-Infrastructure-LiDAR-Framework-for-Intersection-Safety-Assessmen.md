# PRISA: Proactive Infrastructure LiDAR Framework for Intersection Safety Assessment

**Authors:** Tam Bang, Hussam Abubakr, Emiliano de la Garza Villarreal, Truc Phuong Nguyen, Austin Harris et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-17 | **arXiv:** [2607.16156v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16156v1)

## Summary
PRISA is a roadside LiDAR-based system that detects dangerous situations at intersections before crashes happen. It combines privacy-preserving sensors, automatic trajectory prediction, and real-time safety metrics to monitor pedestrians, cyclists, and vehicles — all running fast enough to be deployed at actual intersections.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on three foundational ideas. First, LiDAR-based perception — using laser scanning to detect objects in 3D space, which works well in low light unlike cameras. Second, trajectory prediction — training neural networks to forecast where moving agents will be in the near future, a core problem in autonomous systems and safety monitoring. Third, surrogate safety metrics like Time-to-Collision (TTC) and Post-Encroachment Time (PET), which quantify crash risk without waiting for actual collisions. The framework also leverages edge computing — running inference on local hardware (NVIDIA Jetson) rather than sending data to cloud servers, enabling low-latency real-time decisions.

## Core Contributions
1. **Automatic data curation pipeline**: The system bootstraps training data from its own perception outputs, eliminating manual annotation burden — a practical solution to the cold-start problem in deployed safety systems.
2. **Dual safety metrics**: Combines TTC for head-on/following conflicts with PPET for crossing and vulnerable road user (VRU) interactions, providing more nuanced risk assessment than single metrics.
3. **Edge deployment validation**: Demonstrates end-to-end latency of ~194ms on actual hardware at a real intersection, proving the system meets hard real-time constraints (within 2.4-second prediction horizon).
4. **Privacy-preserving architecture**: Roadside infrastructure processes only anonymized geometric data (positions/velocities), not video, reducing privacy concerns vs. camera-based systems.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[computer-vision]] perception (LiDAR-based 3D object detection), [[multi-agent-systems]] (modeling interactions between vehicles and VRUs), and safety-critical monitoring. It relates to trajectory prediction research in autonomous driving contexts but applies it defensively — using predictions to detect hazards rather than to control a vehicle. The plug-and-play risk assessment module echoes modular design patterns in [[reinforcement-learning]]-based control systems. The automatic data curation approach connects to self-supervised and weakly-supervised learning trends in deep learning.

## Practical Applications
1. **Smart traffic signal control**: Risk scores feed into adaptive signal timing to reduce conflicts at dangerous intersections.
2. **VRU protection systems**: Alert pedestrians/cyclists via phone notifications or roadside displays when collision risk is detected.
3. **Intersection audits**: Cities use historical risk data to identify which intersections need redesign (road geometry, signal timing, signage).
4. **Insurance and liability**: Municipalities document near-miss events to justify infrastructure investments or defend against negligence claims.
5. **Connected vehicle integration**: Risk predictions broadcast to approaching autonomous or semi-autonomous vehicles for cooperative collision avoidance.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Urban Safety Dashboard**: Sell real-time intersection risk monitoring as a SaaS platform to city traffic departments; include heatmaps, anomaly alerts, and predictive maintenance for infrastructure.
2. **VRU Warning System**: Hardware + app service that alerts pedestrians and cyclists of high-risk zones in real-time, monetize through city contracts or insurance premium discounts.
3. **Intersection-as-a-Service**: Deploy modular LiDAR + compute packages at municipalities on capex-light subscription model; white-label the risk assessment engine.
4. **Autonomous Vehicle Safety Validator**: License PRISA's detection pipeline to AV testing facilities to validate their perception and planning stacks at real intersections.
5. **Predictive Infrastructure Maintenance**: Use trajectory and collision near-miss data to forecast pavement/signal wear and schedule maintenance before failure — sell to public works departments.

## Technical Limitations
1. **LiDAR-specific challenges**: Performance may degrade with heavy rain, fog, or snow (reflectivity/penetration issues); also sensitive to mounting angle and calibration drift over months.
2. **Occlusion blind spots**: Roadside sensors have limited field of view; vehicles or pedestrians hidden behind parked cars or buildings are invisible until they enter the sensor range.
3. **Trajectory prediction horizon**: 2.4-second forecasts are useful for slow-speed intersections but may be insufficient for high-speed highway merges; longer horizons increase latency or require more complex models.
4. **Site-specific tuning**: Self-supervised training assumes the automated perception outputs are accurate; systematic labeling errors (e.g., consistent misdetection of cyclists) propagate into the trajectory model.
5. **Multi-agent coordination**: The system detects conflicts but doesn't model high-level intent (e.g., is the driver changing lanes intentionally or drifting?), potentially generating false alarms.
6. **Deployment costs**: LiDAR + edge compute hardware + installation is capital-intensive; cost may limit adoption to high-crash intersections only.

## Open Questions
1. How does PRISA performance scale across diverse intersection geometries (roundabouts, T-intersections, multi-lane grids)? Does a single model generalize or does each site need retraining?
2. Can the trajectory prediction model be pre-trained on public datasets (e.g., nuScenes, Waymo) to reduce cold-start time before self-supervised learning kicks in?
3. What is the false positive rate in live deployment? How sensitive is the system to LiDAR calibration drift and seasonal weather changes?
4. Can the framework handle rare, high-consequence scenarios (e.g., sudden vehicle failure, pedestrian distraction) that may be underrepresented in training data?
5. How should PRISA integrate with connected or autonomous vehicles — should it broadcast warnings or accept input from vehicle sensors to improve its own predictions?

## Key Takeaway
Roadside LiDAR with self-trained trajectory prediction detects intersection crashes milliseconds before impact, enabling real-time urban safety interventions.
