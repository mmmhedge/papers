# A Minimalist Retargeting-Guided Reinforcement Learning Recipe for Dexterous Manipulation

**Authors:** Yunhai Feng, Natalie Leung, Jiaxuan Wang, Lujie Yang, Haozhi Qi et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-13 | **arXiv:** [2607.11874v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11874v1)

## Summary
This paper presents REGRIND, a method for teaching robots dexterous manipulation (like using scissors or screwdrivers) by learning from human demonstrations combined with reinforcement learning. The approach retargets human hand motions to robot references, trains a policy in simulation to track object-centric landmarks, and transfers it directly to real hardware—achieving fluid, human-like behavior without extensive real-world retraining.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on two established paradigms: (1) **motion retargeting** — the process of mapping human movements onto robot kinematics while preserving task-relevant spatial relationships — and (2) **sim-to-real reinforcement learning** — training policies in simulation and transferring them to physical systems. Recent successes in whole-body humanoid control (e.g., full-body locomotion) demonstrated that retargeting human demonstrations as reference trajectories, then using RL to learn tracking policies, can scale to complex behaviors. However, dexterous manipulation presents additional challenges: contact-rich dynamics (friction, collision), mode switching (grasping vs. sliding), and force control requirements that don't obviously follow the whole-body recipe. The paper leverages residual learning (where a neural network learns corrections to a reference policy) to bridge the gap between the retargeted reference and achievable robot behavior.

## Core Contributions
1. **Contact-aware retargeting**: A retargeting scheme that preserves hand-object spatial and contact relationships—not just kinematic structure—enabling meaningful reference trajectories for multi-fingered hands in contact-rich tasks.
2. **Object-centric tracking objective**: Rather than tracking joint angles or end-effector poses directly, policies learn to maintain landmarks on the manipulated object, reducing sensitivity to robot morphology differences.
3. **Systematic sim-to-real analysis**: Identifies and empirically validates key factors enabling zero-shot transfer in dexterous manipulation (e.g., accurate system identification, contact modeling fidelity, residual policy architecture choices).
4. **Practical demonstrations**: End-to-end pipeline validated on two different multi-fingered hands (suggesting generality) across tool-use tasks requiring sustained contact and precision.

## Connections to Other Work
This extends [[RLHF]]-adjacent ideas (using human guidance to shape RL learning) into the robotics domain, building on recent success of retargeting-guided control in [[humanoid locomotion]]. The object-centric keypoint tracking objective relates to visual imitation learning and contact-implicit planning methods that avoid explicit contact mode specification. The systematic sim-to-real transfer analysis complements ongoing work in [[domain randomization]] and [[sim-to-real]] bridging, though this paper emphasizes contact dynamics and force control—areas where standard approaches often fail. The residual learning formulation is conceptually similar to policy distillation and correction networks used in imitation learning pipelines.

## Practical Applications
1. **Robotic assembly lines**: Manufacturing tasks (inserting screws, fastening bolts, precision alignment) where human demonstrations are readily available but dexterous execution is complex.
2. **Home robotics**: Learning manipulation skills (opening jars, using utensils, handling fragile objects) from video or kinesthetic demonstrations.
3. **Surgical robotics**: Acquiring delicate manipulation skills from surgeon demonstrations for minimally invasive procedures.
4. **Prosthetics and exoskeletons**: Translating human hand motions directly to assistive devices with multi-fingered hands.
5. **Tool-use learning**: Rapid skill acquisition for robots in unstructured environments (construction, maintenance) where task-specific policies are needed quickly.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Dexterous Manipulation-as-a-Service**: Cloud platform where manufacturers upload videos of manual assembly tasks, receive trained, sim-validated robot policies, and deploy them with hardware integration support.
2. **Robotic Skill Library**: Marketplace for pre-recorded human demonstrations and corresponding trained policies for common manipulation tasks (tool use, assembly, food prep), reducing engineering time for robot deployment.
3. **Imitation Learning Consulting**: Service helping manufacturers retarget their workforce's expertise into robot control policies during factory automation transitions, preserving institutional knowledge.
4. **Real-time Teleoperation with Learning**: VR/AR interface that streams human hand gestures to multi-fingered robots in real time, logging demonstrations that automatically fine-tune policies for autonomous repetition.
5. **Contact-Rich Simulation Engine**: Specialized robotics simulator (as-a-service) optimized for accurate contact dynamics and sim-to-real transfer validation, targeting dexterous manipulation teams lacking in-house simulation expertise.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Single demonstration dependency**: The method relies on a single human demo; robustness to demo variation or coverage of manipulation modes (grasping transitions, force adjustments) under perturbation is unclear.
2. **Contact modeling assumptions**: Accurate sim-to-real transfer assumes contact dynamics are well-modeled in simulation; errors in friction parameters, contact geometry, or material properties could compound during skill transfer.
3. **Object morphology specificity**: Policies trained on specific objects may not generalize to different sizes or shapes of the same category (e.g., scissors of different lengths).
4. **Scalability to longer horizons**: Experiments focus on short-horizon tool-use tasks; applicability to tasks requiring sustained multi-stage manipulation or recovery from perturbations is untested.
5. **Hardware specificity**: Results shown on two hands; transferability to significantly different morphologies (e.g., underactuated hands, cable-driven systems) not demonstrated.
6. **System identification burden**: Zero-shot transfer requires careful system ID; the practical overhead and sensitivity to ID errors are not fully characterized.

## Open Questions
1. **Multi-demo learning**: How does policy performance scale with multiple human demonstrations of the same task? Can diversity improve robustness?
2. **Contact mode prediction**: Can the method learn to anticipate and initiate contact mode switches (grasp-to-slide, etc.) without explicit state supervision?
3. **Generalization across objects**: How sensitive are learned policies to object geometry, size, and material properties? Can meta-learning improve generalization?
4. **Failure mode recovery**: Do residual policies learn meaningful recovery behaviors when tracking diverges, or do they simply fail gracefully?
5. **Human-in-the-loop adaptation**: Could policies be efficiently refined with limited additional real-world interactions without retraining from scratch?
6. **Larger-scale task chaining**: Can retargeting guide policies for multi-step tasks (e.g., using a tool, storing it, fetching another)? How do reference concatenations affect transfer?

## Key Takeaway
Learning dexterous robot skills from human video via contact-aware retargeting and residual RL transfers directly to hardware without real-world retraining.
