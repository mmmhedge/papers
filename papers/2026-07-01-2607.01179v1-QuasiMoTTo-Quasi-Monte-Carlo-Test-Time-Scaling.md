# QuasiMoTTo: Quasi-Monte Carlo Test-Time Scaling

**Authors:** Michael Y. Li, Anthony Zhan, Kanishk Gandhi, Noah D. Goodman, Emily B. Fox
**Published:** 2026-07-01 | **arXiv:** [2607.01179v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01179v1)

## Summary

This paper introduces QuasiMoTTo, a method that makes inference compute more efficient by generating correlated samples instead of independent ones when scaling language model outputs. Rather than drawing samples randomly and independently (wasting compute on redundant solutions), QuasiMoTTo uses quasi-Monte Carlo sampling to spread samples more evenly across the solution space, achieving the same accuracy with 25–47% fewer samples while maintaining mathematical correctness for policy-gradient training.

## Theoretical Background

The paper builds on two key ideas: (1) **test-time scaling** — the practice of improving model performance by generating multiple candidate outputs per problem and selecting the best one (measured by pass@k metrics); and (2) **quasi-Monte Carlo methods** — numerical techniques that replace random uniform sampling with carefully structured, low-discrepancy sequences that cover a space more evenly than independent random sampling. The authors exploit a reparameterization of **autoregressive sampling** (how language models generate text token-by-token) as inverse-CDF sampling, which reduces the problem to drawing uniform random variables. By drawing these uniforms via QMC instead of i.i.d., they preserve the marginal distribution of each sample while introducing beneficial correlation across the batch.

## Core Contributions

1. **Reframing autoregressive sampling as inverse-CDF sampling**, making it amenable to QMC methods — a non-obvious connection that enables drop-in replacement of i.i.d. sampling.

2. **An unbiased bootstrap estimator for pass@k** that works correctly with correlated samples, since standard pass@k estimators break when samples are not independent.

3. **Empirical demonstration that QuasiMoTTo saturates a theoretical upper bound** on pass@k performance for any marginal-preserving sampler, suggesting the method is near-optimal within its design space.

4. **Extension to policy-gradient RL (GRPO)**, showing 50% fewer training steps needed to match i.i.d. baselines, indicating the method improves learning signals in reinforcement learning settings.

## Connections to Other Work

This work sits at the intersection of inference-time scaling (related to [[chain-of-thought prompting]] and [[majority voting]]), [[reinforcement learning]] applied to language models, and classical numerical methods. It complements existing approaches like [[RLHF]] and [[DPO]] by making the sampling process more efficient rather than changing the learning objective. The theoretical framing (marginal-preserving samplers, upper bounds on pass@k) extends classical sampling theory to the language model domain. The RL application connects to recent work on process-reward models and iterative refinement during training.

## Practical Applications

1. **Cost reduction in inference**: Organizations running inference at scale (e.g., API providers, search engines) can reduce compute spend by 25–50% while maintaining quality.

2. **Training language models with RL**: When fine-tuning models via policy gradients (e.g., for reasoning or code generation), QuasiMoTTo reduces the number of forward passes needed per training step.

3. **Real-time reasoning systems**: Systems that must generate multiple attempts quickly (e.g., competitive mathematics solvers, code verification) benefit from faster sample generation without quality loss.

4. **Decoding at resource-constrained edges**: Mobile or embedded deployment scenarios where inference compute is the bottleneck gain proportionally more from reduced sample counts.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Correlation breaks downstream assumptions**: While QuasiMoTTo preserves marginals, correlated samples may violate independence assumptions in other parts of the pipeline (e.g., ensemble diversity metrics, some uncertainty estimation techniques).

2. **Empirical vs. theoretical guarantee**: The paper empirically saturates an upper bound on pass@k, but this bound is derived for marginal-preserving samplers and may not account for all real-world factors (e.g., diversity preferences, downstream task structure).

3. **Limited to parallel generation**: QMC sampling must happen in parallel per problem; sequential, adaptive sampling strategies (e.g., best-first search) cannot directly use QMC without loss of structure.

4. **Dimensionality curse in QMC**: As sequence length grows, the effective benefit of QMC may degrade due to the curse of dimensionality in high-dimensional spaces; the paper does not fully explore this boundary.

5. **RL stability**: While GRPO results are promising, policy-gradient training is notoriously sensitive to batch composition; correlation-induced changes to batch structure could interact badly with off-policy corrections or other RL tricks.

## Open Questions

1. **How does QuasiMoTTo perform on very long-horizon reasoning tasks** where sequence length is orders of magnitude larger? Does the benefit persist?

2. **Can adaptive QMC schemes (e.g., randomized low-discrepancy sequences) further improve performance** by balancing correlation and exploration?

3. **How does QuasiMoTTo interact with other inference-time tricks**, such as [[speculative decoding]], dynamic batch sizing, or early exit strategies?

4. **What is the theoretical pass@k upper bound for QuasiMoTTo relative to oracle optimal samplers** (not just marginal-preserving ones)?

5. **Does QuasiMoTTo help or hurt diversity-sensitive tasks** (e.g., brainstorming, open-ended generation) where you want variety, not just quality?

6. **Can the method be extended to non-autoregressive or diffusion-based generation** where the sampling structure is fundamentally different?

## Key Takeaway

By replacing independent samples with correlated samples drawn via quasi-Monte Carlo, QuasiMoTTo cuts the number of model samples needed by ~30–50% while preserving correctness for both inference evaluation and RL training, opening a new lever for compute-efficient scaling.
