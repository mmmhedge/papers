# LLM-Based Examination of Eligibility Criteria from Securities Prospectuses at the German Central Bank

**Authors:** Serhii Hamotskyi, Akash Kumar Gautam, Christian Hänig
**Published:** 2026-06-25 | **arXiv:** [2606.27316v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27316v1)

## Summary
This paper presents the first LLM-based system for automating eligibility verification of securities as collateral at the German Central Bank, replacing traditional NER-based approaches with a generative information extraction pipeline. The system decomposes the task into extraction, normalization, and interpretation stages, achieving 91% precision on document-level eligibility decisions while handling noisy OCR, linguistic variance, and bilingual (German-English) prospectuses—challenges that rigid span-based NER methods struggle with.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on two main traditions:

1. **Information Extraction (IE)**: Prior efforts used Named Entity Recognition (NER), which relies on rigid span-based constraints and typically requires manually annotated training data for each entity type. NER is brittle when facing OCR noise and linguistic variation.

2. **Generative Language Models**: Recent advances in LLMs have demonstrated strong performance on open-ended text understanding tasks without task-specific training data. This paper applies the generative IE paradigm—where LLMs output structured information as free-form text—rather than constraining outputs to predefined spans.

3. **Evaluation Methodology**: Traditional IE metrics (precision/recall at span level) don't capture semantic correctness. The paper introduces LLM-as-a-judge evaluation, which assesses whether extracted information has the right meaning and value, not just position in the text.

## Core Contributions
1. **First LLM application to securities eligibility verification**: Shifts from NER to generative IE for a real-world regulatory compliance task at a central bank.

2. **Task decomposition pipeline**: Breaking eligibility examination into three explicit stages (extraction → normalization → interpretation) allows handling of OCR noise and code-switching without rigid constraints.

3. **Value-based evaluation methodology**: Introduces LLM-as-a-judge scoring that evaluates semantic correctness rather than span-level F1, better capturing what actually matters in compliance (did we correctly determine if a security is eligible?).

4. **Bilingual robustness**: Demonstrates effective handling of interleaved German-English text, a practical challenge in international financial documents.

5. **Conservative operating profile**: The system minimizes false acceptance (high precision trade-off), critical for compliance where false negatives (rejecting valid collateral) are less costly than false positives.

## Connections to Other Work
- **[[Named Entity Recognition]]**: Traditional baseline; this work shows LLM-based generation can outperform rigid NER on noisy, semi-structured legal/financial text.
- **[[Domain Adaptation]]**: Securities eligibility is a domain-specific task; the paper demonstrates LLMs' ability to adapt without task-specific training data.
- **[[Information Extraction]]** and **[[Entity Relation Extraction]]**: Generative IE is an emerging alternative to span-based extraction; this is a real-world validation of that paradigm.
- **[[Multilingual NLP]]**: The German-English code-switching aspect connects to multilingual robustness literature.

## Practical Applications
1. **Central bank collateral management**: Automating daily eligibility checks for thousands of bond prospectuses submitted as collateral, reducing manual review time from hours to minutes.

2. **Regulatory compliance automation**: Extending the approach to other central banks (ECB, Federal Reserve) for similar asset verification tasks.

3. **Financial document analysis**: Insurance companies, pension funds, or investment firms verifying eligibility of securities under their own regulatory frameworks.

4. **Legal document screening**: Law firms automating initial screening of contracts, prospectuses, or regulatory filings to extract material clauses and verify compliance with specific criteria.

5. **Multilingual document processing**: Any financial institution dealing with cross-border assets in multiple languages (e.g., EU-wide operations) could use this for consistent interpretation across language variants.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Compliance-as-a-Service (CaaS) platform**: A B2B SaaS tool for central banks, regulators, and large financial institutions to automatically verify asset eligibility against custom regulatory criteria. Could support pluggable rule sets for different jurisdictions.

2. **Financial document intelligence engine**: An API that ingests prospectuses, bond documents, and regulatory filings, then extracts and normalizes key financial/legal metadata (coupon rates, covenants, issuer ratings, eligibility criteria) in structured JSON. Marketed to asset managers and fintechs.

3. **Bilingual legal document processor**: Specialized service for institutions with German, French, and English documents (common in EU banking). Handles OCR-corrupted scans, extracts terms, and flags contradictions across language versions.

4. **Risk assessment workflow tool**: A UI tool for bank risk teams where LLM extraction populates a database of securities with eligibility flags, confidence scores, and human-review queues (prioritizing borderline cases). Reduces manual review by 70%+.

5. **Knowledge base for collateral management**: Aggregate anonymized eligibility decisions across institutions to build a "collateral knowledge graph" that helps smaller banks and asset managers understand what characteristics make securities eligible, sold as a subscription intelligence product.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Hallucination and confidence calibration**: LLMs can fabricate plausible-sounding financial details (e.g., coupon rates, maturity dates) that don't appear in the document. The paper doesn't deeply address how to detect or mitigate this—critical for regulatory use.

2. **Explainability and auditability**: When an LLM decides a security is ineligible, regulators may demand explicit reasoning. Generative IE is less transparent than rule-based or span-based methods; the paper doesn't solve the black-box audit problem.

3. **Scale and cost**: Calling an LLM API for each prospectus can be expensive at scale (e.g., processing thousands of documents daily at a central bank). No discussion of latency, throughput, or cost optimization.

4. **Dependency on model versions**: The system is tied to a specific LLM version (likely GPT-4 or similar). Future model updates or changes in OpenAI/other APIs could break the pipeline; no fallback strategy discussed.

5. **Limited error analysis**: The paper doesn't deeply analyze failure modes—which types of prospectuses or criteria cause the 9% error rate? Are there systematic biases (e.g., certain languages or issuer types)?

6. **Assumption of well-formed input**: While the system handles OCR noise better than NER, extreme corruption or hand-written documents might still break it. Practical limits are unclear.

7. **Evaluation scope**: Evaluation is on a single task (German Central Bank eligibility); generalization to other central banks or regulatory regimes is not demonstrated.

## Open Questions
1. **How does hallucination scale with document complexity?** Can the system reliably extract precise financial figures (dates, percentages, ratings) without fabrication? What confidence thresholds should trigger human review?

2. **Can the approach work with truly zero-shot prompting, or does it require in-context examples?** The paper doesn't clearly separate zero-shot vs. few-shot performance, important for deployment at new institutions.

3. **How sensitive is the system to LLM model choice?** Would smaller, open-source models (Llama, Mistral) achieve similar performance, enabling on-premise deployment and cost reduction?

4. **What is the cost-benefit analysis vs. human review?** What is the actual time/cost savings per document? Does reducing false acceptance rate by 1% justify the cost of API calls?

5. **Can the task decomposition (extraction → normalization → interpretation) be automated end-to-end, or does it require human validation at each step?** What is the bottleneck?

6. **How does the system handle edge cases like ambiguous criteria or criteria that conflict across prospectus versions?** Can it flag unresolvable conflicts?

7. **Can LLM-as-a-judge evaluation be validated against ground truth human decisions?** Is there inter-rater agreement among human experts, and does the LLM judge align with human consensus?

## Key Takeaway
LLM-based generative information extraction can replace rigid NER systems for regulatory compliance verification in finance, achieving high precision and robustness to OCR noise and linguistic variance—but only if hallucination, explainability, and cost are carefully managed in production.
