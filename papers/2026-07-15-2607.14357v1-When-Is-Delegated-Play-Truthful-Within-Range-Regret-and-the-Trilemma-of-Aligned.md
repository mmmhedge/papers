# When Is Delegated Play Truthful? Within-Range Regret and the Trilemma of Aligned Delegation

**Authors:** Taksch Dube
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.14357v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14357v1)

## Summary
This paper asks a simple but overlooked question: when should someone tell the truth to an automated agent (like an autobidder or language-model proxy) acting on their behalf? The authors show that honesty is optimal only when the proxy is already playing the best action it can reach — a concept they call "loyalty" — and prove that no safety constraint can simultaneously be binding, truthful, and capability-preserving. The work bridges mechanism design theory with practical alignment problems in LLMs and automated bidding.

## Theoretical Background
The paper sits at the intersection of mechanism design and principal-agent theory. It draws on the revelation principle — the foundational result that truthful mechanisms are without loss of generality in classical auction and mechanism design. The authors extend this to a setting where the principal (user/advertiser) communicates with an intermediary (proxy/agent) rather than directly with the mechanism. The key innovation is measuring what the proxy can actually achieve, not just what the mechanism allows. Related work includes autobidding in ad auctions (where advertisers delegate bidding to algorithms) and recent work on faithful elicitation from language models (e.g., Huang et al.) that assumes agents will accurately report their true objectives.

## Core Contributions
1. **Within-range regret identity (Theorem 1)**: Formalizes when honest reporting is incentive-compatible. A principal gains from misreporting exactly by the amount the proxy's honest action underperforms relative to other actions the principal could steer it toward. Honesty is optimal iff the proxy is already loyal (playing its best reachable action).

2. **The alignment trilemma (Theorem 2)**: Proves no guardrail (bid cap, alignment layer, safety constraint) can simultaneously satisfy three properties: bindingness (it actually constrains what the proxy does), truthfulness (honest reporting stays optimal), and capability-preservation (the best outcome remains reachable). Any two force the third to fail. This explains why jailbreaking and prompt-engineering work — tighter constraints break truthfulness incentives.

3. **Computational and empirical analysis**: Shows within-range regret is #P-hard but estimates it from samples and tracks it efficiently as models update. Applies this to production LLMs under alignment-style constraints, finding that honest reporting leaves "surplus unclaimed" on all tested models.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper reframes classical [[Revelation Principle]] for delegation settings. It connects to recent work on [[RLHF]] and alignment layers — showing these create incentive misalignment rather than solving it. The autobidding results unify scattered findings in mechanism design for ad auctions. The work also relates to interpretability and control: understanding what a proxy can do (its reachable set) is as important as knowing what it wants to do. The trilemma echoes impossibility results in [[Alignment]] and constraint design more broadly.

## Practical Applications
1. **Autobidding in ad auctions**: Advertisers routinely mis-report budgets and targets to autobidders because current guardrails break truthfulness incentives. This framework identifies which constraints preserve honesty.

2. **Language model agent elicitation**: Practitioners using LLM agents for task delegation (customer support, code generation, research assistance) often resort to prompt-engineering because alignment layers make straightforward instruction-following suboptimal. The theory explains why and predicts when this happens.

3. **Safety constraint design**: When deploying guardrails on models (toxicity filters, capability limitations, output constraints), this framework identifies which combinations are self-defeating and which preserve user-honest communication.

4. **Delegation audits**: Regulators and auditors can use within-range regret estimation to detect when users are incentivized to strategically misreport to their proxies — a sign of misaligned delegation.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Proxy incentive auditor**: SaaS tool that measures within-range regret for deployed agents (LLMs, autobidders, etc.) and alerts when users face suboptimal honesty incentives. Targets ad-tech and AI deployment teams.

2. **Constraint-compliant guardrail generator**: Automated tool that designs safety constraints satisfying the trilemma — helping teams apply two constraints without sacrificing the third (e.g., maintaining capability while binding behavior and preserving truthfulness).

3. **Autobidding incentive alignment service**: Consulting + software for advertisers: analyze their autobidder setup, identify where honest reporting is suboptimal, and recommend bidding strategies or proxy reconfigurations that restore alignment.

4. **Delegation protocol certifier**: Certification/compliance product for enterprises deploying LLM agents. Tests whether the agent + constraint setup preserves honest task specification, with continuous monitoring as models update.

5. **Interactive proxy tuning platform**: Let users see their proxy's reachable-action set and within-range regret in real time, then adjust guardrails or proxy parameters to restore incentive alignment without sacrificing safety.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Computational hardness**: Within-range regret is #P-hard, so exact computation is intractable. The sampling-based estimator trades off accuracy for tractability; sample complexity bounds and convergence rates are not analyzed.

2. **Reachable-action identification**: The framework requires knowing (or approximating) the proxy's reachable set. For LLMs, this is fuzzy — what counts as "reachable" depends on how broadly we define prompt variants, few-shot examples, and CoT strategies. No principled boundary is given.

3. **Single-principal setting**: The paper assumes one principal per proxy. With multiple users (e.g., many advertisers using the same autobidder), incentive structures become interdependent — the framework doesn't address coordination or congestion effects.

4. **Static within-range regret**: The proxy's capability improves over time (model updates, fine-tuning, RLHF). The paper tracks regret as the model drifts but doesn't model long-term capability evolution or principal adaptation.

5. **Measurement gaps**: The empirical section ("running it on production language models") lacks detail — which alignment caps? How were estimates validated? How sensitive are results to sample size and model heterogeneity?

## Open Questions
1. **Sample complexity**: What is the sample and computational complexity of estimating within-range regret to within ε error? Can adaptive or importance-weighted sampling improve it?

2. **Multi-principal dynamics**: How do incentives change when multiple principals use the same proxy? Can information cascades or strategic signaling emerge?

3. **Trilemma in practice**: Can a designer ever satisfy all three properties of the trilemma by relaxing the constraint set (e.g., randomized guardrails, soft constraints)? What's the trade-off frontier?

4. **Principal learning**: If a principal observes their proxy's behavior over time, can they learn the reachable set and optimal misreporting strategy? How does this affect long-run equilibrium?

5. **Heterogeneous proxies**: How does within-range regret vary across proxy architectures, training procedures, and alignment methods? Can we predict it from model properties?

6. **Behavioral validation**: Do humans actually mis-report to proxies in ways predicted by this theory? Laboratory or field experiments testing the theory would strengthen it.

## Key Takeaway
Honest delegation to AI agents only works if the agent can already reach the best outcome — otherwise, users rationally misreport, making alignment constraints self-defeating.
