# Learning Standard Model structure from LHC data with Riemannian flow matching

**Authors:** Midori Kato, Kevin A. Urquía-Calderón, Inar Timiryasov, Oleg Ruchayskiy
**Published:** 2026-07-17 | **arXiv:** [2607.16144v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16144v1)

## Summary

This paper demonstrates that a single transformer-based generative model can learn the structure of particle physics from real LHC collision data without being explicitly taught physics rules. The model, called ShellFlow, uses Riemannian flow matching to generate particles that satisfy physical constraints (like the on-shell condition), and it discovers fundamental parameters like particle masses and mixing angles entirely from data—a surprising result suggesting that core physics can be reverse-engineered from observations.

## Theoretical Background

High-energy physics experiments like ATLAS at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) produce collision events recorded as sets of measured particles. Traditionally, physicists use Monte Carlo simulators that encode known physics rules to generate synthetic collision data. Flow matching is a modern generative modeling technique that learns to transform simple noise distributions into complex data distributions by following learned velocity fields. Riemannian flow matching extends this to curved manifolds—here, the "on-shell manifold" is the mathematical surface where particles satisfy Einstein's mass-energy relation (E² = p²c² + m²c⁴). The paper builds on transformer architectures (neural networks using attention mechanisms to process sequences) and combines them with geometric constraints from special relativity.

## Core Contributions

1. **ShellFlow architecture**: A novel conditional flow matching model that generates particles constrained to on-shell manifolds, using only two physics priors: the on-shell condition and the invariant-mass formula.

2. **Single-model coverage across five decades**: The trained model reproduces particle physics across an enormous energy range (sub-GeV to TeV) from one training run—a feat no single Monte Carlo sample achieves.

3. **Emergent physics discovery**: Without explicit training objectives, the model learns:
   - Dilepton resonances (J/ψ, Υ, Z bosons) at their correct masses
   - The Weinberg angle (electroweak mixing parameter)
   - W boson and top-quark masses
   - Inter-particle correlations not in the training objective

4. **Minimal physics inductive bias**: Training on ~10⁹ real ATLAS collision events with almost no physics guidance suggests fundamental physics structure is highly learnable from raw data.

## Connections to Other Work

This work sits at the intersection of **[[generative-models]]** (flow matching) and physics-informed machine learning. It relates to:
- **Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs)**: Unlike traditional PINNs that enforce physics as hard constraints in loss functions, ShellFlow learns physics softly through geometric structure.
- **Simulation-based inference**: Inverting this problem—using ML to replace Monte Carlo generators rather than augment them.
- **[[mechanistic-interpretability]]**: The discovery of Standard Model parameters without explicit supervision hints at what structure neural networks can extract from data, relevant to understanding what models "know."
- **Energy-dependent generative models**: Prior work on diffusion models in physics focused on lower-dimensional problems; this scales to realistic LHC complexity.

## Practical Applications

1. **Faster simulation for high-energy physics**: ShellFlow could replace or supplement slow Monte Carlo generators, enabling real-time event generation for detector optimization and rare-event searches.

2. **Anomaly detection in particle physics**: A well-trained generative model of Standard Model events can identify deviations (new particles, unexpected decay modes) by flagging low-likelihood events.

3. **Detector calibration and uncertainty quantification**: The model's learned parameter distributions could improve systematic uncertainty estimates in mass measurements.

4. **Physics discovery acceleration**: By learning what the data naturally "wants" to produce, the model might highlight overlooked correlations or hint at physics beyond the Standard Model.

5. **Educational tool**: A generative model that outputs interpretable physics parameters could demystify what LHC data actually encodes.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **ML-based HEP simulation engine**: A commercial software package replacing Pythia/Herwig for physics labs and industry (medical imaging, security), licensed per research institution or per GPU-hour.

2. **Real-time particle detector optimization platform**: Cloud service that uses ShellFlow to rapidly simulate detector responses, helping design next-generation colliders or smaller experiments.

3. **Anomaly detection-as-a-service for particle physics**: Platform allowing physicists to upload collision data and automatically identify events inconsistent with Standard Model—useful for smaller labs without ML expertise.

4. **Physics parameter extraction tool**: SaaS that takes raw LHC data and outputs posterior distributions of fundamental constants (masses, mixing angles, couplings), replacing traditional fitting pipelines.

5. **Generative physics benchmark/API**: Offer trained models via API for benchmarking new ML methods in physics, similar to how LLM APIs work—monetize by usage or subscription for researchers.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Training data requirement**: The model trained on ~10⁹ events—not all physics labs have access to this volume. Generalization to smaller datasets unclear.

2. **Implicit physics learning**: While the model discovers masses correctly, it's unclear *how* it encodes physics internally. Unlike Monte Carlo, there's no transparent particle decay chain—harder to debug or trust.

3. **High-dimensional generation**: Generating full event kinematics (momenta of all particles) is harder than generating summary statistics; scalability to higher particle multiplicities untested.

4. **Unknown physics**: The model learns the Standard Model structure present in training data. If new particles exist but are rare, the model won't discover them—it's fundamentally conservative.

5. **Riemannian geometry overhead**: Computing flows on curved manifolds is computationally expensive. Inference speed vs. Monte Carlo not reported.

6. **Limited to the training energy regime**: The model covers sub-GeV to TeV because training data spans this. Extrapolation beyond the training distribution (e.g., future higher-energy colliders) untested.

## Open Questions

1. **How sensitive is learning to the choice of on-shell manifold?** What if the manifold were slightly misspecified—would the model still recover correct physics?

2. **Can the model discover new physics?** If trained on a mix of Standard Model and BSM (beyond Standard Model) events, can it separately identify the BSM component?

3. **What is the minimum training set size?** How much data is actually needed to learn the dilepton resonances and Weinberg angle?

4. **Interpretability**: Which attention heads correspond to which physical processes? Can we reverse-engineer the learned coupling constants or decay widths?

5. **Generalization to unseen processes**: Train on electron/muon final states; does it generate tau leptons correctly without seeing them?

6. **Computational cost comparison**: Full wall-clock time comparison with Pythia/Herwig for generating equivalent event samples.

## Key Takeaway

Transformer flow models can learn fundamental particle physics directly from collision data, recovering Standard Model parameters without explicit physics rules.
