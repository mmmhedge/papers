# Fast, Reliable, and Error-Bounded Option Pricing with Pretrained Neural Networks: A GJR--GARCH Study

**Authors:** Thijs van den Berg
**Published:** 2026-06-13 | **arXiv:** [2606.15502v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.15502v1)

## Summary
This paper presents a neural surrogate method for fast, error-bounded option pricing in models without closed-form solutions (like GJR-GARCH). Using a Mixture Density Network to map parameters to terminal return densities as Gaussian mixtures, the method achieves microsecond pricing with rigorous error bounds—reaching within 10% of the theoretical Monte Carlo noise floor—while maintaining arbitrage-free guarantees.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on classical quantitative finance (stochastic volatility models like GJR-GARCH, Black-Scholes framework) and modern neural density estimation. Key foundations include:
- **Mixture Density Networks (MDN)**: mapping inputs to mixture component parameters
- **Gaussian mixture closed-form derivatives**: analytical Greeks and implied volatility from mixtures of lognormals
- **CDF-matching loss**: aligning surrogate training to pricing error metrics rather than likelihood
- **Distribution-free Monte Carlo error analysis**: the $\sqrt{1/(6N)}$ noise floor from classical statistics, decomposing out-of-sample error into four controllable terms

The theoretical innovation is connecting neural density estimation directly to financial pricing with verifiable bounds.

## Core Contributions
1. **Error-bounded surrogate recipe**: A general framework applicable to any simulation-based density model, not just GJR-GARCH
2. **Noise floor decomposition**: Rigorous derivation of the $\sqrt{1/(6N)}$ lower bound and factorization of out-of-sample error into (i) simulation variance, (ii) approximation error, (iii) generalization gap, (iv) optimization gap—each controllable
3. **Arbitrage-free pricing**: Automatic consistency via lognormal mixture representation ensures no calendar arbitrage or put-call violations
4. **CDF-matching loss**: Training objective explicitly aligned to option pricing error rather than density likelihood, improving practical accuracy
5. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates 1.4×10⁻⁴ CDF error on GJR-GARCH—within 10% of noise floor—with microsecond inference

## Practical Applications
1. **Real-time risk management**: Pricing thousands of options per second for intraday portfolio rebalancing and VaR/CVaR computation
2. **Exotic option valuation**: GJR-GARCH and similar jump/volatility-clustering models for American options, barriers, lookbacks requiring accurate densities
3. **Calibration acceleration**: Fitting volatility models to market data—currently slow due to nested Monte Carlo loops; neural surrogates reduce cost by 100–1000×
4. **Regulatory reporting**: Fast, audit-friendly pricing for daily mark-to-market and stress testing across millions of derivative positions
5. **Low-latency trading**: Microsecond Greeks for algorithmic hedging in options markets

## Technical Limitations
1. **Training data overhead**: Requires substantial simulation budget to generate training pairs; not eliminated, only optimized
2. **Model-specific surrogates**: Each volatility/pricing model (GJR-GARCH, Heston, SV with jumps) needs its own pretrained network; limited transfer between architecturally different models
3. **High-dimensional parameter spaces**: Scalability to 10+ parameters (multiple volatility factors, jumps, correlations) not demonstrated; curse of dimensionality may degrade error bounds
4. **Extrapolation risk**: Performance outside training parameter ranges (extreme moneyness, far future maturities) untested; neural networks extrapolate unreliably
5. **Mixture component explosion**: As density complexity increases, number of mixture components may grow, reducing computational advantage
6. **Empirical validation scope**: Demonstrated only on GJR-GARCH; generalization to other "any simulation-based density model" claimed but not proven

## Open Questions
1. How does the method scale to 5–10+ dimensional parameter spaces (e.g., multi-factor volatility + jump models)? Does the noise floor decomposition still guide architecture design?
2. Can transfer learning reduce training cost when switching between related models (e.g., GJR-GARCH → EGARCH)?
3. What is the optimal trade-off between mixture components and error? Is there a principled pruning strategy?
4. How sensitive is the method to distribution shift in market regimes (crisis vs. normal)?
5. Can the framework extend to American/exotic options with early exercise or path dependence, or is it fundamentally limited to European vanilla?
6. What is the actual wall-clock speedup vs. modern GPU-accelerated Monte Carlo on parallel hardware?

## Key Takeaway
Neural surrogates can achieve rigorous, quantifiable error bounds for derivative pricing by explicitly training on distributional density targets and decomposing achievable accuracy into four interpretable, tunable error sources—turning slow but trustworthy simulation into fast, verifiable computation.
