# Deep Reinforcement Learning for Dynamic Battery Management of Autonomous Order Pickers

**Authors:** Taniya Shaji, Abhay Sobhanan, Christof Defryn
**Published:** 2026-07-06 | **arXiv:** [2607.05683v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05683v1)

## Summary
This paper applies deep reinforcement learning to optimize battery charging decisions for autonomous mobile robots (AMRs) in warehouse operations. The problem is inherently dynamic—robots must decide when and where to charge as orders arrive unpredictably—and involves coordination among multiple agents, making traditional fixed scheduling rules suboptimal. The proposed PPO-based approach learns both charging station selection and duration, achieving 6% higher order-completion rates and reduced total charging time compared to existing methods.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two main pillars: (1) **Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)**, a policy gradient reinforcement learning algorithm that learns action strategies by directly optimizing the probability distribution over decisions; and (2) classical **queueing theory** and **operations research**, which traditionally addressed warehouse and resource allocation problems through analytical models and heuristic rules.

The core challenge is a **Markov Decision Process (MDP)** where the state includes robot battery levels, queue lengths at charging stations, and outstanding orders; actions are charging decisions; and rewards reflect order completion rates and operational efficiency. Prior work relied on static policies (e.g., "charge when battery drops below 30%"), which ignore dynamic environmental conditions like stochastic order arrivals and multi-robot competition for limited charging infrastructure.

## Core Contributions
1. **Problem formulation**: Explicit modeling of multi-AMR charging as a stochastic, dynamic optimization problem with anticipated queuing delays—moving beyond treating charging as an isolated subproblem.
2. **PPO-based DRL framework**: A practical deep RL solution that learns *joint* decisions (station selection + duration) rather than treating them sequentially, with explicit representation of queuing effects in the state space.
3. **Empirical validation**: Systematic benchmarking across warehouse layouts and arrival rates, with 6% improvement over strong baselines, plus policy interpretability analysis (showing what the learned policy actually does).
4. **Scalability demonstration**: Validation across diverse warehouse configurations, suggesting generalization beyond a single test case.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[reinforcement-learning|RL]] for operations and [[multi-agent-systems|multi-agent coordination]]. It relates to:
- **Fleet management & AMR scheduling**: Prior work used optimization (integer programming) or heuristics; this applies learning-based approaches.
- **Resource allocation under uncertainty**: Similar to dynamic pricing in online platforms or task scheduling, but in a physical, collaborative setting.
- **Policy gradient methods**: Standard PPO contribution; the novelty is domain-specific (charging) rather than algorithmic.
- **Queueing-aware RL**: The explicit modeling of anticipated queue lengths is a practical refinement often omitted in baseline RL formulations.

Does not directly engage with [[mechanistic-interpretability]] (understanding *why* the learned policy works) beyond post-hoc analysis, nor with multi-agent [[alignment]] (ensuring robots' incentives align globally).

## Practical Applications
1. **Warehouse automation**: Real-time charging orchestration for large-scale AMR fleets in fulfillment centers (Amazon, DHL, logistics hubs) where throughput is revenue-critical.
2. **Last-mile delivery fleets**: Dynamic charging decisions for delivery robots operating from distributed depot locations with limited charging infrastructure.
3. **Manufacturing plants**: Coordination of mobile material-handling robots that must balance work efficiency with battery constraints in job-shop or flow-shop environments.
4. **Retail/pharmacy automation**: Autonomous shelf-scanning or order-picking robots that must self-manage energy without disrupting service levels.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **AMR Fleet Management SaaS**: A cloud-based software platform that ingests real-time warehouse order data, robot telemetry, and charging infrastructure specs, then serves optimal charging recommendations to AMR fleets via an API. Charge per robot per month.

2. **Charging Infrastructure Optimizer**: Consulting/software tool that recommends optimal placement and capacity of charging stations in existing warehouses using DRL-based simulation, then provides real-time charging orchestration. Target: warehouse operators unhappy with fixed-rule charging behavior.

3. **Battery Health & Lifespan Platform**: Extend this to jointly optimize charging duration *and* battery longevity (thermal stress, cycle count). Sell to robotics manufacturers as a value-add for their robots.

4. **Heterogeneous Fleet Manager**: Extend to mixed fleets (different robots, different batteries, different mission priorities). Provide white-label integration for robotics OEMs (Boston Dynamics, MiR, Fetch, etc.).

5. **Energy-Cost Optimization Layer**: Use time-of-use electricity pricing as an additional reward signal, optimizing both throughput and energy cost. Sell to energy-conscious warehouse operators aiming for net-zero goals.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability to very large fleets**: Experiments likely tested dozens or hundreds of robots; behavior with thousands of robots and non-convex charging bottlenecks is unknown. Computational overhead of training/inference may become prohibitive.

2. **Sim-to-real gap**: The model is validated in simulation. Real robots have latency, communication delays, and stochastic movement times that could degrade learned policies. Transfer learning or fine-tuning requirements are unexplored.

3. **Non-stationary environments**: If order arrival distributions shift seasonally or due to business changes, retraining overhead is not discussed. Online learning/adaptation is absent.

4. **Charging heterogeneity**: Assumes homogeneous robots and charging stations. Real warehouses have mixed-age equipment, variable charge rates, and maintenance downtime—not modeled.

5. **Reward design**: The reward function choice (order completion rate vs. minimizing recharge time) is somewhat ad-hoc. Sensitivity to reward weighting and multi-objective trade-offs is not thoroughly explored.

6. **Computational cost**: Training a PPO policy requires many episodes. For a real system, the upfront training cost and subsequent inference latency per decision may be material compared to instantaneous heuristic rules.

## Open Questions
1. **Online adaptation**: How does the policy perform when order arrival distributions shift? Can incremental learning or meta-RL keep the policy fresh without full retraining?

2. **Communication & decentralization**: Does every robot need access to a central policy, or can a decentralized multi-agent RL approach (where each robot learns locally) scale better?

3. **Battery degradation**: Can the framework explicitly minimize battery aging (calendar fade, cycle stress) as a secondary objective while maintaining throughput?

4. **Fairness & load balancing**: Does PPO naturally balance charging load across stations, or can some stations become congested while others idle? How sensitive is policy to station placement?

5. **Human-in-the-loop**: Can operators override or guide the policy (e.g., "prioritize robot X's order")? How does this affect performance and safety?

6. **Theoretical guarantees**: Can convergence rates or optimality gaps be bounded? What is the sample complexity compared to classical optimization?

## Key Takeaway
Deep reinforcement learning can learn to dynamically balance energy efficiency and throughput in multi-robot warehouse systems better than hand-crafted rules, but real-world deployment will require handling non-stationary demand, heterogeneous equipment, and communication latency.
