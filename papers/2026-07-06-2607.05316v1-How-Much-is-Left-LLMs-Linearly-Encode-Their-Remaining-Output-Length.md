# How Much is Left? LLMs Linearly Encode Their Remaining Output Length

**Authors:** Mohamed Amine Merzouk, Dmitri Carpov, Mirko Bronzi, Damiano Fornasiere, Adam Oberman
**Published:** 2026-07-06 | **arXiv:** [2607.05316v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05316v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates whether large language models (LLMs) internally estimate how many tokens remain in their output while generating. Using linear probes (simple machine-learning classifiers) on frozen model activations, the authors find that response length is predictable from the hidden states of frozen 7–8B parameter models, even before generation begins. This matters because it provides empirical evidence that LLMs maintain implicit "plans" about output structure — a window into how these models organize their reasoning and generation.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on [[mechanistic-interpretability]] — the effort to understand what neural networks compute at a mechanistic level by examining their internal representations. It uses linear probing, a standard technique where a simple linear classifier is trained on top of frozen model activations to test whether information is present in those representations. The paper contrasts with formal impossibility results showing transformers cannot perform exact counting, instead arguing for approximate length estimation. The research also engages with broader questions about how sequential models maintain long-range structure despite generating one token at a time.

## Core Contributions
1. **Pre-generation length predictability**: Response length is decodable from the final hidden state of the prompt *before any output is generated* — suggesting length information is computed during prompt processing.
2. **Transfer of learned probes**: Probe directions trained on natural-language datasets generalize to synthetic completions never seen during training, and this generalization is asymmetric (forward transfer works better than reverse), indicating the learned representations capture something robust about length planning.
3. **Dynamic probe behavior**: Per-position estimates from probes shift upward at moments when the model retracts and restarts solutions, a directional behavior that position-only baselines cannot capture — hinting at active length management during generation.
4. **Distinction from exact counting**: Frames the finding as approximate length estimation, methodologically distinct from prior work proving transformers cannot count exactly.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to recent mechanistic-interpretability work probing what information is encoded in transformer representations (e.g., studies of subject-verb agreement, factual knowledge). It complements research on [[attention mechanisms]] and how transformers maintain structural information. The work is adjacent to interpretability research on reasoning steps in [[chain-of-thought prompting]] — understanding whether models plan multi-step outputs. It also touches alignment concerns: if models maintain implicit length estimates, this could be relevant to controlling output verbosity or detecting deceptive length manipulation.

## Practical Applications
1. **Adaptive generation stopping**: Systems could use length probes to predict when a model will finish, enabling better user interfaces that estimate response time or interrupt overly long generations.
2. **Output length control**: Fine-tuning or steering methods could directly manipulate length estimates to enforce brevity or verbosity constraints without retraining.
3. **Factuality & confidence signals**: If length estimation correlates with task difficulty or confidence, probes could flag uncertain or hallucinatory outputs before they're emitted.
4. **Retrieval-augmented generation optimization**: For RAG systems, length estimates could inform when to fetch additional context or when the model is "done thinking."

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **LLM Output Validator**: A tool that uses length probes to detect malformed or suspiciously-short outputs before they reach users, flagging potential hallucinations or early termination.
2. **Adaptive Verbosity API**: An API layer that intercepts LLM outputs and adjusts length estimates in real-time, allowing downstream applications to request "concise" or "detailed" responses with explicit token budgets.
3. **Chain-of-Thought Debugger**: A development tool for prompt engineers that visualizes and plots the model's estimated remaining length at each generation step, helping identify where reasoning breaks down.
4. **Length-Aware RAG System**: A retrieval system that uses length probes to decide mid-generation whether to fetch more context, optimizing both latency and answer completeness.
5. **Controlled Generation for Compliance**: A compliance tool for regulated industries (legal, medical) that steers model length estimates to enforce response constraints, with audit logs of length predictions.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Correlation ≠ causation**: Linear decodability proves information *exists* in activations but not that the model *uses* it for generation control. The causal mechanism remains unclear.
2. **Small model scale**: Results are limited to 7–8B parameter models. Behavior may differ in larger or smaller models, and scaling properties are unexplored.
3. **Dataset specificity**: Transfer is tested on a limited set of datasets. Performance on highly out-of-distribution tasks (e.g., code generation, dialogue) is unknown.
4. **Qualitative evidence**: The retraction-restart behavior ("per-position estimate shifts") is described qualitatively, not quantified at scale. Statistical significance is unclear.
5. **Frozen representations**: Probes train on static hidden states; the model's *use* of length information during decoding is not directly observed, only inferred.

## Open Questions
1. **Causal role**: Can interventions on length estimates (e.g., causally manipulating the decodable information) actually shorten or lengthen outputs? Is the signal used or merely encoded?
2. **Scaling**: How does length estimation behavior change across model sizes, architectures, and training procedures? Is it universal?
3. **Fine-grained length structure**: Does the model also estimate lengths of sub-components (e.g., "2 steps, then a conclusion"), not just total length?
4. **Training data dependence**: How much of this behavior comes from the model's training data (which may naturally vary in length per task) versus learned representations?
5. **Mechanism**: Which layers encode length information most strongly? How does it evolve during prompt processing?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs appear to maintain an approximate internal estimate of remaining output length, encoded in their hidden states before generation begins — suggesting they plan output structure implicitly, even though they cannot count exactly.
