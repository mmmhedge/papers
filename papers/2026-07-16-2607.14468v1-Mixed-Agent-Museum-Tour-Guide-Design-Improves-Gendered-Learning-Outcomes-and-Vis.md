# Mixed-Agent Museum Tour Guide Design Improves Gendered Learning Outcomes and Visitor Preferences

**Authors:** Annette M. Masterson, Wonse Jo, Helena C. Sieh, Lionel P. Robert,, Dawn Tilbury
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.14468v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14468v1)

## Summary
This paper presents a mixed-agent museum tour guide system that combines a physical robot with a projected virtual agent to guide visitors. Through a study of 30 participants, the researchers found that the dual-agent setup improved learning for female visitors and was preferred by all visitors, suggesting that multi-agent interaction designs can enhance both engagement and educational outcomes in public spaces.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on two key research streams: (1) Human-robot interaction (HRI) — the study of how people collaborate with and respond to robotic systems — and (2) multi-agent systems — networked agents that coordinate to achieve shared goals. Prior work has explored robots as museum guides and educational tools, but most deployed single agents. The paper also draws on educational psychology research showing that conversational and interactive learning improves retention, and on gender differences in learning preferences and STEM engagement. The authors frame their innovation as achieving "interaction richness" — the complexity and depth of back-and-forth engagement — through a hybrid physical-virtual agent design.

## Core Contributions
1. **Novel hybrid architecture**: A single platform that deploys both a physical mobile robot and a synchronized projected virtual agent, creating the appearance and feel of two distinct interactive agents without doubling hardware costs.
2. **Gender-moderated learning effects**: Empirical evidence that mixed-agent conversational styles produce differential learning gains by gender, with significant improvement for female participants — a finding that challenges one-size-fits-all design assumptions.
3. **Preference-performance decoupling**: Demonstration that participant *preference* for mixed-agent teams (reported uniformly across genders) diverges from *learning performance* gains (which were gender-dependent), suggesting preference and learning outcomes are distinct design targets.
4. **Validated in-the-wild evaluation**: Rigorous within-subjects study with multimodal data collection (surveys, behavioral sensors, interviews) in an actual museum context, not simulation.

## Connections to Other Work
This research connects to:
- **[[Multi-agent systems]]** literature on agent coordination and user perception of team dynamics.
- **Human-robot interaction** and social robotics, which has explored robot personalities, communication styles, and accessibility.
- **Educational technology** and adaptive learning systems — this work suggests that agent *configuration* (not just content) can be a lever for inclusive learning outcomes.
- **Gender in HRI and AI** — a growing but still under-studied area examining how gender mediates interactions with AI and robotic systems.
- **Embodied cognition** — the idea that physical presence and spatial interaction influence learning; the mixed-agent design tests whether adding a virtual layer enhances this effect.

## Practical Applications
1. **Museum and cultural heritage education**: Physical robot + virtual agent pairs deployed in galleries to provide personalized, accessible tours that adapt to visitor demographics.
2. **K-12 STEM classrooms**: Use mixed-agent tutoring systems to improve engagement and learning outcomes for underrepresented groups (e.g., girls in computer science classes).
3. **Corporate onboarding and training**: Hybrid agent systems for employee orientation, where a physical robot handles logistics and a virtual agent provides conversational tutoring.
4. **Healthcare patient education**: Robot + projected agent pairs in hospitals or clinics to explain procedures, medications, or post-care instructions to patients of varying literacy and learning styles.
5. **Accessibility in public spaces**: Train stations, airports, or libraries using mixed-agent guidance to serve visitors with different sensory, cognitive, and linguistic needs.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability**: A single physical robot + projection system is hardware-constrained; deploying across multiple galleries or simultaneously serving large crowds is not addressed.
2. **Virtual agent realism**: Projected agents lack embodiment (no physical presence, limited gesture range). The study doesn't isolate how much of the learning gain comes from dual *conversation* vs. dual *physical presence*.
3. **Generalization of gender effects**: Study size (N=30) is modest; gender-moderated effects may not replicate across different demographics, cultural contexts, or tour content domains.
4. **Causal mechanisms unclear**: The paper shows *that* mixed-agent configurations help female learners but does not explain *why* — is it reduced social anxiety, increased perceived support, better memory encoding, or something else?
5. **Static evaluation**: No longitudinal data on retention; learning gains are measured immediately post-tour, not over time.
6. **Confounding variables**: Agent configuration is entangled with conversational style; it's unclear whether gender differences arise from agent *number*, *modality* (physical vs. projected), or *dialogue strategy*.

## Open Questions
1. **Mechanism discovery**: What specific features of dyadic agent interaction (simultaneous speech? complementary roles? perceived emotional support?) drive the female learning advantage? Ablation studies varying one feature at a time would help.
2. **Cross-context validation**: Do gender-moderated learning effects hold in other domains (science, history, art) or other cultural/geographic settings? Is this specific to museum education?
3. **Long-term retention**: Do participants retain more information weeks or months later? Do preference and learning outcomes realign over time?
4. **Scalability and cost trade-offs**: What is the minimal virtual-agent design (e.g., text-only chat, 2D avatar, 3D hologram) that still yields the observed benefits? How does cost scale with tour group size?
5. **Intersectionality**: How do gender-moderated effects interact with age, educational background, STEM familiarity, or cultural background? Are there subgroups who benefit even more?
6. **Agent role assignment**: Does it matter *which* agent (physical vs. projected) plays the "expert" vs. "companion" role? Would swapping roles change outcomes?

## Key Takeaway
Pairing a physical robot with a projected virtual agent improves learning for female museum visitors while maintaining broad preference across all visitors.
