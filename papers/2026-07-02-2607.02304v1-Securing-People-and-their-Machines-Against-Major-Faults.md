# Securing People and their Machines Against Major Faults

**Authors:** Ohad Eitan, Idit Keidar, Ehud Shapiro
**Published:** 2026-07-02 | **arXiv:** [2607.02304v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02304v1)

## Summary
This paper presents a decentralized recovery system for grassroots platforms — peer-to-peer networks of people and their devices — to survive major faults like stolen private keys or lost smartphones. The key insight is that recovery doesn't require a central authority; instead, it relies on a social graph where friends and designated identity custodians collectively authorize and execute key recovery. The work matters because it makes decentralized systems practical for ordinary users who inevitably lose devices or compromise credentials.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on distributed systems theory, particularly Byzantine fault tolerance and consensus mechanisms, but applies them in a novel social context. It uses the model of eventually synchronous message-passing (systems where messages eventually arrive within bounded time, a realistic assumption for real networks). The core mathematical framework is *guarded multiagent atomic transactions* — a formal way to specify concurrent operations across multiple agents that either all succeed or all fail together. The paper also draws on social network theory: the social graph is the backbone for trust and recovery, not just an artifact of users' relationships.

## Core Contributions
1. **Social graph as recovery infrastructure**: Formalizes how friends in a social graph can collectively serve as state custodians to recover lost or compromised identity without a global trusted third party.
2. **Identity custodian model**: Introduces a person-designated trust delegation mechanism where a supermajority of chosen custodians can authorize key recovery — more fine-grained than typical threshold schemes.
3. **Formal specification and proof**: Specifies the social graph recovery system as guarded atomic transactions, then implements it via communicating volitional agents and proves the implementation correctly maps faulty runs to specification-compliant runs.
4. **Application to grassroots currency**: Demonstrates the framework generalizes beyond identity to state recovery in decentralized coins and bonds, ensuring no double-spending after recovery.
5. **Off-chain recovery**: Shows that key generation, device replacement, and custodian consent all happen outside the protocol, reducing on-chain burden.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to classical distributed systems work on [[Byzantine Fault Tolerance]] and quorum-based recovery, but reframes recovery as a social problem rather than a purely technical one. It shares concerns with [[RLHF]]-style human-in-the-loop systems in that humans (custodians, friends) are part of the security mechanism, not external to it. The work is adjacent to blockchain and decentralized identity systems (e.g., DID standards, social recovery wallets like Argent), but differs by formalizing the protocol and proving correctness. The eventually synchronous model echoes Lamport and others' work on asynchronous distributed systems with timing assumptions.

## Practical Applications
1. **Decentralized wallet recovery**: Smartphone-based cryptocurrency wallets that recover from loss or theft by contacting trusted friends, avoiding centralized recovery services.
2. **Grassroots identity platforms**: Systems for unbanked or underserved populations to establish verifiable identity without relying on government or corporate infrastructure.
3. **Community-managed access control**: Small organizations (co-ops, mutual aid networks) managing member credentials and recovering from compromise via peer consensus.
4. **Decentralized social networks**: Platforms where users own their accounts (identified by public keys) and recover access through their social graph rather than email/SMS recovery codes.
5. **Grassroots currency and remittance systems**: Off-grid payment systems where peers collectively verify state recovery and prevent double-spending after device loss.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **"Friends Wallet"**: A smartphone app for remittance workers and unbanked populations that stores crypto/digital currency; if the phone is lost, recovery requires approval from 3–5 trusted friends, not a corporation. The business model is transaction fees on grassroots currency flows.
2. **Decentralized ID as a Service**: A SaaS platform for NGOs and mutual aid networks to issue and recover member credentials (proof of citizenship, voting rights, health records) using social graph recovery, charging per member.
3. **Community Custodian Networks**: A protocol-as-a-service where local organizations (e.g., microfinance groups, community centers) act as professional identity custodians, taking a small fee for key recovery approvals.
4. **Offline-First Recovery Protocol Library**: A developer toolkit that bundles the guarded transaction specification and volitional agent implementation so startups can bake social recovery into any grassroots platform (social networks, payments, supply chains).
5. **Peer-to-Peer Insurance Pool**: A decentralized service where users pool small amounts and collectively insure each other against key/device loss, with recovery managed by the protocol rather than a company.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Custodian availability and incentives**: The protocol assumes identity custodians will be available and willing to approve recovery when needed, but provides no economic incentive or penalty mechanism. In practice, custodians may be offline, unreachable, or lose interest over time.
2. **Social graph brittleness**: If a person's friend group is unstable (friends move, relationships end), the recovery network degrades. The paper does not address how to maintain or repair a social graph over years.
3. **Supermajority assumption**: Recovery requires a supermajority of custodians to agree. The paper does not discuss what happens if custodians collude to block recovery or if a person's social circle disagrees on legitimacy.
4. **Off-chain ceremony is informal**: Key generation and custodian assignment happen "off-chain," meaning the security of recovery depends on out-of-band social processes (phone calls, in-person meetings) that the protocol cannot verify or enforce.
5. **Eventually synchronous model limitation**: The protocol assumes eventual synchrony, which may not hold in partitioned networks or during widespread infrastructure failures. Behavior under network partition is not fully characterized.
6. **Scalability of social graph state**: As the grassroots platform grows, the social graph becomes a global state structure that must be queried and updated consistently. The paper does not address how to shard or partition this for large networks.

## Open Questions
1. **How should the social graph be maintained securely as it grows?** The paper specifies recovery but not continuous consensus on friendship lists. What if two friends disagree on whether they are connected?
2. **Can the framework tolerate Byzantine custodians (malicious, not just faulty)?** The paper assumes crash failures; can it handle custodians who actively lie or collude to authorize false recoveries?
3. **How do you incentivize custodians to stay available and approve legitimate recoveries?** Is there a fee structure, reputation system, or legal framework that makes this work at scale?
4. **What happens during network partitions?** If the eventually synchronous assumption breaks, can the protocol detect and recover gracefully?
5. **Can recovery be made faster and more usable?** Current design requires off-chain ceremonies and supermajority voting. Are there UX-friendly variants that sacrifice security less than traditional email recovery?
6. **How do grassroots coins prevent Sybil attacks?** If identities are recovered via social graph, can an attacker create fake identities and build fake friendships to recover coins maliciously?

## Key Takeaway
Decentralized systems can make identity and state recovery practical for ordinary users by leveraging their social relationships as a trust foundation, rather than requiring a central authority — but only if the off-chain social process is reliable and custodians stay engaged over time.
