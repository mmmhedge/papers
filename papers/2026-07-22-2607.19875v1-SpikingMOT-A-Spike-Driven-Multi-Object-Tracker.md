# SpikingMOT: A Spike-Driven Multi-Object Tracker

**Authors:** Yiding Sun, Xiangyang Yang, Dongxu Zhang, Qirui Wang, Zijie Xu et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-22 | **arXiv:** [2607.19875v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19875v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces SpikingMOT, a multi-object tracking system that uses spiking neural networks (SNNs)—brain-inspired networks that process information through sparse, event-driven activations—instead of traditional dense neural networks. The key insight is that tracking doesn't need constant, full activation of neurons; sparse, selective firing can actually predict motion just as well while using 86% less energy and 72% fewer parameters. The work achieves state-of-the-art tracking accuracy on challenging benchmarks (SportsMOT and DanceTrack) while being far more efficient.

## Theoretical Background
Multi-object tracking requires predicting where multiple targets will move next, then correctly associating detections across frames. Traditional deep learning approaches use densely activated networks—meaning most neurons fire on every input. This paper builds on two prior insights: (1) spiking neural networks, inspired by how biological brains work through discrete, sparse firing events rather than continuous signals, and (2) activation sparsity—the observation that not all neurons need to fire to solve a task. The authors formalize this via "activation sparsity preference" (ASP), theoretically showing that sparse gating (selectively choosing which neurons activate) performs as well as state-independent dropout (randomly deactivating neurons) when controlling for activation rate. The trajectory prediction problem is decomposed into learning "pseudo-trajectory bases"—learned components that can be recombined—and using prediction error to refine estimates for the next frame.

## Core Contributions
1. **Formal theoretical framework for activation sparsity in tracking**: Shows sparse gating is theoretically sound compared to dropout under equal sparsity constraints.
2. **SNN-based tracking architecture**: First application of spiking neural networks to multi-object tracking, replacing standard dense networks with event-driven computation.
3. **Adaptive sparse trajectory modeling**: A mechanism that decomposes trajectories into bases and recalibrates predictions using error signals (a brain-inspired feedback loop).
4. **Massive efficiency gains with competitive accuracy**: Achieves SOTA or near-SOTA results (74.9 HOTA on SportsMOT, 56.5 HOTA on DanceTrack) while reducing parameters by 72% and energy by 86.7%.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of neuromorphic computing and computer vision. It relates to broader efforts to bring SNNs into practical visual tasks (most SNN work has been in image classification). The trajectory decomposition and error-correction loop echo Bayesian filtering approaches (like Kalman filters) traditionally used in tracking, but reformulated for SNNs. The sparse activation principle connects to neural network pruning and efficient inference, though here sparsity is baked into the model architecture rather than removed post-hoc. Within [[Multi-Object Tracking]], it challenges the assumption that dense networks are necessary, similar to how [[RLHF]] and other works have questioned conventional training paradigms in other domains.

## Practical Applications
1. **Edge and embedded tracking**: Autonomous vehicles, drones, and robots with limited power budgets can run tracking on-device with minimal energy.
2. **Real-time sports analytics**: Camera systems in stadiums (SportsMOT is a benchmark here) can process multi-player tracking live with lower computational cost.
3. **Surveillance and crowd monitoring**: Large-scale camera deployments in public spaces benefit from 86% energy savings across thousands of devices.
4. **Mobile computer vision**: Smartphones and AR/VR devices need efficient tracking for gesture recognition, object following, or scene understanding.
5. **Low-power IoT applications**: Embedded systems with strict power constraints (e.g., wildlife monitoring cameras) can afford continuous tracking.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Limited evaluation scope**: Tested only on sports/dance datasets; unclear how well it generalizes to traffic, surveillance, or other complex outdoor MOT scenarios with occlusions, lighting changes, or varying target scales.
2. **SNN training complexity**: SNNs are harder to train than standard RNNs/CNNs due to non-differentiable spiking; the paper doesn't fully detail training procedures, making reproducibility uncertain.
3. **Latency not measured**: Energy savings are reported, but actual inference latency compared to dense networks isn't discussed—sparse activations may still have wall-clock overhead on conventional hardware.
4. **No hardware implementation**: Results assume ideal sparse computation; real-world speedup depends on neuromorphic hardware or specialized sparse kernels, which are not yet mainstream.
5. **Pseudo-trajectory basis learning**: The decomposition strategy is not deeply analyzed—sensitivity to the number of bases, or how it scales with object count, is unclear.
6. **Baseline comparisons**: Not clear how much improvement comes from sparsity vs. the specific trajectory recalibration mechanism; ablations on these components are limited.

## Open Questions
1. How does SpikingMOT perform on dense, real-world tracking scenarios (highway traffic, crowded marketplaces) with heavy occlusions and scale variations?
2. Can the sparse trajectory model be extended to handle variable-speed motion or sudden direction changes better than current results show?
3. What is the actual wall-clock latency on standard GPUs/CPUs, and how much speedup is achieved on neuromorphic hardware?
4. How sensitive is the method to the number and initialization of pseudo-trajectory bases?
5. Can this approach be combined with transformer-based detection backbones, or does sparsity conflict with attention mechanisms used in modern MOT?
6. Does the brain-inspired error-correction loop outperform simpler Bayesian filtering approaches (Kalman, particle filters) in a fair comparison?

## Key Takeaway
Spiking neural networks enable multi-object tracking with state-of-the-art accuracy while using 86% less energy and far fewer parameters than dense networks.
