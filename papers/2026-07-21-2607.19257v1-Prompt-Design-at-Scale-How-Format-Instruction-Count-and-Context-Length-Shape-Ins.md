# Prompt Design at Scale: How Format, Instruction Count, and Context Length Shape Instruction Adherence and Hallucination in Large Language Models

**Authors:** Netanel Eliav
**Published:** 2026-07-21 | **arXiv:** [2607.19257v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19257v1)

## Summary
This paper systematically tests how three prompt-design choices—formatting style, number of simultaneous instructions, and context length—affect whether language models follow instructions accurately and avoid hallucinating. The authors ran thousands of controlled experiments across five models using a custom synthetic dataset, finding that instruction adherence collapses around 80 rules, recall degrades sharply near context limits (with format-dependent effects), and models refuse to answer rather than make things up when overloaded.

## Theoretical Background
This work sits at the intersection of prompt engineering and mechanistic understanding of LLM behavior. It builds on empirical observations that LLMs are sensitive to surface-form properties (how instructions are written) and context windows, but lacks the controlled, multi-factor evidence needed for reliable guidance. The paper treats instruction-following as a testable engineering problem: How do format (markdown, plain text, prose, tables), instruction count, and context depth independently and jointly affect output quality? The theoretical framing is pragmatic rather than foundational—it asks "what actually works?" rather than "why do models work this way?"

## Core Contributions
1. **Systematic instruction-count experiment**: First controlled measurement showing instruction-following degrades predictably with rule count (N=10 to 160), with perfect compliance collapsing by N≈80 across all models and formats.
2. **Format comparison at scale**: Tests four formatting styles across placement locations; finds no universal markdown advantage (contradicting folk wisdom), with model-specific and task-dependent trade-offs.
3. **Long-context behavior characterization**: Measures recall, sycophancy, and fabrication across 2k–512k token contexts, showing recall remains stable to 64–128k tokens, then degrades sharply with 48-point format-dependent spreads at boundaries.
4. **Refusal-vs.-hallucination distinction**: Demonstrates that near context limits, models refuse to answer (79–90% refusal rate) rather than sycophancy or fabrication, a distinction absent from prior work.
5. **Reproducible benchmark**: Releases VeyraBench with deterministically regenerable synthetic corpus (8,780 entities) and full experiment harness, enabling replication and future work.

## Connections to Other Work
This complements research on [[prompt engineering]] best practices, but adds rigor where prior work relied on anecdote. It relates to studies of [[context window]] limitations and [[in-context learning]], showing format matters as much as length. The finding that refusal is the primary failure mode near capacity limits contrasts with feared [[hallucination]] and [[sycophancy]] scenarios in [[alignment]] research. The work is also adjacent to [[mechanistic interpretability]] efforts—understanding *why* format and count matter would require probing internals, which this paper does not do.

## Practical Applications
- **Prompt optimization tools**: Systems that automatically test formatting and instruction-count combinations for a user's domain to maximize compliance before rollout.
- **Model-specific system-prompt templates**: Derived from these results (e.g., "Claude prefers placement X with format Y for 40+ rules").
- **Context-aware instruction truncation**: Agents that dynamically drop or summarize low-priority rules when approaching context limits, trading off rule count for recall.
- **Quality gates in production**: Pre-flight checks that warn practitioners when their prompt exceeds known collapse thresholds for their chosen model.
- **Benchmark for prompt robustness**: Internal testing harness for teams evaluating new models or fine-tunes, using the reproducible corpus.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **"PromptOps" SaaS**: Upload your system prompt and corpus; get automated A/B tests across formatting, rule count, and context depth, with per-model recommendations and refresh alerts when new models release.
2. **Prompt compliance checker**: Browser extension or IDE plugin flagging risky prompt patterns (e.g., "You are adding rule #95—this model collapses at ~80 rules").
3. **Format converter + optimizer**: Tool that takes a prose prompt and auto-generates markdown, plain-text, and tabular versions, runs cheap synthetic tests, and returns the best fit for your model and latency budget.
4. **Per-model prompt libraries**: Marketplace of pre-tested system prompts tuned for GPT-4, Claude, Llama-2, etc., with transparency on tested formats and rule counts—monetized via subscription or per-test API.
5. **Corporate LLM audit service**: Consult firms using VeyraBench to audit internal prompts, measure compliance on proprietary datasets, and deliver model-specific playbooks to engineering teams.

## Technical Limitations
- **Synthetic corpus only**: Book of Veyra is clean, deterministic, and isolated—real-world prompts face noisy, shifting, adversarial contexts that may behave differently.
- **Five models tested**: Conclusions may not generalize to smaller models (e.g., Gemma-2B), future architectures, or proprietary/fine-tuned variants.
- **No mechanistic explanation**: The paper measures *what* breaks but not *why*—understanding whether instruction collapse is attention saturation, tokenization overhead, or training-objective conflicts requires model internals.
- **Format tokenization not isolated**: Token overhead (22–37% for non-plain formats) conflates format legibility with raw context pressure; hard to disentangle.
- **Single corpus domain**: All experiments use entity-recall and rule-following tasks; generalization to reasoning, code, or creative tasks unknown.
- **No intervention testing**: The paper identifies failure modes but does not test mitigation strategies (e.g., instruction compression, hierarchical rules, or fine-tuning).

## Open Questions
1. Do instruction-collapse thresholds shift with fine-tuning, prompt caching, or retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)?
2. Can models be trained to sustain higher rule counts, and at what cost to other capabilities?
3. How do dynamically selected or hierarchical rule sets perform vs. flat lists of N rules?
4. What is the relationship between refusal and model uncertainty—does refusal correlate with low confidence on recall tasks?
5. Do these findings hold for multilingual prompts, code-focused models, or vision-language models?
6. Can token-efficient instruction encoding (e.g., embedding rules as vectors rather than text) push collapse thresholds higher?

## Key Takeaway
Format and instruction count both break model compliance; plain-text often works as well as markdown, and rules collapse around 80—not format-agnostic rules per prompt.
