# Self-supervision drives representational convergence in medical foundation models more than clinical supervision

**Authors:** Soroosh Tayebi Arasteh, Sebastian Ziegelmayer, Mahshad Lotfinia, Lisa Adams, Sven Nebelung et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-22 | **arXiv:** [2607.20274v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20274v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates whether medical image encoders from different organizations learn similar representations, and what drives that convergence. The authors systematically compare 18 image and 7 text encoders across multiple datasets and find that convergence is real but modest—and surprisingly, it's driven by the self-supervised training objective, not by clinical expertise or model scale. This challenges the assumption that bigger, clinically-trained models automatically learn shared, interchangeable representations.

## Theoretical Background
Foundation models in medical imaging are trained using different objectives: self-supervised learning (learning from unlabeled data by predicting parts of the input), clinical supervision (learning from labeled diagnostic tasks), and image-text alignment (matching images to text descriptions). The implicit assumption has been that these different approaches would converge on similar representations at scale—the idea being that if you optimize for medical tasks with enough data and parameters, all models should learn similar features. This paper tests that assumption rigorously by measuring representational similarity using methods like linear classifier transfer and direct geometric alignment.

## Core Contributions
1. **Systematic empirical dissection**: Controlled comparison of 18 image and 7 text medical encoders (7M–27B parameters) across five imaging modalities and six chest X-ray datasets with 650,982 images.
2. **Causal isolation**: Trained encoders that vary *only* the training objective while holding data, architecture, and scale constant—isolating the effect of objective from other factors.
3. **Quantification of convergence**: Self-supervised encoders aligned most (40.4% similarity on chest X-rays), far exceeding label-supervised (21.1%) and image-text (3.3%) objectives.
4. **Convergence does not grow with scale**: Larger models did not show stronger alignment (Spearman ρ=0.302, p=0.223).
5. **Clinical gap**: Convergence is within-modality, does not reproduce radiologist similarity judgments, and does not reach clinical language semantics—yet linear classifiers transfer reliably across encoders and hospitals (85% retention).

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to broader work on [[foundation models]] and [[representation learning]] convergence—similar questions have been asked in NLP about whether large language models converge despite different architectures. It also connects to debates around [[CLIP]] and multimodal learning (image-text alignment), and to transfer learning in medical imaging. The finding that self-supervised objectives drive convergence more than clinical labels is relevant to understanding what [[self-supervised learning]] actually optimizes for—a mechanistic question about what inductive biases emerge from contrastive or masked prediction tasks versus explicit clinical signals.

## Practical Applications
1. **Standardized encoder interoperability**: Clinical AI systems can swap encoders from different vendors or research groups with confidence—85% performance retention means minimal recalibration needed for downstream diagnostic classifiers.
2. **Federated learning in hospitals**: Systems can share encoders across institutions without retraining, knowing that linear adapters will preserve diagnostic utility even if absolute alignment is imperfect.
3. **Model procurement**: Hospitals can evaluate encoders on transfer-learning tasks (linear classifier) rather than waiting for full fine-tuning, reducing evaluation costs.
4. **Cross-dataset generalization**: The finding that linear classifiers transfer across five held-out hospitals suggests robust features for multi-site clinical trials and retrospective studies.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **MedEncoder Marketplace**: Hosted model zoo offering pre-trained encoders with transparent convergence metrics—clinicians and ML teams choose based on modality and transfer performance rather than hype.
2. **Diagnostic Adapters as a Service**: SaaS offering minimal linear classifiers (logistic regression) that plug onto any standard encoder, enabling small hospitals to deploy AI without retraining.
3. **Encoder Compatibility Audit Tool**: Software that measures how well a new encoder will work in existing clinical pipelines by testing convergence and transfer on local data—de-risks vendor switching.
4. **Multi-modal Foundation Model Evaluator**: Benchmark platform that validates convergence and clinical alignment before purchase, certifying whether an encoder reproduces radiologist similarity judgments.
5. **Federated Learning Router**: Infrastructure that selects the best encoder per hospital based on local patient demographics and disease prevalence, using the convergence insights to justify swaps.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Modest absolute convergence**: 40% alignment for self-supervised encoders is meaningful but not interchangeability—there is still substantial heterogeneity in learned features.
2. **Chest X-ray bias**: Most experiments use one modality (CXR); whether findings generalize to CT, MRI, pathology, or other imaging is unclear.
3. **Similarity measure fragility**: The paper notes that "similarity measures behind such claims are fragile"—different metrics (cosine, Procrustes, linear transfer) may yield different conclusions about convergence.
4. **Clinical validation gap**: Transfer performance (85%) is measured on diagnostic classifiers, not on whether shared geometry actually matches clinical reasoning or radiologist feature importance.
5. **Subgroup performance unknown**: The paper flags that convergence should be validated "across patient subgroups"—disparities in how encoders handle different demographics are not characterized.
6. **Text encoder findings marginal**: Image-text alignment yielded only 3.3% convergence; unclear whether this is a true signal or a measurement floor.

## Open Questions
1. **Does convergence emerge during training?** Do encoders start different and drift together, or are they similar from initialization? A training dynamics study would clarify whether it's an objective property or learned.
2. **What *features* drive convergence?** A mechanistic interpretability study could identify whether self-supervised objectives converge on similar low-level anatomical features (edges, texture) or high-level concepts (pathology patterns).
3. **Can clinical supervision be made convergent?** Is 21.1% low because clinical objectives are fundamentally diverse, or because current labeling/weighting schemes are suboptimal?
4. **How does architecture choice matter?** The paper holds architecture fixed within experiments; varying transformer vs. CNN vs. vision transformer across the same objective could isolate architecture's role.
5. **Does convergence correlate with clinical utility?** Is stronger geometric alignment actually better for downstream diagnostics, or is 21% alignment from supervised learning sufficient?
6. **Subgroup performance and fairness**: Do encoders converge similarly across age, sex, and disease prevalence groups? Does convergence hide disparity?

## Key Takeaway
Self-supervised pretraining, not scale or clinical labels, drives medical encoders toward shared representations—enabling interoperability despite modest absolute alignment.
