# CausalSteward: An Agentic Divide-Conquer-Combine Copilot for Causal Discovery

**Authors:** Nicholas Tagliapietra, Gian Lorenzo Marchioni, Moritz Willig, Juergen Luettin, Lavdim Halilaj et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-02 | **arXiv:** [2607.01936v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01936v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces CausalSteward, a multi-agent system that helps discover causal relationships in high-dimensional data by breaking the problem into smaller, manageable parts and combining the results with human guidance. The work is important because causal discovery from real-world data is extremely difficult—existing methods often fail when assumptions are violated—and this approach shows how collaborative AI agents and human expertise together can assemble more reliable causal models.

## Theoretical Background
Causal discovery aims to infer which variables causally influence which others from observational data—a fundamentally harder problem than correlation discovery because multiple causal structures can produce identical statistical patterns. Traditional approaches rely on conditional independence tests (statistical procedures that check whether two variables are independent given a third) and assumptions like acyclicity or no hidden confounders. However, these assumptions frequently break in real-world settings, leading to identifiability issues where the true causal model cannot be uniquely determined from data alone. Prior knowledge—expert information about known causal relationships—is abundant but difficult to integrate systematically into discovery pipelines. Divide-and-conquer strategies partition large problems into smaller ones, analyze them independently, and then combine results; this is particularly useful for high-dimensional causal discovery where analyzing all variables simultaneously becomes computationally intractable.

## Core Contributions
1. **Multi-agent collaborative framework**: CausalSteward uses multiple AI agents that specialize in different aspects (partitioning variables, analyzing local structures, integrating knowledge) and coordinate their work rather than solving the whole problem monolithically.

2. **Hybrid knowledge integration**: The system fuses prior knowledge (from retrieval augmented generation—fetching relevant external knowledge to ground reasoning) with data-driven statistical tests, rather than treating them as separate pipelines.

3. **Interactive human-in-the-loop design**: Humans iteratively validate, correct, and guide agent decisions at key points, improving both accuracy and trustworthiness of final causal models.

4. **Systematic evaluation of multi-agent causal reasoning**: The paper empirically examines what causal reasoning tasks multi-agent systems can reliably perform and where they fail, contributing to understanding of LLM capabilities for structured inference.

## Connections to Other Work
This work intersects several research directions:
- **Causal inference literature**: Builds on conditional independence testing frameworks and causal identifiability theory, but addresses the practical gap of integrating domain knowledge.
- **Multi-agent AI**: Related to [[multi-agent-systems]] and [[agents]] research on coordination, communication, and division of labor among autonomous systems.
- **LLM reasoning**: Extends work on using [[large-language-models]] for structured tasks (causal reasoning, symbolic manipulation) beyond typical NLP applications.
- **Human-in-the-loop ML**: Similar in spirit to interactive machine learning approaches where human feedback iteratively improves system performance.
- **Knowledge integration in AI**: Complements retrieval augmented generation (RAG) techniques by applying them to a non-traditional domain (causal discovery rather than question-answering).

## Practical Applications
1. **Drug discovery and pharmacology**: Discovering causal relationships between genetic markers, biomarkers, and treatment outcomes in personalized medicine, where domain knowledge from medical literature is abundant.

2. **Climate and environmental modeling**: Inferring causal mechanisms in complex climate systems by integrating physical laws and observational data, with expert climate scientists in the loop.

3. **Supply chain optimization**: Identifying causal dependencies between supplier decisions, logistics constraints, and demand to improve resilience and cost.

4. **Financial risk modeling**: Understanding causal drivers of market stress, portfolio returns, and systemic risk by combining market data with economic theory and expert judgment.

5. **Epidemiology and public health**: Discovering causal pathways linking social determinants, behavioral factors, and health outcomes from observational studies with prior epidemiological knowledge.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **CausalDiscovery-as-a-Service (SaaS)**: Cloud platform where domain experts upload datasets, prior knowledge (journal papers, expert graphs), and collaborate with AI agents to validate causal models; multi-tenancy for pharma, biotech, finance.

2. **Causal Model Marketplace**: Platform where researchers and companies publish validated causal models (e.g., "customer behavior → churn"), and other organizations query them via agents to build custom models for their domains.

3. **Expert-AI Collaboration Studio**: Visual editor + multi-agent backend where scientists iteratively refine causal hypotheses; agents suggest partitions, run tests, flag contradictions; exports to publication-ready causal diagrams.

4. **Causal Root-Cause Analysis Tool**: Embed in observability/monitoring software (Datadog, New Relic style); when anomalies occur, agents discover causal factors from metrics + logs, ranked by confidence; for DevOps teams.

5. **Regulatory Compliance Assistant**: For industries (pharma, energy, finance) where causal understanding is mandated; system helps executives document causal reasoning behind decisions, reducing audit friction and legal risk.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability of agent coordination**: As the number of agents grows or problem complexity increases, communication overhead and disagreement resolution may become bottlenecks; unclear how well this scales beyond "small teams of agents."

2. **Quality of prior knowledge**: The system is only as good as the knowledge it can retrieve; if domain knowledge is absent, outdated, or contradictory, RAG cannot overcome it—no mechanism for detecting or reconciling conflicting priors.

3. **Conditional independence test reliability**: Underlying statistical tests assume sufficient sample size and no unmeasured confounders; violations of these assumptions propagate through agent reasoning without explicit uncertainty quantification.

4. **LLM reasoning limitations**: LLMs may hallucinate causal relationships, misinterpret conditional independence, or make logical errors; human-in-the-loop helps but doesn't guarantee correctness, and can introduce human bias.

5. **Identifiability remains unsolved**: The fundamental problem—multiple causal structures fitting the same data—is not resolved; the system can narrow possibilities via priors and human judgment, but cannot guarantee uniqueness without strong untestable assumptions.

6. **Computational cost**: Iterative multi-agent discovery with human feedback loops is likely expensive in terms of agent calls, human time, and inference cost; real-time applicability unclear for streaming or time-sensitive domains.

## Open Questions
1. **Quantifying agent disagreement**: When agents partition variables differently or propose conflicting causal edges, how should disagreement be measured and resolved? Is consensus always better, or can minority opinions signal missed assumptions?

2. **Minimizing human effort**: What is the minimal amount of human interaction needed for high-quality causal discovery? Can the system learn from human corrections to improve autonomously?

3. **Theoretical guarantees**: Under what conditions does the divide-and-conquer approach recover the true causal structure? Can identifiability results from causal inference theory be adapted to this multi-agent setting?

4. **Generalization across domains**: How well do agents trained/tuned on one domain (e.g., biology) transfer to another (e.g., finance)? Is causal reasoning domain-general or domain-specific?

5. **Comparison to baselines**: How does CausalSteward compare quantitatively to state-of-the-art causal discovery algorithms (e.g., constraint-based, score-based methods) on benchmark datasets, especially when priors are noisy or incomplete?

6. **Interpretability of agent reasoning**: Can we audit why agents made specific partitioning or causal decisions? What explainability techniques would build user trust in a high-stakes domain?

## Key Takeaway
Multi-agent systems with human oversight can make causal discovery more practical by dividing large problems and systematically integrating domain knowledge, but this approach trades off some theoretical guarantees for pragmatic scalability and interpretability.
