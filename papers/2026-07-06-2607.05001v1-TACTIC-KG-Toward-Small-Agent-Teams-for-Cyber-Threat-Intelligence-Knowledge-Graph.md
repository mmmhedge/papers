# TACTIC-KG: Toward Small Agent Teams for Cyber Threat Intelligence Knowledge Graph Construction

**Authors:** Mouhamed Amine Bouchiha, Gregory Blanc
**Published:** 2026-07-06 | **arXiv:** [2607.05001v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05001v1)

## Summary
This paper presents TACTIC-KG, a multi-agent framework that converts unstructured cybersecurity threat reports into structured knowledge graphs by breaking down the task into specialized sub-tasks (extraction, typing, verification, curation) handled by smaller language models. Rather than using one large model to do everything, the authors show that smaller 3–8B parameter models working together as agents produce better results — higher accuracy, more stable performance, and lower deployment costs — compared to single large models attempting the same work.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two main streams of prior work. First, **knowledge graph construction** from unstructured text — a classical NLP problem where entities and their relationships must be extracted and linked into a structured format. Second, recent trends in **agentic LLM systems** (language models that decompose complex tasks into subtasks and coordinate their execution) and **in-context learning (ICL)** — where LLMs perform tasks by seeing examples in their input prompt rather than being fine-tuned. The authors argue that monolithic approaches — feeding an entire CTI report to one large LLM and asking it to extract and structure everything — is inefficient and error-prone. Their innovation is to frame CSKG (Cybersecurity Knowledge Graph) construction as a **multi-agent problem**, where specialization and task decomposition can improve both quality and cost.

## Core Contributions
1. **TACTIC-KG framework design**: A novel decomposition of CTI-to-CSKG extraction into four modular agent roles (extractor, typer, verifier, curator), each with a clear, focused responsibility.
2. **Empirical validation of lightweight agents**: Demonstrates that 3–8B parameter models coordinating via specialized agents outperform larger monolithic baselines on extraction F1-score, typing accuracy, and graph structural similarity — a counterintuitive result suggesting that task modularity and specialization matter more than raw model size.
3. **Practical cost reduction**: Achieves competitive or superior results with significantly smaller total parameter counts, directly addressing deployment costs in cybersecurity contexts.
4. **Benchmark and evaluation**: Human-annotated evaluation dataset and systematic comparison against recent state-of-the-art baselines (e.g., end-to-end LLM extraction).

## Connections to Other Work
TACTIC-KG sits at the intersection of several research areas:
- **[[Knowledge Graph Construction]]** — extending classical IE (information extraction) and entity linking work to the security domain.
- **[[Multi-Agent Systems]]** — drawing parallels to task decomposition and agent coordination in broader multi-agent AI.
- **[[In-Context Learning]]** and **[[Prompting]]** — competes against and builds on recent ICL-based methods for zero-shot knowledge extraction.
- **[[Cybersecurity NLP]]** — a nascent but growing area, including threat report analysis, adversary attribution, and MITRE ATT&CK-based reasoning.
- **Model efficiency & distillation** — aligns with broader trends toward smaller, specialized models rather than one-size-fits-all large models.

The paper implicitly challenges the "bigger is better" paradigm for LLMs in structured prediction tasks.

## Practical Applications
1. **Threat intelligence platforms (Recorded Future, Mandiant, etc.)**: Automate ingestion and structuring of thousands of daily CTI reports into queryable knowledge bases, enabling faster threat correlation and incident response.
2. **SOC (Security Operations Center) automation**: Real-time extraction of IOCs (Indicators of Compromise), attacker techniques, and affected software from raw threat reports, feeding into SIEM systems.
3. **Vulnerability and exploit databases**: Automatically populate structured databases (e.g., NVD, CVE) with attacker tactics, tools, and temporal relationships extracted from disclosed breach reports.
4. **Threat actor profiling**: Build dynamic knowledge graphs linking adversary names, infrastructure, capabilities, and targets without manual curation.
5. **Compliance and regulatory reporting**: Automatically extract and structure cybersecurity incidents mentioned in disclosures or filings for regulatory databases (SEC, industry-specific).

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Lightweight CTI Ingestion SaaS**: A multi-agent powered service that organizations deploy on-premises or in low-cost cloud tiers to convert their private CTI feeds into queryable knowledge graphs; charge per report or indexed asset.
2. **Adversary Intelligence GraphDB**: A real-time, crowd-sourced threat actor knowledge graph built from public and private CTI feeds via TACTIC-KG; offer querying and API access to enterprises (similar to Shodan for threat actors).
3. **Automated Threat Modeling Assistant**: Integrate TACTIC-KG into AppSec workflows — parse vulnerability reports and threat intel, automatically enrich threat models (e.g., STRIDE, DFD) with discovered attack techniques and actor profiles.
4. **Compliance-as-Code for Cybersecurity**: Use TACTIC-KG to auto-generate compliance evidence (e.g., ISO 27001, CIS Controls mappings) by extracting controls and techniques from incident reports and threat intel.
5. **Lightweight Federated Threat Intelligence Network**: Enable security teams to run small-model agents locally while contributing structured threat data to a federated knowledge graph, preserving privacy while benefiting from collective intelligence.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Quality of free-text CTI input**: The framework is only as good as the source reports; ambiguous, poorly written, or adversarially crafted CTI will propagate errors through the pipeline.
2. **Agent coordination overhead**: Four sequential agents may introduce cumulative latency and error propagation (errors in extraction compound in later typing/verification steps); the paper doesn't deeply analyze this failure mode.
3. **Domain specificity**: The framework is tuned for cybersecurity reports (MITRE ATT&CK, typical threat report structure); generalization to other domains or CTI formats is unclear.
4. **Evaluation scope**: Evaluation is on human-annotated CTI reports; scale, robustness on adversarial/synthetic examples, and real-world operational deployment metrics are not discussed.
5. **No reinforcement or feedback loop**: Once constructed, the knowledge graph is static; the paper doesn't describe how to continuously refine agent behavior based on downstream usage or user corrections.
6. **Limited reasoning depth**: The agents appear to perform extraction and classification; more complex reasoning (e.g., inferring implicit relationships, temporal reasoning about campaign evolution) is not addressed.

## Open Questions
1. **Agent failure cascades**: What happens when the extractor misses an entity? Can the verifier or curator detect and correct it, or does the error propagate? A detailed error analysis is needed.
2. **Scalability of specialization**: Does the four-agent decomposition scale if we add more agents (e.g., a relationship reasoner, a temporal reasoner)? Is there an optimal team size?
3. **Cross-domain and cross-format generalization**: How well does TACTIC-KG work on threat reports from non-English sources, adversary blogs, leaked data, or non-standard formats?
4. **Comparison to symbolic/hybrid methods**: The paper doesn't compare against rule-based or lightweight symbolic methods for CTI extraction (e.g., regex + NER pipelines); how much does agentic LLM overhead matter?
5. **Human-in-the-loop integration**: Can human analysts iteratively refine agent behavior or provide feedback? What is the minimum human annotation effort needed to achieve high-quality graphs?
6. **Real-time and streaming scenarios**: Does the framework handle streaming CTI (alerts, syslog entries) or only batch-processed reports?

## Key Takeaway
Decomposing complex information extraction into coordinated teams of smaller, specialized agents achieves better accuracy and stability than single large models while dramatically reducing computational cost — a validated pattern shift for structured prediction in cybersecurity.
