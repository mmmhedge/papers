# Evaluating AI Investment Strategies

**Authors:** Irene Aldridge
**Published:** 2026-06-07 | **arXiv:** [2606.08791v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.08791v1)

## Summary

This paper develops a mathematically exact decomposition for auditing black-box algorithmic decision-makers by decomposing cumulative regret into per-period covariances between costs and decisions. The approach is model-free, requiring only observable inputs and outputs, and extends prior single-period results to full multi-period stochastic dynamic programming, with direct applications to platform auditing, repeated games, and portfolio strategy evaluation.

## Theoretical Background

The paper builds on regret analysis from online learning and stochastic dynamic programming. **Regret** — the cumulative performance gap between an algorithm's decisions and a counterfactual optimal policy — is a standard lens for evaluating sequential decision-makers. Prior work by Aldridge (2026) established a single-period identity connecting regret to covariance between cost vectors and policy decisions. This paper extends that identity to multi-period settings where states evolve stochastically and policies are **Markov** (depend only on current state, not full history). The key theoretical tool is **Bellman recursion** — the dynamic programming principle that optimal value functions satisfy a recursive relationship. The paper also employs **HAC variance** (heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation consistent variance estimation) from econometrics to handle non-i.i.d. noise in trajectory estimation.

## Core Contributions

1. **Multi-period regret decomposition**: Proves that under i.i.d. costs and mean-unbiased Markov policies, cumulative regret equals the sum of per-period covariances $\text{Cov}(c_t, a_t)$ — an exact, closed-form relationship with no approximation error.

2. **Bias corrections for non-stationarity**: Derives explicit corrections for time-varying costs and non-stationary environments, plus a discounted-horizon variant matching standard RL problem formulations.

3. **Rolling-window bias bounds**: Shows that for rolling-window policies, estimation bias decays as $O(d/w)$ (dimension over window size), making the approach practical for high-dimensional problems.

4. **Consistent trajectory estimator**: Develops a model-free estimator that is consistent, asymptotically normal with HAC-corrected variance, and computable in $O(T \cdot nd)$ time — tractable even for large-scale systems.

## Connections to Other Work

This work sits at the intersection of **regret analysis** (standard in online learning and [[reinforcement-learning]]), **mechanism design auditing** (platform accountability), and **strategic behavior** in repeated interactions. The Bellman recursion connection bridges classical [[reinforcement-learning]] algorithms (value iteration, policy gradient) with this covariance-based audit framework. The approach is orthogonal to [[alignment]] concerns but complements them: while alignment focuses on objective specification, this paper audits whether black-box systems actually optimize stated objectives. It relates to model-free policy evaluation but requires no simulator or environment access — only historical decision logs.

## Practical Applications

1. **Platform auditing**: Exchanges, marketplaces, and ad networks can audit algorithmic pricing/matching without access to agent types or private information — only observable bids, prices, allocations, and external performance metrics (e.g., welfare, volume).

2. **Portfolio strategy review**: Asset managers can audit quantitative trading strategies' true cost-to-execution and implicit alpha decay by decomposing realized returns into decision-making and market-structure contributions.

3. **Procurement oversight**: Government and corporate procurement systems can quantify welfare loss from supplier misreporting by estimating the covariance-regret of bidding policies.

4. **Repeated-game diagnosis**: In competitive or collaborative multi-agent settings, covariance reduction signals when a policy is genuinely improving vs. benefiting from environmental drift.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **Algorithmic Audit SaaS**: A compliance tool for fintech platforms and exchanges that ingests historical trade/transaction logs and outputs audit reports quantifying welfare loss, collusion risk, and strategic distortion — requires zero model specification.

2. **Portfolio Performance Attribution Engine**: White-label analytics for hedge funds and asset managers to decompose returns into skill, execution cost, and market regime — competitive edge via model-free auditability.

3. **Mechanism Design Validator**: Consulting tool for B2B platforms (ride-share, freelance marketplaces, DeFi protocols) to certify mechanisms' true welfare impact and detect exploitable covariance structures before launch.

4. **Regulatory Tech (RegTech)**: Automated monitoring service for financial regulators (SEC, CFTC, FCA) to detect unfair algorithmic behavior in markets without requiring algorithm source code or parameter access.

5. **Repeated-Game Analyzer**: Tool for supply-chain networks and business ecosystems to identify whether supplier/buyer policies are genuinely cooperative or extractive, via covariance auditing of transaction histories.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Mean-unbiasedness assumption**: The exact decomposition requires policies to be mean-unbiased Markov — violation (e.g., systematic bias in decision-making) requires bias corrections that may not be model-free.

2. **i.i.d. cost assumption**: While the paper provides non-i.i.d. corrections, highly persistent or autocorrelated cost regimes (e.g., regime-switching markets) may require stronger assumptions or fail to yield consistent estimates.

3. **Observability requirement**: The approach demands full observability of decisions and costs — if the auditor sees only aggregate or noisy versions, estimation bias compounds and $O(d/w)$ scaling may degrade.

4. **Computational scalability**: The $O(T \cdot nd)$ estimator assumes $T$ periods, $n$ state dimensions, $d$ decision dimensions are all manageable; in high-frequency trading or large-scale optimization, $T$ and $d$ may be prohibitive.

5. **Strategic response**: In adversarial settings (e.g., if agents know they're being audited), the decomposition itself becomes a target for manipulation — gaming the covariance structure without changing gross behavior.

## Open Questions

1. **Adaptive vs. optimal policies**: Does the covariance decomposition characterize how close a rolling-window policy is to the Markov-optimal policy? Can you use it to design adaptive online correction?

2. **Partial observability**: How does the framework extend to **partially observable Markov decision processes (POMDPs)** where the auditor sees actions and outcomes but not the true state?

3. **Multi-agent auditing**: In strategic/equilibrium settings (repeated games, market mechanisms), how do you decompose regret when multiple agents' covariances interact? Is there a Nash equilibrium analog?

4. **High-dimensional estimation**: For $d \gg T$ (more decisions than observations), can dimension-reduction or sparsity assumptions make the estimator consistent? What is the minimax rate?

5. **Mechanistic interpretation**: Can covariance-based audit metrics be inverted to infer the *structure* of the policy (e.g., does it use certain state features)? What is the information content of the covariance trajectory alone?

## Key Takeaway

Cumulative regret of a black-box sequential policy can be exactly decomposed into per-period covariances between costs and decisions, enabling model-free auditing of algorithmic systems in mechanism design, repeated games, and finance without access to internal objectives or parameters.
