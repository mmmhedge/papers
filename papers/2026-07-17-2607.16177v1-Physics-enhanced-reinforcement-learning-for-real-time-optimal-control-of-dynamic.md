# Physics-enhanced reinforcement learning for real-time optimal control of dynamical systems

**Authors:** Matteo Tomasetto, Nicolò Botteghi, Gabriele Bruni, Andrea Manzoni
**Published:** 2026-07-17 | **arXiv:** [2607.16177v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16177v1)

## Summary
This paper combines reinforcement learning with physics-based optimization to train controllers for high-dimensional dynamical systems more efficiently. The key insight: when you know the system's equations are differentiable, you can use automatic differentiation to guide the learning process, cutting down the number of trial-and-error interactions needed. The work demonstrates this on fluid dynamics control problems and shows it scales better than standard RL.

## Theoretical Background
Reinforcement learning learns control policies by trial and error—agents take actions, observe outcomes, and adjust. Traditional RL struggles in high dimensions because exploring all possible actions becomes exponentially expensive. Meanwhile, optimal control theory uses gradient-based methods to synthesize controllers when you have explicit knowledge of system dynamics. This paper sits at the intersection: it exploits the fact that many physical systems have differentiable equations (meaning you can compute how small changes propagate forward using calculus). The actor-adjoint algorithm is the technical innovation—the "adjoint" part refers to backward sensitivity analysis, a classical technique from optimal control that efficiently computes how changes in the past affect future outcomes.

## Core Contributions
1. **PEARL framework**: Fuses RL's learning capability with automatic differentiation of known physics, enabling policy gradient computation over short horizons rather than full-trajectory rollouts.
2. **Actor-adjoint algorithm**: Uses adjoint-based sensitivities to approximate value function gradients, reducing environment samples while stabilizing long-horizon learning.
3. **Empirical validation on parametric systems**: Demonstrates generalization across multiple flow configurations (unsteady flow navigation) without requiring dimensionality reduction or multi-agent decomposition.
4. **Scalability demonstration**: Shows RL can handle realistic state/action spaces (high-dimensional) when physics structure is leveraged.

## Connections to Other Work
This builds on [[Policy Gradient Methods]] and [[Differentiable Simulation]] literatures. It echoes themes from [[Physics-Informed Neural Networks]] (PINNs), which embed physics constraints into learning. Related to [[Model-Based Reinforcement Learning]], which also tries to reduce environment interactions by learning dynamics models—but PEARL assumes you already have those models. Contrasts with pure model-free RL and gradient-free methods. The adjoint-sensitivity approach connects to classical methods in [[Optimal Control Theory]] and [[Sensitivity Analysis]].

## Practical Applications
1. **Autonomous vehicle control**: Real-time path planning in complex environments (e.g., urban navigation, obstacle avoidance).
2. **Aerospace control**: Wing design optimization or flight control in transient conditions (landing, takeoff).
3. **Robotics**: Multi-DOF manipulator control where training time is expensive (e.g., surgical robots, industrial arms).
4. **Fluid engineering**: Flow control for drag reduction, mixing enhancement, or noise suppression in real-time systems.
5. **Power systems**: Demand-side management and microgrid stabilization with fast adaptation requirements.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Physics-aware ML platform for industrial control**: A SaaS tool that auto-converts physics models (PDEs, ODEs from CAD/simulation) into efficient RL agents—target: manufacturing (machining, injection molding, chemical reactors).
2. **Real-time flow control software**: Licensed control algorithms for HVAC, ventilation, and cleanroom systems that adapt to parametric changes without retraining.
3. **Autonomous drone guidance system**: Embedded RL controller for drones operating in dynamic wind fields—sell to surveying, delivery, or inspection companies.
4. **Digital twin + adaptive control bundle**: Combine physics simulators with PEARL for equipment manufacturers to offer adaptive maintenance and performance optimization.
5. **Robotics-as-a-service for high-precision tasks**: Deploy physics-enhanced RL controllers in precision manufacturing (semiconductor assembly, biotech pipetting) as a managed service.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Requires differentiable dynamics**: Only works when you have explicit, differentiable equations—excludes systems with black-box simulators or discontinuities (friction, impact).
2. **Short-horizon bias**: Uses short-horizon policy gradients to avoid instability, which may miss long-term dependencies in planning.
3. **Parametric assumption**: Generalization across "parametric scenarios" assumes the variation lies within a known parameter space; novel configurations break down.
4. **Computational overhead of adjoint**: Computing sensitivities scales with state/action dimension; not truly "free" for very high-dimensional systems.
5. **Limited experimental validation**: Only two flow control problems shown; unclear how well it transfers to other domains (robotics, finance, etc.).
6. **Neural network function approximation**: Still relies on NN value/policy approximators, which bring their own approximation errors and training challenges.

## Open Questions
1. **How does PEARL perform on systems with partial observability** or noisy observations (more realistic sensors)?
2. **Can the method handle non-smooth or discontinuous dynamics** (e.g., contact, friction, switching modes)?
3. **What is the theoretical sample complexity** compared to standard RL and optimal control? Is there a formal guarantee?
4. **How does performance degrade when physics is approximate** (e.g., simplified model, unknown parameters)?
5. **Can PEARL be extended to stochastic systems** or incorporate distributional robustness?
6. **Does it scale to 1000+ dimensional state spaces** in practice, or are there computational bottlenecks?
7. **How does it compare to recent neural ODE and differentiable programming approaches** in other contexts?

## Key Takeaway
Mixing physics gradients with reinforcement learning lets you train high-dimensional controllers faster and more reliably than pure RL.
