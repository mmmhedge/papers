# Pareto Optimal Centralized Risk Sharing with Multiple Agents: Inclusivity and Fairness

**Authors:** Debora Daniela Escobar, Wing Fung Chong
**Published:** 2026-06-22 | **arXiv:** [2606.22956v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.22956v1)

## Summary
This paper addresses centralized risk sharing among multiple policyholders where risks are transferred to a central insurer at endogenously determined prices. The authors show that classical Pareto optimality fails to guarantee fair representation of all agents and propose **inclusive and fair Pareto optimality**, a refinement that ensures every agent appears exactly once in a sequential optimization procedure, balancing efficiency with fairness and transparency.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on:
- **Classical Pareto optimality** and its characterizations (particularly Geoffrion-proper Pareto optimality)
- **Multiobjective optimization** theory, where multiple conflicting objectives require careful treatment
- **Risk transfer and insurance economics**: the fundamental mechanism where agents cede risks to a central pool at prices determined by pricing functionals (e.g., Expected Shortfall)
- **Sequential optimization methods** for revealing agent preferences in multiobjective settings
- **Bilateral Pareto characterizations**: the mathematical foundations linking scalarized objectives to Pareto points

## Core Contributions
1. **Diagnosis of classical Pareto optimality's limitations**: Demonstrates that Pareto-optimal allocations can assign zero weight to some agents, obscure individual risk positions through group aggregates, and lack balanced representation in scalarized formulations
2. **Introduction of inclusive and fair Pareto optimality**: A novel refinement concept requiring each agent to appear exactly once (either individually or grouped) in a finite ordered sequence of sequential optimizations
3. **Equivalence theorem**: Proves that inclusive and fair Pareto optimality is equivalent to balanced sequential optimization, positioning it precisely between Geoffrion-proper and classical Pareto optimality
4. **Concrete illustration**: Demonstrates the framework using Expected Shortfall as the pricing functional, with worked examples

## Connections to Other Work
This paper sits at the intersection of:
- **[[Multi-Agent Systems]]**: The central problem involves multiple autonomous agents with conflicting preferences negotiating risk allocations
- **[[Game Theory]]**: The pricing mechanism and agent responses create a game-theoretic setting where agents strategically decide ceded risks
- **[[Portfolio Optimization]]** and **[[Risk-Sensitive Optimization]]**: The allocation problem mirrors portfolio selection but with multiple agents and fairness constraints
- **[[Market Microstructure]]**: Endogenous pricing of ceded risks relates to how prices emerge from agent demand
- **[[Mechanism Design]]**: The paper implicitly addresses how to design a fair risk-sharing mechanism that respects agent representation

## Practical Applications
1. **Insurance pools and consortia**: When multiple insurers or policyholders pool resources, ensuring fair representation prevents dominant players from capturing disproportionate benefits
2. **Reinsurance treaties**: Determining how primary insurers should cede risks to reinsurers while maintaining balanced negotiation positions
3. **Climate risk pools**: For climate-vulnerable regions/countries, allocating catastrophic risks fairly without marginalizing smaller participants
4. **Corporate risk syndicates**: When multiple corporations jointly hedge operational or financial risks through a central vehicle
5. **Mutual insurance cooperatives**: Ensuring member fairness in risk pooling arrangements where governance and capital allocation must balance competing interests

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Fair Risk Allocation Platform**: A software platform for insurance syndicates that computes inclusive and fair Pareto-optimal allocations, ensuring every participant is explicitly represented in negotiation sequences—marketed as "Transparent Risk Sharing"
2. **Cooperative Insurance Exchange**: A blockchain-enabled mutual insurance exchange where members use inclusive Pareto algorithms to automatically determine fair premium and coverage terms, with distributed governance
3. **Climate Risk Facility Manager**: A specialized fund manager for climate-risk pooling across municipalities/small nations, using fair Pareto methods to allocate catastrophic risk without favoring large participants
4. **Reinsurance Optimization SaaS**: Enterprise software for reinsurance brokers that models ceded risk allocations and suggests inclusive Pareto-optimal treaties, improving negotiation outcomes
5. **Actuarial Fairness Auditor**: Consulting service that audits existing risk-sharing arrangements (insurance pools, pension funds, etc.) against inclusive Pareto criteria, identifying underrepresented agents and recommending rebalancing

## Technical Limitations
1. **Computational complexity**: Finding and characterizing all inclusive and fair Pareto points may be computationally expensive as the number of agents grows; scalability to thousands of agents unclear
2. **Pricing functional assumptions**: The framework assumes pricing functionals (e.g., Expected Shortfall) are well-defined and agreed upon; real markets often have heterogeneous, time-varying pricing beliefs
3. **Sequential ordering dependency**: The concept requires a "finite ordered sequence" of optimizations, but the paper does not fully explore how sensitive the resulting allocations are to ordering choices, or whether all orderings yield the same equivalence class
4. **No dynamic analysis**: The paper studies static allocations; real risk sharing is dynamic, with renegotiation, arrivals/departures of agents, and parameter changes
5. **Perfect information assumption**: Assumes agents truthfully reveal preferences; incentive compatibility under incomplete information is not addressed
6. **Limited empirical validation**: The illustrative example uses synthetic data; real-world applicability to complex insurance markets with correlated risks, basis risk, and operational constraints untested

## Open Questions
1. **Computational algorithms**: What is the complexity class for finding inclusive and fair Pareto points? Can polynomial-time algorithms be developed for specific pricing functionals?
2. **Ordering ambiguities**: If different sequential orderings yield different allocations, how should practitioners choose orderings? Is there a canonical "fairest" ordering?
3. **Dynamic generalization**: Can inclusive and fair Pareto optimality be extended to multi-period settings where agents arrive/leave and risks evolve?
4. **Strategic robustness**: If agents anticipate the sequential optimization procedure, do they have incentives to misreport? What mechanism-design safeguards are needed?
5. **Empirical validation**: How do inclusive and fair allocations compare to real market allocations in insurance syndicates or reinsurance treaties? Do practitioners converge to such allocations naturally?
6. **Group structure design**: When agents can be grouped, how should groupings be determined? Should this be endogenous to the optimization?

## Key Takeaway
Inclusive and fair Pareto optimality ensures that in multiobjective risk sharing, efficiency is not achieved by silencing some agents, requiring every participant to appear explicitly in a sequential optimization procedure.
