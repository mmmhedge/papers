# Social Simulations: from Agent-Based Modeling to Digital Twins

**Authors:** Erica Cau, Andrea Failla, Valentina Pansanella, Giulio Rossetti
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.13693v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13693v1)

## Summary
This chapter traces how social simulation has evolved from hand-coded agent models to AI-powered digital twins—computational replicas of real social systems. It shows a progression from abstract models (used to understand general social patterns) to realistic, data-driven simulations (used to represent specific communities or institutions). The work matters because it bridges classical computational social science with modern generative AI, offering a roadmap for building trustworthy simulations of human systems.

## Theoretical Background
Agent-based modeling (ABM) is a long-standing approach where individual agents follow explicit rules and interact in a virtual environment to produce emergent collective behavior. Classical ABM helped social scientists understand phenomena like segregation, consensus formation, and market dynamics without requiring detailed real-world data.

More recently, large language models (LLMs)—neural networks trained to predict sequences of text—have shown they can capture human-like reasoning and language patterns. The chapter bridges these two traditions: using LLM-based agents (agents that reason using language models) to populate simulations, then grounding those simulations in real data to create digital twins (virtual replicas tied to real-world systems via continuous data feeds).

## Core Contributions
- Explicit framework showing three paradigms: classical rule-based ABM → LLM-enhanced simulations → data-grounded social digital twins
- Discussion of methodological transitions: moving from stylized models (explaining mechanisms) to high-fidelity representations (predicting or monitoring specific systems)
- Articulation of trade-offs: abstract models are interpretable and general; realistic digital twins are less transparent but more actionable for specific populations or institutions

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[Agent-Based Modeling]], [[Large Language Models]], and digital twins (a concept from engineering—replicating physical systems digitally). It extends prior work on LLM agents (e.g., using LLMs as controllable behavioral components) by embedding them in social simulation frameworks. The chapter implicitly addresses [[Alignment]] concerns: as simulations become more realistic, ensuring they faithfully represent real social dynamics (not artifacts of the model) becomes critical.

## Practical Applications
- **Epidemic and public health modeling**: Simulating how information spreads through communities during health crises, with realistic human reasoning about vaccines or masks
- **Urban planning**: Digital twins of neighborhoods to test housing policies, transport changes, or community programs before implementation
- **Organizational management**: Simulating workforces or teams to predict impact of new policies, remote-work shifts, or hiring changes
- **Policy evaluation**: Modeling how welfare, education, or taxation changes propagate through a population before rollout
- **Crisis management**: Real-time digital twins of affected populations during disasters to optimize resource allocation

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Policy Simulation Platform**: SaaS for governments to build and test policy changes on digital twins of their populations before implementation; revenue via licensing and consulting
2. **Organizational Digital Twin**: Enterprise tool for HR/OD teams to simulate workforce dynamics, predict churn, test culture changes; target large corporations
3. **Community Resilience Dashboard**: Real-time digital twin of a city or neighborhood for NGOs and local governments to forecast social needs and optimize intervention
4. **Market Research Simulation**: Use LLM agents to simulate consumer behavior in response to new products or campaigns; more flexible than traditional focus groups
5. **Crisis Response Optimizer**: Platform combining digital twins with real-time data (mobile signals, social media) to guide emergency response in real-world events (floods, refugee movements)

## Technical Limitations
- **Data scarcity**: Building a faithful digital twin requires detailed real-world data (mobility patterns, demographics, behavior) that is often proprietary, sparse, or privacy-protected
- **Validation challenge**: How do you verify a complex social simulation is "correct"? Real-world social systems have no ground truth for counterfactual predictions
- **LLM brittleness**: LLM-based agents may reproduce biases in training data, hallucinate plausible but false behaviors, or fail on out-of-distribution scenarios
- **Opacity**: Digital twins that are "high-fidelity" are often black boxes—hard to explain why they predict X outcome, limiting trust and regulatory acceptance
- **Computational cost**: Realistic simulations of large populations (millions of agents with LLM reasoning) are computationally expensive
- **Feedback loops**: If a digital twin is used to make real policy, does the system then adapt in ways the twin did not predict?

## Open Questions
- How do you measure whether a social digital twin is accurate without long-term real-world experiments?
- Can LLM agents learn and adapt within simulations, or do they require retraining/recalibration?
- What privacy and ethical safeguards are needed when building digital twins of real communities?
- How do you combine digital twins (deterministic-ish models) with uncertainty quantification for policy decisions?
- Can digital twins help detect early warning signs of social instability or inform interventions without becoming surveillance tools?
- How does the fidelity-interpretability trade-off affect adoption by policymakers vs. social scientists?

## Key Takeaway
Social simulations are evolving from abstract rule-based models to AI-powered, data-driven digital twins of real communities—more realistic but harder to interpret and validate.
