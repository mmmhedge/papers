# FedLAB: Traceable Semantic Codebooks for Federated Multimodal Graph Foundation Learning

**Authors:** Zekai Chen, Kairui Yang, Xuaner Chen, Xunkai Li, Xun Wu et al.
**Published:** 2026-06-30 | **arXiv:** [2606.32016v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.32016v1)

## Summary

This paper introduces FedLAB, a framework for training multimodal graph foundation models across decentralized clients while keeping data private. Unlike standard federated learning approaches that share parameters or embeddings, FedLAB organizes knowledge into semantic codebooks that explicitly track *why* predictions are made—linking evidence from text, images, node identity, and network structure. The key innovation is maintaining interpretability (what the authors call "semantic traceability") even in federated settings where raw data never leaves individual devices.

## Theoretical Background

Federated learning trains models across distributed clients without centralizing private data—typically by averaging model updates. Multimodal learning combines information from different data types (text, images, etc.). Graph foundation models are large pre-trained models that learn general-purpose representations from graph-structured data with rich node/edge attributes, similar to how transformers (neural networks using attention mechanisms to relate distant tokens) work for language.

The paper builds on: (1) federated learning frameworks that avoid data centralization; (2) multimodal representation learning, which fuses signals from different modalities; (3) semantic codebooks—compact, interpretable dictionaries that map raw inputs to discrete prototype representations. Prior work uses codebooks for compression or transfer, but doesn't explicitly expose *which* modality signals and structural contexts contribute to each decision.

## Core Contributions

1. **Traceable semantic codebook design**: Hierarchically organizes knowledge into typed codebooks for modality evidence (what image/text features activate), node semantics (what a node represents), and topology context (graph neighborhood structure). This structure directly exposes causal reasoning chains.

2. **Federated semantic barycenter pre-training**: A new optimization procedure that refines codebooks across clients without sharing raw multimodal contents or local graph structures. Uses barycenter (geometric center) ideas to align codebooks while maintaining privacy.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: Validates on 10 graph benchmarks across 6 downstream tasks (node classification, link prediction, graph classification), showing up to 7.53% improvement over state-of-the-art while maintaining semantic interpretability.

## Connections to Other Work

Relates to [[Attention Is All You Need]] and modern foundation models (learning reusable representations at scale); federated learning systems like FedAvg (averaging client updates) and recent federated graph learning; and [[mechanistic-interpretability]] work (explicitly exposing model reasoning). Unlike pure privacy-preserving methods (differential privacy, secure aggregation), FedLAB trades some obfuscation for interpretability. Also connects to prototype-based learning and knowledge distillation, but grounds prototypes in explicit multimodal and structural meaning.

## Practical Applications

1. **Healthcare networks**: Hospitals share patient graph data (demographic attributes + medical images + clinical notes + referral networks) for disease prediction without exposing patient records; semantic traces show which clinical evidence + network patterns drive risk scores.

2. **Supply chain transparency**: Suppliers, manufacturers, distributors maintain local knowledge graphs (product attributes, images, supplier relationships); federated learning enables demand forecasting while traces explain which supplier characteristics and network positions influenced predictions.

3. **Decentralized e-commerce recommendation**: Multiple sellers keep inventory graphs (item images, descriptions, sales networks) private; federated foundation models enable cross-platform recommendations with transparent codebooks showing which product features and user-seller relationships matter.

4. **Scientific collaboration networks**: Research institutions preserve local publication graphs (paper text, figures, author networks) while collaborating on meta-analysis; semantic traces expose which document evidence and collaboration patterns shaped predictions.

## Product & Startup Ideas

2. **Semantic audit tool**: Compliance software for regulated industries (banking, healthcare) that certifies federated models by introspecting codebook traces—proving which data actually influenced decisions without accessing raw records.

3. **Graph privacy SDK**: Developer toolkit enabling any organization to adopt FedLAB for internal graph applications. Sell consulting services for implementation in specific domains (logistics, financial crime, clinical networks).

4. **Interpretable recommendation engine**: Next-gen recommendation system for multi-tenant B2B platforms (B2B SaaS, marketplaces) that learns collaboratively without sharing customer data, while showing customers *why* recommendations happened.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Codebook scalability**: As graphs and modalities grow, the number of prototype codes may explode; the paper doesn't discuss memory/communication costs for very large codebooks across hundreds of clients.

2. **Privacy quantification**: "Privacy constraints" are assumed but not formally measured (no differential privacy guarantees, no proof against membership inference attacks). Semantic codebooks *may* leak information if reverse-engineered.

3. **Heterogeneous modalities**: Assumes all clients have aligned modality types (text + image + topology). If clients have radically different data schemas, codebook alignment becomes harder.

4. **Non-convex optimization under federation**: Semantic barycenter pre-training is complex; convergence guarantees under client drift and partial participation are not proven.

5. **Interpretability claims**: "Semantic trace" exposure assumes codebook prototypes are actually human-interpretable. Complex real-world features may be semantically opaque even if organized hierarchically.

## Open Questions

1. **Privacy auditing**: Can you formally bound information leakage from semantic codebooks? How do they compare to differential privacy baselines in practice?

2. **Heterogeneous clients**: How does performance degrade when clients have different modality availability (some missing images, some missing text)? Can partial codebooks work?

3. **Dynamic graphs**: Real networks evolve; how do codebooks adapt as new nodes/edges arrive without full retraining?

4. **Competing incentives**: In multi-client settings, how do we prevent clients from poisoning shared codebooks? What Byzantine-robust variants exist?

5. **Modality importance**: Do codebooks automatically learn which modalities matter most for each task, or does importance depend on the downstream task? Can a single codebook support multiple objectives?

## Key Takeaway

FedLAB makes multimodal graph foundation learning simultaneously private (raw data stays local), interpretable (semantic codebooks explicitly expose reasoning), and effective (beats baselines by up to 7.53%), solving the hard constraint of traceability under strict data isolation.
