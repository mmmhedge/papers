# ASMR: Agentic Schema Generation for Ship Maintenance Report Writing

**Authors:** Sohrab Namazi Nia, Amogh Dalal, Ning Sa, Peter Ly, Marti Zentmaier et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-09 | **arXiv:** [2607.08177v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08177v1)

## Summary
This paper presents ASMR, a system that automatically discovers data schemas (structured templates) from historical ship maintenance reports using two specialized AI agents working together. Rather than manually defining what information should go in each report type, the system learns from past reports to generate compact, consistent schemas that help future report writers be more complete and clear — addressing a real problem in maritime operations where reports are critical but often inconsistent.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on several foundational ideas: **schema induction** (automatically inferring structured data templates from unstructured text), **multi-agent systems** (coordinating specialized agents to solve complex problems), and **reinforcement learning** (training agents to optimize for specific objectives like compactness and informativeness). The paper combines semantic field extraction (pulling meaningful concepts from narratives) with RL-based optimization (finding the best combinations of those fields). This sits at the intersection of **information extraction**, **agentic AI** (systems composed of autonomous agents with specific roles), and **human-in-the-loop AI design** (creating outputs that help humans work better).

## Core Contributions
1. **Modular agentic framework for schema generation**: Two specialized agents (Field Generation and Structural Optimizer) that decompose the schema discovery problem into extraction and optimization phases.
2. **Adaptive multi-granularity clustering**: The Field Generation Agent extracts semantic concepts at varying levels of abstraction from maintenance narratives, rather than using fixed granularity.
3. **RL-based schema optimization**: The Structural Optimizer uses reinforcement learning to trade off three competing objectives — compactness, informativeness, and non-redundancy — without requiring manual weighting.
4. **Domain application**: Demonstrates the approach on maritime maintenance reports, a real but understudied domain where structured schemas could significantly improve operational safety and compliance.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to broader work in **information extraction** and **ontology learning** (automatically discovering domain structure), though those fields typically focus on extracting facts rather than generating schemas. ## Practical Applications
1. **Maritime industry**: Standardized maintenance report schemas improve safety compliance, equipment tracking, and crew communication across vessels and shipping companies.
2. **Aviation maintenance**: Similar agentic schema generation could standardize aircraft maintenance logs, critical for safety and regulatory audits.
3. **Medical record standardization**: Healthcare systems could use this to automatically discover optimal EHR schemas that capture essential clinical information while reducing redundancy.
4. **Industrial equipment maintenance**: Manufacturing plants could auto-generate asset-specific maintenance report templates that fit their actual operational needs.
5. **Insurance claims processing**: Auto-generate schemas from historical claims to ensure adjusters capture all necessary information for faster processing and reduced disputes.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Maritime Compliance SaaS**: A platform that analyzes a shipping company's existing maintenance reports, auto-generates standardized schemas, and provides a guided report-writing interface — reducing compliance violations and improving insurance rates.
2. **Enterprise Schema Discovery Engine**: General-purpose tool for any industry (healthcare, manufacturing, aviation) to auto-discover optimal data schemas from historical records, with role-based templates and audit trails.
3. **AI-Powered Form Builder**: Automatic form generation service that ingests unstructured historical data from a domain and produces fillable, intelligent forms that adapt based on user context.
4. **Maintenance Intelligence Platform**: Combines schema generation with analytics — standardize reports *and* surface actionable insights (common failures, early warning indicators) from the resulting structured data.
5. **Regulatory Document Template Generator**: Helps organizations in regulated industries (pharma, finance, energy) auto-generate compliant documentation templates based on historical submissions and regulatory requirements.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Schema quality depends on input data quality**: If historical reports are themselves poorly written or incomplete, the induced schemas may reflect those biases rather than ideal information structures.
2. **Scalability to very large corpora**: The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity; multi-granularity clustering could become expensive on massive report collections.
3. **Domain adaptation unclear**: It's unclear how well schemas learned from one company's reports transfer to similar but different contexts (e.g., different ship types, different operational regions).
4. **RL reward design**: The paper mentions trading off compactness, informativeness, and non-redundancy, but doesn't fully specify how these objectives are weighted or balanced — this could significantly affect outputs.
5. **Human validation not detailed**: The paper doesn't deeply explore how well domain experts (ship captains, maintenance crews) actually accept or use the auto-generated schemas.
6. **Semantic drift**: As maritime operations evolve, schemas may become stale; the paper doesn't address online learning or schema update mechanisms.

## Open Questions
1. How sensitive are the discovered schemas to the hyperparameters of the clustering algorithm? What's the stability of schema generation across different random seeds?
2. Can schemas transfer across different ship types or companies, or are they entirely domain-specific?
3. How much historical data is needed to reliably discover a good schema? Is there a data efficiency frontier?
4. Could the RL objective be learned from user feedback (e.g., which schemas actually lead to more useful reports) rather than specified upfront?
5. How do the auto-generated schemas compare (in user satisfaction, report completeness, consistency) to expert-designed schemas?
6. Can the framework handle reports with temporal/sequential structure (e.g., a repair that spans multiple days with interdependencies)?

## Key Takeaway
Automatic schema discovery via multi-agent systems can help industries (especially maritime) standardize unstructured operational reports into more complete, consistent, and actionable structured formats.
