# 1-Lipschitz Neural Networks on Hadamard Manifolds

**Authors:** Davide Murari, Marta Ghirardelli, Ben Adcock, Elena Celledoni, Brynjulf Owren et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-21 | **arXiv:** [2607.19335v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19335v1)

## Summary
This paper constructs neural network layers that are provably 1-Lipschitz (slope-bounded) on curved spaces called Hadamard manifolds, rather than flat Euclidean spaces. Controlling Lipschitz constants is a key way to make neural networks robust to small input perturbations; the novelty here is extending this to manifolds (curved spaces) like hyperbolic geometry and matrices of positive eigenvalues. The work matters because many real data naturally live on such manifolds, and ensuring robustness there could improve safety-critical applications.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two main threads. First, **Lipschitz-constrained neural networks** — networks where the maximum rate of output change per unit input change is bounded — are known to improve robustness and stability (prior work by Cisse et al., Tsuzuku et al.). Most methods enforce this in Euclidean space by constraining weight matrices.

Second, the paper works on **Hadamard manifolds** — a class of curved spaces that generalize flat space. Key examples: hyperbolic spaces (negatively curved, used in some NLP embeddings) and the manifold of symmetric positive definite (SPD) matrices (used in covariance estimation, Riemannian optimization). On manifolds, distance and "straight lines" differ from Euclidean geometry.

The core technical tool is **Busemann functions** — a differential geometry concept that acts like a distance-measuring function on manifolds. The paper exploits **Busemann gradient flows** (optimization flows that follow these distance functions) to design layers that preserve the manifold structure while bounding the Lipschitz constant.

## Core Contributions
1. **First explicit construction of 1-Lipschitz layers on Hadamard manifolds** — prior work constrained Lipschitz constants in Euclidean settings; this extends the theory to manifolds using Busemann function geometry.

2. **Concrete layer designs for two important manifolds**: hyperbolic space (via the Poincaré disk) and SPD matrices (Wishart geometry).

3. **Quasi-α-firmly nonexpansive property** — the layers satisfy a stronger geometric property beyond 1-Lipschitz, ensuring convergence in iterative algorithms.

4. **Empirical validation**: robust classification on the Poincaré disk and a covariance reconstruction application (masked-Wishart problem) where the nonexpansive denoiser outperforms Euclidean baselines.

## Connections to Other Work
This extends the [[Lipschitz-constrained neural networks]] literature (e.g., spectral normalization, orthogonal convolutions) from Euclidean to manifold settings. It relates to [[Riemannian neural networks]] and [[Hyperbolic neural networks]] (which embed data in hyperbolic space but typically don't enforce Lipschitz guarantees). The work also connects to **monotone operator theory** and **proximal methods** via firmly nonexpansive operators — concepts used in convex optimization that the paper brings to manifold-based deep learning.

The Plug-and-Play prior approach for covariance reconstruction echoes recent work in inverse problems where learned denoisers replace hand-crafted priors.

## Practical Applications
1. **Robust hyperbolic embeddings** — improving robustness of embedding-based retrieval systems (e.g., hierarchical or tree-like data) against adversarial or noisy perturbations.

2. **Covariance estimation in high dimensions** — portfolio optimization, Gaussian graphical models, and medical imaging often need robust covariance estimates; the SPD-valued denoiser could improve these under partial or corrupted observations.

3. **Adversarial robustness for manifold data** — securing classifiers on non-Euclidean data (e.g., geometric deep learning on point clouds, shape analysis).

4. **Inverse problems with manifold priors** — medical imaging (covariance-based denoising) and seismic inversion where the underlying quantity (covariance, elastic parameters) naturally lies on a manifold.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Computational overhead** — Busemann functions and manifold operations (exponential/logarithmic maps) are more expensive than Euclidean arithmetic; scalability to very large networks or high-dimensional manifolds is unclear.

2. **Limited experimental scope** — only two manifolds (Poincaré disk and SPD matrices) and relatively small-scale numerical tests; generalization to deeper networks or larger datasets is not demonstrated.

3. **Manifold accessibility** — the theory requires knowing the manifold structure explicitly (geodesics, exponential maps). For real data, the true manifold may be unknown or data may only approximately lie on it.

4. **1-Lipschitz strictness** — enforcing exactly 1-Lipschitz (not looser constants like 2-Lipschitz) may be overly conservative and hurt expressiveness; the paper doesn't explore this trade-off empirically.

5. **No comparison to simpler alternatives** — unclear how much the manifold-aware design improves over, say, simply applying standard Lipschitz methods in a tangent space or using Log-Euclidean embeddings (beyond the covariance experiment).

## Open Questions
1. **Scalability** — How do these layers perform in deep networks (10+ layers) on realistic high-dimensional manifolds? Is there a computational bottleneck?

2. **Expressiveness vs. robustness trade-off** — Does enforcing 1-Lipschitz on manifolds limit function approximation more than on Euclidean spaces? Can you loosen it (e.g., 1.5-Lipschitz) and gain expressiveness?

3. **Adversarial robustness** — The paper shows robustness to small hyperbolic perturbations; how does this compare to certified robustness bounds on Euclidean networks? What is the formal adversarial guarantee?

4. **Manifold learning** — Can these layers help *learn* the manifold structure from data, or do they require the manifold to be known a priori?

5. **Extension to other manifolds** — How would the approach generalize to, e.g., Riemannian symmetric spaces, Grassmannians, or quotient manifolds? Is there a general recipe?

6. **Optimization landscape** — How does the curvature of the manifold affect training dynamics and convergence of gradient descent on these networks?

## Key Takeaway
Lipschitz-bounded neural networks on curved spaces enable robust learning when data naturally lives on manifolds like hyperbolic geometry or covariance matrices.
