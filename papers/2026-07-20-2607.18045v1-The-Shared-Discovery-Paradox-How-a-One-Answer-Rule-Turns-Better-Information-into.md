# The Shared Discovery Paradox: How a One-Answer Rule Turns Better Information into Worse Search

**Authors:** Yohei Nakajima
**Published:** 2026-07-20 | **arXiv:** [2607.18045v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18045v1)

## Summary
This paper reveals a paradox in how organizations share pooled information: better aggregated beliefs can paradoxically reduce discovery when everyone acts on the same single recommendation. The authors develop an exact mathematical benchmark with searchers hunting for a target and show that a one-answer protocol compresses a rich portfolio of potential actions into repetitive choices, destroying coverage despite improved accuracy.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on information aggregation theory and multi-agent coordination problems. It connects to classical game theory (Nash equilibrium, potential games) and mechanism design. The paper models a discovery problem where agents have noisy private signals and must collectively find a target. Key concepts: information pooling (combining private clues into a shared ranking), the tension between accuracy (how correct the pooled belief is) and coverage (how many searchers explore different locations), and incentive structures (how payoff rules affect agent behavior). The "price of anarchy" — the ratio of centralized optimal outcomes to decentralized equilibrium outcomes — measures coordination loss.

## Core Contributions
1. **The Shared Discovery Paradox**: Formalizes how one-answer rules degrade coverage (0.8322 → 0.3835) despite improving recommendation accuracy (0.20 → 0.3835). The paradox is a *protocol failure*, not an information failure.

2. **Exact Solvable Benchmark**: A reproducible 16-box, 1-target, 8-searcher model with noisy signals that separates information quality, allocation efficiency, incentives, and correlation into measurable quantities.

3. **Equilibrium Analysis Under Self-Interest**: Shows equal-split prize games form potential games with symmetric anonymous equilibrium following a water-filling rule. Derives exact mixed price of anarchy = 2 − 1/N.

4. **Sole-Rescue Mechanism**: A reward paying only the unique discoverer makes all pure Nash equilibria first-best, aligning selfish incentives with social optimality.

5. **Latent Correlation Model**: Demonstrates how correlated private reports collapse effective discovery channels, with a measurable tipping point (c = 0.788462) where market equilibrium overtakes decentralized following.

## Practical Applications
1. **Organizational Search**: Companies conducting exploratory research or hiring campaigns often create one ranked candidate list — this shows why that can suppress diversity of exploration and how portfolio-based allocation recovers performance.

2. **Market-Making and Trading Desks**: A single price consensus can suppress traders from exploring alternative strategies; diversified order routing improves discovery.

3. **Content Recommendation Platforms**: Single-ranking feeds reduce platform coverage of content; portfolio-based recommendations (showing diverse options) recover discovery metrics.

4. **Crisis Response Coordination**: First responders rely on shared situational reports; the model shows when to split search efforts versus consolidate, and how to incentivize independent discovery (e.g., bonuses for unique rescue).

5. **Clinical Diagnosis**: Hospitals pooling test results into one recommended diagnosis can suppress searching for alternative explanations; the sole-rescue rule parallels rare disease discovery incentives.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Decentralized Search Coordination Platform**: A tool for research teams (venture scouts, pharmaceutical researchers, talent hunters) that explicitly allocates diverse search strategies using portfolio rules, showing when to pool vs. diversify in real time.

2. **Incentive-Aligned Exploration-as-a-Service**: A service that designs reward structures (like the sole-rescue mechanism) for companies running distributed discovery tasks, automatically tuning payoffs to align selfish agent behavior with organizational coverage goals.

3. **Information Dependency Auditor**: Software that analyzes how correlated reports are in an organization's data collection (news feeds, employee feedback, market signals) and predicts the collapse point where consensus destroys discovery, flagging risk.

4. **Adaptive Recommendation Protocol**: A recommendation engine that detects when pooled consensus is harming diversity coverage and automatically switches between single-answer, portfolio, and decentralized modes based on live performance metrics.

5. **Mechanism Design Consultant Tool**: A SaaS for designing multi-agent discovery problems (search competitions, R&D contests, open innovation), auto-tuning reward rules to hit first-best equilibrium given correlation structure and team size.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Toy Problem Scale**: The 16-box benchmark is illustrative but tiny. Real-world discovery spaces are vastly larger (product space, hypothesis space, market opportunities), and scaling properties may not preserve the exact paradox dynamics.

2. **Noiseless Private Signals Assumption**: Agents have noisy signals, but the model doesn't capture how *signal quality varies* across agents or *adaptive learning* as agents update beliefs after observing others' discoveries.

3. **Static Payoff Structure**: The game is one-shot. Real organizations iterate and reputational incentives matter; the model doesn't capture how repeat play, trust, or signaling effects change equilibrium.

4. **No Strategic Lying or Misreporting**: Agents report clues truthfully. In practice, competitors suppress information or exaggerate findings, fundamentally breaking the pooling mechanism.

5. **Finite Searcher Assumption**: Results depend on N = 8. The large-market limit (N → ∞) behavior differs; practical applicability to small teams unclear.

6. **Symmetry and Exchangeability**: The model assumes agents are identical and reports are exchangeable. Real teams have skill heterogeneity, domain expertise, and correlated biases that violate these assumptions.

## Open Questions
1. **Computational Hardness**: Can the paradox be formalized for larger discovery spaces (>1000 options), or does the exact solvability break down?

2. **Adaptive Protocols**: What if the planner can dynamically adjust the pooling rule as private reports arrive and correlation structure becomes visible? Can online algorithms beat the static one-answer rule without losing the information gains?

3. **Signal Heterogeneity**: How do results change if agents have asymmetric noise levels or private information quality? Does the sole-rescue rule still achieve first-best?

4. **Misreporting and Robustness**: If agents can strategically withhold or distort reports, what mechanisms prevent the pooling system from collapsing? Is there a Byzantine-robust version?

5. **Experiential Learning**: In repeated discovery games, how do agents learn to coordinate on efficient mixed strategies? Does the water-filling equilibrium emerge behaviorally?

6. **Continuous Search Space**: The discrete 16-box model is analytically clean, but real discovery (drug discovery, materials science) involves continuous spaces. Do the paradox and recovery results generalize?

## Key Takeaway
Pooling information improves accuracy but kills coverage; split rewards align agents to rediscover diversity.
