# Language Models as Measurement Apparatus for Culture

**Authors:** Kent K. Chang
**Published:** 2026-07-02 | **arXiv:** [2607.02459v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02459v1)

## Summary
This paper argues that using language models to measure cultural phenomena is not neutral observation but an active process where the model, data, and evaluation choices actually shape what "culture" means. Drawing on philosophy of science (specifically Karen Barad's work on how scientific instruments co-create the phenomena they measure), the author shows through studies of TV/film dialogue that language models have already absorbed cultural material they're measuring, making the boundary between tool and subject fundamentally blurred. The paper calls for researchers to treat each design choice as a conscious ethical and methodological commitment.

## Theoretical Background
This work sits at the intersection of NLP, cultural studies, and philosophy of science. It builds on Karen Barad's **agential realism** — the idea that scientific instruments don't passively discover pre-existing phenomena but actively participate in creating them through what Barad calls an "agential cut" (the boundary between observer and observed). Prior NLP work treats cultural measurement as straightforward: train a model, apply it to text, measure outputs. This paper challenges that framing by arguing that models are already culturally saturated — they've learned cultural patterns from their training data, so they cannot be external measuring devices. The approach treats culture not as a fixed property to extract, but as something co-produced through the measurement apparatus itself.

## Core Contributions
1. **Reframes cultural measurement as material-discursive practice**: Rather than asking "does this model measure culture accurately?", asks "what does the model-data-annotation-evaluation system constitute as culture?"
2. **Applies agential realism to NLP**: Shows concretely how design choices (which model? which data? how to annotate?) draw boundaries that enact different versions of cultural reality.
3. **Documents three failure modes of the apparatus**: erasure of cultural markers in preprocessing, misalignment with historical context, and hidden agency in supposedly automated workflows.
4. **Proposes a research program**: theory-driven, empirically rigorous, and culturally contingent — treating each design choice as requiring explicit justification.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper critiques the computational turn in cultural studies and digital humanities that treats text as raw material for extraction. It relates to broader discussions in [[Mechanistic Interpretability]] and [[Alignment]] about what models actually learn and represent, though from a humanities rather than technical angle. It connects to critiques of bias in NLP — but goes deeper, arguing the problem isn't just "bad training data" but the fundamental structure of measurement itself. The work echoes concerns in science studies about how instruments shape their domains (Kuhn, Latour) and applies them to computational culture.

## Practical Applications
- **Media studies and film/TV analysis**: Studios and streamers could use models more thoughtfully to study audience response patterns, narrative structure evolution, or dialogue shifts — but only by explicitly acknowledging what the measurement apparatus constitutes as "culture."
- **Cultural preservation and archiving**: When digitizing cultural materials (oral histories, folklore, marginalized media), the choice of what metadata to extract or how to tag content should be transparent about whose framework is being imposed.
- **Market research and content recommendation**: Entertainment companies measuring "cultural taste" via language models should document how their measurement choices shape what gets counted as culturally significant.
- **Academic humanities scholarship**: Digital humanities projects analyzing large textual corpora should make explicit their ontological commitments — what they're measuring and why that framing matters.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **"Apparatus Audit" consulting service**: Help companies, archives, and research teams document and defend their cultural measurement choices; create reproducible, theory-aware pipelines for cultural analysis.
2. **Interactive cultural measurement framework**: Build a tool where researchers specify design choices (model, data source, annotation scheme) and the tool makes visible how different agential cuts produce different cultural narratives from the same text.
3. **"Culturally Contingent" model cards**: Develop a standard for documentation that forces teams to articulate ethical and methodological commitments before measuring culture, similar to model cards but richer.
4. **Academic publishing platform for media studies**: A venue where digital humanities work must document its apparatus, making the measurement choices as central as results.
5. **Consulting for cultural institutions**: Help museums, archives, and heritage organizations understand how language models reshape how we understand cultural objects and histories.

## Technical Limitations
- The paper is primarily conceptual and philosophical; it does not propose new technical solutions or metrics. Implementation of its framework requires significant interpretive work at each stage.
- The case studies focus on English-language TV/film dialogue; the framework may not generalize equally to all cultural domains or languages with different linguistic structures.
- The paper does not solve the practical problem of *how* to operationalize "cultural contingency" in large-scale measurement — it identifies the problem but leaves recipes for practice underdetermined.
- The reliance on Barad's agential realism, while powerful, is abstract; translating it into concrete NLP workflows requires careful thinking that the paper gestures toward but doesn't fully systematize.
- Does not address how to measure when trade-offs are unavoidable (e.g., training on historical data that reflects historical biases vs. sanitizing data and losing cultural texture).

## Open Questions
- How can researchers operationalize "conscious commitment" to agential cuts without process becoming paralyzing? Are there heuristics or guidelines?
- Does the framework apply equally to models measuring non-Western or minoritized cultures? Does the apparatus itself embed colonial or power-laden assumptions?
- Can the insights generalize beyond cultural measurement to other domains where language models are applied to social phenomena (sociology, anthropology, history)?
- What would empirically rigorous but culturally contingent evaluation metrics look like? How do you validate them?
- How should teams coordinate on agential cuts? Is this a matter of documentation, team diversity, or something else?

## Key Takeaway
Language models don't neutrally measure culture — their design choices actively constitute what culture means, making this a problem of ethics and methodology as much as technical accuracy.
