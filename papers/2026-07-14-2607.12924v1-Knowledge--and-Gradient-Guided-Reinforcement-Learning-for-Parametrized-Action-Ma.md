# Knowledge- and Gradient-Guided Reinforcement Learning for Parametrized Action Markov Decision Processes

**Authors:** Jonas Ehrhardt, René Heesch, Oliver Niggemann
**Published:** 2026-07-14 | **arXiv:** [2607.12924v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12924v1)

## Summary
This paper tackles reinforcement learning in environments where actions have two parts: a symbolic choice (which action to take) and numerical parameters (how to configure it). The key innovation is combining domain knowledge — rules, constraints, expert heuristics — with gradient-based optimization to make agents learn faster and make better decisions. By integrating symbolic knowledge with neural learning, the method achieves better sample efficiency and higher rewards than existing approaches.

## Theoretical Background
Reinforcement learning (RL) trains agents to maximize rewards through trial-and-error in environments called Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). Parametrized Action MDPs (PAMDPs) extend this to actions with continuous or discrete parameters — think of a robot deciding both what task to perform and how intensely to perform it. Traditional RL treats parameter selection as a one-shot estimation problem, wasting data. The paper builds on neuro-symbolic AI — the idea of combining neural networks with symbolic knowledge bases (like Datalog, a logic programming language) — to constrain the search space and guide learning more efficiently.

## Core Contributions
1. **KGRL algorithm**: A neuro-symbolic method that uses a Datalog knowledge base to dynamically prune infeasible actions and constrain parameter spaces for each state, reducing the agent's exploration burden.
2. **Gradient-based parameter refinement**: Instead of one-shot parameter estimation, the method uses iterative gradient-based optimization during both training and deployment to refine parameters toward optima.
3. **Explainability**: The algorithm records which rules were activated during decision-making, providing interpretable explanations for why certain actions were pruned or constrained.
4. **Empirical efficiency**: Demonstrated improvements over state-of-the-art RL baselines in both sample efficiency (fewer training episodes needed) and final performance (episodic return).

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[Reinforcement Learning]] and symbolic AI, extending recent efforts in neuro-symbolic integration. It relates to work on [[constrained optimization]] in RL (e.g., constrained MDPs) and safe RL paradigms that incorporate domain constraints. The gradient-guided parameter refinement echoes ideas from policy gradient methods and [[meta-learning]] where agents adapt parameters during deployment. It contrasts with pure neural approaches that learn end-to-end without explicit knowledge injection, and with pure symbolic planning that lacks learning capacity.

## Practical Applications
- **Robotics and control**: Manufacturing robots selecting actions (grasp, move, rotate) with continuous intensity parameters, constrained by safety rules and physical limits.
- **Autonomous vehicles**: Decision-making (accelerate, brake, turn) with parameterized intensities, guided by traffic rules and safety constraints.
- **Supply chain optimization**: Agents choosing logistics actions (order, ship, store) with quantity parameters, constrained by inventory limits and regulatory rules.
- **Scheduling and resource allocation**: Systems allocating compute or workers to tasks with varying intensity levels, subject to capacity and fairness constraints.

2. **Safe autonomous systems SDK**: A library for robotics/autonomous vehicle companies to inject safety constraints as knowledge bases, ensuring agents never violate critical rules while learning faster.

## Technical Limitations
- **Knowledge base completeness**: The method assumes domain knowledge is available and accurately encoded as Datalog rules. Incomplete or incorrect rules will propagate bias and constrain learning wrongly.
- **Scalability of symbolic reasoning**: Datalog query evaluation can become computationally expensive in large, complex domains with many rules and high-dimensional state spaces.
- **Parameter space structure**: The approach assumes parameter spaces are explicitly definable via constraints. Highly non-convex or implicit parameter relationships may resist simple bounding.
- **Cold-start problem**: Without good initialization or domain knowledge, the method may still struggle early in training (though it should improve faster than baselines).
- **Generalization of rules**: Rules learned or encoded for one environment may not transfer well to similar but different tasks or domains.

## Open Questions
- How sensitive is KGRL to errors or incompleteness in the Datalog knowledge base? What happens if rules are overly restrictive or miss edge cases?
- Can the method automatically infer or refine symbolic rules from data, rather than requiring manual specification?
- How does KGRL scale to very high-dimensional parameter spaces or complex symbolic knowledge bases with thousands of rules?
- Does gradient-based parameter refinement get stuck in local optima, and when do one-shot estimators actually perform comparably?
- How well do the procedural explanations align with actual decision importance, and are they useful to domain experts for debugging?
- Can KGRL extend to multi-agent settings where agents must coordinate actions and parameters?

## Key Takeaway
Combining symbolic rules with neural RL training makes agents learn faster and respect constraints without sacrificing adaptability or explainability.
