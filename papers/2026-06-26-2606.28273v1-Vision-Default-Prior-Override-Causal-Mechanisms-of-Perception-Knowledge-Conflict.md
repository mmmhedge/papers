# Vision-Default, Prior-Override: Causal Mechanisms of Perception-Knowledge Conflict in Vision-Language Models

**Authors:** Niclas Lietzow, Danielle Bitterman, Carsten Eickhoff, William Rudman, Michal Golovanevsky
**Published:** 2026-06-26 | **arXiv:** [2606.28273v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28273v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates how vision-language models (VLMs) resolve conflicts between visual information and memorized world knowledge by identifying the specific neural circuits responsible for each modality's influence. Using activation patching and ablation studies, the authors discover that visual grounding is the default behavior, while knowledge-grounding relies on a sparse set of causally necessary attention heads (2.5–4.8% of the network) concentrated in later layers, establishing an asymmetric causal structure that explains why VLMs sometimes "hallucinate" answers based on prior knowledge despite contradictory visual evidence.

## Theoretical Background
Vision-language models combine visual encoders and language models to process both images and text jointly. When a model sees a strawberry but is prompted "what color is this?" it must choose between visual evidence (the actual image) and memorized knowledge (strawberries are typically red). Prior work has characterized this perception-knowledge conflict behaviorally—measuring when models give which answer—but without explaining *why* at the component level.

This paper builds on mechanistic interpretability (the study of how neural networks compute by examining internal representations and information flow) and activation patching (a causal method that identifies which neurons causally influence model outputs). The authors extend these techniques to the multimodal setting, decomposing VLMs into granular components: residual streams (aggregated information channels), attention heads (selective routing of information), and MLP sublayers (nonlinear transformations).

## Core Contributions
1. **Sparse causal circuit identification**: Pinpoints that only 2.5–4.8% of attention heads are causally necessary for knowledge-grounding; ablating these heads flips 68–96% of knowledge-grounded predictions back to visual grounding.

2. **Asymmetric causal structure**: Establishes that visual grounding is the natural default, whereas knowledge-grounding requires active intervention via specific heads—not the reverse. Ablating knowledge heads barely affects visually-grounded answers (0.8–7.5% change).

3. **Functional decomposition of causal heads**: Classifies the identified heads into routing heads (modulate information flow) and writing heads (directly project answer tokens into the residual stream), with consistent patterns across model families and scales.

4. **Multi-granularity analysis**: Uses activation patching at three levels (residual stream, attention head, MLP sublayer) to achieve fine-grained mechanistic insight, going beyond black-box behavioral measurement.

## Connections to Other Work
This work extends mechanistic interpretability research (e.g., [[Attention Is All You Need]], [[Transformer Circuits Thread]]) into the multimodal domain. It complements behavioral studies of vision-language model hallucinations and knowledge conflicts (e.g., prior work on "visual question answering" anomalies) by providing causal explanations. The asymmetric structure resonates with findings in [[RLHF]] and alignment research, where specific model components are responsible for aligning behavior to human intent—here, knowledge-grounding acts as a form of "prior override" that must be actively maintained. The identification of sparse causal subnetworks aligns with lottery ticket and pruning literature, suggesting that knowledge reconciliation may be mediated by highly selective circuits.

## Practical Applications
1. **Improving VLM reliability in safety-critical domains**: Medical imaging analysis where hallucinated knowledge about typical symptoms could override actual visual evidence. Identifying and monitoring these circuits enables auditing.

2. **Controlled knowledge-grounding in specialized domains**: Robotics systems that need to weigh real-time visual input (e.g., obstacles) against learned navigation priors. Selective intervention on these heads allows fine-tuning the balance.

3. **Reducing hallucinations in product recommendations**: E-commerce systems where VLMs classify products in images but should defer to inventory/database truth. Modulating these heads prevents false confidence in visual-only answers.

4. **Debugging multimodal AI for accessibility**: Screen-reader systems that describe images; understanding these circuits helps prevent confidently incorrect descriptions that contradict stored metadata.

## Technical Limitations
- **Limited scope of conflict scenarios**: The paper tests perception-knowledge conflicts, but real-world VLM failures often involve more nuanced misalignments (e.g., out-of-distribution images, subtle distribution shifts).
- **Assumption of sparse, localized circuits**: The identified heads may not generalize to different architectures (e.g., vision transformers with radically different tokenization) or training procedures (e.g., models trained on different data distributions).
- **Ablation vs. causal intervention**: While ablation shows necessity, it doesn't fully establish sufficiency or reveal how these heads coordinate with broader network computation under adversarial or naturally-occurring conflicts.
- **Scale and family coverage**: Tested on three VLM families; unclear if the 2.5–4.8% proportion and head decomposition hold for much larger models (100B+ parameters) or architectures using different fusion mechanisms.
- **Static knowledge assumption**: The paper assumes memorized knowledge is fixed; doesn't address dynamic contexts where knowledge should legitimately override vision (e.g., when an image is known to be doctored).

## Open Questions
1. **How do these causal heads interact with the vision encoder?** Do they receive distinct visual vs. language embeddings, or do they integrate both? Can you trace back the visual signal through the patch embeddings?
2. **What triggers routing head activation?** Is it the prompt, the image content, both? Can you manipulate this trigger to force visual-grounded answers in knowledge-biased contexts?
3. **Do these circuits emerge during training?** At what stage do knowledge-grounding heads become causally important? Is this tied to memorization vs. generalization phases?
4. **Cross-modal transfer**: Do analogous circuits exist in text-only models when resolving factual conflicts? How do they compare structurally?
5. **Adversarial robustness**: Can you craft adversarial prompts or images that break the discovered circuit structure, forcing a shift in which heads are causally necessary?
6. **Scalability of causal structure**: Do the identified proportions (2.5–4.8%) hold at 1T+ parameter scales, or does the sparsity change?

## Key Takeaway
Vision-language models resolve perception-knowledge conflicts via a sparse, asymmetric causal circuit concentrated in late-layer attention heads—visual grounding is the default, and only a small set of routing and writing heads actively implement knowledge-override.
