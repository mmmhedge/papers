# From Personas to Plot: Character-Grounded Multi-Agent Story Generation for Long-Form Narratives

**Authors:** Aayush Aluru, Chloe Ho, Muhammad Hammouri, Kerry Luo, Myra Malik et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-01 | **arXiv:** [2607.00918v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.00918v1)

## Summary
This paper presents MAGNET, a system that generates long-form stories (100+ pages) by simulating multiple character agents with distinct personas who propose actions based on a shared world state and evolving story goals. The work addresses a key weakness of large language models: they tend to lose plot coherence and introduce contradictions when generating extended narratives. The authors also introduce ATLAS, a verification tool that detects hallucinations by comparing world representations across scenes, and show their approach reduces hallucinations by 50% compared to baseline LLM generation.

## Theoretical Background
Large language models like GPT and Claude excel at local coherence but struggle with global consistency—tracking which characters are where, what has happened, and whether new plot points contradict earlier ones. Prior work in [[narrative generation]] has used hierarchical planning and constrained decoding, but these often fail at scale. This paper builds on two key ideas: (1) multi-agent simulation, where independent agents with goals and personas make decisions that drive story forward (drawing from [[multi-agent-systems]] research), and (2) explicit world-state tracking—maintaining a structured representation of the story world (characters, locations, events) rather than letting the language model implicitly track everything in its token stream. The paper also invokes graph-based scene comparison for consistency checking, treating narrative coherence as a verification problem rather than purely a generation problem.

## Core Contributions
1. **MAGNET architecture**: A persona-grounded multi-agent framework where each character is an independent agent with defined goals, traits, and decision-making logic; agents propose actions that are integrated into a shared world state, which then becomes context for the next story beat.

2. **ATLAS verification pipeline**: A graph-based method that extracts world-state representations (entities, relationships, attributes) from each scene and compares them across the story to detect contradictions and hallucinations—turning consistency checking into a structured comparison problem rather than relying on LLM judgment alone.

3. **Empirical validation at scale**: Demonstrated on 100-page narratives with 41–50% reduction in hallucinations and annotations compared to single-model baselines and IBSEN (a prior multi-agent storytelling system).

4. **Human evaluation results**: Pairwise rubric scoring by LLM editors shows MAGNET produces narratives with better structural coherence than existing approaches.

## Connections to Other Work
This work bridges [[generative-models]] (using LLMs as proposal generators) and [[multi-agent-systems]] (coordination among personas). It extends prior narrative systems like IBSEN by adding explicit world-state tracking and a separate verification layer. The approach is complementary to [[RLHF]] and [[alignment]] work—rather than fine-tuning models to be "honest," it creates external consistency checks. It also relates to planning-based generation (hierarchical task networks, goal-oriented planning) by making story goals explicit and agent-centric. The graph-based verification echoes knowledge-graph consistency checking in open-domain QA systems.

## Practical Applications
1. **Interactive fiction / choose-your-own-adventure games**: Publishers could use MAGNET to generate branching storylines with guaranteed consistency, reducing manual editing of long narratives.

2. **Serialized content platforms** (Wattpad, Royal Road, Substack): Creators could use MAGNET as a co-writer for multi-chapter stories, maintaining character consistency across hundreds of pages without losing plot threads.

3. **Screenwriting assistants**: Film studios could generate treatment drafts for TV series (multi-season arcs) where character arcs and world consistency are critical.

4. **Personalized narrative games**: Tabletop RPG systems (D&D Encounter generators, automated dungeon masters) could use persona-grounded agents to simulate NPC behavior that remains consistent across sessions.

5. **Content moderation / hallucination detection**: ATLAS-style verification could be applied to long-form LLM outputs in domains like legal writing, research summaries, or technical documentation.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability of world-state tracking**: As stories grow beyond 100 pages, maintaining a complete and consistent world state becomes computationally expensive; the paper doesn't discuss how ATLAS scales to longer narratives or how to handle ambiguous/conflicting information.

2. **Agent decision quality**: The system relies on the underlying LLM to generate sensible character actions given goals and world state. If the LLM makes nonsensical proposals, the multi-agent framework can't fix it—it can only verify downstream consistency.

3. **Goal specification**: The paper doesn't deeply explain how story goals are set or evolved; this seems to require human input or a separate goal-planning module that isn't fully specified.

4. **Limited to prose fiction**: The approach is validated on English-language narrative; applicability to other languages, genres (poetry, non-linear narrative), or non-text media is unclear.

5. **Evaluation bias**: Pairwise rubric scoring by LLM editors may reflect LLM preferences rather than human reader preferences; no human evaluation reported.

6. **Graph representation gaps**: ATLAS assumes world state can be cleanly extracted as a graph; implicit, nuanced, or ambiguous narrative elements (tone, character subtext) are likely missed.

## Open Questions
1. How does MAGNET perform on stories with intentional unreliable narrators, POV shifts, or non-linear timelines—where world-state "contradictions" are narratively valid?

2. Can the system handle open-ended, emergent stories where goals evolve dynamically, rather than being set upfront?

3. How sensitive is ATLAS to errors in world-state extraction? If the graph parser misses a key fact, does verification fail cascadingly?

4. Does persona consistency improve if character agents are trained (or fine-tuned) on the specific story corpus, rather than relying on generic LLM conditioning?

5. Can human evaluation validate that readers perceive MAGNET narratives as more coherent, or only that they have fewer detectable contradictions?

6. How does the approach generalize to collaborative storytelling (multiple human + AI authors) where goals may conflict?

## Key Takeaway
Maintaining coherence in long-form text requires treating storytelling as explicit multi-agent simulation with world-state tracking and external consistency verification, rather than relying on a single language model to implicitly track everything.
