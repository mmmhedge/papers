# Diffusing Blame: Task-Dependent Credit Assignment in Biologically Plausible Dual-Stream Networks

**Authors:** Yutaro Yamada, Luca Grillotti, Rujikorn Charakorn, Sebastian Risi, David Ha et al.
**Published:** 2026-06-30 | **arXiv:** [2606.31700v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31700v1)

## Summary
This paper develops a biologically plausible learning rule called Error Diffusion (ED) that works in neural networks constrained by Dale's principle — a biological constraint requiring each neuron to be either exclusively excitatory or inhibitory. The key innovation is "modulo error routing," which allows error signals to be distributed through a dual-stream network architecture without backpropagation or weight transport. The authors show this approach scales from MNIST (96.7%) to CIFAR-10 (61.7%) and works in reinforcement learning tasks, bridging the gap between biological realism and practical performance.

## Theoretical Background
Dale's principle is a fundamental constraint in neuroscience: biological neurons maintain consistent polarity (excitatory or inhibitory), whereas standard artificial neural networks allow each weight to be positive or negative without structural constraint. Traditional backpropagation violates this constraint and also requires the "weight transport problem" — neurons must somehow use the transposed weights from forward connections during learning, which has no clear biological mechanism. Prior work proposed biologically plausible alternatives like [[Direct Feedback Alignment]] (using random feedback matrices) and Error Diffusion (distributing error signals), but these struggled to scale beyond toy problems like MNIST. This paper builds on the observation that dual-stream architectures with separate excitatory and inhibitory populations can route error signals without transposed weights, but the challenge is how to assign credit meaningfully across layers and tasks.

## Core Contributions
1. **Modulo error routing**: A novel extension of Error Diffusion that handles multi-class classification by routing error signals modulo the number of output classes, enabling the method to work beyond binary problems.
2. **Scaling demonstration**: First evidence that Dale's principle can be maintained while achieving meaningful performance on CIFAR-10 (61.7%), a substantial step beyond prior MNIST-only results.
3. **Task-dependent credit assignment analysis**: Ablation studies showing that the three architectural innovations (layer-specific sigmoid widths, batch-centered class errors, asymmetric initialization) have *reversed importance* between MNIST and CIFAR-10, revealing hidden bottlenecks invisible in single-benchmark evaluation.
4. **RL integration**: Successful integration of ED with Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) on continuous control and exploration tasks, showing the method generalizes beyond supervised learning.

## Connections to Other Work
This work directly extends [[Error Diffusion]] and compares to [[Direct Feedback Alignment]], both biologically plausible alternatives to backpropagation. It sits in the broader ecosystem of mechanistic neuroscience-inspired learning rules (e.g., local learning rules, feedback alignment variants) that aim to reconcile biological realism with learning performance. The ablation methodology revealing task-dependent bottlenecks connects to interpretability work showing that model properties are often benchmark-dependent. The RL results complement research on biologically plausible reinforcement learning, though most prior work there used less strict biological constraints.

## Practical Applications
1. **Neuromorphic hardware**: Spiking neural networks and neuromorphic chips (Intel Loihi, etc.) often implement or benefit from Dale's principle constraints; ED could improve learning efficiency on these platforms.
2. **Robotic control in resource-constrained environments**: Robots with limited power (drones, microcontrollers) could use ED-based learning for online adaptation without backpropagation's computational overhead.
3. **Edge AI and on-device learning**: Mobile and embedded systems where backpropagation is too memory-intensive could adopt ED-based training for local model updates.
4. **Brain-inspired AI systems**: Applications prioritizing biological plausibility (e.g., cognitive modeling, simulations for neuroscience) could leverage this framework.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Performance gap**: CIFAR-10 performance (61.7%) remains substantially below standard baselines (~90%+), limiting practical applicability beyond niche domains valuing biological plausibility.
2. **Task-dependent brittleness**: The reversal in importance of architectural choices between MNIST and CIFAR-10 suggests the method is sensitive to problem structure; it's unclear how to configure it reliably for new domains.
3. **Scalability to modern architectures**: All experiments use relatively shallow, simple networks. The scalability to ResNets, vision transformers, or other modern architectures is unexplored.
4. **Limited RL evaluation**: RL results are on relatively simple environments (Brax, Craftax); no comparison to modern RL methods on complex tasks.
5. **Hyperparameter tuning**: The method introduces several domain-specific hyperparameters (sigmoid widths, initialization schemes, batch centering) that require careful tuning, raising engineering burden.
6. **Computational efficiency claims unverified**: While ED avoids weight transport, actual wall-clock training time and memory usage compared to backprop are not reported.

## Open Questions
1. How does performance scale to ImageNet-scale datasets and modern architectures (ResNets, transformers)? Is there a fundamental ceiling under Dale's principle, or is it an engineering problem?
2. Can the task-dependent bottleneck be predicted or automatically detected? What theoretical properties of a task determine which architectural choices are critical?
3. How does ED compare to other biologically plausible methods (e.g., three-factor rules, local Hebbian learning) on a standardized benchmark suite?
4. Can ED be combined with online/continual learning scenarios where weight constraints are particularly beneficial?
5. What is the information-theoretic cost of enforcing Dale's principle? Is 61.7% on CIFAR-10 close to the achievable upper bound?
6. Does ED transfer or meta-learn better than backprop-trained networks, despite lower final performance? (Biological plausibility might trade off optimization for learning flexibility.)

## Key Takeaway
Error Diffusion with modulo routing proves that biologically plausible neural networks respecting Dale's principle can learn useful representations beyond toy problems, but at a significant performance cost that remains impractical for most modern applications without further breakthroughs.
