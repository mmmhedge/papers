# Deep Learning for Dynamic Programming with Recursive Utility Using First-order Conditions

**Authors:** Xianhua Peng, Wu Guo, Songyan Wang, Jianfei Zhu
**Published:** 2026-07-10 | **arXiv:** [2607.09461v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09461v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces CEFOL (Certainty-Equivalent First-Order Learning), a deep learning algorithm for solving dynamic programming problems where agents have recursive preferences (preferences that depend on both immediate and future consumption in a nonlinear way). The key innovation is using neural networks to represent the certainty equivalent — a mathematical object that appears in the optimality conditions but is expensive to compute — allowing the algorithm to directly enforce first-order optimality conditions rather than just approximating value functions. The method achieves high precision on realistic economic problems like consumption-saving decisions and macroeconomic models.

## Theoretical Background
Dynamic programming is the standard mathematical framework for sequential decision-making under uncertainty — it breaks multi-period problems into simpler one-period subproblems via the Bellman equation. Traditional approaches (value function iteration, or VFI) iterate on the value function directly. Recursive utility generalizes the standard expected utility model used in economics; instead of assuming agents maximize a simple linear combination of current and future utility, recursive utility (like Epstein-Zin preferences) allows utility to depend on future utility in a nonlinear way through a certainty equivalent operator. The certainty equivalent is a risk-adjustment term that captures how agents' risk preferences affect their decisions. First-order optimality conditions (Euler equations) are the marginal conditions that characterize optimal choices; solving them directly is often more numerically stable than iterating on the value function itself. KKT conditions extend this to constrained optimization problems.

## Core Contributions
1. **Neural representation of certainty equivalent**: Instead of directly computing or approximating the nonlinear certainty equivalent, CEFOL introduces a dedicated neural network to learn it. This sidesteps the numerical difficulties of evaluating the certainty equivalent at many points.

2. **First-order condition learning**: The algorithm constructs loss functions from Bellman residuals *and* first-order optimality residuals (Euler equations, KKT conditions). This directly enforces the model's economic structure rather than just fitting value/policy functions.

3. **Native constraint handling**: By working with KKT residuals, the method naturally handles occasionally binding constraints (constraints that are active only in some states) without penalty functions or problem reformulation — a major practical advantage over penalty-based methods.

4. **Empirical validation across domains**: The paper demonstrates the method on risk-sensitive problems, Epstein–Zin preferences, robust control, and a full DSGE (dynamic stochastic general equilibrium) model with stochastic volatility, achieving precision of 1e-4 to 1e-3 on residuals — competitive with or better than traditional VFI benchmarks.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[reinforcement-learning]] (using neural networks to learn policies and value functions) and computational economics (solving dynamic programming problems with recursive preferences). It relates to recent efforts to use [[deep-learning]] for solving PDEs and operator equations (e.g., physics-informed neural networks), except here the target is discrete-time economic Bellman equations. The approach contrasts with standard [[reinforcement-learning]] algorithms (which typically work with expected utility and don't enforce optimality conditions directly) and with traditional numerical methods in macroeconomics (VFI, endogenous grid methods, perturbation methods). The use of multiple neural networks (value, policy, Lagrange multipliers) to represent different components of the solution is inspired by Lagrangian dual methods in [[optimization]].

## Practical Applications
1. **Macroeconomic forecasting & policy simulation**: Central banks and policy institutions can use CEFOL to quickly solve large DSGE models with realistic preference structures, enabling faster scenario analysis and policy counterfactuals.

2. **Portfolio optimization**: Asset managers can apply this to solve consumption-saving and portfolio choice problems with recursive preferences (which better capture real investor behavior than simple expected utility), especially under time-varying volatility.

3. **Corporate investment decisions**: Firms can model capital and financing decisions under uncertainty with recursive preferences, solving for optimal policies under occasionally binding financial constraints.

4. **Insurance and pension design**: CEFOL can optimize insurance contracts and pension schemes where individuals have recursive preferences and face binding constraints on contributions or payouts.

5. **Risk management for nonlinear preferences**: Financial institutions with complex recursive preference structures can use CEFOL to compute optimal hedging and allocation policies.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Curse of dimensionality**: Like all neural network methods, CEFOL will struggle with very high-dimensional state spaces (many state variables). The paper tests on moderate-dimensional problems; scalability to 20+ state dimensions is unclear.

2. **Training cost**: Requires training multiple neural networks jointly; no discussion of wall-clock time, training complexity, or when CEFOL becomes slower than traditional VFI for a given problem size.

3. **No convergence theory**: The paper provides no convergence guarantees or rates. It's empirical validation only — no proof that the algorithm converges to a true solution or characterization of when it might fail.

4. **Architecture dependence**: Success likely depends heavily on network architecture choices (depth, width, activation functions), but the paper provides limited guidance on architecture selection for new problems.

5. **Out-of-sample performance**: Residuals are computed on test points, but no analysis of how prediction error degrades far outside the training region or how to choose the state space over which to train.

6. **Model misspecification**: The method assumes the Bellman equation and first-order conditions are correct; misspecified models will still minimize residuals but give incorrect economic insights.

## Open Questions
1. **How does solution time and accuracy scale with problem dimension?** A systematic comparison of wall-clock time vs. traditional VFI/endogenous grid methods across 2D to 10D+ problems would clarify when CEFOL is preferable.

2. **Can convergence be guaranteed under reasonable assumptions?** Is there a theory of when the algorithm converges, at what rate, and to the true solution?

3. **How sensitive is the method to neural network architecture?** A principled architecture search or sensitivity analysis would make the method more accessible.

4. **Can this extend to continuous-time problems?** Many economic problems are naturally continuous-time; does CEFOL work for solving HJB (Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman) equations with recursive utility?

5. **How does CEFOL handle highly nonlinear certainty equivalents?** Are there regimes (extreme risk aversion, fat tails) where the certainty-equivalent network becomes hard to learn?

6. **Can the method be used for inverse problems** (e.g., inferring preferences from observed choices)? This would be valuable for empirical work.

## Key Takeaway
CEFOL shows that directly enforcing first-order optimality conditions with neural networks can solve complex economic dynamic programming problems more naturally and accurately than traditional value function iteration, especially when constraints are occasionally binding.
