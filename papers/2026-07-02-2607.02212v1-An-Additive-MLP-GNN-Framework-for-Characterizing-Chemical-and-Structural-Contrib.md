# An Additive MLP-GNN Framework for Characterizing Chemical and Structural Contributions to Aqueous Solubility

**Authors:** Sampreeti Bhattacharya, Arkaprava Roy
**Published:** 2026-07-02 | **arXiv:** [2607.02212v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02212v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes a hybrid deep-learning model that separately processes chemical descriptors (via a neural network) and molecular graph structure (via a graph neural network) to predict aqueous solubility of drug compounds. Instead of merging these two information sources into a single representation—which obscures what the model actually learned—the framework keeps them separate and combines them only at the final prediction step, making it possible to understand whether predictions come from chemistry, structure, or both. The work shows competitive accuracy while providing transparency about which aspects of a molecule drive solubility predictions.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two established deep-learning paradigms. **Graph neural networks (GNNs)** are neural architectures designed to process molecular structures by representing them as graphs where atoms are nodes and bonds are edges; they learn representations by iteratively aggregating information from neighboring atoms. **Multilayer perceptrons (MLPs)** are standard feed-forward neural networks that learn non-linear transformations of input features. Prior work in molecular property prediction typically concatenates or merges graph-based and descriptor-based features early in the pipeline, losing interpretability about their individual contributions. The paper also leverages **transfer learning**—pretraining on large datasets then fine-tuning on smaller ones—which is well-established in deep learning but underexplored for molecular property prediction. Interpretation techniques like **GNNExplainer** (which produces attribution masks showing which graph edges matter for predictions) and linear projections are borrowed from mechanistic interpretability research.

## Core Contributions
1. **Additive decomposition architecture**: A novel design that enforces separation of chemical (MLP) and structural (GNN) branches throughout training, combining them additively (with optional multiplicative interaction) only at prediction time. This is distinct from prior work that merges representations early.

2. **Systematic interpretability analysis**: Three complementary interpretation methods applied to understand each branch: (a) best linear projections showing alignment with known physicochemical properties, (b) embedding visualizations stratified by solubility class, and (c) aggregated GNNExplainer attributions over functional groups.

3. **Transfer learning validation**: Demonstration that pretraining on AqSolDB (larger) and fine-tuning on BigSolDB2 (smaller) improves generalization and reduces variance, providing empirical evidence for feature reuse in molecular domains.

4. **Competitive performance with transparency**: Achieves state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art predictive accuracy while being more interpretable than end-to-end black-box models.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[mechanistic-interpretability]] (understanding what neural networks learn) and **molecular property prediction**. It responds to critiques that merged representations in prior models obscure feature contributions—similar concerns drive [[attention]] visualization work in NLP and CAM-style methods in [[computer-vision]]. The additive decomposition echoes ideas from **interpretable machine learning** (e.g., additive models like GAMs) but applies them to modern deep learning. The [[GNNExplainer]] attribution method is adapted from graph interpretability research. Transfer learning for molecular tasks parallels successes in [[large-language-models]] and [[transformers]], though this application is more domain-specific. The work implicitly supports the principle that **separating information sources at the architectural level can improve both interpretability and generalization**, a theme gaining traction in mechanistic interpretability.

## Practical Applications
1. **Early-stage drug discovery**: Pharmaceutical chemists can use the model to quickly screen compound libraries for oral bioavailability, identifying which structural features or physicochemical properties are limiting solubility and guiding synthesis of analogues.

2. **Lead optimization**: During medicinal chemistry optimization, the model's decomposition helps teams understand whether to modify a molecule's bulk properties (lipophilicity, polarity) or its graph-local features (functional groups, ring systems) to improve solubility.

3. **Regulatory and safety assessment**: The interpretable decomposition provides justification for solubility predictions to regulatory bodies, reducing black-box concerns in submissions.

4. **Formulation guidance**: Biochemists developing drug formulations can understand whether solubility bottlenecks stem from intrinsic molecular structure (hard to change) or from modifiable physicochemical properties (e.g., ionization state), informing pH or excipient strategies.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **SolubilityAI Studio**: A SaaS platform for medicinal chemists that accepts SMILES strings or drawn structures, predicts solubility, and **visually separates chemical vs. structural drivers** with atom-level explanations and suggestions for structural modifications (e.g., "replace meta-substitution with para").

2. **Molecular Property Debugger**: An interactive web tool where chemists input a compound and the model highlights which functional groups or physicochemical properties most constrain solubility, linking to literature on analogous compounds with improved properties.

3. **Solubility Optimization Engine**: A backend API for CDMO (contract development and manufacturing organization) partners that accepts target solubility specifications and returns optimized molecular scaffolds ranked by synthesis feasibility, paired with interpretability scores for each candidate.

4. **Pharma Benchmark Suite**: Standardized benchmark dataset and leaderboard for solubility prediction where models are scored not only on accuracy but on **interpretability metrics** (e.g., agreement between model attributions and known chemistry), encouraging industry adoption of explainable models.

5. **Pretraining Repository**: An open-source library of pre-trained MLP and GNN branches on large solubility corpora, with fine-tuning scripts, that allows smaller biotech firms to leverage transfer learning without expensive in-house data collection.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Limited to aqueous solubility**: The framework is task-specific; generalization to other molecular properties (e.g., permeability, metabolic stability) requires retraining and is not demonstrated.

2. **Additive + multiplicative assumption**: The assumption that chemistry and structure combine additively (or via a simple multiplicative interaction) may be overly rigid for cases where they interact in complex, non-separable ways.

3. **Dependence on descriptor quality**: The chemical branch relies on hand-crafted physicochemical descriptors, which can miss important chemical patterns; the model is only as good as those descriptors.

4. **GNN expressiveness**: GNNs have known limitations in capturing long-range dependencies and certain graph properties; the structural branch may miss relevant three-dimensional conformational effects or long-range electronic interactions.

5. **Data imbalance and scale**: The fine-tuning dataset (BigSolDB2) is smaller; performance on extreme solubility ranges or rare chemical scaffolds is likely degraded and not thoroughly analyzed.

6. **Interpretability ground truth**: The paper validates alignment with "familiar physicochemical descriptors" but doesn't validate against expert chemist judgment or prospective synthesis data, so interpretability claims are indirect.

## Open Questions
1. **Generalization to other molecular properties**: Does the additive decomposition framework improve interpretability and transfer learning for other ADMET (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, toxicity) properties, or is it specific to solubility?

2. **Optimal interaction complexity**: Is the simple multiplicative interaction sufficient, or would learned non-linear interactions (e.g., attention between branches) improve accuracy further while preserving interpretability?

3. **Three-dimensional structure**: Current approach is 2D; how much solubility variance is explained by 3D conformational effects, and could a parallel 3D structural branch capture this?

4. **Out-of-distribution robustness**: How does the model perform on novel chemical scaffolds far from training data? Does the decomposition help or hurt robustness?

5. **Active learning**: Could the branch-wise decomposition be used to design informative queries for active learning—e.g., prioritizing compounds where chemical and structural signals disagree?

6. **Causal interpretation**: Are the branch attributions causal (intervening on a molecule's structure deterministically changes solubility) or merely correlational? Could causal discovery methods strengthen claims?

## Key Takeaway
By keeping chemical and structural information separate throughout a deep model's computation and combining them only at prediction, the paper achieves both competitive solubility prediction and interpretable decomposition of what the model actually learned—demonstrating that architectural transparency and performance need not be in tension.
