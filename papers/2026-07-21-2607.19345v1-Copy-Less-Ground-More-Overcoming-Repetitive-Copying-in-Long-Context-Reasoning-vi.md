# Copy Less, Ground More: Overcoming Repetitive Copying in Long-Context Reasoning via Evidence-Aware Reinforcement Learning

**Authors:** Lizhe Fang, Weizhou Shen, Tianyi Tang, Yisen Wang
**Published:** 2026-07-21 | **arXiv:** [2607.19345v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19345v1)

## Summary

This paper identifies and fixes a critical failure in long-context language models: they waste effort copying irrelevant text from the input instead of reasoning productively. The authors show this problem gets worse as context grows, diagnose its root cause (poor focus on relevant evidence), and propose GEAR — a reinforcement learning method that rewards models for grounding answers in key evidence while penalizing reliance on distractors. The method consistently improves reasoning accuracy by up to 4.6 points while reducing wasted copying.

## Theoretical Background

Step-by-step reasoning (chain-of-thought) in large language models has become standard for complex tasks, but extending it to long-context settings — where inputs contain thousands of tokens — reveals a problem: models copy indiscriminately from the input. This builds on prior work in reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) and reward shaping — techniques that guide model training by assigning credit to desired behaviors. The paper's innovation is diagnosing *why* copying happens (insufficient grounding in task-relevant evidence) and using that diagnosis to design a smarter reward signal rather than just punishing incorrect answers. The core insight is that models need explicit incentives to distinguish signal from noise in long contexts.

## Core Contributions

1. **Problem diagnosis**: Empirically demonstrates that frontier long-context LLMs exhibit pathological repetitive copying, and isolates the root cause as failure to focus on task-relevant evidence vs. distractors — not general reasoning inability.

2. **GEAR reward shaping**: A novel reward function that augments accuracy signals with (a) a grounding reward for text overlap with identified key evidence and (b) a penalty for overlap with irrelevant context.

3. **Automated evidence annotation pipeline**: A generalizable system to automatically extract and label task-relevant evidence from arbitrary documents, enabling GEAR training on natural language data without manual annotation.

4. **Empirical validation**: Consistent improvements across multiple model scales and benchmarks (+4.6 average points), with larger gains in longer contexts and measurable reduction in copying length.

## Connections to Other Work

This work extends [[RLHF]] and reward shaping frameworks by introducing *structural* signals (evidence vs. distractor) rather than just outcome signals (right/wrong). It relates to recent work on [[mechanistic-interpretability|interpretability]] in long-context settings — understanding *why* models fail — and complements research on retrieval-augmented generation, which also addresses grounding. The paper also touches on problems studied in [[alignment]] around model behavior in adversarial or noisy environments. Unlike pure scaling solutions, GEAR targets a specific behavioral pathology, echoing work on targeted fine-tuning to fix identified failure modes.

## Practical Applications

1. **Customer support systems**: Long-context retrieval over FAQ databases or ticket history currently suffers from models copying irrelevant old tickets; GEAR-trained models could reduce hallucination and improve accuracy.

2. **Legal document analysis**: Lawyers need models to cite specific clauses when analyzing contracts; repetitive copying of boilerplate defeats the purpose; GEAR enforces focus on contractual obligations.

3. **Scientific literature synthesis**: When models generate summaries from multiple papers, they currently waste space parroting titles and abstracts; GEAR forces focus on novel findings.

4. **Multi-turn dialogue over conversation history**: Chatbots applied to long conversation logs currently copy irrelevant prior exchanges; better grounding improves coherence and reduces confusion.

5. **Code reasoning and retrieval**: Models analyzing large codebases copy irrelevant functions; GEAR-style grounding could force focus on code paths that matter.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **LLM fine-tuning as a service for long-context tasks** — Platform that applies GEAR or similar evidence-aware RL to customers' proprietary long documents (contracts, manuals, knowledge bases) to produce specialized long-context models without massive compute spend.

2. **Evidence-grounded RAG system** — Enhance retrieval-augmented generation pipelines with automated evidence annotation and grounding rewards, sold as an API/middleware to improve accuracy in production RAG systems.

3. **Automated legal contract analyzer** — SaaS tool that uses GEAR-trained models to analyze contracts and cite specific relevant clauses (with grounding) rather than rambling summaries; target: in-house counsel, paralegal firms.

4. **Scientific knowledge synthesis engine** — Startup that generates high-quality literature reviews by training models with GEAR on citation-grounded reasoning, ensuring claims are anchored to actual papers rather than hallucinated.

5. **Distractor-aware model evaluator** — Benchmarking tool that tests whether long-context models can distinguish signal from noise, and certifies models for high-stakes applications (medical records analysis, financial due diligence) by measuring grounding quality.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Evidence annotation dependency**: The automated pipeline must correctly identify what counts as "key evidence" vs. "distractor," which is task-dependent; weak or incorrect annotations degrade the method.

2. **Scalability of reward computation**: Computing overlap between generated text and evidence requires string matching at each RL step, which may slow training; not clear how this scales to very long reasoning traces.

3. **Generalization of evidence labels**: The paper doesn't fully explore whether evidence labels learned from one domain transfer to others, or whether the method requires task-specific tuning.

4. **Long-horizon credit assignment**: Standard RL struggles to credit actions early in long reasoning chains; GEAR's grounding reward helps but doesn't fully solve this, especially for multi-step reasoning where relevance is indirect.

5. **Trade-off between brevity and completeness**: Penalizing copying might incentivize models to omit necessary context-setting information that isn't explicitly marked as key evidence, risking brevity at the cost of clarity.

## Open Questions

1. **Can GEAR transfer to truly novel evidence?** What happens when test-time evidence differs significantly in language or style from training data? Does grounding remain robust?

2. **How does GEAR interact with other long-context techniques** like sparse attention, compression, or hierarchical retrieval? Could they be combined?

3. **What is the minimal evidence annotation needed?** Could weaker supervision (e.g., implicit from task success) work, or is explicit evidence labeling necessary?

4. **Does GEAR apply to reasoning about *absence* of evidence?** Many tasks require noting what is *not* in the context; does the method handle this?

5. **Can this inspire new model architectures?** Rather than post-hoc reward shaping, could the grounding mechanism be baked into attention heads or routing layers to avoid the copying pathology entirely?

## Key Takeaway

Long-context LLMs waste reasoning effort copying irrelevant text; training with evidence-aware rewards forces focus on task-relevant information and improves accuracy.
