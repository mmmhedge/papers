# How Surprising Is Historical Italian to Language Models? Tokenization Tax, Comprehension Tax, and a Simple Mitigation

**Authors:** Maria Levchenko
**Published:** 2026-06-25 | **arXiv:** [2606.27275v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27275v1)

## Summary
This paper diagnoses why historical text is difficult for language models by decomposing the problem into tokenization cost, predictive uncertainty, semantic robustness, and context sensitivity. Using a newly curated 17th-century Italian corpus alongside 19th-century Italian and 18th-century Russian texts, the authors show that while historical documents incur consistent encoding penalties (25–30% tokenization inflation), their comprehension difficulty varies sharply—17th-century Italian is 2.4× more surprising to predict than modern Italian, yet embedding representations remain robust—and propose a simple temporal context prompt that reduces surprisal by ~60%.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on several foundational concepts in NLP and machine learning:

- **Tokenization and subword decomposition**: How BPE and related algorithms fragment text and the computational cost this imposes (related to [[Byte Pair Encoding]], [[SentencePiece]])
- **Surprisal and predictive entropy**: Information-theoretic measures of how unexpected a token is given context; historically tied to cognitive language processing models
- **Embedding robustness and semantic similarity**: The assumption that distributed representations capture meaning even when surface-level generation is uncertain
- **Domain shift and distribution mismatch**: How pretraining on modern data creates a gap when encountering out-of-distribution historical text (connected to [[Domain Adaptation]])
- **Prompt-based mitigation**: Leveraging in-context learning to adapt model behavior without retraining (related to [[In-Context Learning]], [[Prompt Engineering]])

The paper treats historical text as a controlled stress test for understanding what linguistic properties (orthography, grammar, lexical frequency) most challenge modern LLMs.

## Core Contributions

1. **Diagnostic decomposition framework**: Moves beyond monolithic "historical difficulty" to isolate four independent dimensions (tokenization, surprisal, semantic robustness, context sensitivity), enabling precise diagnosis of model failure modes.

2. **Newly curated 17th-century Italian corpus**: A rare, rigorously digitized dataset (1610–1689) from original page images, filling a gap in historically annotated resources for evaluation.

3. **Dissociation between encoding and comprehension**: Empirically demonstrates that high tokenization cost does not predict high predictive difficulty—Russian and Italian incur similar tokenization penalties but diverge sharply in surprisal—overturning a simple assumption.

4. **Embedding robustness paradox**: Shows that embeddings remain semantically robust (>0.85 similarity) even when generation is unreliable, suggesting orthogonal failure modes and enabling safe deployment for retrieval tasks.

5. **Minimal, model-agnostic mitigation**: A temporal context prompt reduces historical surprisal by ~60% without fine-tuning or architecture changes, offering immediate practical value for digital libraries.

## Connections to Other Work

This paper extends research in several directions:

- **[[Domain Adaptation]]**: Addresses distribution shift, but focuses on temporal rather than domain-specific mismatch; complements work on adapting models to specialized vocabularies.
- **[[Transfer Learning]]** and **[[Low-Resource Learning]]**: Historical texts are naturally low-resource; findings inform how to deploy pretrained models on data distant from training distribution.
- **[[Representation Learning]]**: Challenges the assumption that representational quality correlates with generation quality; related to probing work ([[Mechanistic Interpretability]]) on what embeddings encode.
- **[[Multilingual NLP]]**: Historical language is analogous to code-switching or minority language scenarios; orthographic variation parallels transliteration challenges.
- **[[In-Context Learning]]**: The temporal context prompt is an example of efficient, prompt-based adaptation without gradient updates.
- **Digital humanities**: Complements OCR error correction, historical NLP annotation, and digital library infrastructure.

## Practical Applications

1. **Semantic search in digital archives**: Museums and libraries can index historical manuscripts using embedding-based retrieval without retraining; robustness guarantees enable safe deployment despite generation brittleness.

2. **Named entity linking in historical documents**: Extract and disambiguate person/place names in old texts; embedding robustness suggests that cosine-similarity-based linking will work reliably even when the model's confidence is low.

3. **Optical Character Recognition (OCR) correction**: Use temporal context prompts to improve spell-checking and post-processing of imperfect OCR output on historical documents.

4. **Literary and historical analysis**: Assist scholars in summarization, thematic tagging, and distant reading of large historical text collections; embedding robustness makes semantic clustering interpretable.

5. **Cross-lingual historical corpora**: Extend analysis to parallel historical texts (Latin, Greek, Old English, medieval manuscripts); surprisal metrics identify which linguistic features (inflectional morphology, phonological drift) most challenge models.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **HistoryAI Archive**: A SaaS platform for museums and libraries integrating OCR, temporal context prompt injection, and embedding-based semantic search for digitized collections. Pricing tied to corpus size; target: large cultural institutions, universities, national libraries.

2. **Temporal Language Adapter**: A lightweight inference middleware that automatically detects historical text and applies context-aware prompting (dating by document metadata or linguistic features). Sells as a plugin to enterprise document management systems.

3. **ScholarSearch**: AI-powered full-text search specifically for historical texts and rare books; leverages embedding robustness to offer semantic search where traditional keyword search fails (handles spelling variation, archaic vocabulary). B2B model: subscription to academic libraries.

4. **Surprisal Analytics Dashboard**: A fine-grained diagnostic tool for digital humanities researchers showing per-token surprisal heatmaps, identifying which passages are linguistically unusual. Helps scholars annotate and interpret difficult historical texts interactively.

5. **Historical Language Fine-tuning Service**: Pre-packaged fine-tuning pipelines (using the curated datasets) to specialize existing LLMs for specific languages, time periods, or genres (e.g., 17th-century Italian legal documents). Target: publishers, archives, genealogy platforms.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Limited language/period coverage**: The paper evaluates only Italian and Russian; generalization to other languages, scripts (especially non-Latin), and periods remains untested. Medieval Old English, ancient Greek, or East Asian historical texts may exhibit different dissociations.

2. **Model-specific findings**: All experiments use a single base model family (implied by embedding similarity metrics); results may not transfer to newer architectures, larger models, or entirely different tokenizers.

3. **Tokenization tax conflation**: The paper attributes tokenization cost to orthographic variation, but doesn't disentangle this from simple frequency effects; a modern word with rare spelling would also incur high tokenization cost.

4. **Temporal context prompt engineering**: The 60% reduction assumes oracle access to accurate document dating; in practice, OCR-digitized texts may lack reliable metadata, and prompt design may require manual tuning per corpus.

5. **Semantic robustness ceiling**: Embedding similarity is measured at aggregate levels; fine-grained semantic phenomena (pragmatics, historical sense distinctions, polysemy) may degrade more subtly than global embedding metrics reveal.

6. **No task-specific evaluation**: Robustness is shown for retrieval; actual end-user tasks (summarization, question answering, machine translation from historical text) are not directly evaluated, leaving deployment risk.

## Open Questions

1. **Fine-grained linguistic analysis**: Which morphosyntactic or phonological features most drive surprisal increase? Do inflectional systems, word order, or lexical gaps matter more? Ablation or probing studies would sharpen diagnosis.

2. **Cross-lingual temporal patterns**: Does surprisal increase correlate with linguistic distance (e.g., Romance vs. Slavic families)? Can a unified theory of temporal distance be built across languages?

3. **Scaling laws for historical text**: How does model size affect the tokenization/surprisal dissociation? Do larger models show more robust embeddings or lower surprisal on historical text?

4. **Optimal temporal context design**: What metadata (date, genre, author, region) most effectively reduces surprisal? Can this be learned automatically, or does prompt design require manual expertise?

5. **Generative applications on historical text**: The paper shows embeddings are robust, but which generative tasks (translation, paraphrase, summarization) become feasible with temporal prompting? What's the ceiling?

6. **Multimodal historical corpora**: The dataset includes OCR from page images; does page layout, typography, or handwriting
