# Learning in Infinitesimal Non-Compositional Sketches

**Authors:** Sridhar Mahadevan
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.15107v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15107v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes LINCS (Learning in Infinitesimal Non-Compositional Sketches), a categorical framework for understanding when machine learning models fail to satisfy their design constraints. Instead of viewing failures as numerical prediction errors, the paper formalizes them as obstructions to categorical factorization problems — and introduces a method using infinitesimal perturbations (tangent space analysis) to detect and repair compositional breakdowns before they happen.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on **category theory** — a branch of abstract mathematics that studies structure-preserving relationships between mathematical objects. Core concepts include:

- **Sketches**: Formal specifications of mathematical structures defined as graphs with commutativity conditions, limits, and colimits (these capture geometric and algebraic constraints).
- **Tangent functors** (from differential geometry): Map objects to their infinitesimal perturbation spaces, used here to analyze how small changes propagate through a learning system.
- **Factorization problems**: Universal property checks that ask whether a diagram "commutes" — whether outputs flow through intermediate structures as intended.
- **Coalgebras and fixed points**: Used to formalize ML as a search for equilibrium points where perturbation unfoldings stabilize.

The work extends categorical approaches to learning (viewing ML as universal property satisfaction) into the infinitesimal setting, connecting to differential geometry.

## Core Contributions
1. **Formalization of non-compositionality**: Defines compositional failure purely as obstruction to factorization through quotient sketches, decoupled from arithmetic error metrics.
2. **Tangent lift methodology**: Introduces the INC endofunctor, which iterates tangent functors to produce a tower of factorization problems ($D, TD, T^2D, \ldots$), asking whether infinitesimal perturbations preserve constraints.
3. **Existence proof**: Uses the Aczel–Mendler theorem to prove existence of final INC coalgebras (fixed points where the system stabilizes) under mild set-theoretic conditions.
4. **Generalization beyond loss functions**: Extends the framework to handle limit cones and colimit cocones, moving beyond classical scalarized loss assumptions.

## Connections to Other Work
This extends the categorical view of learning (where models satisfy universal properties) into the **tangent category** setting. It relates to:

- **[[mechanistic interpretability]]** (understanding when internal structure violates design intent)

The infinitesimal perturbation analysis resembles sensitivity analysis and adversarial robustness checking, but framed categorically.

## Practical Applications
1. **Constraint verification for modular ML systems**: Detecting when subsystems violate compositional contracts before deployment (e.g., in multi-component NLP pipelines or control systems).
2. **Diagnosing emergent failures in large models**: Identifying when LLMs violate intended design specifications through infinitesimal analysis.
3. **Compositional RL verification**: Checking whether learned policies preserve task decomposition structure as models train.
4. **Modular neural architecture design**: Certifying that neural modules interact as intended without silent failures in information flow.
5. **Model surgery and patching**: Predicting where small local changes will break global constraints, informing targeted fixes.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **"CompositionCheck"** — A deployment-time audit tool for ML systems that formally verifies compositional constraints on black-box models before production release, using tangent-based analysis.
2. **"SketchML"** — An IDE/library that lets engineers specify ML systems as categorical sketches with built-in constraint checking, generating warnings when models violate design intent during training.
3. **"IntentGuard"** — A monitoring service for LLMs and RL agents that continuously checks whether infinitesimal perturbations (e.g., minor prompt changes, slight policy drift) preserve compositional integrity.
4. **"Modular Safety"** — Consulting/tooling for large tech companies building multi-component AI systems (e.g., recommendation + ranking + safety filters), formally verifying that components compose as intended.
5. **"ConstraintLearn"** — An optimization library that replaces standard loss functions with categorical sketch specifications, automatically detecting compositional failures and steering training away from them.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Computational intractability**: Computing factorization obstructions in large categorical structures may be undecidable; no complexity analysis is provided for real systems.
2. **Set-theoretic assumptions**: Existence of final coalgebras depends on finding "set-based class realizations" — not guaranteed for all ML problems, especially infinite-dimensional ones.
3. **Tangent functor applicability**: Requires the base category to admit a well-defined tangent functor; unclear how to instantiate this for discrete or non-smooth domains (e.g., language tokens, discrete RL actions).
4. **No concrete ML instantiation**: Paper defers experimental evaluation to "companion papers"; unclear how abstract constructions map to real neural network architectures or optimization algorithms.
5. **Scalability to modern models**: Deep learning and LLM training operate at scales (billions of parameters) where categorical reasoning may be infeasible.
6. **Loss of numerical interpretability**: Reframing failures as categorical obstructions might obscure actionable, numerical debugging signals practitioners rely on.

## Open Questions
1. How does the INC tower actually stabilize in practice? What is the convergence rate, and does it relate to SGD dynamics or other standard training phenomena?
2. How do you efficiently compute or approximate factorization obstructions for real neural networks? Is there a tractable algorithm?
3. What is the relationship between the LINCS tangent lift and standard backpropagation/automatic differentiation? Can they be unified?
4. Does the theory specialize to anything interpretable for concrete architectures (CNNs, Transformers, RNNs)? What do sketches look like for these?
5. Can LINCS detect compositional failures that existing robustness or interpretability methods miss? What are concrete, actionable warnings it produces?
6. How does non-compositionality relate to known failure modes like distribution shift, adversarial examples, or specification gaming?

## Key Takeaway
Category theory formalizes ML failures as broken structural constraints, detectible via infinitesimal analysis, offering an alternative to numerical error metrics.
