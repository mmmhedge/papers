# Notes to Self: Can LLMs Benefit from Experiential Abstractions?

**Authors:** Chang Liu, Xinyu Li, Artur Dubrawski
**Published:** 2026-07-22 | **arXiv:** [2607.20372v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20372v1)

## Summary

This paper investigates whether large language models (LLMs) can learn from their own problem-solving experiences by extracting and reusing natural-language abstractions — essentially distilled strategies or lessons learned. The researchers build a library of these "notes to self" from solved math problems, then test whether retrieving or training with these abstractions helps LLMs solve new problems better. The work suggests LLMs can benefit from experiential learning in ways that mirror how humans leverage accumulated knowledge.

## Theoretical Background

The paper builds on two foundational ideas. First, humans routinely abstract experience into generalizable rules (e.g., "always check for edge cases in division problems"), which they retrieve and apply to new situations. Second, recent work has shown that LLMs can improve reasoning through prompting techniques like chain-of-thought (showing step-by-step reasoning) and in-context learning (providing examples at test time). The paper also draws on reinforcement learning (RL) frameworks, where models learn policies by receiving reward signals for desired behaviors. Together, these strands suggest a hypothesis: if LLMs can extract abstract lessons from their own solution traces, they might improve performance just as humans do through reflection and knowledge reuse.

## Core Contributions

1. **Extraction mechanism**: A method to automatically distill natural-language abstractions (strategies, insights, caveats) from LLM solution traces on the MATH dataset, either via teacher supervision or self-extraction.

2. **Dual usage modes**: Two distinct ways to apply these abstractions — (i) retrieval at inference time (fetch relevant abstractions before solving a new problem), and (ii) augmented RL training (use abstractions in prompts during reinforcement learning fine-tuning).

3. **Empirical validation**: Demonstration that both teacher-extracted and self-extracted abstractions improve performance on mathematical and logical reasoning benchmarks, with self-extracted matching teacher-quality.

4. **Transfer and generalization**: Evidence that the abstraction framework transfers across datasets and different LLM architectures, suggesting the approach is not dataset-specific.

## Connections to Other Work

This work connects to several threads:
- [[Chain-of-thought prompting]] and in-context learning, which show that intermediate reasoning steps improve LLM performance.
- [[Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)]], which finetunes models using reward signals; here, abstractions enrich the training signal.
- Meta-learning and few-shot learning literature, where models adapt quickly by leveraging past examples.
- Interpretability research questioning what internal knowledge LLMs possess and how they reuse it.
- The broader theme of continual or lifelong learning in neural networks.

## Practical Applications

- **Tutoring systems**: Automatically generate personalized problem-solving hints by retrieving student-specific or cohort-specific abstractions from past mistakes.
- **Mathematical or coding assistants**: Maintain a dynamically updated knowledge base of strategies and edge cases encountered during problem-solving sessions, improving accuracy over time.
- **Professional reasoning tools** (legal analysis, medical diagnosis): Extract and surface domain-specific decision rules and cautionary lessons from prior cases or consultations.
- **Automated curriculum design**: Use extracted abstractions to structure learning progressions—start with foundational strategies, then combine them for harder problems.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **Personal AI Tutor with Memory**: An LLM-based tutoring platform that learns each student's recurring mistakes, extracts remedial abstractions, and surfaces them as contextual hints—differentiated learning at scale.

2. **Enterprise Knowledge Distillation Engine**: A tool for professional services (law, consulting, finance) that ingests past work products, extracts decision-making abstractions automatically, and retrieves them during new client work—institutional memory as a service.

3. **Adaptive Coding Assistant**: A code completion and debugging tool that learns from a developer's codebase, extracts architecture patterns and common pitfalls, and suggests them in real-time—like GitHub Copilot but with organizational muscle memory.

4. **Math Competition Training Platform**: A competitive math platform where the system extracts problem-solving strategies from high-performing students and feeds them to LLMs to generate customized practice problems with embedded hints.

5. **Knowledge Audit & Compliance Tool**: For regulated industries, automatically extract and surface past risk insights, precedents, and warnings from internal problem-solving traces, ensuring consistent application of learned lessons.

## Technical Limitations

- **Abstraction quality**: The paper does not deeply analyze *which* abstractions are extracted or their semantic validity—garbage-in-garbage-out risk if solution traces are noisy or incorrect.
- **Scalability of retrieval**: As the abstraction library grows, efficiently retrieving the *most relevant* abstractions becomes a search problem; the paper does not detail how this scales to millions of problems.
- **Domain specificity**: The approach is validated primarily on MATH benchmarks; transferability to domains with less formal structure (open-ended writing, real-world reasoning) is unclear.
- **Compute overhead**: RL fine-tuning with abstraction-augmented prompts likely increases training cost; cost-benefit trade-offs are not quantified.
- **Interpretability of extractions**: No human evaluation of whether extracted abstractions are actually sensible or whether LLMs are truly "understanding" them vs. pattern-matching on surface similarity.
- **Comparison baselines**: Limited ablation—unclear how much improvement comes from the abstraction content vs. simply adding more context/tokens to prompts.

## Open Questions

1. **What makes an abstraction useful?** Can you characterize which extracted abstractions transfer across problem domains and which don't? Is there a taxonomy of abstraction types?

2. **Efficiency of retrieval**: How do you efficiently index and retrieve relevant abstractions as libraries scale? Does approximate nearest-neighbor search work, or is semantic understanding required?

3. **Failure modes**: When do abstractions hurt performance? Are there problem types where reuse of past lessons leads to overfitting or negative transfer?

4. **Interaction with prompting**: How do abstraction-augmented training and prompts interact with other prompt engineering techniques (few-shot, chain-of-thought)? Is there redundancy?

5. **Human evaluation**: Do human experts agree that extracted abstractions are valid and insightful? Is the model's improvement due to genuinely learned concepts or mere statistical correlation?

6. **Generalization across domains**: Can abstractions learned on MATH transfer to science or logic problems? Or are they too specialized?

## Key Takeaway

LLMs can extract and reuse distilled problem-solving strategies to improve reasoning, mirroring how humans learn from experience.
