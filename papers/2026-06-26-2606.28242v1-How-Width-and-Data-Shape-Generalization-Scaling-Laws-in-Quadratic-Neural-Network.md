# How Width and Data Shape Generalization Scaling Laws in Quadratic Neural Networks

**Authors:** Julius Girardin, Emanuele Troiani, Yizhou Xu, Vittorio Erba, Florent Krzakala et al.
**Published:** 2026-06-26 | **arXiv:** [2606.28242v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28242v1)

## Summary
This paper provides a theoretical analysis of how generalization error in quadratic neural networks scales jointly with model width (number of parameters) and dataset size. By studying ℓ2-regularized two-layer networks on structured data, the authors derive explicit phase diagrams showing distinct scaling regimes and demonstrate that generalization follows data-dependent power laws controlled by the spectral properties of the learning target.

## Theoretical Background
Scaling laws describe how model performance improves as we increase computational resources (parameters, data, compute). Most prior theoretical work either fixes the feature representation (fixed-feature regime) or assumes infinite model width, and typically studies online stochastic gradient descent (SGD). This paper takes a different angle: it analyzes feature-learning models at finite width and in a batch setting.

The core technical tool is studying ℓ2-regularized empirical risk minimization — a framework where we minimize training error plus a penalty proportional to the squared weight norm, which prevents overfitting. The specific architecture is a quadratic two-layer network, a model simple enough to admit closed-form analysis yet rich enough to exhibit feature learning (where the network learns useful representations, not just fixed features).

The key insight is that the spectral structure of the target function (roughly, how its information is distributed across different frequency or complexity levels) controls the generalization scaling exponents.

## Core Contributions
1. **Explicit phase diagram for finite-width networks**: The paper characterizes distinct regimes depending on the ratio of samples to parameters, with sharp transitions between them. This goes beyond prior work that typically focuses on a single scaling regime.

2. **Data-dependent power-law exponents**: Rather than universal exponents, the generalization error's power-law behavior depends on the eigenspectrum of the data covariance and target structure. This explains why different problems may generalize at different rates.

3. **Interpolation threshold characterization**: The paper identifies when the model transitions from underparameterized (fewer parameters than samples) to overparameterized (more parameters than samples) regimes and quantifies the generalization impact at this boundary.

4. **Finite-sample analysis with structured data**: Unlike asymptotic results, this provides concrete bounds applicable to realistic sample sizes and incorporates the structure of real data distributions.

## Connections to Other Work
This contributes to the broader theory of [[scaling laws]] in deep learning — a central empirical and theoretical question since work like the Chinchilla scaling laws. It complements fixed-feature analyses (e.g., kernel methods, random features) by studying actual feature learning. The phase-transition perspective aligns with statistical mechanics approaches to learning, particularly the "jamming transition" in neural networks studied in the physics literature. It also relates to [[double descent]] phenomena, where test error non-monotonically depends on model size.

The regularization framework connects to classical statistical learning theory (bias-variance tradeoff, Rademacher complexity) but extends it to the high-dimensional feature-learning setting.

## Practical Applications
1. **Hyperparameter tuning guidance**: The phase diagrams provide principled heuristics for choosing model width and regularization given a fixed data budget.

2. **Data acquisition planning**: Understanding which spectral properties of data drive scaling helps practitioners decide whether to collect more data or expand model capacity.

3. **Transfer learning strategy**: When fine-tuning on small datasets, the finite-width analysis suggests whether to train shallow or wide models given sample size constraints.

4. **Interpretable scaling predictions**: For practitioners building systems with specific data distributions (e.g., tabular or structured data), these predictions may be more accurate than universal power laws.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **AutoML tuning service**: A cloud platform that profiles a user's data spectral properties and recommends optimal width/regularization/sample-size combinations before expensive training runs.

2. **Data valuation software**: Tools that quantify how much a new dataset contribution improves generalization curves, helping organizations prioritize data collection investments.

3. **Neural architecture search (NAS) optimizer**: Incorporate phase-diagram insights into NAS algorithms to intelligently prune candidate architectures based on dataset size and spectral properties.

4. **Scaling law prediction API**: Offer per-model, per-domain scaling law predictions trained on the user's data; helps estimate whether scaling to 10x data/compute is worth the investment.

5. **Regularization strategy advisor**: Real-time tool that suggests optimal ℓ2 regularization (and width) given streaming data, adapting as the dataset grows.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Restricted architecture**: Analysis covers only quadratic two-layer networks. Real deep networks have many nonlinear layers and more complex feature dynamics; applicability is unclear.

2. **Idealized data**: Assumes structured, well-behaved data distributions. Adversarial, noisy, or heavily imbalanced real-world data may violate assumptions.

3. **ℓ2 regularization only**: Modern techniques (dropout, batch norm, early stopping, data augmentation) are not analyzed. The regularization landscape is likely far richer.

4. **Batch/offline setting**: Doesn't cover online learning or SGD dynamics, which differ substantively from the empirical risk minimization framework.

5. **Spectral assumptions**: Results hinge on spectral properties of the target being known or learnable; high-dimensional, complex targets might defy clean spectral characterization.

6. **Extrapolation**: Scaling law estimates may break down far outside the regime studied (e.g., extreme overparameterization).

## Open Questions
1. **Deeper networks**: Can similar phase diagrams be derived or empirically verified for networks with 3+ hidden layers? Do transitions become sharper or more complex?

2. **Nonlinear activations in intermediate layers**: How do results change if the first layer is linear but the second is nonlinear, or vice versa?

3. **Implicit regularization**: What role does implicit regularization (via SGD or early stopping) play compared to explicit ℓ2 regularization? Do phase transitions persist?

4. **Non-quadratic targets**: For genuinely nonlinear target functions beyond quadratic, do power-law exponents follow similar spectral rules?

5. **Empirical validation at scale**: Can the predicted scaling laws be validated on real datasets and modern architectures (transformers, CNNs)? Which predictions fail and why?

6. **Multi-task and transfer regimes**: How do generalization scalings change when the target is related to but not identical to the training distribution?

## Key Takeaway
Generalization in finite-width feature-learning networks exhibits distinct phase-dependent scaling regimes determined by the spectral structure of data and target, not just universal power laws.
