# AutoSynthesis: An agentic system for automated meta-analysis

**Authors:** Moein Taherinezhad, Sebastian Maier, Gerardo Vitagliano, Francesco Pierri, Stefan Feuerriegel
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.15247v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15247v1)

## Summary
AutoSynthesis is an AI system that automates meta-analysis—the process of combining results from multiple scientific studies into a single quantitative conclusion. Given a research question in plain English, it retrieves papers, screens them, extracts data, and produces a standardized meta-analysis report. This matters because meta-analysis is essential for turning scattered research into reliable evidence for medicine, policy, and science, but doing it manually is slow and doesn't scale.

## Theoretical Background
Meta-analysis is a statistical technique that pools effect sizes (standardized measures of how large an effect is—e.g., Hedges' *g*) across studies to estimate an overall treatment effect and examine variation across contexts. Traditional meta-analysis requires humans to manually search literature, read full texts, extract numbers, assess study quality, and apply random-effects statistical models.

The paper builds on recent advances in large language models (LLMs—neural networks trained on massive text corpora that can understand and generate human language) and multi-agent systems (architectures where multiple specialized AI components communicate to solve complex tasks). AutoSynthesis chains these components to replicate the cognitive steps a human researcher would perform: formulating search queries, filtering studies, reading abstracts and full texts, extracting data, and applying statistical methods.

The system is evaluated against PRISMA guidelines—an international standard for reporting meta-analyses—ensuring outputs are transparent and methodologically sound.

## Core Contributions
1. **End-to-end automation of meta-analysis**: A fully integrated pipeline that moves from natural-language research question to publication-ready meta-analysis report, eliminating the need for manual intervention at each step.
2. **Multi-agent decomposition**: Breaks the meta-analysis task into subtasks (search, screening, eligibility, extraction, effect-size computation, heterogeneity analysis, bias assessment), each handled by specialized agents that can be improved independently.
3. **Empirical validation against manual work**: Demonstrates that AutoSynthesis produces effect estimates (Hedges' *g*) close to expert-conducted meta-analyses, not just plausible results, but statistically comparable ones.
4. **PRISMA-aligned reporting**: Outputs structured, transparent reports that meet publication standards, enabling direct use in academic and policy contexts.

## Connections to Other Work
AutoSynthesis relates to several research threads:
- **[[Reasoning with LLMs]]**: Uses language models for tasks requiring multi-step logical reasoning (literature screening, eligibility assessment).
- **[[Agent-based orchestration]]**: Extends work on [[ReAct]] and similar frameworks that decompose complex tasks into tool-use and reasoning loops.
- **[[Information extraction from scientific text]]**: Builds on prior work in automatic data extraction from papers, but applies it at scale across dozens of studies.
- **[[Evidence-based policy]]**: Addresses the same bottleneck as [[Semantic Scholar]] and automated citation analysis—making research actionable for decision-makers.

The novelty is not in any single component but in their integration into a system that handles the full workflow of evidence synthesis, which has been nearly untouched by automation despite its high value.

## Practical Applications
1. **Rapid evidence synthesis for policy**: Governments and health agencies need quick meta-analyses to inform pandemic responses, drug approvals, or education policy. AutoSynthesis could turn around reports in days instead of months.
2. **Systematic review acceleration in healthcare**: Pharmaceutical companies and hospitals spend millions on manual meta-analyses for clinical decision-making. Automation reduces cost and latency.
3. **Academic literature surveys**: PhD students and researchers writing background sections could generate meta-analytic summaries of a field instantly.
4. **Insurance and risk assessment**: Insurers evaluating new treatments or interventions could use AutoSynthesis to synthesize evidence on outcomes and cost-effectiveness.
5. **Regulatory approval timelines**: FDA and EMA regulators reviewing new drugs could use the system to synthesize supporting evidence faster.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Evidence Synthesis as a Service (ESaaS)**: A SaaS platform where researchers or clinicians upload a research question and receive a publication-ready meta-analysis report within 24 hours, with full audit trails and PRISMA compliance. Price per synthesis ($500–$5k) or subscription model.
2. **Policy-ready Evidence Dashboard**: A B2B tool for government agencies, NGOs, and international bodies (WHO, World Bank) that continuously monitors evidence across domains (health, education, criminal justice) and flags when new meta-analyses emerge, surfacing emerging consensus.
3. **ChatBot for Systematic Reviews**: Integrate AutoSynthesis into a conversational interface where clinical teams ask questions like "What's the effect of cognitive behavioral therapy on depression?" and get interactive meta-analytic summaries with confidence intervals and study details.
4. **White-label Integration for Academic Publishers**: License AutoSynthesis to *Nature*, *Science*, *JAMA* etc. to auto-generate meta-analytic summaries of emerging topics and suggest related papers, increasing discoverability and citation impact.
5. **Insurance & Health Economics Tool**: Develop a specialized version for actuaries and health economists that synthesizes cost-effectiveness and safety data, generating reports for pricing, coverage decisions, and regulatory submissions.

## Technical Limitations
1. **LLM hallucination and errors**: LLMs sometimes "hallucinate" facts—inventing data or misquoting papers. At scale across 28+ studies, even small error rates accumulate. The paper does not quantify extraction accuracy per claim.
2. **Non-English bias**: If the system searches primarily in English, it will miss non-English-language studies, biasing meta-analytic estimates in favor of English-speaking countries.
3. **Data extraction from complex figures**: Extracting numbers from tables and figures in PDFs is fragile. The paper does not discuss how the system handles scans, low-quality PDFs, or unconventional data presentations.
4. **Heterogeneity analysis limitations**: The paper mentions heterogeneity analysis but does not detail how well the system identifies and interprets moderators (variables that explain differences across studies), which requires deeper understanding of study designs.
5. **Quality assessment subjectivity**: Assessing risk of bias involves subjective judgment (e.g., "Was randomization properly concealed?"). Fully automating this without human oversight may produce misleading conclusions.
6. **Small study population**: Validation was performed on a single application with 28 studies. Generalization to other domains (education, engineering, social sciences) is unclear.
7. **Statistical assumptions**: The system applies random-effects models without discussing assumptions (e.g., normal distribution of effects) or when fixed-effects models might be more appropriate.

## Open Questions
1. **Robustness across domains**: Does AutoSynthesis generalize to meta-analyses in education, social policy, or engineering, or is it primarily tested on medical/health domains?
2. **Error analysis and human-in-the-loop correction**: What happens when the system makes an extraction error? Can humans easily correct it, or must the entire synthesis be redone?
3. **Comparison of search strategies**: How sensitive are results to the search strategy formulated by the system? Would human-curated searches yield different conclusions?
4. **Long-tail heterogeneity**: Can AutoSynthesis detect and analyze rare moderators (e.g., geographic, demographic) that explain effect-size variation but appear in few studies?
5. **Transparency and auditability**: Beyond PRISMA reporting, can users inspect which studies contributed to each claim, and challenge the system's decisions (e.g., "Why did you exclude this paper")?
6. **Real-time meta-analysis**: Could the system continuously monitor new publications and update meta-analytic estimates, supporting "living systematic reviews"?
7. **Interaction with prior knowledge**: Does the system allow incorporation of prior beliefs (Bayesian meta-analysis), or does it only perform frequentist random-effects models?

## Key Takeaway
AI agents can now automatically synthesize evidence from multiple studies into reliable meta-analyses, making evidence-based decisions faster for medicine, policy, and science.
