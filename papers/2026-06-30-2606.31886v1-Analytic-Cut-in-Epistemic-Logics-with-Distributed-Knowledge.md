# Analytic Cut in Epistemic Logics with Distributed Knowledge

**Authors:** Ryo Murai, Sizhuo Liu, Katsuhiko Sano
**Published:** 2026-06-30 | **arXiv:** [2606.31886v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31886v1)

## Summary
This paper develops proof-theoretic foundations for reasoning about distributed knowledge in multi-agent systems — a formal notion of what groups of agents collectively know, defined as the intersection of their individual knowledge. The authors prove that analytic cut (a weaker but still powerful proof principle) holds for epistemic logics based on K45, KD45, and S5 modal systems, enabling sound and complete sequent calculi where reasoning stays within relevant formulas.

## Theoretical Background
Epistemic logic is formal logic for reasoning about knowledge and belief. Modal logics like K45, KD45, and S5 extend propositional logic with modality operators (roughly: "it is known/believed that..."); they differ in which logical axioms they adopt (K45 and KD45 add transitivity and negative introspection; S5 adds positive introspection). In multi-agent systems, distributed knowledge captures a collective notion: what a group could deduce if they shared all their information. Technically, this is modeled via the intersection of individual agents' epistemic accessibility relations — the set of possible worlds consistent with each agent's knowledge. Sequent calculi are formal proof systems that decompose logical formulas into structured rules; cut elimination is a classical result proving these systems only need "analytic" cuts (using subformulas), which is essential for computational decidability and interpolation results (theorems about what information can be transferred between logical statements).

## Core Contributions
1. **Failure of full cut elimination**: The authors show that standard cut elimination fails for distributed-knowledge epistemic logics—a negative result that motivates the positive one.
2. **Analytic cut property**: They establish (for K45, KD45, and S5 with distributed knowledge) that cut can be restricted to subformulas of the conclusion, preserving the computational and logical benefits of cut elimination without requiring it in full form.
3. **Craig interpolation as corollary**: The analytic cut property immediately yields Craig interpolation (if A ⊢ B, there exists intermediate formula C with A ⊢ C ⊢ B using only shared propositional variables).
4. **Extension to empty groups**: Results hold when the empty group is allowed, interpreting distributed knowledge of the empty group as the global modality (knowledge true in all possible worlds).

## Connections to Other Work
This work refines proof-theoretic understanding of [[multi-agent epistemic logic]] — a classical area in knowledge representation. It extends Takano's (2018) analytic cut strategy (originally for other modal systems) to the distributed-knowledge setting. The paper contrasts with approaches using [[Kripke semantics]] directly; it provides syntactic tools for reasoning. Related to work on [[RLHF]] and alignment insofar as multi-agent epistemic logic underpins formal models of agent communication and consensus, though this paper is purely foundational. The global modality connection links to work on [[lax logic]] and other enriched modal systems.

## Practical Applications
1. **Formal verification of multi-agent protocols**: Proving properties of distributed systems where agents must reach consensus or share information (e.g., Byzantine fault-tolerant algorithms).
2. **Automated reasoning in collaborative robotics**: Verification that robot teams correctly model what other robots know and can jointly achieve goals.
3. **Security policy analysis**: Reasoning about information flow in systems where groups of principals (users, processes) have collective access rights.
4. **Game-theoretic agent reasoning**: Formal analysis of common knowledge assumptions in game theory and auction mechanisms.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability not addressed**: Proof complexity and decidability time for large numbers of agents is not discussed; the analytic cut property guarantees exist but may be computationally expensive.
2. **No algorithm provided**: The paper is purely theoretical; no implementation or concrete proof-search algorithm is given.
3. **Closure under substitution**: The analytic cut property depends on restricting cuts to subformulas, which may weaken practical expressiveness compared to unrestricted cut.
4. **Empty group edge case**: While the extension to empty groups is elegant, its practical interpretation (global modality = truth in all worlds) is somewhat abstract; semantics may break in richer settings (e.g., dynamic epistemic logic with public announcements).
5. **No empirical validation**: No experiments comparing the complexity of proofs using analytic cut vs. other strategies.

## Open Questions
1. **Proof complexity**: How does proof length grow with analytic cut vs. unrestricted cut? Can tight bounds be established?
2. **Extension to richer logics**: Can analytic cut be established for dynamic epistemic logic (adding actions/updates) with distributed knowledge?
3. **Implementation & automation**: Can a practical proof search algorithm be designed, and how does it perform on realistic multi-agent specifications?
4. **Quantitative distributed knowledge**: Can the framework extend to probabilistic or weighted notions of distributed knowledge?
5. **Interpolation algorithm**: Does Craig interpolation yield a constructive algorithm to synthesize the intermediate formula C?

## Key Takeaway
Analytic cut (subformula restriction) is sufficient for sound and complete reasoning about distributed knowledge in multi-agent epistemic logics, even where full cut elimination fails, enabling both formal verification and interpolation results.
