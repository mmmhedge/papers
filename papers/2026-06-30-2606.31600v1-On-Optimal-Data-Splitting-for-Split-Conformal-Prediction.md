# On Optimal Data Splitting for Split Conformal Prediction

**Authors:** Sayan Das, Bahram Yaghooti, Todd A. Kuffner, Soumendra N. Lahiri
**Published:** 2026-06-30 | **arXiv:** [2606.31600v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31600v1)

## Summary
This paper solves a fundamental but overlooked problem: how to optimally split data into training and calibration sets for split conformal prediction (a method that creates prediction intervals with guaranteed coverage). The key insight is that the optimal split ratio depends on the underlying model structure, and the authors derive closed-form formulas showing exactly what split minimizes interval width while maintaining coverage guarantees—then demonstrate these formulas across linear regression, nonparametric methods, and neural networks.

## Theoretical Background
Split conformal prediction is a distribution-free uncertainty quantification method that works in two stages: train a model on one subset of data, then use a held-out calibration set to determine prediction intervals with finite-sample coverage guarantees (e.g., 90% of true labels fall in the predicted interval). Prior work established that this approach is valid across any data distribution, but practitioners typically chose split ratios (e.g., 50-50 or 80-20) arbitrarily. The paper builds on classical statistical theory of sample allocation and optimal experimental design—the idea that how you divide limited data affects downstream inference—and extends it to the conformal prediction setting where you're optimizing interval length (the practical width of your uncertainty estimate) rather than just estimation variance.

## Core Contributions
1. **General theoretical framework**: Derives analytical expressions for the length-optimal training-calibration split ratio that work across different model classes, separating "symmetric" regimes (where model performance scales identically with training data) from "asymmetric" ones.
2. **Model-specific characterizations**: Shows concretely how optimal splits differ for linear regression (where the ratio depends on dimensionality and noise), nonparametric methods (where it depends on smoothness and sample size scaling), and neural networks (where it depends on network complexity).
3. **Data-driven selection method**: Provides a practical algorithm to estimate the optimal split from observed data, not just theoretical prescriptions.
4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates across synthetic and real datasets that the theory-derived split ratios actually produce shorter intervals in practice.

## Connections to Other Work
This work relates to [[cross-validation]] and sample reuse strategies in machine learning, which also grapple with the train-test tradeoff. It extends classical optimal allocation theory (from statistics) into the modern conformal prediction paradigm. The framework is complementary to recent work on adaptive and weighted conformal prediction, which tune the method itself rather than the data split. It also connects to broader literature on [[uncertainty quantification]] and predictive inference—split conformal prediction is one of the few methods with distribution-free guarantees, making the efficiency question especially important.

## Practical Applications
1. **Medical diagnosis systems**: Hospitals using ML to recommend diagnoses need tight prediction intervals (narrow uncertainty bands) with guaranteed coverage; optimal splitting ensures narrower intervals without sacrificing reliability.
2. **Autonomous vehicle perception**: Safety-critical systems need calibrated confidence estimates; optimizing the data split improves interval tightness for object detection confidence scores.
3. **Financial forecasting**: Banks and trading firms use conformal methods for price prediction intervals; optimal splits reduce unnecessary conservatism in interval width, improving decision-making.
4. **Manufacturing quality control**: Factories using ML to predict part dimensions need prediction intervals with strict coverage; the framework tells you how much data to reserve for calibration.
5. **Drug efficacy trials**: Pharmaceutical research uses prediction intervals for patient outcome estimation; optimal splits maximize statistical power given fixed sample sizes.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Model class assumption**: The framework requires you to know (or assume) which model class you're using; misspecification could lead to suboptimal splits. Real-world model selection uncertainty is not addressed.
2. **Finite-sample approximations**: The analytical formulas are asymptotic; finite-sample corrections may be needed for small datasets (the paper acknowledges data-based selection methods, but their finite-sample properties are not deeply analyzed).
3. **Exchangeability requirement**: Like all conformal methods, the framework assumes training, calibration, and test data are exchangeable (i.i.d. samples from the same distribution); it breaks under distribution shift or temporal dependence.
4. **Computational overhead**: For high-dimensional or complex nonparametric models, computing the optimal split may require retraining multiple times; the paper doesn't discuss computational cost or scalability.
5. **Asymmetric regression gap**: The paper covers linear regression and neural networks well, but the connection between theoretical predictions and practical neural network behavior (where scaling laws are complex) remains somewhat loose.

## Open Questions
1. **Distribution shift robustness**: How does the optimal split change if the calibration and test distributions differ? Can you design splits that are robust to mild shift?
2. **Multi-stage learning**: Modern ML often uses multiple validation stages (train/val/test/holdout); how should you optimally allocate data across three or more stages in a conformal framework?
3. **Adaptive/dynamic splits**: Can you update the split ratio online as you observe calibration performance, or would that violate coverage guarantees?
4. **High-dimensional asymptotics**: The paper handles fixed dimensionality; what happens when the number of features grows with sample size? Do the formulas still hold?
5. **Comparison with other uncertainty methods**: How do conformal prediction intervals with optimal splits compare to Bayesian credible intervals or bootstrap-based approaches in terms of interval width across different regimes?

## Key Takeaway
The optimal way to split data for conformal prediction is not arbitrary—it depends on your model's structure in a precise, calculable way, and using theory-guided splits can substantially tighten prediction intervals without sacrificing coverage guarantees.
