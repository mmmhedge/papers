# Not Birds of a Feather: Personality-Based Partner Selection in LLM Agents

**Authors:** Tao Wang, Hsiang-Ling Chiu, Chihang Wei, Yang Xiu, Zhonghao Hou
**Published:** 2026-07-22 | **arXiv:** [2607.19785v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19785v1)

## Summary
This paper studies how LLM agents select partners based on personality traits when capability is held constant. The researchers found that agents show strong, task-dependent personality preferences (e.g., choosing "open" agents for creative tasks, "conscientious" agents for strategic ones) that contradict both human similarity-attraction patterns and human evidence about what personalities actually make teams perform well — raising concerns about bias in AI agent marketplaces.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on two distinct literatures. In organizational psychology, the Big Five personality model (openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, neuroticism) is well-established, and decades of team-performance research show that agreeableness and conscientiousness predict collective outcomes. In multi-agent AI systems, agents are increasingly given personas or personalities to make interactions more natural. The novelty is treating agent personality selection as an empirical question: do LLM agents exhibit the same personality-based partner preferences that humans do, and if so, do those preferences align with evidence about effective teams?

## Core Contributions
1. **Empirical demonstration of personality-driven selection**: With capability held constant, LLM agent partner selection is not random and follows task-stereotype associations (e.g., open agents win 100% of creative tasks).

2. **Dissociation from human similarity-attraction**: Contrary to the well-documented human tendency to choose similar others (homophily), agents *avoid* self-similar personality partners (11.1% vs. 16.7% chance baseline) and instead seek trait distance.

3. **Miscalibration against team-performance science**: The strong task-stereotyped preferences don't match human meta-analytic evidence about which personalities actually drive team performance. For instance, agents rarely select agreeable partners despite agreeableness being one of the strongest human team-performance predictors.

4. **Quantified effect sizes**: Chi-square and Cramér's V statistics show these patterns are highly significant (p<.001, V=.74).

## Practical Applications
1. **Auditing agent marketplaces**: platforms that allow agents to form teams (e.g., in enterprise automation, customer service networks) can now test for and detect personality-based selection bias before deployment.

2. **Ensuring diverse agent teams**: if task performance benefits from agreeableness (as human evidence suggests) but agents avoid it, manual composition or debiasing interventions are needed.

3. **Debugging multi-agent system failures**: if a deployed agent coalition underperforms, personality-based selection misalignment could be one root cause worth investigating.

4. **Personality-aware agent design**: systems can be tuned to weight agent selection differently (upweight agreeableness, downweight task-stereotype stereotyping) based on known performance correlates.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Controlled experiment scope**: six validated archetypes and five task categories is a small design space. Generalization to continuous personality dimensions, more task types, or larger agent populations is unclear.

2. **No causal mechanism**: the paper shows *that* agents select based on personality but not *why*. It could be token statistics, training-data artifacts, or learned associations — mechanistic interpretability work would be needed.

3. **Single-shot decisions**: partner selection is measured as a one-time choice. Real multi-agent systems involve repeated collaboration, trust-building, and dynamic team revision; single-shot results may not predict long-term behavior.

4. **Human baseline confounds**: Study 2 (personality-assigned hosts) uses host agents, not humans, so the claim about human similarity-attraction vs. LLM dissimilarity is cross-species comparison. A direct human-LLM parallel experiment would strengthen the claim.

5. **Capability hold-out not fully validated**: while the paper states capability is constant, subtle performance differences encoded in the prompt or persona text could influence selection.

6. **No task-performance data**: the paper doesn't measure whether selected teams actually perform better or worse, only that selection patterns mismatch human performance science. Closing that loop requires end-to-end performance measurement.

## Open Questions
1. **Mechanistic explanation**: What patterns in the LLM's training data or weights explain the task-stereotype associations? Can we identify and intervene on the causal factors?

2. **Temporal dynamics**: Do agent preferences change after observing partner performance? Can agents learn to correct their personality-based biases?

3. **Scaling and generalization**: Do these patterns hold with larger populations of agents, longer task lists, or different persona design approaches? Are the biases robust across model architectures and scales?

4. **Performance impact**: When agents select based on personality, do teams actually underperform relative to human-designed or capability-matched compositions? Measure task completion, error rates, and efficiency.

5. **Debiasing interventions**: What prompting, fine-tuning, or architectural changes would make agent selection align with human team-performance science?

6. **Cross-cultural personality**: Big Five is Western-centric. Do similar patterns emerge if agent personas are grounded in other personality taxonomies or cultural frameworks?

## Key Takeaway
LLM agents pick partners based on personality-task stereotypes, avoiding similar others — patterns misaligned with how real teams succeed.
