# Triadic Werewolf: A Jester Role for Multi-Hop Theory of Mind in LLMs

**Authors:** Avni Mittal
**Published:** 2026-06-26 | **arXiv:** [2606.27909v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27909v1)

## Summary
This paper evaluates theory-of-mind capabilities in large language models by introducing a three-faction variant of the Werewolf social deduction game with a Jester role whose winning condition (being voted out) creates inverted incentives. The triadic structure forces models to reason simultaneously across three opposing utility functions, revealing gaps in multi-agent reasoning that dyadic games obscure—particularly that even GPT-4.1 fails catastrophically by self-defeatingly eliminating the Jester early.

## Theoretical Background
Theory of mind (ToM) is the capacity to model others' beliefs, desires, and likely actions; it's foundational to social reasoning. Prior work has evaluated LLMs' ToM using social deduction games like Werewolf, where hidden roles create asymmetric information. In traditional dyadic (two-sided) setups, however, a model can rely on surface-level linguistic cues and statistical patterns without truly simulating opponent incentives—making strong performance difficult to interpret as genuine strategic reasoning. Game theory provides the formal framework: utility functions define what each agent wants, and optimal play requires anticipating how others will act given *their* goals, not just the speaker's. The Jester is a classic game-theory device introducing misaligned incentives: it wins by the very outcome (being eliminated) that other factions want to prevent, forcing agents to reason about why someone might want the opposite of what they claim.

## Core Contributions
1. **Triadic game design**: Extends Werewolf with a third faction (Jester) whose utility function is explicitly inverted—winning by elimination rather than survival—creating a structure where no single heuristic suffices.
2. **Systematic failure modes**: Demonstrates that GPT-4.1 votes out the Jester 60–70% on day 1, a move that actively *worsens* its chances and strengthens Werewolves, revealing that even frontier models fail to properly model three-way incentive structures.
3. **Differential learning curves**: Shows self-learning helps DeepSeek and Llama but *hurts* GPT-4.1 (specifically degrading Villager performance), suggesting different architectures have asymmetric blindspots in multi-agent reasoning.
4. **Jester dominance despite inference parity**: The Jester wins 60–70% while Werewolves never exceed 20%, suggesting that *strategic misalignment of incentives* confers a stronger advantage than information asymmetry in exposing model weaknesses.
5. **Subtle strategy emergence**: Only DeepSeek learns the fine-grained strategy of appearing suspicious *without appearing deliberately deceptive*, a hallmark of genuine multi-step theory of mind.

## Practical Applications
1. **Evaluation benchmarks for multi-agent AI systems**: Organizations deploying LLMs in collaborative or adversarial multi-agent settings (e.g., negotiation, content moderation, supply-chain coordination) could use triadic incentive structures to probe real weaknesses before deployment.
2. **Red-teaming and adversarial testing**: Security teams can use Jester-like inverted-incentive scenarios to identify whether models can be socially engineered or exploited by agents with misaligned goals.
3. **Game AI and NPC behavior**: Video game designers could use this evaluation approach to verify that AI opponents model player intent correctly across complex multiplayer scenarios.
4. **Organizational decision-making simulations**: Companies running multi-stakeholder simulations (e.g., merger dynamics, regulatory responses) could embed Jester-like actors to test whether their strategic-reasoning systems fail when incentives invert.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Small sample size**: 60 games total across three models is modest; statistical noise in win rates (e.g., 60–70% for Jester) could obscure whether differences are robust or sampling artifacts.
2. **No ablation on game parameters**: The paper doesn't vary Jester mechanics (e.g., reveal timing, voting rules) to isolate which aspects of inverted incentives cause reasoning collapse.
3. **Confounded effects of self-learning**: Self-learning "on and off" is unclear—does this mean fine-tuning on game data? Iterative play? The mechanism is underspecified, making the differential performance across models hard to interpret.
4. **Evaluation scope limited to text**: No evaluation of whether visual or multimodal cues (tone, body language) would allow models to detect the Jester's true intent—triadic reasoning might be easier with richer signals.
5. **No introspection into model decisions**: The paper reports win rates but doesn't include model confidence scores, internal reasoning traces, or error analysis—we don't know *why* GPT-4.1 votes out the Jester or whether it's overconfidence, miscalibration, or true inability to model inversions.
6. **Role assignment bias**: If the Jester role assignment or initial suspicion framing is unbalanced across games, this could artificially inflate Jester win rates independent of reasoning capability.

## Open Questions
1. **Does explicit incentive communication help?** What if the model is told "the Jester wins by being eliminated" in plain language before play? Does this remove the reasoning gap, or is the problem deeper (inability to *integrate* that knowledge into strategic planning)?
2. **How does performance scale with more factions?** Does a 4- or 5-faction game reveal additional reasoning collapses, or is triadic the "phase transition" point for current models?
3. **Can fine-tuning on game data recover performance?** If you fine-tune GPT-4 on hundreds of Werewolf games with explicit loss signals for strategic coherence, does it learn the Jester dynamic, or is the reasoning gap architectural?
4. **What role does context length play?** Do longer conversation histories (more suspicion-tracking) help models form better models of opponents, or is the bottleneck attention capacity, world-state tracking, or reasoning depth?
5. **Do models with explicit "utility modeling" modules perform better?** Could an LLM with auxiliary networks that explicitly output opponent utility functions avoid the Jester trap? This would test whether the gap is representational vs. architectural.
6. **How does human play compare?** What's the human win rate for each faction in the same setup? Are humans' advantages due to richer intuition about deception, or better Bayesian updating of opponent models?
