# Same Game, Different Story: A Minimal Conservative Strategic Robustness Benchmark for Large Language Model Agents

**Authors:** Seyed Pouyan Mousavi Davoudi, Alireza Amiri-Margavi, Amin Gholami Davodi, Hamidreza Hasani Balyani, Arshia Gharagozlou
**Published:** 2026-07-22 | **arXiv:** [2607.19670v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19670v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces a benchmark to test whether large language model agents make consistent decisions when the same strategic game is presented using different narratives or framings. The authors find that LLMs like GPT-4 and LLaMa-2 shift their behavior substantially based on social context (e.g., "business" vs. "friend-sharing" framing) even when payoffs stay identical—a concerning sign that these models lack robustness in real strategic interactions.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on game theory, which studies strategic interactions where outcomes depend on multiple agents' choices. A key insight from behavioral economics is that humans are sensitive to framing—the linguistic presentation of identical choices can alter decisions. The paper extends this concern to LLMs: if a model's action distribution (the probability it selects each choice) changes when the same game is reframed, it violates a basic consistency principle. The authors define "strategic robustness" as invariance of action distributions under payoff-preserving changes in framing. This connects to prior work showing LLMs exhibit context-sensitivity and prompt-engineering effects, but this paper formalizes robustness as a measurable property in multi-agent settings.

## Core Contributions
1. **Formal definition of strategic robustness**: Introduces a precise, testable criterion—action distributions should remain invariant when a game is presented in different but payoff-equivalent ways.
2. **Benchmark design**: Proposes "Same Game, Different Story" as a minimal benchmark using social-dilemma games with explicit business vs. friend-sharing framings.
3. **Empirical finding**: Demonstrates substantial framing effects across GPT-3.5, GPT-4, and LLaMa-2 (0.307 increase in cooperation under friend-sharing framing), with pooled strategic robustness of only 0.783.
4. **Methodological contribution**: Argues that strategic robustness and strategic competence (ability to play well) are orthogonal concerns and should be evaluated separately.

## Connections to Other Work
This work relates to broader concerns about [[alignment]] and reliability of LLM agents in strategic contexts. It echoes findings on [[prompt-engineering]] sensitivity and the brittleness of language model outputs. The paper also connects to classical game-theoretic analysis of rationality and consistency—specifically, the principle that a rational agent's choices should depend only on payoff structures, not presentation. Prior work on LLM behavior in social dilemmas (the source studies it analyzes) studied *competence* (whether models cooperate), but this paper isolates a distinct failure mode: *inconsistency* across framings, independent of whether cooperation is "good."

## Practical Applications
1. **Deploying LLM agents in markets or negotiations**: If an agent's behavior changes based on how a deal is pitched, it becomes unreliable and exploitable by adversaries who can manipulate framing.
2. **Judicial or policy recommendation systems**: An LLM that recommends different outcomes for materially identical cases presented with different narratives raises fairness and accountability concerns.
3. **Multi-agent coordination**: When LLMs must collaborate with humans or other systems in repeated interactions, inconsistency erodes trust and breaks equilibrium-based reasoning.
4. **Automated trading or resource allocation**: Framing-induced shifts in agent behavior could lead to unintended volatility or gaming.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Strategic Robustness Testing Platform**: A service that automatically generates payoff-equivalent reframings of strategic scenarios and benchmarks LLMs (or human teams) on consistency; sell to enterprises deploying autonomous agents.
2. **Adversarial Framing Generator**: A tool that audits LLM agents by systematically searching for framings that exploit behavioral inconsistencies—position as a red-team service for AI safety.
3. **Game-Theoretic Contract Verifier**: A product that validates negotiation and contract terms by checking whether an LLM agent's behavior is consistent across linguistically different (but payoff-equivalent) presentations; target legal tech and procurement.
4. **Multi-Agent Robustness Certification**: Offer certification or "robustness scores" for LLM agents used in competitive or collaborative settings (e.g., marketplace algorithms, supply chain).
5. **Framing-Aware Prompt Optimizer**: A tool that learns to reframe strategic problems in ways that push LLM agents toward robust (frame-invariant) decisions, targeting teams building reliable autonomous negotiators.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Data reconstruction**: Trial-level data were unavailable; estimates were reconstructed from published figures (approximate counts), making this illustrative rather than a rigorous replication. Results are therefore suggestive, not definitive.
2. **Limited game set**: Only four social-dilemma games tested (24 model-game-context cells total). Generalization to other game classes (competitive, mixed-motive, etc.) is unknown.
3. **Framing scope**: Only business vs. friend-sharing framings explored. Other dimensions of framing (e.g., gain vs. loss, temporal framing, authority framing) untested.
4. **No mechanism analysis**: The paper does not explain *why* framing changes behavior—whether it is tokenization artifacts, learned associations, or something deeper remains unclear.
5. **Static evaluation**: Does not test whether robustness improves with better prompting, fine-tuning, or few-shot examples.
6. **Assumption of payoff-equivalence**: Assumes the two framings truly preserve payoffs and incentives from the model's perspective; but if the model interprets "friend" and "business" contexts as semantically distinct games, the equivalence assumption may not hold.

## Open Questions
1. **Causal mechanisms**: What linguistic or semantic features drive framing effects in LLM agents? Is it learned associations, in-context learning, or something in the attention mechanism?
2. **Scaling with model size and alignment**: Does strategic robustness improve with larger, better-aligned models? How does [[RLHF]] or other alignment techniques affect robustness?
3. **Diversity of framings**: How many linguistically independent framings of a single game would an LLM need to see before it achieves invariance? Is there a training-based solution?
4. **Combination with other payoff structures**: Do results generalize to zero-sum games, bargaining, signaling games, or other strategic settings beyond social dilemmas?
5. **Human baseline**: How do human subjects perform on the same "Same Game, Different Story" benchmark? Is this a uniquely LLM pathology or a feature of bounded rationality more broadly?
6. **Interactive vs. single-shot robustness**: In repeated interactions with adaptive opponents, does framing inconsistency compound into larger behavioral failures?

## Key Takeaway
LLM agents shift their strategic choices based on narrative framing even when payoffs are identical, signaling a robustness gap that should be tested separately from strategic competence.
