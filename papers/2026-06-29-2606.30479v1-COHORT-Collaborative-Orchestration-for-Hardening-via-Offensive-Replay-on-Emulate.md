# COHORT: Collaborative Orchestration for Hardening via Offensive Replay on Emulated Topologies

**Authors:** Chen Frydman, Aviram Zilberman, Rubin Krief, Abed Showgan, Andres Murillo et al.
**Published:** 2026-06-29 | **arXiv:** [2606.30479v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30479v1)

## Summary
COHORT automates the enterprise cybersecurity workflow of deriving, implementing, and validating network mitigations against real adversaries. It uses a role-decomposed multi-agent LLM system to generate mitigation candidates as vendor commands, then evaluates them on a high-fidelity network emulator by replaying the original attack—achieving 4.4× better results than single-agent baselines while preserving legitimate connectivity.

## Theoretical Background
This work sits at the intersection of three areas: **multi-agent orchestration** (using specialized agents with defined roles to decompose complex tasks), **LLM-driven automation** (leveraging large language models to generate executable configurations), and **cyber resilience validation** (verifying that defenses work against real threat patterns). Prior work in configuration generation for networks has relied on simulation environments, reward signals, or expert judgment rather than authentic adversarial replay. The key insight is that true validation requires re-executing the actual attack on the proposed mitigation to measure effectiveness in a paired comparison—mimicking how human analysts would verify a fix.

## Core Contributions
1. **First end-to-end automated framework** for the complete mitigation workflow (generation → implementation → validation) that produces deployable vendor commands rather than abstract policies.
2. **Role-decomposed multi-agent LLM workflow** where agents take on specific personas (proposer, implementer, critic) to improve quality over monolithic approaches.
3. **Offensive replay as evaluation metric**: validates mitigations by re-running the original attack on the defended network, rather than relying on proxies like reward functions or expert judgment.
4. **Dual-check validation**: connectivity-regression testing (LAN ping, HTTP probes) ensures mitigations don't break legitimate traffic, and cumulative stacking surfaces compound effects.
5. **High-fidelity emulation**: runs on GNS3 with real vendor firmware (firewalls, switches, routers), bridging the gap between simulation and production.
6. **Empirical demonstration**: 46.7% of generated mitigations both disrupt the attack and preserve connectivity across three topologies and four real attack scenarios.

## Connections to Other Work
This builds on trends in [[reinforcement-learning]] for network configuration, but replaces reward signals with ground-truth adversarial outcomes—a more robust validation paradigm. It extends prior work on LLM-driven cybersecurity tooling (e.g., code generation for security patches) by enforcing role decomposition and critique loops. The multi-agent choreography resembles work in [[multi-agent-systems]] for planning and consensus, but applied to security operations. Related to [[alignment]] insofar as the system must align generated mitigations with both security intent (disrupting attacks) and operational constraints (preserving connectivity).

## Practical Applications
1. **Enterprise SOC automation**: Security Operations Centers can feed observed attack patterns into COHORT to auto-generate and test firewall/router rule changes, reducing expert analyst bottlenecks.
2. **Rapid incident response**: When a novel attack is detected, COHORT can propose and validate mitigations in hours rather than weeks, compressing the mean time to remediate (MTTR).
3. **Policy compliance & governance**: Generated mitigations are deployable vendor commands with full audit trails, simplifying compliance reviews and change management.
4. **Resilience testing for MSPs**: Managed Service Providers can offer proactive mitigation generation and testing as a service to clients without human expert scaling.
5. **Network segmentation hardening**: Automatically propose and validate micro-segmentation rules (VLANs, ACLs) to contain lateral movement, with quantified impact on real attacks.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Mitigation-as-a-Service (MaaS) platform**: SaaS offering that ingests observed attack metadata (logs, alerts) and delivers ranked, validated, deployable mitigation configs; subscription model tied to topology size and update frequency.
2. **LLM-powered SOC co-pilot**: Desktop/cloud tool that sits in the analyst's workflow, suggesting validated mitigations in real-time as incidents are triaged, with one-click deployment.
3. **Vendor-agnostic configuration hardening engine**: White-label backend for security hardware vendors (Palo Alto, Cisco, Fortinet) to embed automated mitigation generation in their platforms.
4. **Incident replay & validation marketplace**: A shared repository where enterprises upload sanitized attack artifacts and replay topologies; crowdsourced validation of community-proposed mitigations.
5. **Compliance & risk scoring tool**: Generates mitigations, scores them not just on attack disruption but on compliance impact (e.g., PCI-DSS, HIPAA rule violation risk), helping orgs balance security and business rules.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Emulation fidelity gap**: GNS3 with real firmware is much higher fidelity than pure simulation, but still may not capture all production complexities (hardware latency, specific traffic patterns, third-party integrations).
2. **Attack scenario coverage**: The framework's effectiveness depends on the quality and completeness of the replay attack—it cannot generate defenses against attack types not represented in observed logs.
3. **Scalability to large networks**: Emulating complex enterprise topologies with thousands of nodes and segments may become computationally expensive; unclear how well the approach scales beyond the three test topologies.
4. **LLM reliability & hallucination**: While critique loops help, the system still depends on LLMs generating syntactically correct vendor commands; complex proprietary CLI syntax or rare device types may confuse the model.
5. **Configuration drift & environmental variance**: The emulator snapshot is static; real networks have constant change (patches, new devices, traffic shifts), so a validated mitigation may not generalize.
6. **False negatives in replay**: An attack may fail in the emulated network due to minor simulation gaps (timing, protocol subtleties) that don't exist in production, creating false confidence in mitigations.

## Open Questions
1. How does COHORT perform on zero-day or adversarial attacks not seen during training of the LLM agents? Does the approach degrade gracefully?
2. Can the framework extend to multi-stage attacks requiring coordination across devices, or is it limited to single-device or single-link mitigations?
3. What is the computational cost (time, memory) of offensive replay for large topologies, and is there a practical limit to network size?
4. How sensitive is the 46.7% success rate to the quality of attack logs and the realism of the GNS3 firmware images used? Would actual production network data change the results?
5. Can mitigations validated on one topology generalize to different network architectures (e.g., cloud-native vs. on-prem), or does each topology require re-validation?
6. How do the role-decomposed agent prompts need to evolve to handle new attack classes or vendor devices not seen during development?

## Key Takeaway
COHORT is the first fully automated framework to generate and validate enterprise network mitigations through adversarial replay on high-fidelity emulators, replacing expert judgment and simulation with ground-truth attack re-execution.
