# Decision-Aligned Evaluation of Uncertainty Quantification

**Authors:** Annika Schneider, Tommy Rochussen, Joshua Stiller, Vincent Fortuin
**Published:** 2026-06-25 | **arXiv:** [2606.26990v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26990v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces *decision-alignment*, a framework for evaluating uncertainty quantification (UQ) metrics based on their actual utility in downstream decision-making tasks, rather than generic statistical properties. The authors show that standard UQ evaluation metrics (negative log-likelihood, expected calibration error) often fail to predict real decision value and can embed pathological prior beliefs about tasks. They propose prior-weighted utility metrics as a principled alternative that consistently aligns evaluation with actual decision utility.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on foundational work in:
- **Proper scoring rules** — the theoretical basis for evaluating probabilistic forecasts, ensuring incentive compatibility
- **Calibration literature** — extensive prior work on measuring and improving alignment between predicted and empirical uncertainty
- **Decision theory** — the principle that evaluation metrics should ultimately measure utility relative to downstream applications, not just statistical fit
- **Bayesian inference** — implicit modeling of prior beliefs encoded in uncertainty metrics

The key insight is that generic UQ metrics optimize statistical properties that may be orthogonal to actual decision utility.

## Core Contributions
1. **Decision-alignment framework** — a formal criterion distinguishing which evaluation metrics meaningfully predict downstream decision utility
2. **Empirical demonstration of misalignment** — systematic evidence that NLL, ECE, and other standard metrics fail to correlate with realized decision value across benchmark tasks
3. **Prior-weighted utility metrics** — a novel class of proper scoring rules that encode task-specific decision structures and consistently recover alignment
4. **Real-world case studies** — validation showing the framework's practical relevance beyond synthetic benchmarks

The novelty lies not in individual components but in the systematic diagnosis and remedy of a widespread evaluation gap in UQ practice.

## Connections to Other Work
This work intersects with:
- **[[Calibration]]** literature (Guo et al., Niculescu-Mizil & Caruana) — extending beyond statistical calibration toward decision relevance
- **[[Proper scoring rules]]** — recovering and extending classical theory (Brier, log-loss) with task-awareness
- **Risk-sensitive optimization** — related concern that statistical metrics may not capture decision-maker objectives
- **Bayesian decision theory** — formal framework underlying the alignment criterion
- **Active learning & experimental design** — where uncertainty estimates directly influence downstream decisions
- **Model validation** — broader concern that evaluation protocols may miss failure modes

The paper importantly critiques a foundational assumption in modern ML: that "better uncertainty" universally means better calibration by conventional metrics.

## Practical Applications
1. **Medical diagnosis systems** — where false negatives and false positives have asymmetric costs; decision-aligned metrics ensure calibration matches clinical consequences
2. **Autonomous vehicle safety** — uncertainty in object detection or trajectory prediction must align with actual safety-relevant decision thresholds, not generic statistical properties
3. **Financial risk modeling** — VaR and expected shortfall estimation, where prior beliefs about tail behavior encoded in metrics directly affect portfolio decisions
4. **Fraud detection** — asymmetric costs of false positives (customer friction) vs. false negatives (fraud loss) require decision-aligned thresholds
5. **Clinical trial monitoring** — early stopping rules depend on uncertainty estimates; misaligned metrics can lead to incorrect stopping decisions with large consequences

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **UQ Auditing Platform** — SaaS tool that automatically evaluates whether an organization's uncertainty quantification metrics are decision-aligned for their specific loss functions; generates audit reports and recommends metric adjustments
2. **Industry-Specific UQ Libraries** — pre-calibrated, decision-aligned metrics for healthcare, finance, and autonomous systems, bundled with best practices for each domain
3. **Decision-Aware Model Evaluation Dashboard** — real-time monitoring system that tracks not just model accuracy but alignment between predicted uncertainty and realized decision utility; alerts when drift occurs
4. **Consulting Service** — help ML teams identify and fix misalignment in their existing evaluation protocols, with focus on high-stakes domains (medical, autonomous, financial)
5. **Automated Hyperparameter Optimization for UQ** — tool that jointly optimizes model + uncertainty estimation parameters to maximize decision-alignment rather than generic metrics

## Technical Limitations
1. **Task specification burden** — the framework requires explicit specification of downstream decision problems and loss functions; in practice, these may be contested, time-varying, or unknown
2. **Computational cost** — evaluating decision-aligned metrics may require simulating downstream decisions repeatedly; scalability to high-dimensional decision spaces unclear
3. **Multiple stakeholders** — when different users have conflicting utilities (e.g., patient vs. insurer in healthcare), no single metric can be universally decision-aligned
4. **Distribution shift** — decision-alignment is evaluated on historical tasks; utility functions and decision contexts may change after deployment
5. **Limited theoretical guarantees** — the paper is primarily empirical; theoretical characterization of when and why misalignment occurs is underdeveloped
6. **Benchmark realism** — synthetic benchmarks may not capture the full complexity of real-world decision environments (multi-stage decisions, feedback loops, adversarial behavior)

## Open Questions
1. **Theoretical characterization** — Under what conditions do generic metrics provably fail to be decision-aligned? Can we derive necessary and sufficient conditions?
2. **Multi-stakeholder settings** — How should we design evaluation metrics when different downstream users have conflicting utility functions?
3. **Online adaptation** — How can decision-aligned metrics be updated when the decision context itself is non-stationary?
4. **Reverse engineering** — Given only historical data on decisions and outcomes (without explicit utility specification), can we infer the decision-aligned metric?
5. **Scalability to complex decisions** — How do these ideas extend to sequential decision-making, hierarchical planning, or multi-agent settings?
6. **Interaction with model training** — Should model training objectives themselves be decision-aligned, or is evaluation-time alignment sufficient?
7. **Fairness implications** — How do decision-aligned metrics interact with fairness constraints? Can decision-alignment mask discriminatory utility functions?

## Key Takeaway
Standard uncertainty quantification metrics often fail to predict actual decision utility because they optimize for statistical properties divorced from downstream task structure — decision-aligned evaluation closes this critical gap.
