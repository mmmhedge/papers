# LLMs and Agentic AI Systems for Smart Grids: A Tutorial on Architectures and Applications

**Authors:** Daniela Rojas, Abdulwahab Albassam, Aidan G. Leung, Jett Ngo, Ryan Luo et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-20 | **arXiv:** [2607.18147v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18147v1)

## Summary
This paper shows how to safely deploy large language models (LLMs) and agentic AI systems in power grid management by grounding their outputs in trusted numerical solvers rather than relying on LLM reasoning alone. The key insight is that LLMs excel at planning, orchestration, and explanation, but should hand off actual computation to verified tools—a principle demonstrated across wind forecasting, EV charging, and grid diagnostics with significant improvements in correctness and safety.

## Theoretical Background
LLMs are neural networks trained on vast text corpora to predict sequences of tokens; they have recently been extended beyond text generation to orchestrate multi-step workflows by calling external tools (retrieval, computation, verification). Agentic AI systems layer planning and tool-use on top of LLMs—they break problems into steps, decide which tools to invoke, and synthesize results. Smart grids (power systems) require real-time optimization and forecasting under physical constraints; traditional approaches rely on specialized solvers like CVXPY (convex optimization) and domain-specific algorithms. The core tension is that LLMs can produce outputs that sound plausible but violate physical laws or optimization constraints—a problem the paper addresses through a "solver-grounded" design principle that treats the LLM as an orchestrator, not a calculator.

## Core Contributions
1. **Solver-grounded design principle**: numerical results are only reported when they come from a trusted tool and pass explicit verification—a simple but powerful guard against LLM hallucination in technical domains.
2. **Empirical comparison across four domain tasks** (wind forecasting, EV charging scheduling, power flow analysis, contingency diagnosis) showing LLM-only baselines fail catastrophically (producing infeasible results) while agentic+solver variants reproduce optimal solutions.
3. **Four-group evaluation framework** that holistically assesses agentic systems: task utility, correctness, faithfulness/safe failure modes, and computational cost—addressing the lack of unified evaluation protocols in the literature.
4. **Two concrete agentic systems**: EVAgent (7.5–9.5× reduction in unmet energy vs. LLM-only) and GridDebugAgent (repairs 17/39 contingencies, 52.3% violation reduction).
5. **Clear division of labor insight**: LLMs orchestrate, retrieve, and explain; solvers compute; verification gates decide what to report—a template for safe LLM deployment in other technical domains.

## Practical Applications
1. **Utility dispatch and control rooms**: operators query an agentic system for forecasts, scheduling recommendations, and fault diagnosis; the system explains decisions and reports only verified results, improving operator confidence and compliance.
2. **Autonomous microgrid management**: in island mode or with high renewables, the agentic system dynamically forecasts wind/solar, optimizes storage and load, and detects contingencies—reducing manual intervention and human error.
3. **Contingency analysis at scale**: utilities need rapid "what-if" analysis when lines fail; an agentic system can retrieve system topology, call power-flow solvers, diagnose violations, and recommend corrective actions in seconds.
4. **EV charging network coordination**: at scale (thousands of EVs), an agentic system schedules charging to avoid grid stress, minimizing cost and emissions while respecting physical constraints—much faster than operators or naive heuristics.
5. **Grid resilience and planning**: long-term resource planning for renewables, storage, and transmission; the agentic system retrieves historical data, runs optimization scenarios, and generates natural-language summaries for regulators and executives.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Solver availability and latency**: the approach requires access to trusted solvers (CVXPY, power-flow tools) and assumes they are fast enough; if solving takes minutes, real-time grid control is infeasible. Multi-region grids or very large optimization problems may hit computational limits.
2. **LLM brittleness in prompt design**: the prompting strategies and agentic workflows are hand-crafted for specific tasks; generalization to new grid topologies, regulatory requirements, or problem types is unclear. Requires domain expertise to update.
3. **Incomplete problem coverage**: case studies focus on four tasks; behavior on out-of-distribution problems (novel contingencies, equipment types, renewable sources) is unknown. Edge cases (conflicting constraints, degenerate optimization) may cause silent failures.
4. **Verification gate design**: the paper assumes verification rules (e.g., "power balance must hold") are easy to specify; in practice, some physical constraints are complex, implicit, or debated. Hard to write a foolproof verification oracle.
5. **Explainability limits**: while agentic systems can report *which* tool was called, explaining *why* the solver chose a particular output (e.g., a counterintuitive power routing) remains difficult. LLM explanations may still confabulate.
6. **Integration with legacy systems**: most utilities rely on decades-old SCADA and EMS; retrofitting an LLM+agentic layer requires careful API design and cybersecurity vetting. Regulatory approval is slow.

## Open Questions
1. **How does the approach scale to grid-wide, multi-timescale problems** (seconds to days)? Can a single agentic system coordinate across real-time control, unit commitment, and transmission planning?
2. **What happens when solvers disagree or are infeasible?** The paper assumes solvers always return a result; how should the agentic system respond when optimization fails or constraints are contradictory?
3. **Can the agentic system learn from past decisions** (e.g., which recommendations actually worked) to improve future prompts and tool selection, or does it require manual retraining?
4. **How sensitive is performance to the quality of the LLM?** Would a smaller, cheaper model degrade significantly, or is the solver grounding enough to make cheapness viable?
5. **Can this framework extend to security-critical scenarios** (e.g., intentional grid attacks, adversarial inputs)? Are verification gates robust to adversarial prompts or data poisoning?
6. **How do we formally verify that the agentic system never reports an infeasible result**, even under edge cases or novel scenarios? Is testing sufficient, or do we need formal methods?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs are powerful coordinators but terrible calculators; grounding their outputs in verified solvers eliminates hallucination in power grids.
