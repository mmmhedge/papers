# Delay-Aware Active Triangulation with Uncertainty-Driven Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning for Counter-UAS

**Authors:** Seungwook Lee, David Hyunchul Shim
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.05957v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05957v1)

## Summary
This paper tackles 3D localization of aerial targets (drones) using multiple coordinated camera-equipped agents that must communicate and act despite communication delays. The key innovation is a reinforcement learning framework that explicitly accounts for latency (using Age-of-Information tracking) and uncertainty sources, achieving 0.547 m localization accuracy in simulation. This matters because real-world Counter-UAS systems must operate in noisy, delayed environments—ignoring these factors degrades performance dramatically.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on three foundations: (1) **Multi-agent reinforcement learning**, particularly decentralized partially observable Markov decision processes (Dec-POMDPs), where agents have incomplete information and must coordinate without centralized control; (2) **Active vision geometry**, where mobile observers with controllable cameras jointly triangulate 3D target positions—precision depends on the baseline between observers and observation angles; (3) **Information-theoretic delay modeling**, using Age-of-Information (AoI)—a metric measuring how stale sensor data is—to quantify cumulative latency from detection, transmission, and decision propagation. Prior work assumed instantaneous state feedback, which is unrealistic; this paper makes delay an explicit state variable.

## Core Contributions
1. **Dec-POMDP with AoI augmentation**: Reformulates the target localization problem as a decentralized POMDP where observation timestamps and communication lags are tracked. AoI improves triangulation validity by 10.6 percentage points, demonstrating that explicit delay awareness matters.

2. **Perception-consistent reward design**: Compares two reward schemes trained on identical noisy observations: one optimizes for actual measurement error (perception-consistent), the other for clean privileged ground-truth (idealized). The perception-consistent policy achieves 0.547 m RMSE vs. 0.633 m and 27% fewer track losses, showing that the reward function should match the actual decision-making constraints.

3. **Multi-source covariance propagation**: Derives analytical uncertainty propagation through pixel noise, pose error, gimbal calibration, and camera intrinsics. Restricting to angular noise alone causes 2.8× RMSE degradation, proving that practitioners must account for all noise sources.

4. **Recurrent memory necessity**: Demonstrates that recurrent policies (trained with MAPPO) achieve 78.1% triangulation validity vs. 0.7% for MLPs, proving memory is essential for handling latency and partial observability.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[Reinforcement Learning|MARL]] (particularly [[MAPPO]], a policy-gradient algorithm for multi-agent coordination) and **active perception** / **sensor fusion**. It extends prior active triangulation work by incorporating realistic constraints from communication networks (similar to [[Age-of-Information]] metrics in network control). The uncertainty propagation approach aligns with classical Kalman filtering and covariance analysis but applies it to RL policy design. Unlike centralized planning methods, the Dec-POMDP formulation respects decentralized decision-making—each agent sees only its own observations and communicated state estimates.

## Practical Applications
1. **Counter-UAS defense systems**: Real airports and critical infrastructure deploy radar + camera networks to detect and localize hostile drones; this framework improves both localization accuracy and robustness to communication delays.

2. **Multi-robot surveillance networks**: Border patrols, large facility monitoring, and search-and-rescue operations where teams of UAVs must autonomously triangulate targets with constrained communication bandwidth.

3. **Collaborative 3D reconstruction**: Construction or archaeological teams using drones to jointly reconstruct structures; delay-aware coordination prevents redundant captures.

4. **Swarm robotics coordination**: Decentralized control for robot swarms performing visual inspection or object tracking in GPS-denied environments.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Simulation-only validation**: Results are from MAPPO trained in 4096 parallel sim environments. Sim-to-real transfer is not addressed—real sensor delays, occlusions, and non-linear gimbal dynamics may break assumptions.

2. **Scalability unknown**: Tests appear limited to ~4 agents. Decentralized communication bandwidth and partial observability likely become severe bottlenecks with 10+ agents.

3. **Target dynamics**: The paper does not specify how target motion (evasive, hovering, or ballistic) is handled. Faster-moving targets may invalidate the delay compensation.

4. **Communication model**: Assumes perfect message delivery; packet loss, dropped frames, or Byzantine agents are not modeled.

5. **Geometric degeneracies**: No discussion of what happens when agents are collinear or all tracking the same bearing—triangulation becomes ill-conditioned regardless of RL.

6. **Computational cost**: MAPPO training in 4096 parallel environments requires substantial compute; real-time deployment on embedded drones is unclear.

## Open Questions
1. **Sim-to-real transfer**: How much domain randomization and fine-tuning is needed to deploy this on real aerial platforms? Does the controller degrade gracefully under unexpected delays or sensor faults?

2. **Scalability & distributed learning**: Can this framework extend to 8–16 agents with limited communication bandwidth? What are the information-theoretic limits?

3. **Adversarial targets**: How does the controller perform against targets that actively evade or employ jamming? Is there a game-theoretic equilibrium?

4. **Hybrid human-AI**: Can a human operator override or adjust the agents' plan in real-time? How does latency in the human loop interact with AoI tracking?

5. **Comparison with classical methods**: How does MAPPO compare to optimal centralized planning (offline) or distributed consensus-based triangulation (e.g., distributed Kalman filters)?

6. **Interpretability**: What decision rules does the recurrent policy actually learn? Can we extract human-understandable heuristics?

## Key Takeaway
Explicitly modeling communication delay (via Age-of-Information) and aligning reward functions with actual perception constraints (rather than idealized ground-truth) is essential for multi-agent coordination in real-world systems, improving both accuracy and robustness.
