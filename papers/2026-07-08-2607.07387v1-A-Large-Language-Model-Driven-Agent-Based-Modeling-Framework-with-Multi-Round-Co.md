# A Large Language Model-Driven Agent-Based Modeling Framework with Multi-Round Communication for Simulating Vaccine Opinion Dynamics

**Authors:** Bo Zhang, Na Jiang
**Published:** 2026-07-08 | **arXiv:** [2607.07387v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07387v1)

## Summary
This paper embeds a large language model (Qwen3-8B) into agent-based simulations to model how people form and change opinions about vaccines. The key insight is that different cognitive modules — like memory and prompt diversity — have measurably different effects on emergent group-level opinion patterns, and the framework successfully reproduces known non-linear social influence effects seen in real data.

## Theoretical Background
Agent-based modeling (ABM) is a simulation technique where you create computational agents with heterogeneous traits and social networks, then let them interact to see what macro-level patterns emerge. Large language models are neural networks trained on vast text that can generate human-like responses and reasoning. This work sits at the intersection: instead of hard-coded decision rules, agents use an LLM to reason through decisions given their profiles, memories, and social context. Prior work in computational social science has used simpler models for opinion dynamics; this paper asks whether LLM-driven cognition can better capture the mechanisms that drive real behavioral shifts in populations.

## Core Contributions
1. **Framework design**: A novel integration of LLMs into ABM with explicit cognitive modules (memory, prompt diversity) that can be toggled on/off to isolate causal effects.
2. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates that the framework reproduces non-linear social influence patterns known from empirical research, achieving what ABM researchers call "level 3 validation" (matching observed dynamics, not just summary statistics).
3. **Mechanistic insight**: Shows that memory and prompt diversity have *opposite* effects on opinion consensus — a finding that suggests LLM cognition models can reveal how information processing shapes collective outcomes.

## Connections to Other Work
This bridges [[reinforcement-learning]] (agents learning over time) and [[mechanistic-interpretability]] (understanding what drives agent decisions). It relates to work on [[RLHF]] and alignment in that it explores how different "cognitive prompting" strategies change agent behavior. The agent-based modeling approach contrasts with purely statistical or equation-based models of opinion dynamics (e.g., bounded confidence models, voter models) by introducing realistic heterogeneity and language-driven reasoning.

## Practical Applications
1. **Public health communication**: Simulating vaccine hesitancy dynamics to test messaging strategies before large campaigns.
2. **Policy stress-testing**: Understanding how different information environments (social media algorithms, news sources) shape vaccination uptake in populations.
3. **Misinformation resilience**: Testing which cognitive interventions (fact-checking, trusted messengers) most effectively counter vaccine skepticism at scale.
4. **Epidemiological forecasting**: Coupling opinion dynamics with disease transmission models to predict outbreak scenarios under different social conditions.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Opinion Dynamics Simulation Platform**: SaaS tool for public health agencies to run scenario analyses on vaccine, drug, or health policy adoption — train on organization-specific data.
2. **Targeted Communication Optimizer**: AI system that uses the framework to recommend messaging strategies and messenger selection for maximizing vaccination rates in specific demographic segments.
3. **Misinformation Early Warning System**: Real-time dashboard that simulates how false claims spread given current social network structures and cognitive biases; alerts health communicators to emerging narratives.
4. **Corporate Social Listening + Simulation**: Tool for brands/NGOs to model opinion shifts around their policies (e.g., climate, diversity) and stress-test messaging before launch.
5. **Academic ABM-as-a-Service**: Hosted platform letting social scientists build custom LLM-agent models without heavy infrastructure; focus on accessibility and replicability.

## Technical Limitations
1. **LLM consistency & bias**: Qwen3-8B's outputs are probabilistic; identical agent profiles may give different responses, and the model may encode biases from training data that distort vaccine opinion dynamics.
2. **Prompt engineering sensitivity**: Results likely depend heavily on how prompts are worded; unclear how robust findings are to small prompt changes.
3. **Computational cost**: Running LLM inference for many agents over many time steps is expensive; scalability to very large populations unclear.
4. **Social network realism**: Real social networks have temporal dynamics, homophily, and clustering the paper may not fully capture.
5. **Validation scope**: Only tested on vaccination; generalization to other opinion domains (political, religious) unproven.
6. **Ground truth gap**: "Level 3 validation" matches observed patterns but doesn't prove the LLM actually captures human cognitive mechanisms — could be fitting noise.

## Open Questions
1. How sensitive are results to prompt engineering and LLM choice? Does a larger or smaller model change emergent dynamics?
2. Can you disentangle which LLM capabilities (reasoning, memory, language understanding) actually drive opinion changes, vs. which are noise?
3. How do temporal effects play in? If agents update their memories, do long-run dynamics stabilize or oscillate in realistic ways?
4. Can the framework predict real-world vaccination rate changes in specific communities, or is it primarily explanatory?
5. What happens when you add adversarial agents (coordinated misinformation spreaders) — does the framework's realism hold?

## Key Takeaway
LLM-driven agents can reproduce empirically observed opinion dynamics in ways simpler models cannot, suggesting that language-based reasoning may be a better proxy for human collective decision-making than hard-coded rules.
