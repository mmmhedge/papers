# Leveraging unlabelled data for generalizable neural population decoding

**Authors:** Ximeng Mao, Nanda H. Krishna, Avery Hee-Woon Ryoo, Matthew G. Perich, Guillaume Lajoie
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.14086v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14086v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces MOJO, a training framework that combines self-supervised learning (learning from unlabeled data) and supervised learning to decode neural activity from brain recordings. The key innovation is using masked autoencoding — a technique where parts of neural spike patterns are hidden and the model learns to predict them — alongside traditional labeled behavioral tasks. This approach works better than supervised-only methods, especially when labeled data is scarce, and generalizes across different brain regions, animal species, and even different types of neural recordings (spikes vs. continuous signals).

## Theoretical Background
Neural decoding aims to predict behavior or stimulus from recorded brain activity. Recent advances use spike tokenization — converting continuous spike times into discrete tokens, similar to how language models process words — enabling transfer learning across recording sessions. The paper builds on two complementary learning paradigms: supervised learning (which requires paired neural and behavioral labels) and self-supervised learning (SSL), particularly masked autoencoding — a technique borrowed from language models (e.g., [[BERT]]) where the model reconstructs hidden parts of its input without external labels. Prior work showed spike-based tokenization improves multi-session performance, but was limited to supervised objectives. MOJO extends this by adding masked reconstruction as an auxiliary task, allowing the model to learn from unlabeled neural recordings.

## Core Contributions
1. **MOJO framework**: A joint training approach combining masked autoencoding (SSL) and supervised learning objectives for spike-tokenized neural data — the first to apply masked reconstruction at spike level for neural decoding.
2. **Label-efficient learning**: Demonstrates substantial gains in few-shot finetuning scenarios where only small amounts of labeled data from new recording sessions are available.
3. **Cross-modal generalization**: Shows the approach works beyond spike data to human electrocorticography (continuous voltage signals), bridging different neural recording modalities.
4. **Emergent interpretability**: SSL improves performance on downstream tasks (brain region classification, spike statistics prediction) without explicit optimization, suggesting learned representations are more structured and neuroscientifically meaningful.
5. **Empirical validation**: Tests on three diverse datasets (monkey reaching, multi-region mouse vision/decision-making, human speech).

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of neurotechnology and foundation models. It extends [[neuro-foundation models]] (NFMs) — general-purpose models for neural data — by showing that SSL can match or exceed their performance while using simpler, more interpretable architectures. The masked autoencoding technique parallels recent successes in [[self-supervised learning]] for vision ([[MAE]]) and language ([[BERT]]), applying that paradigm to neuroscience. It relates to broader efforts in transfer learning and domain adaptation for neural recordings, and connects to mechanistic interpretability in neuroscience by showing that jointly trained representations better capture brain structure.

## Practical Applications
1. **Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs)**: When a new patient receives a BCI implant, labeled calibration data is expensive and time-consuming. MOJO reduces calibration burden by leveraging the patient's own unlabeled recordings (during spontaneous activity) to bootstrap the decoder before formal training sessions.
2. **Closed-loop neurotechnology**: In real-time experiments (e.g., optogenetic feedback loops), unlabeled recordings between explicit behavioral blocks can improve decoder robustness without interrupting the protocol.
3. **Multi-species neuroscience**: Combining spike data from multiple animal studies (mice, monkeys, etc.) on related tasks — using SSL to extract shared structure even when labeling standards differ across labs.
4. **Clinical monitoring**: Continuous ECoG or spike monitoring in epilepsy patients generates large unlabeled datasets; MOJO could detect seizure precursors or behavioral states with less labeled annotation.
5. **Longitudinal BCI adaptation**: As a patient's neural recordings drift over weeks/months, SSL on recent unlabeled data can help the decoder adapt without retraining from scratch on new labeled sessions.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Adaptive BCI Calibration Platform**: A SaaS tool that runs MOJO-style pretraining on a patient's continuous home-monitoring neural data, automatically fine-tuning BCIs with minimal in-clinic calibration sessions — reduces patient burden and accelerates clinical deployment.
2. **Neural Data Marketplace & Transfer Learning Hub**: A federated platform where neuroscience labs upload anonymized unlabeled spike recordings; MOJO pretrained foundations are shared, labs then fine-tune on their proprietary labeled datasets — democratizes access to large pretrained models.
3. **Real-time Closed-Loop Therapy Engine**: Hardware-software system for neurostimulation (e.g., deep brain stimulation for Parkinson's) that uses MOJO to decode symptom states from unlabeled streaming data, enabling adaptive closed-loop interventions without constant recalibration.
4. **Multimodal Neural Analytics for Research**: A cloud platform ingesting spiking, LFP, fMRI, and behavioral data across species; MOJO's cross-modality capability harmonizes these signals, enabling meta-analyses across labs without standardized labeling.
5. **Rapid Functional Mapping Tool**: Software for neurosurgery or intraoperative monitoring that quickly maps eloquent cortex by pretraining on the patient's own spontaneous activity (no task required), then fine-tuning on a few probe responses.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Spike detection assumption**: The method assumes clean, reliable spike detection. In noisy recordings or during artifact, tokenization quality degrades, potentially undermining SSL benefits.
2. **Scalability of labeled data**: While SSL helps with *limited* labels, performance likely still improves with more labeled data; unclear if this asymptotes or if purely unsupervised decoding is feasible.
3. **Session/animal variability**: Generalization across different brain regions or species is shown empirically but not theoretically understood — no guarantees that shared representations will emerge for new unseen contexts.
4. **Computational cost**: Joint SSL+SL training is more expensive than SL alone; unclear if the cost-benefit trade-off is favorable for real-time BCI deployment.
5. **Biological interpretability**: While downstream tasks improve, it's unclear whether masked autoencoding recovers neuroscientifically interpretable dimensions (e.g., population-level coding principles) or merely task-predictive abstractions.
6. **Limited baseline comparisons**: No direct comparison to other semi-supervised methods (e.g., pseudo-labeling, consistency regularization) or to simpler regularization approaches.

## Open Questions
1. **Why does SSL help?** Is it primarily regularization, learning better initializations, or discovering interpretable structure? Ablations and representational analyses would clarify.
2. **How much unlabeled data is optimal?** Is there a saturation point, or does more unlabeled pretraining always improve few-shot finetuning?
3. **Cross-species transfer**: Can a model pretrained on monkey spike data directly fine-tune on mouse data? What properties of neural coding are shared vs. species-specific?
4. **Truly zero-shot decoding**: Can MOJO's pretrained encoder, with no task-specific labels, predict novel behaviors or generalize to unseen brain areas?
5. **Temporal dynamics**: Current approach may treat spikes as a bag of tokens; does the model learn temporal structure? Would explicit temporal SSL (e.g., contrastive prediction) help?
6. **Biological plausibility**: Do the learned representations align with known neuroscience (e.g., manifolds, low-rank structure)? Could this be a tool for discovering principles of neural coding?

## Key Takeaway
Self-supervised learning on unlabeled spikes improves neural decoders with scarce labels, scaling across species and recording modalities.
