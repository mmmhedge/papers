# An Explainable Agentic System for Detection of Conversational Scams with Summary-Based Memory

**Authors:** Ahmed Omar Salim Adnan, Yogananda Manjunath, Shivanjali Khare
**Published:** 2026-07-13 | **arXiv:** [2607.11707v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11707v1)

## Summary
This paper presents an AI system that detects sophisticated scams conducted over many messages (like romance scams) by analyzing entire conversations rather than isolated texts. Unlike existing systems that flag individual phishing messages, this approach tracks how scammers gradually build trust and manipulate victims over weeks or months—and it explains its decisions to users, reaching 97.8% accuracy while helping people become more confident in identifying real scams.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on phishing and spam detection, which traditionally operates at the message level using text classification. Recent advances in [[large-language-models]] and [[generative-models]] have created new risks: more convincing, contextually-aware social engineering attacks. The paper extends this by introducing conversation-level detection—treating scam detection as a sequential reasoning problem where context and temporal patterns matter. It also emphasizes explainability, drawing on interpretable AI principles to help users understand *why* a conversation is flagged as dangerous, rather than presenting a black-box prediction.

## Core Contributions
1. **Conversation-level scam detection**: Shifts from single-message classification to multi-turn dialogue analysis, capturing the gradual trust-building and manipulation tactics unique to long-term scams.
2. **Summary-based memory mechanism**: Uses compressed summaries of conversation history to maintain context while managing computational load—a practical solution for analyzing long interactions.
3. **ConScamBench-278 benchmark**: First public dataset for conversational scam detection with eight scam types, enabling reproducible research and future model evaluation.
4. **Explainability-first design**: System provides reasoning for its decisions, validated through user studies showing increased trust and confidence (all improvements at p < 0.001).
5. **Human-centered validation**: Two user studies (N=100, N=45) confirm users struggle with scam detection and that the system meaningfully improves their judgment.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to broader work on [[alignment]] and trustworthy AI—users need to understand and trust safety systems. It extends single-message [[nlp]]-based classification (standard phishing filters) to temporal reasoning similar to [[agents]] that maintain state across interactions. The explainability component echoes interpretability research, ensuring humans remain in the loop. The work also touches multi-turn dialogue understanding, relevant to conversational [[large-language-models]] safety and adversarial robustness.

## Practical Applications
- **Email/messaging platform integration**: Slack, Gmail, WhatsApp, and other messaging apps can embed this as a warning layer for users in suspicious conversations.
- **Online dating platform protection**: Tinder, Bumble, Hinge, and similar services can flag potential romance scams before financial requests escalate.
- **Customer support security**: Banks and financial institutions can monitor customer service chats for impersonation or social engineering attempts.
- **Victim support and law enforcement**: Help police and support organizations identify and disrupt active scam networks by analyzing conversation patterns.
- **Enterprise threat detection**: Organizations can monitor internal messaging (Slack, Teams) to catch impersonation or credential-harvesting attempts.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **ConScamShield**: A browser extension and mobile app that analyzes conversations in real time across messaging platforms, highlighting suspicious patterns with explanations before users send money or share data.
2. **TrustGuard for Dating Apps**: White-label API for dating platforms to embed scam detection; flags and educates users about romance scams during conversation.
3. **CyberSense for SMEs**: Enterprise tool monitoring internal messaging (Slack, Teams, email) for sophisticated business email compromise and impersonation, with audit trails for compliance.
4. **ScamPrevent Analytics**: B2B SaaS for financial institutions, telcos, and payment platforms providing real-time conversation analysis and quarterly scam trend reports to support compliance and customer protection.
5. **Guardian AI for Seniors**: Mobile app specifically designed for older adults, with larger UI, simplified explanations, and family-sharing features to help vulnerable populations recognize and report scams.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Benchmark scale**: ConScamBench-278 is modest in size; broader coverage across scam types, languages, and platforms is needed for generalization.
2. **Summary-based memory trade-off**: Compressing long conversations into summaries may lose fine-grained linguistic markers of deception; the paper doesn't deeply explore this loss.
3. **User study design**: Pre/post comparison is uncontrolled and self-reported; no randomized control group or objective behavioral metrics beyond self-report.
4. **Scam evolution**: Scammers adapt quickly to detection systems; the paper doesn't address robustness to adversarial scam strategies designed to evade the detector.
5. **Cross-domain generalization**: Unclear how well the system transfers to new scam types, languages, or platforms not represented in training data.
6. **Latency and inference cost**: Real-time conversation analysis on messaging platforms at scale may require computational optimization not discussed.

## Open Questions
1. How does the system perform against adversarially-crafted conversations designed specifically to fool it?
2. What linguistic and behavioral features does the summary mechanism capture or lose, and how does this affect detection accuracy?
3. Can the explainability module be personalized to different user populations (age, literacy, prior scam exposure) to improve trust?
4. How does the system generalize to non-English scams and low-resource languages?
5. What is the false-positive rate in truly benign but emotionally-manipulative conversations (e.g., awkward but honest dating)?
6. Can the conversation summarization approach scale to very long interactions (months-long scams) without memory or latency degradation?
7. How do scammers adapt once they learn the system exists, and what is the adversarial robustness of the detection mechanism?

## Key Takeaway
An AI system detects long-running scams by analyzing full conversations with explainable reasoning, outperforming message-level detection and boosting user confidence.
