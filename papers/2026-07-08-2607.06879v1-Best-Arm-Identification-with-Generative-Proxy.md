# Best-Arm Identification with Generative Proxy

**Authors:** Tianyi Ma, Hanzhang Qin, Ruihao Zhu, Jierui Zuo
**Published:** 2026-07-08 | **arXiv:** [2607.06879v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06879v1)

## Summary
This paper studies how to efficiently identify the best option among many alternatives when getting true rewards is expensive, but cheap proxy predictions (like LLM outputs) are available. The key insight is that by learning the correlation between proxy and reward online, a standard control-variate adjustment lets you reduce the number of costly samples needed—but you must carefully estimate residual variance without breaking the statistical guarantees. The authors propose PROBE, an algorithm that maintains valid confidence bounds and achieves near-oracle sample complexity in experiments ranging from synthetic problems to real auto-loan pricing.

## Theoretical Background
Best-arm identification is a foundational problem in decision-making under uncertainty: given a set of alternatives with unknown reward distributions, how many samples do you need to confidently pick the best one? This differs from regret minimization (cumulative reward) by focusing on confidence-dependent efficiency rather than exploration-exploitation tradeoffs. Control-variate adjustment is a classical variance-reduction technique in statistics: if you have a correlated auxiliary variable, you can subtract its prediction to reduce variance of your main estimate. The paper's heteroscedastic identification framework extends these ideas to cases where the noise level itself varies across samples—a realistic but harder setting than standard homoscedastic (constant-variance) problems. The theoretical lens is fixed-confidence PAC (probably approximately correct) bounds: an algorithm is δ-PAC if it identifies the best arm with confidence 1−δ using a number of samples that scales gracefully with problem hardness.

## Core Contributions
1. **Model formulation**: Formalizes best-arm identification with unknown reward-proxy correlation ρ as a problem where ρ must be learned from the same costly samples used for identification.
2. **Variance-reduction insight**: Shows that control-variate adjustment with estimated residual variance 1−ρ² can improve sample complexity, but plug-in estimation of this variance is anti-conservative (biased optimistically), risking incorrect conclusions.
3. **PROBE algorithm**: Introduces a phase-elimination approach that maintains an upper confidence certificate on residual variance using OLS fits, leveraging the chi-square distribution of OLS residuals to guarantee validity without knowing ρ.
4. **Theoretical guarantee**: Proves PROBE achieves δ-PAC with sample complexity matching the oracle (known-ρ) rate up to constant factors, plus a small calibration overhead.
5. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates on synthetic and real auto-loan data that sample savings scale with reward-proxy correlation strength, confirming theoretical predictions.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of classical sequential decision-making (best-arm identification, multi-armed bandits) and modern machine learning practice where cheap auxiliary models are abundant. It relates to [[reinforcement-learning]] in the broader sense of learning under uncertainty, but differs by asking "which is best?" rather than "how to accumulate reward?". The use of proxy/auxiliary information connects to transfer learning and meta-learning paradigms, though the focus here is more statistical—how to leverage known auxiliary means and learned correlations. The anti-conservative bias problem it identifies echoes issues in [[mechanistic-interpretability]] and [[alignment]] when trusting learned estimates without proper uncertainty quantification. The phase-elimination strategy is standard in bandit theory but the novel application is to the heteroscedastic regime where variance itself must be learned.

## Practical Applications
1. **A/B testing with cheap proxies**: E-commerce platforms (Amazon, Netflix) could use LLM-scored relevance or recommendation strength alongside small samples of true user engagement to decide which variant wins, reducing costly A/B tests.
2. **Auto-loan and credit pricing**: Financial institutions can use tabular ML models or LLMs to screen loan risk cheaply, then verify a few with actual default outcomes to confidently set price tiers.
3. **Clinical trial design**: Medical researchers could use biomarker proxies (blood tests, imaging) cheaply to narrow down promising drug candidates, then invest expensive patient outcome trials only on the best few.
4. **Ranking and recommendation systems**: Platforms can use offline recommendation scores (LLM or collaborative filtering) to quickly identify top-K items, then run expensive online A/B tests only on finalists.
5. **Hyperparameter tuning**: Machine learning practitioners could use cheap validation set proxies (e.g., holdout or synthetic data) alongside sparse true-world performance metrics to identify best configurations.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Known offline marginal means**: The algorithm assumes the marginal mean of the proxy is known (learned offline), which may not hold if proxy distributions shift between offline training and online deployment.
2. **Linearity assumption**: The control-variate model assumes a linear relationship between proxy and reward; nonlinear or regime-dependent correlations could violate this and degrade savings.
3. **Sample complexity of learning ρ**: While the additive calibration cost is constant, the multiplicative constant factor hiding in "near-oracle" may be large if ρ is close to zero or correlation is weak, reducing practical speedup.
4. **Homogeneity across arms**: The analysis assumes the reward-proxy correlation ρ is the same for all arms; arm-specific correlations would require additional samples to learn and could break the guarantee.
5. **Proxy bias**: If the proxy is biased (nonzero mean difference from reward), even if the marginal mean is corrected offline, local time-varying biases could degrade performance.
6. **Computational cost of OLS and chi-square inversion**: Maintaining confidence certificates via OLS and chi-square quantiles adds computational overhead not fully analyzed in the paper.

## Open Questions
1. **Arm-dependent correlations**: Can PROBE be extended to handle different reward-proxy correlations across arms, and what is the sample complexity cost?
2. **Nonlinear proxies**: Can the framework be adapted to nonlinear relationships (e.g., neural network mappings) while maintaining formal guarantees?
3. **Multiple proxies**: How should the algorithm combine multiple cheap proxy signals (LLM + tabular model + heuristic) when correlations between them are unknown?
4. **Adaptive proxy selection**: Could PROBE actively choose which arms to pair with expensive rewards to minimize total cost, rather than treating rewards as fixed?
5. **Distribution shift**: How do guarantees degrade under shift in the offline-learned proxy marginal means or in the reward-proxy correlation over time?
6. **Finite-sample tightness**: Is the multiplicative constant factor in the sample complexity bound tight, or can it be improved by a different algorithm?

## Key Takeaway
By carefully learning the correlation between cheap proxy predictions and expensive true rewards from the same samples, you can reduce the cost of identifying the best option—but you must maintain validity through upper-confidence certificates on residual variance rather than plug-in estimates.
