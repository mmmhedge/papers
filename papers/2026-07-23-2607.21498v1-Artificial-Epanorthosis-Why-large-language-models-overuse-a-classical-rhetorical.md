# Artificial Epanorthosis: Why large language models overuse a classical rhetorical figure, and how to mitigate it

**Authors:** Federico Boggia
**Published:** 2026-07-23 | **arXiv:** [2607.21498v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21498v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates why large language models systematically overuse epanorthosis—a classical rhetorical figure involving self-correction (e.g., "This is not a course. It is a journey of transformation")—and proposes methods to calibrate this behavior to match human writing patterns. The work bridges classical rhetoric with modern LLM analysis, showing that the overuse stems from training data distribution and preference tuning rather than fundamental generation constraints, and demonstrates practical techniques to reduce the figure to human-appropriate levels across different genres.

## Theoretical Background
The paper grounds itself in two millennia of rhetorical theory: epanorthosis is a figure of thought catalogued by Cicero and Quintilian, and further classified by Fontanier as a device for emphasis and persuasion. The modern context involves large language models—transformer-based neural networks that generate text token-by-token—which are typically trained on diverse internet text and then refined via RLHF (reinforcement learning from human feedback), a preference-tuning method that reinforces outputs rated as more helpful or desirable by human raters. The paper argues that left-to-right generation (the standard autoregressive decoding process) alone doesn't explain the phenomenon; instead, training distribution and reward signals do.

## Core Contributions
1. **Empirical discovery and measurement**: Systematic documentation that LLMs overuse epanorthosis, with quantitative analysis across model scales using an Epanorthosis Index (density relative to human baseline rates).

2. **Genre-specific miscalibration analysis**: Evidence that models diverge from human rhetorical norms in different directions depending on register—overshooting dramatically in oratory (2–3× human rate, especially in larger Italian models) while undershooting in informal Q&A, yet matching humans in journalism and encyclopedic prose.

3. **Mechanistic insight**: Attribution of overuse to trained dispositions (training data skewed toward promotional prose + RLHF incentives for confident, emphatic phrasing) rather than architectural constraints of generation.

4. **Lightweight mitigation techniques**: Demonstration that LoRA adapters and supervised fine-tuning, guided by a single-line instruction, can reduce epanorthosis by 50–75%, with SFT removing it almost entirely while maintaining controllable scaling back to human rates.

5. **Normative framework**: The argument that the goal should be calibration to human-appropriate rates per genre, not wholesale elimination.

## Connections to Other Work
This work intersects several research threads:

- **[[RLHF]]** and preference tuning: Shows how reward models can inadvertently amplify non-human stylistic biases.
- **[[Mechanistic interpretability]]**: The paper traces a behavioral artifact to its training origins, exemplifying how to debug emergent model behaviors.
- **Stylistic bias and fairness in NLG**: Related to broader concerns about whether LLMs learn undesirable linguistic patterns from their training corpus (e.g., promotional, manipulative language).
- **Human-AI alignment**: Calibration to human writing norms is a form of distributional alignment—aligning model outputs not just on task performance but on stylistic fidelity.
- **Linguistic analysis of neural models**: Connects to work examining how LLMs diverge from human linguistic conventions in syntax, pragmatics, and rhetoric.

## Practical Applications
1. **Content generation systems for professional writing** (journalism, business, academia): Deploying calibrated models that avoid over-rhetorical, self-promotional tone in contexts where neutral or evidence-based voice is expected.

2. **Chatbot and Q&A systems**: Ensuring conversational models don't overshoot confidence or verbosity in informal contexts where brevity and honesty are valued.

3. **Multilingual NLG systems**: The Italian-specific findings suggest that calibration may need language-specific tuning, useful for global LLM products.

4. **Rhetorical analysis and stylometry tools**: Using the Epanorthosis Index as a diagnostic for detecting over-tuned or skewed model outputs; potentially flagging generated vs. human text.

5. **Educational and brand voice applications**: Helping organizations maintain consistent, human-appropriate tone when automating written communication (marketing, customer service, internal memos).

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **LLM Style Calibrator (B2B SaaS)**: A plug-and-play LoRA adapter library (organized by genre, language, and brand voice) that companies integrate into their LLM pipelines to automatically correct stylistic miscalibration. Charge per API call or per deployed model.

2. **Rhetoric Quality Assurance Tool**: A browser extension or API that scores generated text against human rhetorical norms using the Epanorthosis Index and other classical figures; alerts users to suspicious over-rhetorical content. Target: content moderation, misinformation detection, AI-generated text flagging.

3. **Human-Aligned Copywriting Agent**: A fine-tuned LLM service for enterprise copywriting (emails, landing pages, ads) that matches your brand's actual tone—measured against your own content corpus—rather than defaulting to generic corporate-speak.

4. **Linguistic Audit Platform**: SaaS for enterprises to audit their LLM outputs (internal chatbots, customer-facing systems) for rhetorical drift, generating reports and recommending model retuning via LoRA.

5. **Rhetorical Decoder for Language Teachers**: An educational tool that visualizes classical rhetorical figures in LLM outputs, helping students and linguists understand how modern neural models inherit and distort ancient persuasion techniques.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scope and generalizability**: Analysis focuses on one instruction-tuned model family across three scales; results may not transfer to other architectures (e.g., diffusion-based, mixture-of-experts) or training approaches (e.g., contrastive learning, constitutional AI).

2. **Human baseline variability**: The "human rate" is measured from a corpus, but human rhetorical norms vary by author, subculture, and time period; genre definitions are fuzzy (where does oratory end and journalistic opinion begin?).

3. **Mitigation trade-offs not fully explored**: While LoRA adapters and SFT reduce epanorthosis, the paper doesn't deeply measure potential side effects on other dimensions of quality (helpfulness, engagement, task success rate, other stylistic properties).

4. **Italian-specific overuse not explained**: The paper documents that Italian models show even higher epanorthosis overshooting but doesn't explain why (is Italian training data more promotional? Are Italian speakers more rhetorical? This remains open).

5. **Scalability of mitigation**: LoRA adapters and SFT are lightweight, but deploying a separate adapter per genre and language could become cumbersome at production scale.

6. **Causal mechanism unproven**: The paper attributes overuse to RLHF + training distribution, but doesn't isolate which dominates or run controlled ablations (e.g., training on non-promotional data with same RLHF, or using promotional data without RLHF).

## Open Questions
1. **Why does epanorthosis specifically get amplified?** Are there other classical rhetorical figures that LLMs also overuse (antithesis, climax, anaphora)? Is this a general pattern?

2. **What is the causal decomposition?** A controlled experiment varying (a) training corpus composition, (b) RLHF reward model, and (c) decoding temperature would isolate the root drivers.

3. **Do humans notice or dislike over-rhetorical LLM output?** A user study measuring perception, trust, and readability of calibrated vs. uncalibrated outputs would validate the practical importance.

4. **Does the mitigation generalize across model families and sizes?** Will a LoRA adapter trained on one model family work on GPT, Claude, Llama, etc.?

5. **Can we predict other genre-specific miscalibrations?** Is there a systematic way to measure and correct divergence in other stylistic dimensions (formality, length, abstraction, hedging)?

6. **Does recalibration affect downstream task performance?** Does reducing epanorthosis hurt performance on persuasion, sales, or other tasks where the figure might be genuinely helpful?

7. **How do multilingual interactions affect this?** If an English-trained model is prompted in Italian, does the behavior change?

## Key Takeaway
Large language models overuse classical self-correction rhetoric because of training data and tuning incentives, and lightweight adapters can recalibrate them to match human writing norms by genre.
