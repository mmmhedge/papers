# Non-Asymptotic Best Policy Identification Guarantees in Online Reinforcement Learning

**Authors:** Joseph Lazzaro, Alessio Russo, Aldo Pacchiano
**Published:** 2026-07-19 | **arXiv:** [2607.17201v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.17201v1)

## Summary
This paper provides the first non-asymptotic sample complexity bounds for the Best Policy Identification problem in online tabular reinforcement learning — a setting where an agent must identify an optimal policy while minimizing exploration cost. Previous work showed these bounds were asymptotically optimal but only in the limit of infinite samples; this work makes those guarantees concrete and finite, revealing how MDP structure (connectivity, curvature) affects the actual cost of learning.

## Theoretical Background
Best Policy Identification (BPI) is a sequential hypothesis testing problem: given a Markov Decision Process (MDP — a formal model where an agent takes actions in states to earn rewards), determine which policy (action strategy) is optimal using as few samples as possible. This differs from standard RL which optimizes average reward; BPI wants *certainty* about which policy is best. Prior work by Lazzaro and colleagues developed the Navigate and Stop (NaS) algorithm, which is known to be asymptotically optimal — meaning its sample complexity rate is optimal as the confidence requirement grows infinitely tight. However, "asymptotic" means those guarantees only apply in a limiting regime; this work makes the bounds explicit for finite sample budgets by characterizing how instance-dependent quantities (the characteristic time, MDP connectivity, optimal value function curvature) contribute to sample complexity.

## Core Contributions
1. **First non-asymptotic bounds for NaS**: The paper replaces asymptotic analysis with explicit, finite-sample complexity guarantees that hold for any confidence level.
2. **Structural decomposition**: Identifies and quantifies four sources of sample complexity: characteristic time (known from prior work), MDP diameter/connectivity, curvature of the characteristic time function, and other instance-dependent quantities.
3. **Tight dependency characterization**: Shows how each structural property of the MDP — not just problem difficulty but its geometric properties — directly influences sample complexity.

## Connections to Other Work
This work extends [[Navigate and Stop]] (NaS) algorithm analysis from asymptotic to non-asymptotic regimes, paralleling similar progressions in [[best-arm identification]] in multi-armed bandits (a simpler setting where you pick one action from many). The use of hypothesis testing framing connects to information-theoretic lower bounds in active learning. Related to [[lower confidence bounds]] and exploration strategies in tabular RL, though BPI is strictly about identification rather than cumulative reward optimization.

## Practical Applications
- **Safety-critical systems**: Before deploying a controller in a robot or autonomous vehicle, certify which policy is provably optimal under a confidence threshold.
- **Medical treatment protocols**: Identify the best clinical policy (e.g., drug dosage schedule) with statistical guarantees before widespread rollout.
- **A/B testing at scale**: Determine a winning recommendation algorithm in an online platform while bounding exploration cost.
- **Drug discovery**: Efficiently screen candidate molecules by identifying the best synthesis policy with high confidence.

## Product & Startup Ideas

2. **Clinical trial optimizer**: Software for medical device companies to design adaptive trials that identify superior treatment policies with guaranteed statistical power and minimal patient exposure.
3. **Recommendation system accelerator**: API that wraps NaS for e-commerce platforms; identifies best-performing recommendation policy in near-real-time with formal confidence bounds, reducing exploration cost vs. standard bandit approaches.

5. **Active learning consultant**: B2B analytics consulting that applies best-policy identification to operational decisions (logistics, supply chain scheduling) using this framework.

## Technical Limitations
- **Tabular-only**: Requires discrete, enumerable state/action spaces; does not extend to continuous or high-dimensional settings.
- **Deterministic rewards**: The analysis assumes reward is fully determined by state/action; stochastic rewards require different analysis.
- **MDP knowledge**: Assumes the structure of the MDP (transition dynamics, reward function) is known; learning MDP structure adds non-trivial overhead.
- **Computational cost ignored**: Bounds measure sample complexity (interactions with the environment) but not the computational cost of planning and optimization within NaS.
- **Instance-dependent quantities hard to compute**: While the paper identifies that connectivity and curvature matter, computing these quantities for a real MDP may itself be expensive.

## Open Questions
- Can non-asymptotic bounds be extended to continuous state/action spaces (e.g., using function approximation)?
- How do the bounds scale with unknown MDP structure — what is the additional sample cost of simultaneously learning the MDP and identifying the best policy?
- Are there problem classes where the connectivity and curvature terms provably dominate, making NaS suboptimal relative to alternative algorithms?
- Can this framework accommodate stochastic (noisy) rewards, and how much does stochasticity degrade sample complexity?
- How tight are these non-asymptotic bounds empirically — are they loose by constant factors, or do they accurately predict exploration cost on real problems?

## Key Takeaway
Non-asymptotic bounds show NaS's true cost depends on MDP structure, not just characteristic time.
