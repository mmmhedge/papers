# Post-Training Shifts Confidence: A Three-Stage Analysis of How SFT, RL, and OPD Shape Pre-, Intra-, and Post-CoT Calibration

**Authors:** Shuhao Li, Guodong Du, Anhao Zhao, Wanyu Lin, Tianyu Yuan et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.13753v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13753v1)

## Summary
This paper studies how different post-training methods (supervised fine-tuning, reinforcement learning, and on-policy distillation) affect how confident language models are during chain-of-thought reasoning—not just whether they get the right answer, but whether they *know* they're right at each stage. The authors show that confidence reliability depends on *when* and *where* in the reasoning you measure it, and propose a position-aware strategy that uses each method's confidence signals only when they're actually trustworthy, improving both answer selection and early stopping decisions.

## Theoretical Background
Large language models are pretrained on massive text corpora and then refined through post-training methods: supervised fine-tuning (SFT) learns from high-quality examples, reinforcement learning (RL) optimizes for task-specific rewards, and on-policy distillation (OPD) trains models to imitate their own best rollouts. Chain-of-thought reasoning — where models generate intermediate reasoning steps before outputting an answer — has become a standard technique for complex tasks like mathematics. Calibration refers to whether a model's confidence scores actually correlate with correctness; a well-calibrated model that outputs confidence 0.8 should be right roughly 80% of the time. Prior work typically evaluated post-training only on final-answer accuracy, largely ignoring the *trajectory* of confidence during reasoning.

## Core Contributions
1. **Three-stage calibration framework**: Operationalizes confidence evaluation at three distinct phases—pre-CoT (before reasoning starts, for difficulty estimation), intra-CoT (during reasoning steps, for early stopping decisions), and post-CoT (final aggregation of multiple solutions).

2. **Empirical discovery of method-specific calibration profiles**: Shows that no single post-training method dominates across all stages. OPD excels at pre-reasoning confidence, SFT provides the clearest online stopping signal, and RL produces the most reliable intermediate-step confidence.

3. **Position-dependent calibration phenomenon**: Reveals that confidence reliability is not uniform across token positions in reasoning. RL confidence becomes informative only *after* a "path-commitment phase," while OPD confidence inverts (becomes anticorrelated with correctness) in later positions.

4. **PosConf strategy**: A practical, position-aware selection mechanism that restricts each method's confidence to its reliable relative-position intervals, yielding concrete improvements (+6.1 points on RL aggregation over majority voting, +4.3 points on OPD early stopping under token budget).

## Connections to Other Work
This work builds on the [[Chain of Thought]] reasoning paradigm and connects to broader literature on [[Mechanistic Interpretability|mechanistic interpretability]] of reasoning in language models—understanding *how* models solve problems, not just whether they do. It extends calibration research, typically studied in classification settings, to the sequential reasoning domain. The findings also relate to [[Uncertainty Estimation]] and [[Truthfulness]] in LLMs, which ask whether model confidence aligns with actual knowledge. The comparison across SFT, RL, and OPD echoes recent work examining trade-offs between different post-training objectives (e.g., [[RLHF]] versus supervised learning in alignment contexts). Early stopping based on confidence connects to resource-aware inference and [[Speculative Decoding]] strategies.

## Practical Applications
1. **Adaptive reasoning for constrained inference**: Mobile or edge deployments with token budgets can use OPD's early-stopping signal to terminate low-confidence reasoning branches early, saving computation while maintaining accuracy.

2. **Ensemble aggregation for high-stakes reasoning**: Medical diagnosis, legal research, or financial analysis systems can weight multiple reasoning traces by RL's trace-level confidence rather than simple majority voting, improving reliability.

3. **Difficulty estimation for curriculum learning**: Using OPD's pre-reasoning confidence to identify hard problems and prioritize them in model training or flagging them for human review.

4. **Quality filtering in data labeling**: When using LLMs to generate synthetic data for downstream tasks, position-aware confidence can separate high-confidence from low-confidence outputs without manual annotation.

5. **Interactive reasoning agents**: Systems that ask for user clarification or switch strategies mid-reasoning based on intra-CoT confidence drops detected via the proposed framework.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Calibration-aware LLM API**: A wrapper service around existing LLM APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic) that applies the three-stage framework and PosConf to return calibrated confidence scores alongside reasoning, enabling better downstream decision-making. Charge per API call with confidence annotation.

2. **Reasoning-as-a-Service for cost-sensitive domains**: Target regulated industries (healthcare, finance) offering LLM reasoning with adaptive early stopping based on OPD confidence, cutting inference costs while guaranteeing output quality via calibration guarantees.

3. **Synthetic data quality control platform**: A tool for teams generating LLM-based synthetic training data that uses position-aware confidence to flag low-quality reasoning traces, automatically filtering them or routing to human review.

4. **LLM fine-tuning optimizer**: A SaaS product that analyzes customers' LLM post-training (SFT vs. RL vs. OPD) and recommends which method's confidence signals to trust for specific downstream tasks (e.g., early stopping for chatbots, aggregation for summarization).

5. **Interactive explanation debugger for AI systems**: A developer tool that visualizes confidence trajectories across reasoning steps, helping engineers identify where their fine-tuned models lose confidence and debug alignment or capability issues.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Benchmark-specific findings**: Evaluation limited to mathematical reasoning tasks; generalization to other domains (QA, summarization, code generation) unknown. Confidence patterns may differ dramatically across task types.

2. **Position-dependency assumption**: The concept of "path-commitment phase" is identified empirically but lacks a principled definition. It's unclear whether it's a property of the model architecture, the task, or the post-training procedure—or some combination.

3. **Computational cost of position-aware inference**: PosConf requires identifying the reliable position interval for each method, which may require multiple passes or calibration data. Real-time inference cost overhead is not quantified.

4. **OPD inverse-calibration unexplained**: While the paper documents that OPD confidence becomes anticorrelated with correctness in later positions, the *mechanism* is not explained. This limits reproducibility and understanding across different model scales or architectures.

5. **Limited scale and baselines**: Experiments on mathematical benchmarks with specific model sizes; no comparison to other confidence calibration methods (e.g., temperature scaling, Platt scaling, conformal prediction).

6. **Aggregation strategy assumption**: The majority-voting baseline may be too weak. Other aggregation schemes (weighted voting, Bayesian aggregation) could reduce the apparent gap.

## Open Questions
1. **Why is OPD confidence anti-calibrated later?** Is it a training signal divergence, a data-distribution shift, or model-capacity saturation? Can this be fixed during OPD training?

2. **Does the position-dependent pattern hold across model scales?** Do 7B and 405B models show the same "path-commitment phase" structure, or is it an artifact of mid-scale models?

3. **Can we predict position-dependent reliability from the data alone?** Rather than requiring empirical calibration, can we identify which positions are reliable from the reasoning traces or model architecture?

4. **How do these findings transfer to other domains?** Do the same three-stage calibration patterns emerge in QA, code generation, or open-ended reasoning tasks?

5. **What is the theoretical foundation for position-dependent calibration?** Is there a principled explanation (e.g., information-theoretic, dynamical systems) that predicts when and why confidence becomes unreliable?

6. **Can we design post-training objectives that maintain calibration throughout reasoning?** Is there an RL or SFT variant that produces uniformly well-calibrated confidence across all positions and stages?

## Key Takeaway
Post-training methods shape confidence differently across reasoning stages; using each method's signal only in its reliable positions significantly improves accuracy and efficiency.
