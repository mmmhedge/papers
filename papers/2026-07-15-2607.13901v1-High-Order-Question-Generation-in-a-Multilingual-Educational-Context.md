# High-Order Question Generation in a Multilingual Educational Context

**Authors:** Suna-Şeyma Uçar, Itziar Aldabe, Nora Aranberri, Orphée De Clercq
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.13901v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13901v1)

## Summary
This paper explores how large language models can generate high-order questions—the kind that develop critical thinking rather than mere recall—across three languages (Basque, Spanish, English) using alternative frameworks to Bloom's Taxonomy. The work matters because educators struggle to create such questions in practice, and most prior work has focused narrowly on English and a single pedagogical framework.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two foundational ideas: Bloom's Taxonomy (a framework classifying learning objectives from simple recall to complex evaluation) and large language models' demonstrated ability to follow structured prompts for task-specific generation. Prior research showed that [[Bloom's Taxonomy]]-based prompting could guide LLMs to generate higher-order questions, but this work was limited to English. The authors introduce two alternative pedagogical frameworks: Claim-Evidence-Reasoning (CER), which emphasizes supporting assertions with evidence and logical justification, and Divergent Questioning, which encourages multiple valid responses and explores different perspectives. The underlying assumption is that different frameworks may yield different question types and quality profiles.

## Core Contributions
1. **Multilingual evaluation**: First systematic study of high-order question generation across Basque, Spanish, and English—extending beyond English-only prior work.
2. **Alternative frameworks**: Proposes and tests Claim-Evidence-Reasoning and Divergent Questioning prompts as complements or alternatives to Bloom's Taxonomy, showing they produce structurally and conceptually varied questions.
3. **Teacher validation gap**: Identifies a critical finding: while LLMs generate grammatically correct, answerable questions across languages, only ~50% of answerable questions are recognized by teachers as genuinely high-order—revealing a gap between model output and pedagogical utility.

## Connections to Other Work
This work extends [[prompt engineering]] research in LLMs by applying it to educational contexts beyond the dominant Bloom's Taxonomy framework. It relates to broader questions in [[generative-models]] about whether task-specific prompting reliably produces desired outputs, and connects to work on [[multilingual]] [[nlp]] systems evaluating whether capabilities transfer evenly across languages. The teacher validation insight echoes concerns in [[alignment]] literature about misalignment between model outputs and human-intended outcomes.

## Practical Applications
- **Automated question banks for educators**: Teachers in resource-constrained settings (especially in non-English languages) could use these systems to rapidly generate initial question drafts for lesson planning.
- **Adaptive learning platforms**: Educational software could use these frameworks to dynamically generate contextual high-order questions tailored to student responses and curriculum level.
- **Multilingual tutoring systems**: Language-learning apps could embed high-order question generation to push learners beyond rote memorization across multiple languages.
- **Teacher professional development**: Systems could generate diverse question exemplars to help educators internalize patterns of high-order questioning.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Lesson Copilot** — A web app for K-12 teachers where you paste lesson content and select a pedagogical framework; it generates 10–15 high-order question options per minute, rated by difficulty and framework, with teacher feedback loops to improve recognition accuracy.
2. **Multilingual Assessment Generator** — B2B SaaS for educational publishers and assessment companies, auto-generating localized test questions across languages using CER and Divergent frameworks; focus on underserved languages (Basque, others) with higher margins.
3. **Critical Thinking Tutor** — Mobile app for students that poses high-order questions based on study materials, with scaffolded hints and explanations; uses the three frameworks to ensure cognitive variety.
4. **EdTech API** — Sell the question-generation pipeline (models + framework prompts) as an API for LMS platforms (Canvas, Blackboard, Moodle), allowing those platforms to embed high-order question generation natively.
5. **Teacher Quality Assurance Tool** — Audit tool for school districts that compares teacher-written questions against LLM-generated high-order baselines to identify professional development needs.

## Technical Limitations
- **Teacher recognition gap (50%)**: The model output doesn't reliably match expert pedagogical judgment, meaning deployment requires human review—limiting automation benefits.
- **Language coverage**: Only three languages tested; generalization to truly low-resource or morphologically complex languages (e.g., Turkish, Finnish) is unclear.
- **Answerability vs. quality**: The paper distinguishes between "answerable" questions and "high-order" questions but doesn't deeply explore what makes a question high-order beyond teacher judgment.
- **Context dependency**: No details on whether questions are sensitive to source text quality, domain, or student prior knowledge; real classrooms vary widely.
- **Framework complementarity unproven**: While CER and Divergent frameworks produce varied outputs, the paper doesn't show they systematically improve learning outcomes—only that they generate different questions.
- **Scalability of validation**: Teacher evaluation is labor-intensive; unclear how to scale feedback loops to retrain or steer models toward higher recognition rates.

## Open Questions
- **Why does the 50% teacher recognition gap exist?** Are models generating technically high-order questions that don't fit local curricula, or do they miss subtle pedagogical nuances?
- **Can frameworks be combined or weighted?** Would a hybrid prompt (e.g., 60% CER + 40% Divergent) outperform pure frameworks?
- **Do students learn better from LLM-generated high-order questions?** This paper validates question structure; follow-up work should measure learning gains.
- **How sensitive are results to model choice and prompting?** Does instruction-tuning, in-context learning, or chain-of-thought improve the recognition gap?
- **Can the system be fine-tuned on teacher feedback?** Could a smaller, domain-specific model trained on validated high-order questions outperform larger generalist models?
- **Do multilingual models preserve quality symmetrically?** Are some languages systematically better or worse at generating high-order questions?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs can generate high-order questions across multiple languages, but teachers only recognize half as truly educational—suggesting the gap between capability and pedagogy remains.
