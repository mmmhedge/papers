# Evaluating Open-Weight LLMs for Generating Structured Threat Information for Autonomous Vehicle Vulnerabilities

**Authors:** Md Erfan, Ahmed Ryan, Md Kamal Hossain Chowdhury, Md Rayhanur Rahman
**Published:** 2026-07-17 | **arXiv:** [2607.16175v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16175v1)

## Summary
This paper evaluates open-weight large language models (LLMs) on their ability to automatically convert unstructured vulnerability descriptions from the CVE database into STIX — a standardized structured format for threat information. The work is important because connected and autonomous vehicles have complex attack surfaces, and security teams need machine-readable threat data to prioritize defenses; automating this conversion from plain text to structured formats could accelerate threat intelligence workflows.

## Theoretical Background
This work sits at the intersection of natural language understanding and cybersecurity information standards. It builds on prior work in information extraction and structured prediction with neural networks, but applies these techniques to a specialized domain: converting free-text security vulnerability descriptions into formal threat models.

The key conceptual foundations are:
- **STIX (Structured Threat Information Expression)**: a standard format for representing cybersecurity threat information as domain objects (SDOs), relationship objects (SROs), and supporting mappings.
- **CVE (Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures)**: the standard vulnerability database where information is stored as unstructured text.
- **CWE (Common Weakness Enumeration)** and **MITRE ATT&CK**: hierarchies that classify the underlying weakness types and attack techniques used by adversaries.
- **Sequence-to-structure generation**: the task of predicting structured outputs (STIX objects) from unstructured text input — a form of information extraction that modern LLMs can attempt via few-shot prompting.

## Core Contributions
- **CAV-STIXGen dataset**: A new benchmark mapping 200+ Connected and Autonomous Vehicle CVE descriptions to STIX domain objects, relationship objects, CWE classifications, and MITRE ATT&CK techniques.
- **Systematic evaluation** of 11 open-weight LLMs (4B to 120B parameters) on STIX generation, testing different prompting strategies and temperature settings to understand which configurations work best.
- **Comparative analysis** showing that SDO prediction achieves high F1 scores (~0.94) but relationship prediction (SROs) remains harder (~0.63), with multi-agent setups providing a moderate improvement path.
- **Domain threat pattern analysis**: Identification of recurring CWE and ATT&CK co-occurrence patterns specific to the CAV threat landscape, demonstrating practical value of the structured extraction.

## Connections to Other Work
This work relates to broader trends in [[Large Language Models]] and information extraction, particularly:
- **Semantic parsing & structured prediction**: Similar to work on converting natural language to formal representations (logical forms, SQL, etc.), but applied to cybersecurity ontologies.
- **Domain-specific NLP**: Shares methodology with biomedical and legal NLP, where unstructured documents must be mapped to standardized taxonomies.
- **Few-shot learning with LLMs**: Uses prompting techniques common in recent work on in-context learning, rather than fine-tuning.
- **Autonomous vehicle security**: Complements broader CAV security research by automating the intelligence synthesis step that humans currently perform manually.

## Practical Applications
- **Automated threat intelligence pipelines**: Security teams at automotive OEMs can ingest new CVEs and automatically generate STIX bundles for ingestion into threat intelligence platforms (TIPs) like MISP.
- **Defense prioritization**: Structured MITRE ATT&CK mappings enable automated correlation with existing defensive controls, helping teams focus patching on highest-impact techniques.
- **Supply chain risk assessment**: Vendors and suppliers can automatically map component vulnerabilities to standardized threat formats, accelerating risk reviews in procurement workflows.
- **Compliance and incident response**: Structured threat data feeds support automated logging, alerting, and forensic correlation for SOC teams responding to vehicle-related incidents.
- **Fuzzy matching across CVE sources**: Multi-database consolidation of vulnerability data becomes easier when all sources are normalized to STIX.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **CAV Vulnerability Intelligence Platform**: A SaaS product that ingests raw CVE feeds, uses fine-tuned LLMs to generate STIX, and serves a real-time threat dashboard for automotive security teams with attack-chain visualization and patch impact scoring.

2. **Autonomous Vehicle Threat Modeling Assistant**: A tool that takes a CAV architecture diagram and vendor BOM (bill of materials), queries CVE databases, auto-generates STIX threat models, and recommends security controls — integrating with existing automotive SOTIF/functional safety workflows.

3. **Supply Chain Risk Scoring API**: An API that takes vehicle component identifiers, maps them to CVEs, converts to STIX, and returns a quantitative risk score for procurement decisions and OEM governance.

4. **Incident Response Playbook Generator**: Enterprise tool that converts STIX threat data into executable playbooks (SOAR-compatible) — for vehicles, this could auto-generate firmware rollback, sensor override, and isolation procedures.

5. **Threat Intelligence Syndication Network**: A blockchain or consortium-based platform where automotive OEMs, Tier-1 suppliers, and security vendors collectively contribute CAV vulnerabilities, which an LLM pipeline standardizes to STIX and distributes with fine-grained access control.

## Technical Limitations
- **SRO prediction is weak**: Relationship objects (connections between threat objects) only achieve 0.63 F1, suggesting the LLM struggles with understanding threat causality and interdependencies — likely because the training data for such relationships is sparse.
- **MITRE ATT&CK mapping remains unsolved**: The paper notes this stays "challenging," implying the models do not reliably map tactics and techniques, which limits the utility for attack-chain analysis.
- **Dataset scale and bias**: The CAV-specific dataset is relatively small (200+ entries); results may not generalize to novel vehicle models or emerging vulnerability classes not well-represented in training.
- **Hallucination and false positives**: LLMs can invent plausible-sounding but incorrect STIX objects (e.g., fictitious software or weapon relationships); the paper does not deeply analyze failure modes or confidence calibration.
- **Domain specificity of open-weight models**: The evaluated models are general-purpose; they lack domain pretraining on cybersecurity or automotive documentation, likely limiting precision on specialized terminology.
- **Prompt sensitivity**: Results vary with temperature and prompting strategy, meaning deployment requires careful tuning; there is no principled way to select these hyperparameters a priori.

## Open Questions
- **Can fine-tuning on larger CAV-specific corpora improve SRO and MITRE ATT&CK performance significantly?** The paper evaluates only zero-shot and few-shot; a supervised fine-tuning experiment would clarify the ceiling for these harder tasks.
- **How do these models generalize to out-of-distribution CVEs** (e.g., emerging vulnerability classes, novel attack vectors not seen during evaluation)?
- **What is the confidence calibration of the models?** Can they reliably flag when they are uncertain, enabling human-in-the-loop workflows?
- **Can graph-based decoding or constraint-based generation improve relationship prediction?** The paper uses standard sequence generation; structured prediction techniques might reduce hallucination.
- **How do results change with multi-modal inputs?** (e.g., adding code snippets, architecture diagrams, or sensor specs to the CVE text.)
- **What is the cost-benefit analysis of LLM-assisted STIX generation vs. manual annotation or simpler rule-based extraction for real-world deployment?**

## Key Takeaway
LLMs can reliably extract threat objects from vehicle CVEs, but struggle with predicting relationships and attack techniques — requiring human review for high-stakes security decisions.
