# Curated retrieval versus open web search in public AI information services: a coverage-trust trade-off

**Authors:** Hafsteinn Einarsson, Hafsteinn Birgir Einarsson, Jón Gunnar Ólafsson, Jón Gunnar Þorsteinsson
**Published:** 2026-07-06 | **arXiv:** [2607.05217v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05217v1)

## Summary
This paper evaluates whether AI-powered public information services can reliably answer citizens' questions while citing trustworthy sources. Researchers tested two approaches—using a curated knowledge base versus open web search—by having domain experts rate 449 answers about the European Union on a quality rubric and flag problematic sources. They found that web search answers more questions but cited untrustworthy or irrelevant sources in over a third of cases, while curated sources were trustworthy but limited in scope; the work highlights that source trustworthiness is largely invisible in current AI systems despite being critical for public institutions.

## Theoretical Background
This paper sits at the intersection of information retrieval (IR) and responsible AI deployment. It builds on retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) — a technique where LLMs retrieve relevant documents from a knowledge base before generating answers — and compares it against open web search. The work implicitly draws on decades of IR evaluation (precision, recall, relevance) and extends this to a new dimension: source *credibility* as a measurable quality metric. It also reflects broader concerns in [[alignment]] about ensuring AI systems behave reliably in safety-critical contexts like public institutions.

## Core Contributions
1. **First large-scale empirical evaluation** of source trustworthiness in LLM-powered public services: 551 expert evaluations revealing that 35% of web-search answers cite at least one flagged source (untrustworthy or irrelevant).
2. **Quantified coverage-trust trade-off**: curated corpora are reliable but limited; web search expands coverage at the cost of citation quality.
3. **Demonstration that weak prompt-level steering fails**: a "trusted domain list" in the system prompt only raised citations to those domains from 12% to 21%, showing that LLMs do not reliably follow source-quality guidance.
4. **Concrete finding of systemic source omission**: the system never cited RÚV (Iceland's public broadcaster and most-used news source) despite its availability, even though it would have been relevant.

## Connections to Other Work
This complements research on [[RLHF]] (reinforcement learning from human feedback) and instruction-following in LLMs by showing that even explicit instructions about source quality have minimal effect. It extends classical IR evaluation frameworks (which focus on relevance) to include *credibility* as a first-class quality dimension. The work also resonates with broader [[alignment]] concerns about ensuring AI systems in high-stakes domains (healthcare, law, governance) cite reliable sources. It implicitly critiques naive retrieval strategies in favor of more deliberate curation—a tension between [[generative-models|generative capabilities]] and reliability.

## Practical Applications
1. **Government and institutional AI services**: Any public body (health ministries, social agencies, educational institutions) deploying LLM-based Q&A systems needs to know whether their infrastructure cites trustworthy sources; this framework lets them audit and improve it.
2. **Democratic participation platforms**: During elections, referenda, or policy debates, citizens need accurate, trustworthy information; this paper provides a concrete methodology for evaluating whether AI mediates information responsibly.
3. **Corporate compliance and customer support**: Financial services, legal firms, and other regulated industries using LLMs to answer client questions can use this rubric to detect when the system cites unreliable sources.
4. **Newsrooms and fact-checking organizations**: Publications using LLMs for initial research or summarization need assurance that cited sources meet editorial standards.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Source-quality auditing SaaS**: A tool that integrates with existing LLM-powered services (chatbots, Q&A platforms) and automatically flags citations as trustworthy, outdated, irrelevant, or untrustworthy using a combination of domain databases, publisher reputation scores, and expert human review. Sell to governments, enterprises, and regulated industries.
2. **Curated knowledge-base-as-a-service for public institutions**: A managed platform that pre-curates and maintains domain-specific knowledge for government and civic use cases (EU info, healthcare, legal), eliminating the need for ad-hoc web search while reducing the need for constant re-review. Charge per query or per institution.
3. **Trustworthy source directory + retrieval system**: Build a verified, continuously-updated registry of authoritative sources (public broadcasters, government agencies, peer-reviewed journals) in each domain, then offer an open-source or commercial RAG layer that *only* retrieves and cites from this curated list. License to news organizations and public bodies.
4. **LLM fine-tuning service for credible citations**: Train domain-specific LLMs (or LoRA adapters) on expert-curated Q&A pairs where the "right" answers cite verified sources, then offer this as a model or API. Sell to regulated industries (healthcare, finance, law).
5. **Transparency overlay for AI answers**: Browser extension or API that takes any LLM-generated answer, extracts citations, looks up their trustworthiness in a crowd-sourced or expert database, and visually flags risky sources to users. Free-to-use for individuals; premium B2B licensing for platforms.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Expert evaluation is costly and slow**: The study used only 5 domain experts for 449 answers; scaling this to thousands of services or millions of queries is impractical. Automated source credibility detection remains unsolved.
2. **Context-dependent trustworthiness**: A source trustworthy on healthcare may not be on finance; the paper doesn't address multi-domain evaluation or how to transfer trust judgments across domains.
3. **Temporal decay not fully captured**: The rubric flags outdated sources, but doesn't quantify how quickly information becomes stale for different question types (technical facts vs. policy).
4. **No solution offered, only diagnosis**: The paper shows the problem but doesn't propose a working architecture beyond "use curation." Prompt steering failed (12%→21% uplift is negligible); deeper fine-tuning or retrieval-ranking may help but aren't tested.
5. **Limited generalization**: Study focuses on EU-referendum context in Iceland; unclear how findings transfer to other domains, languages, or political contexts.
6. **Omission of systemic sources unexplained**: The paper documents that RÚV was never cited but doesn't isolate *why* (e.g., is it a retrieval problem, a ranking problem, or does the model simply not "know" to prefer it?).

## Open Questions
1. **Can automated source credibility detection match expert judgment?** What machine-learning or heuristic approach could flag untrustworthy sources at scale without human review?
2. **How deep must model fine-tuning go to steer citation quality?** The prompt-level experiment failed; what about retrieval ranking, in-context example selection, or full model re-training?
3. **What architectural hybrid minimizes the coverage-trust trade-off?** E.g., curated corpus + web search only for unmatched questions; curated + web with automated credibility filtering; or something else entirely?
4. **How do source omissions (like RÚV never being cited) arise mechanistically?** Is it a retrieval gap, a ranking bias, or does the model actively avoid certain sources? (Connects to [[mechanistic-interpretability]].)
5. **Does transparency about source quality improve citizen trust and decision-making?** The paper mentions "transparency-oriented responses" but doesn't evaluate their downstream impact.
6. **How do findings generalize to other languages, domains, and political contexts?** Does Icelandic language specificity, the referendum context, or EU-focused knowledge bias the results?

## Key Takeaway
Public AI services face a hard trade-off between coverage and source reliability—web search gives broader answers but cites untrustworthy sources one-third of the time, while curation ensures trustworthiness but sacrifices reach—and weak prompt-level steering is insufficient to resolve it.
