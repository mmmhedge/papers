# Formal Mechanisms for Market Stability in Self-Interested Agent Societies: A Marketplace Simulation Study

**Authors:** Eugene Ng Yi Sheng, Bingquan Shen
**Published:** 2026-07-09 | **arXiv:** [2607.08652v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08652v1)

## Summary
This paper asks: what rules and mechanisms keep self-interested agents honest in a marketplace where they could defect for personal gain? The researchers simulated 18 LLM-based agents trading with each other and tested eight different governance mechanisms (like mediation, reputation systems, etc.) under increasing adversarial attack. They found that "Mediation"—having a neutral third party resolve disputes—performed best and remained stable even when attacked by sophisticated, AI-optimized trolls that reduced honest traders' profits by only 13.3%.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on game theory and the study of repeated social dilemmas—situations where agents gain more by cooperating but have incentives to cheat. It also draws on mechanism design, the field concerned with creating rules that produce desired outcomes even when participants act selfishly. The work uses large language models (LLMs) as agents—neural networks trained to generate human-like text—to simulate realistic negotiation and trade behavior. The experimental setup combines marketplace simulation (agents must trade to maximize utility in a constrained network) with adversarial robustness testing, where researchers systematically try to break the mechanism.

## Core Contributions
1. **Empirical comparison of eight market-stability mechanisms** tested under controlled, progressive adversarial conditions—most prior work is theoretical.
2. **Mediation identified as superior** to alternatives like reputation systems or voting in sustaining honest-agent utility.
3. **Adversarial robustness framework**: the paper defines and measures robustness as a mechanism's ability to keep honest agents profitable under *optimized* attack, not just random disruption.
4. **LLM-driven red-teaming at scale**: iteratively optimizing attack prompts (v1 through v6) using the same LLM class that forms the marketplace, showing how the attack surface evolves.
5. **Concrete finding**: Mediation can be degraded but not broken—honest-agent utility stays positive even under the best attack.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to [[multi-agent reinforcement learning]] and the challenge of achieving cooperation in decentralized systems. It extends mechanism design literature by using LLMs as realistic agent proxies rather than simplified game-theoretic players. The adversarial testing approach mirrors robustness work in [[alignment]]—ensuring systems remain stable under distribution shift or deliberate sabotage. The paper also connects to [[market-microstructure]] research on how trading rules affect price discovery and stability, though here applied to LLM agents rather than human or algorithmic traders. The social-dilemma framing echoes work on defection in repeated games and prisoner's dilemma variants.

## Practical Applications
1. **Decentralized marketplace design**: e-commerce platforms or DAOs (decentralized autonomous organizations) could adopt mediation-based dispute resolution for peer-to-peer trading.
2. **Supply chain governance**: multi-party supply networks where autonomous agents negotiate contracts could use mediation to prevent coordination breakdown.
3. **AI multi-agent systems for procurement**: large organizations deploying multiple AI agents to source materials could enforce mediation to prevent collusion or non-compliance.
4. **Trustless trading networks**: blockchain-based or peer-to-peer trading platforms could embed formal mediation mechanisms as smart contracts to stabilize prices and prevent market attacks.
5. **Mechanism stress-testing for policy**: governments or regulators considering AI involvement in critical markets could use this framework to test mechanism robustness before deployment.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Mechanism-as-a-Service platform**: SaaS tool that lets marketplace operators test their governance rules against LLM-based adversaries before launch, with automated red-teaming and robustness scoring.
2. **Decentralized mediation protocol**: blockchain-integrated mediation service (human or AI mediators) for peer-to-peer marketplaces, taking a small fee and backed by reputation scores from the simulation framework.
3. **DAO governance auditor**: consultancy that runs multi-agent simulations of proposed DAO rules (voting, treasury management, conflict resolution) to predict failure modes and optimize for robustness.
4. **Autonomous supply-chain optimizer**: B2B SaaS where companies upload supplier/buyer networks and let AI agents negotiate trades; system uses mediation to guarantee stability and prevent supply shocks.
5. **AI agent insurance/bonding service**: product that sells insurance against agent defection or collusion in multi-agent procurement networks, priced using adversarial-robustness metrics derived from this research.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Limited agent diversity**: all 18 agents are LLMs from a single family (DeepSeek-V3); real markets include humans, traditional algorithms, and heterogeneous AI systems with different training objectives.
2. **Small network scale**: 18 agents in a constrained social network is far smaller than real marketplaces (millions of traders); mechanism robustness may degrade with scale.
3. **Simplified utility and production**: agents have "complementary specialties" and fixed trade constraints; real markets have continuous goods, price discovery, and dynamic supply/demand.
4. **Controlled troll model**: adversarial agents are optimized to exploit Mediation in a white-box setting; real attackers operate with less transparency and different time horizons.
5. **No financial incentives for deviation**: agents maximize utility within the simulation, but don't face real-world cost/benefit tradeoffs or external pressures that might increase defection rates.
6. **Mediation assumption**: assumes a trusted mediator exists; the paper doesn't solve the mechanism for mediator selection or preventing mediator corruption.

## Open Questions
1. **How does Mediation scale?** Would it remain robust with 1,000 or 10,000 agents, or does dispute resolution become a bottleneck?
2. **Can agents learn to coordinate?** If the simulation runs for thousands of rounds, do agents learn to cooperate without Mediation, or do mechanisms remain necessary?
3. **What if mediators are imperfect or corruptible?** How does noisy mediation (wrong decisions, biased mediators) affect stability compared to perfect mediation?
4. **Cross-mechanism hybrid approaches**: could combining Mediation with lightweight reputation or voting improve robustness further?
5. **Human-LLM mixed markets**: how do mechanisms perform when some agents are humans negotiating with LLMs, where communication patterns differ?
6. **Real-world validation**: can any of these mechanisms transfer to actual peer-to-peer or DAO markets, or do simulation results overestimate robustness?

## Key Takeaway
Mediation—delegating dispute resolution to a neutral third party—is a simple, robust mechanism that keeps self-interested LLM agents trading honestly even under sophisticated adversarial attack, suggesting it could stabilize real decentralized marketplaces.
