# Train the Model, Not the Reader: Decodability Supervision for Verifiable Activation Explanations

**Authors:** Hiskias Dingeto
**Published:** 2026-07-22 | **arXiv:** [2607.20379v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20379v1)

## Summary
This paper exposes a critical flaw in how we verify explanations of neural network activations: the standard reconstruction-based test (does the explanation let us regenerate the activation?) can pass while being fundamentally unfaithful. The authors propose RECAP, a training-time method that keeps internal model representations independently verifiable, making it harder for models to game explanations during safety audits.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on mechanistic interpretability — the effort to understand what specific neurons and hidden states in neural networks actually represent. Recent work has used natural-language autoencoders (systems that convert activations into English explanations) to make this interpretable. The core idea: if we can reconstruct an activation from an explanation, the explanation is faithful. However, this assumes reconstruction is a reliable fidelity test, which this paper challenges. The authors ground their critique in information theory and auditing: a claim is truly encoded only if flipping it changes reconstruction, not merely if reconstruction happens to be high.

## Core Contributions
1. **Empirical failure case**: On released models, reconstruction scores are high but only ~2% of specific claims actually drive the reconstruction — the test tracks "gist" rather than factual content.
2. **Mechanistic diagnosis**: Shows that standard training produces co-adapted private codes — false wording patterns the model secretly learns to depend on, making explanations unfaithful even under synthetic ground truth.
3. **RECAP method**: A lightweight training-time fix: auxiliary linear probes trained alongside the main model to enforce that designated content (e.g., "neuron fires when seeing 'dog'") remains linearly decodable.
4. **Two audit protocols**: The grounded-vs-true cross and evaluator swap — stress tests that catch gaming in explanation scores.
5. **Robustness validation**: Shows RECAP transfers: even fresh verbalizers (trained after RECAP) state designated content truly, and adversarial attacks that maximize reconstruction while lying are caught by RECAP-trained probes (AUC 0.95 vs 0.51 for control).

## Practical Applications
1. **Red-teaming LLMs before deployment**: Use RECAP-trained probes to verify that safety-relevant internal states (e.g., "model detects jailbreak attempt") are genuinely represented, not just claimed by explanations.
2. **Automated neuron labeling for model cards**: Label what neurons do with guarantees that explanations aren't gaming the verifier.
3. **Adversarial robustness for interpretability**: Detect when a model's explanations lie under pressure (e.g., when a prompt tries to make it deny harmful content).
4. **Audit trails for high-stakes models**: Medical or financial models can have their internal reasoning independently verified by held-out probes, not just trusted explanations.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Interpretability-as-a-service platform**: Offer RECAP-trained verification for foundation models; let companies audit their fine-tuned models' internal representations before deployment.
2. **Neural veracity checker**: A tool that takes a model's explanation and runs adversarial attacks to test whether claims are real or gamed; report trustworthiness scores.
3. **Model audit SaaS**: For enterprises deploying LLMs, provide continuous monitoring via RECAP-style probes to ensure designated safety features (toxicity detection, jailbreak awareness) are genuinely encoded.
4. **Mechanistic transparency layer**: Wrapper that enforces RECAP-style constraints at inference time, allowing end users to query whether a model's internal state matches its explanation.
5. **AI safety consulting**: Help organizations transition from trusting natural-language explanations to probe-based verification for safety-critical claims in their models.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability**: RECAP requires training auxiliary probes for each designated content; doesn't scale to auditing all possible internal states in billion-parameter models.
2. **Linear assumption**: Auxiliary linear heads may fail if content is encoded in nonlinear, entangled ways — the paper doesn't address nonlinear probes systematically.
3. **Cost**: +0.001 nats on sandbox models, but real cost on large-scale systems with many probes is unclear.
4. **Limited to designated content**: RECAP only verifies claims we explicitly want to check; doesn't catch emergent representations we don't anticipate.
5. **Fresh verbalizer gap**: Even with RECAP, fresh verbalizers only convey designated content partially (truth 0.44–0.46), limiting practical deployment.
6. **Assumes cooperative setting**: If an adversary has access to probe targets, they could craft explanations to fool both verbalizer and probe.

## Open Questions
1. How does RECAP scale to large models (7B+) with hundreds of probes? What's the inference overhead?
2. Can nonlinear probes (e.g., neural networks) improve verification while maintaining interpretability?
3. Does RECAP transfer across model families, or is it specific to the architecture used during training?
4. How can we systematically decide which content is worth making decodable? Is there a principled way to select priorities?
5. Can RECAP be applied post-hoc to already-trained models, or must it be baked into training from the start?
6. How do you defend against an adversary who knows the probe structure and actively tries to fool it?

## Key Takeaway
Model explanations can be gamed; making internal content independently verifiable through auxiliary probes catches lies that reconstruction tests miss.
