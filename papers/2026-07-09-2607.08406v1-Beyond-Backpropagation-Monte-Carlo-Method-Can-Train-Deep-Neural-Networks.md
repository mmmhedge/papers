# Beyond Backpropagation: Monte Carlo Method Can Train Deep Neural Networks

**Authors:** Hong Zhao
**Published:** 2026-07-09 | **arXiv:** [2607.08406v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08406v1)

## Summary
This paper demonstrates that a simple Monte Carlo method—randomly mutating parameters and keeping changes that reduce loss—can train deep neural networks without backpropagation or gradients. The work challenges the assumption that gradient-based optimization is necessary for deep learning, showing the method works on networks with 20+ layers, wide networks, and even Transformers, without requiring batch normalization or residual connections.

## Theoretical Background
Backpropagation has been the standard training algorithm since the 1980s, but it suffers from vanishing and exploding gradients—problems where gradients become too small or too large as they propagate backward through deep networks, making learning inefficient or unstable. The search for gradient-free alternatives has been ongoing but largely overshadowed by backpropagation's effectiveness when combined with architectural innovations (batch normalization, residual connections). This paper revisits a simple stochastic optimization idea: random search with accept/reject decisions based on loss improvement. The theoretical foundation rests on the observation that neural networks have substantial redundancy and that simple random perturbations can navigate loss landscapes effectively without explicit gradient information.

## Core Contributions
1. **Empirical proof that pure Monte Carlo training works at scale**: Successfully trains networks with 20+ layers and wide architectures (up to 16,384 hidden units) using only random mutations and loss-based acceptance—no gradients required.
2. **Gradient-free training without architectural scaffolding**: Achieves this without batch normalization, residual connections, or other techniques typically needed to stabilize deep learning.
3. **Flexibility for unconventional setups**: Demonstrates applicability to discrete weights, pruning-only training, and non-standard activation functions (e.g., Gaussian).
4. **Multi-domain validation**: Tests on both vision (MNIST) and NLP (Tiny Shakespeare character-level modeling) tasks, including a Transformer architecture.
5. **Insights into network redundancy**: Reveals that the success of this naive method points to substantial redundancy and self-organizing properties in deep networks.

## Connections to Other Work
This work relates to several research threads:
- **Evolutionary algorithms and neuroevolution**: Genetic algorithms and evolution strategies (ES) have long explored non-gradient optimization, but this paper shows even simpler mutation-acceptance works.
- **Gradient-free optimization**: Connects to zeroth-order optimization literature and recent interest in methods robust to gradient pathologies.
- **Mechanistic understanding of neural networks**: Aligns with [[mechanistic-interpretability]] research suggesting networks are over-parameterized and self-organize despite training inefficiencies.
- **Alternatives to backprop**: Related to local learning rules and biologically plausible learning algorithms that don't require global gradient computation.
- **Lottery ticket hypothesis and pruning**: The success with discrete weights and pure pruning echoes findings about network redundancy and sparse subnetworks.

## Practical Applications
1. **Hardware-constrained training**: Gradient computation and backpropagation require memory for storing activations; pure Monte Carlo could enable training on devices with limited memory (embedded systems, edge devices).
2. **Analog and physical neural networks**: Neuromorphic hardware or optical computing systems may find it easier to implement stochastic parameter updates than precise gradient computation.
3. **Discrete and quantized networks**: Training networks with low-bit weights or discrete parameters (for deployment efficiency) becomes naturally simpler without gradient tracking.
4. **Robustness in noisy environments**: Systems where exact gradient computation is corrupted by noise might benefit from a method inherently tolerant of parameter perturbations.
5. **Biologically-inspired learning**: Systems attempting to model biological neural learning without backpropagation could use this as a foundation.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Edge AI training platform**: SaaS/library for training small-to-medium models on memory-constrained devices (mobile, IoT, robotics) using gradient-free methods, marketed as "training anywhere without backprop overhead."

## Technical Limitations
1. **Computational cost**: Random mutation without directionality likely requires many more function evaluations than gradient-based methods; the paper doesn't provide detailed wall-clock time comparisons or convergence speed benchmarks.
2. **Scaling to large models**: Tested on relatively modest networks (20 layers, 16K hidden units); unclear how this scales to modern large language models (billions of parameters) or large vision models.
3. **Limited task complexity**: MNIST and character-level language modeling are relatively simple; performance on ImageNet, CIFAR-100, or downstream NLP tasks is unknown.
4. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: The method likely has mutation rate or step-size hyperparameters; sensitivity analysis and tuning difficulty not discussed.
5. **Stochasticity and reproducibility**: Pure Monte Carlo introduces high variance; no discussion of variance reduction techniques, statistical significance, or number of random seeds tested.
6. **Comparison rigor**: No direct wall-clock time or final-loss comparisons with optimized backprop training on identical hardware and fair tuning budgets.

## Open Questions
1. **Computational efficiency**: How does total wall-clock time and GPU utilization compare to backprop on the same hardware? What is the constant factor overhead?
2. **Scaling laws**: How does convergence degrade as network size increases? Is there a practical size limit?
3. **Why does it work?**: What properties of the loss landscape and network initialization make random search effective? Can theoretical analysis explain this?
4. **Variance reduction**: Can importance sampling, adaptive step sizes, or learned proposal distributions dramatically improve sample efficiency?
5. **Biological plausibility**: Does the method offer insights into actual biological learning, or is the comparison superficial?
6. **Complex tasks**: Can this scale to ImageNet, CIFAR, or real-world language modeling benchmarks? What is the quality gap vs. backprop at scale?
7. **Hybrid approaches**: Could combining gradient-free updates with occasional gradient steps (or using this as a warm-start) improve the best of both worlds?

## Key Takeaway
Simple random mutation with loss-based acceptance can train surprisingly deep networks without gradients, revealing both a practical alternative for specialized hardware and a window into the redundancy and self-organization of neural networks.
