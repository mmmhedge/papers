# From Triggers to Emotions: A CPM-Grounded Appraisal Multi-Agent for Dynamic Emotional Evolution in Persona-Based Dialogue

**Authors:** Jingyao Cai, Shuaijun Liu, Abdul Rehman, Yutong Guo, Qin Tian et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-08 | **arXiv:** [2607.07824v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07824v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes CPM-MultiAgent, a framework that models how dialogue agents' emotions evolve dynamically during conversations, rather than treating emotions as fixed personality traits. It applies the Component Process Model (CPM)—a psychology theory where emotions emerge from appraising events—to create persona-based dialogue agents that respond emotionally to conversational triggers in healthcare, counseling, and storytelling applications.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two separate research streams. First, **persona-based dialogue systems** use LLMs to generate responses that match a character's defined personality. Second, **affective dialogue research** focuses on empathy—helping agents recognize and respond to users' emotions. The missing piece: modeling how the *agent itself* changes emotionally over time.

The psychological foundation is the **Component Process Model (CPM)**, which treats emotion not as a static state but as a continuous cycle where external events trigger cognitive appraisals, which then reshape emotional responses. This contrasts with simpler approaches that encode emotion as a fixed attribute (e.g., "this character is always cheerful").

Prior work on dialogue agents has largely ignored emotional evolution—they either keep emotions constant or only respond empathetically to users without evolving the character's own internal state.

## Core Contributions
1. **CPM-grounded architecture**: Operationalizes psychological appraisal theory into a multi-agent system where emotion is a latent state continuously updated by dialogue events.
2. **Affective trigger extraction**: Identifies emotionally salient moments in dialogue that should shift a character's emotional state.
3. **Collaborative appraisal mechanism**: Multiple agents coordinate to evaluate triggers through CPM dimensions (e.g., relevance, coping potential, norm compatibility).
4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates through human evaluation that the framework produces more emotionally coherent role-play across multi-turn conversations than baselines that treat emotion as static.

## Connections to Other Work
This work bridges [[RLHF]] and dialogue safety (ensuring agents behave consistently with their persona) with psychological grounding. It relates to broader challenges in [[Alignment]] for agents—ensuring they maintain stable, interpretable emotional behavior. The multi-agent coordination aspect connects to [[multi-agent-systems]] research on agent collaboration. Unlike pure [[generative-models]] approaches that condition on emotion tokens, this paper models emotion as an evolving latent variable informed by theory.

## Practical Applications
- **Mental health chatbots**: Therapist or counselor agents that respond realistically to patient disclosures, maintaining appropriate emotional boundaries and evolution.
- **Interactive storytelling platforms**: Game NPCs or narrative agents that respond to player actions with emotionally consistent character arcs rather than canned dialogue.
- **Customer service with empathy**: Support agents that recognize frustration triggers and adjust tone appropriately over long conversations.
- **Educational dialogue**: Tutoring agents that adapt not just teaching strategy but emotional supportiveness based on student struggle patterns.
- **Grief counseling simulations**: Training agents for counselors that model realistic emotional responses to difficult disclosures.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **TherapyBot Studio**: A no-code platform for mental health professionals to design and deploy emotional dialogue agents for patient self-help, with CPM-based emotion tuning.
2. **NPC Engine for Game Studios**: Middleware that adds psychologically grounded emotional evolution to game characters, making them feel "alive" through trigger-driven state changes.
3. **Emotion Coach API**: Provide-as-a-service emotional consistency checks for dialogue systems; enterprises call an endpoint to validate that their chatbot's emotional arc matches intended persona.
4. **Narrative AI**: Content creation tool for writers/filmmakers to simulate character dialogue with realistic emotional arcs before scripting.
5. **HR Training Simulator**: Realistic difficult-conversation role-play partners (coaching managers on firing, promotion denial) that respond with emotionally complex reactions tied to appraisal logic.

## Technical Limitations
- **Psychology-to-implementation gap**: CPM is a descriptive theory; translating it to explicit appraisal components requires manual design choices that may not generalize across cultures or contexts.
- **Scalability of coordination**: The multi-agent architecture requires coordination overhead; unclear how well it scales to very long conversations or many concurrent agents.
- **Evaluation ceiling**: Human evaluation is the main metric; no ground truth for "correct" emotional evolution. Subjective disagreement about what emotions *should* evolve from a trigger.
- **LLM brittleness**: Relies on prompting and fine-tuning of base models; adversarial inputs or out-of-distribution triggers may cause emotional inconsistency.
- **Persona stability**: The framework updates emotional state but doesn't explicitly constrain drift away from core persona traits—long conversations could devolve into inconsistent characterization.

## Open Questions
- How does CPM-MultiAgent perform with personas from non-Western psychological frameworks or cultures with different emotion conceptualizations?
- Can the framework generalize to unseen triggers, or does it require explicit training on trigger types?
- What is the computational cost of the multi-agent appraisal process at inference time, and how does it compare to simpler baselines?
- How robust is emotional consistency to adversarial or off-topic inputs that try to derail the character?
- Can appraisal weights be learned from data rather than hand-designed, and would that improve generalization?

## Key Takeaway
Treating character emotion as a dynamically evolving latent state shaped by conversational triggers—rather than static traits—produces more realistic persona-based dialogue agents, especially for emotionally sensitive domains.
