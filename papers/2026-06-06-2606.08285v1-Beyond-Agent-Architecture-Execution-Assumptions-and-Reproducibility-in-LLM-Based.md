# Beyond Agent Architecture: Execution Assumptions and Reproducibility in LLM-Based Trading Systems

**Authors:** Junyi Yao, Zihao Zheng
**Published:** 2026-06-06 | **arXiv:** [2606.08285v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.08285v1)

## Summary
This paper conducts a systematic reproducibility audit of 30 LLM-based trading research studies, revealing that while agent architecture is well-documented, critical execution assumptions—transaction costs, timing, turnover treatment, and data splits—are inconsistently reported and often unrealistic. The authors argue that standardized reporting of execution realism, not just better agent design, is essential for meaningful comparison and reproduction of LLM trading results.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two convergent literatures: (1) **agentic AI systems** applied to finance, where LLMs are increasingly cast as decision-makers in trading loops, and (2) **quantitative finance methodology**, particularly the empirical evaluation standards established in portfolio optimization and algorithmic trading. It implicitly draws on the literature of **backtesting bias** (data snooping, look-ahead bias, survivorship bias) and the tension between academic performance reporting and realized execution. The methodological framework echoes reproducibility audits in ML/AI (e.g., [[RLHF]], code release standards) but applies it to a domain where execution friction is economically material—a difference from typical NLP benchmarks where latency or throughput matter less.

## Core Contributions
1. **Evidence matrix taxonomy**: A structured 7-dimensional audit framework (point-in-time controls, split transparency, held-out evaluation, cost/turnover treatment, execution semantics, universe definition, artifact release) applied systematically to 30 papers.
2. **Gap quantification**: Empirical demonstration that architecture clarity does not correlate with execution assumption clarity—a key finding that challenges the field's implicit assumption that better models solve trading.
3. **Worked example with friction**: A 10-equity case study showing how realistic transaction costs and timing assumptions can "materially compress" reported active-strategy returns—concrete evidence that the gap is not academic.
4. **Prescriptive standards proposal**: Not a new algorithm, but a call for explicit reporting conventions (e.g., cost models, universe constraints, trade execution timing) as infrastructure for reproducibility in financial AI.

## Connections to Other Work
- **[[Backtesting and optimization bias]]**: This paper extends classical quantitative finance concerns (Arnott et al., Zabarankin et al.) to the LLM+RL domain.
- **[[Reproducibility in ML]]**: Related to broader ML reproducibility crises (e.g., in NLP benchmarks, RL) but with financial domain specificity—execution friction is not a side effect here, it's the business model.
- **[[Agent-based financial modeling]]**: Connects to ABM literature but critiques the LLM variant for under-specifying market microstructure assumptions.
- **[[Time-series forecasting evaluation]]**: Similar temporal evaluation challenges (train/test leakage) but with the added layer of trading semantics (order timing, slippage, market impact).
- **[[Reinforcement learning in finance]]**: Relevant but RL papers often face similar reproducibility gaps; this audit extends to the LLM-as-agent case.

## Practical Applications
1. **Institutional risk management**: Trading desks can use the audit framework to evaluate LLM trading systems before deployment, preventing over-optimistic backtests that fail in live trading.
2. **Fund manager due diligence**: When evaluating third-party LLM trading strategies or research, institutional investors can apply the evidence matrix to assess whether claimed Sharpe ratios are realistic or artifacts of loose assumptions.
3. **Regulatory compliance**: As regulators scrutinize algorithmic trading, the reporting standards proposed here provide a template for transparency—e.g., SEC could require cost model disclosure for AI-driven trading systems.
4. **Internal benchmarking**: Hedge funds and prop trading firms can standardize their evaluation of in-house LLM agents using consistent cost and timing assumptions, improving governance.
5. **Academic publication standards**: Journals can adopt the evidence matrix as a checklist for trading research, raising the bar for acceptance.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **LLM Trading System Validator**: SaaS tool that runs submitted trading strategies through the audit framework, producing a reproducibility scorecard and cost-adjusted performance report before live deployment.
2. **Execution Assumption Marketplace**: A library of calibrated, peer-reviewed transaction cost models and execution templates (order routing, slippage curves) that researchers/funds can plug into backtests, reducing variance in cost modeling.
3. **Backtesting-as-a-Service with Friction**: A cloud platform specifically for LLM trading strategy evaluation, with embedded, realistic market microstructure (latency, partial fills, market impact) and mandatory cost transparency.
4. **Regulatory Reporting Tool for AI Trading**: Compliance software that ingests an LLM trading system's execution logs and automatically generates standardized reports (universe definition, cost attribution, turnover) for fund audits or SEC filing.
5. **Trading Agent Benchmark Suite**: Public benchmark (similar to GLUE or SuperGLUE) for LLM trading systems with standardized datasets, universes, cost models, and a leaderboard—solving coordination on evaluation standards.

## Technical Limitations
1. **No algorithmic solution to execution realism**: The paper is diagnostic, not prescriptive—it identifies the problem but does not propose a technical fix. The worked example is illustrative, not a general model.
2. **Historical cost assumptions may not generalize**: Transaction costs, slippage, and market impact vary by asset class, venue, market regime, and time; no static cost model is universally applicable.
3. **Limited scope (30 papers)**: The audit covers published LLM trading papers, but excludes unpublished work, industry grey literature, and non-English research—selection bias toward published (possibly higher-quality) work.
4. **Execution semantics are context-dependent**: The paper cannot prescribe a single "correct" execution model—a high-frequency prop shop has different constraints than a long-only mutual fund, making unified standards difficult.
5. **No temporal dynamics of agent drift**: The paper does not address whether LLM agents' trading behavior changes over time or adapts to market shifts, which could invalidate even well-designed backtests.
6. **Data snooping risk in the audit itself**: The authors' choices about what to audit (which 7 dimensions) could miss domain-specific pitfalls unknown to them.

## Open Questions
1. **Can we formalize a "gold standard" backtesting protocol?** Should one emerge from academic consensus, or should it be market-specific (equities vs. futures vs. crypto)?
2. **How sensitive are LLM trading results to cost model assumptions?** A systematic sensitivity analysis across the 30 papers would show which are robust and which collapse with realistic friction.
3. **Do LLM agents generalize across market regimes?** Do models trained on 2020–2022 data remain profitable in 2024–2025? (Out-of-distribution regime testing.)
4. **What is the overhead of full reproducibility?** If researchers adopt the reporting standards, how much additional computational/disclosure burden does it impose?
5. **Can execution realism be automated?** Could a hybrid LLM+market simulator provide realistic execution feedback in training loops, addressing the problem at the source?
6. **Do reported results correlate with live trading outcomes?** A longitudinal study matching published backtest results to actual fund performance would validate or invalidate the audit's assumptions.

## Key Takeaway
LLM trading research's credibility crisis stems not from weak agent architectures but from opaque, inconsistent execution assumptions—and standardizing the reporting of friction and timing is as urgent as improving the models themselves.
