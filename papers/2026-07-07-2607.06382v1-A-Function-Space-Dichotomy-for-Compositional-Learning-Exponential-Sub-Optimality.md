# A Function-Space Dichotomy for Compositional Learning: Exponential Sub-Optimality of the Neural Tangent Kernel

**Authors:** Arkaprabha Ganguli, Emil Constantinescu
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06382v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06382v1)

## Summary
This paper explains a long-observed mystery: why trained neural networks generalize better than their neural tangent kernel (NTK) limit on compositional tasks. Using functions on the unit circle, the authors prove that NTK regression can require exponentially more samples than necessary—the gap is a fundamental mismatch between the kernel's smoothness assumptions and compositional structure in the target function, not a quirk of training dynamics.

## Theoretical Background
The neural tangent kernel (NTK) is a theoretical framework that approximates neural network training in the infinite-width limit as kernel regression. Researchers have observed empirically that finite-width networks often beat NTK predictions, especially on tasks with compositional structure (e.g., functions that decompose into nested simpler pieces). This paper formalizes that gap through two competing complexity measures: **Fourier complexity** (controls what the NTK can learn efficiently) and **architectural complexity** (controls what a finite depth-$L$, width-$w$ ReLU network can learn with bounded weight variation). The key insight is that when these two measures decouple, neural networks gain exponential advantage.

## Core Contributions
1. **Quantified the NTK–network gap**: Proves NTK regression needs $\Omega(4^L)$ samples on depth-$L$ iterated sawtooth functions while the network minimax rate is polynomial in $L$.
2. **Characterized the minimax rate** for ReLU networks with bounded weight variation: pinned down the rate to $\tilde{O}(L^2w^2R^2/n)$ (up to log factors and a factor of $L$).
3. **Identified the root cause**: The gap is a *function-space property*—a mismatch between the kernel's smoothness bias and compositional structure—rather than a training phenomenon.
4. **Validated empirically**: Demonstrated on bandlimited targets (NTK competitive) vs. sparse-parity (networks beat NTK by 4–6 orders of magnitude).

## Practical Applications
- **AutoML architecture search**: Guides design of network depth/width for compositional tasks (e.g., decision trees, program synthesis) where NTK analysis would be misleading.
- **Kernel method selection**: Predicts when practitioners should abandon NTK-like methods (e.g., Gaussian processes, Support Vector Machines) in favor of neural networks for structured data.
- **Compositional vision/NLP**: Informs architecture design for hierarchical visual or semantic tasks where compositionality matters (e.g., object detection with part hierarchies).
- **Curriculum learning**: Suggests using NTK for smooth targets early, then switching to deeper networks for compositional targets.

## Product & Startup Ideas

## Technical Limitations
- **Domain restriction**: Analysis confined to the unit circle; generalization to higher dimensions or irregular domains unclear.
- **Architecture scope**: Results specific to ReLU networks; unclear how they extend to other activations or modern architectures (attention, normalization layers).
- **Weight variation bound**: The assumption that weight norms are bounded (variation norm $\leq R$) may not reflect actual SGD training dynamics, especially early in optimization.
- **Sparse-parity as proxy**: While sparse-parity is a canonical hard problem, it's artificial; unclear how well the theory predicts on natural compositional tasks (e.g., images, language).
- **Sample complexity vs. optimization**: The paper addresses statistical complexity but does not analyze *how long* neural networks take to optimize—training dynamics and optimization landscape remain unexplored.

## Open Questions
- How does the dichotomy extend to multidimensional and irregular domains?
- What do these bounds predict for modern architectures (ResNets, attention, BatchNorm)?
- Can the theory be sharpened to remove the factor of $L$ in the minimax upper bound?
- How do SGD dynamics and implicit bias interact with the dichotomy—do they achieve the minimax rate?
- Are there natural real-world datasets (images, text) where Fourier and architectural complexity measurably decouple, and can practitioners detect this trade-off?
- Does the dichotomy hold for other loss functions (cross-entropy, hinge) and non-Euclidean domains?

## Key Takeaway
Neural networks beat kernels on compositional tasks not because of training dynamics, but because compositional functions decouple Fourier and architectural complexity—the kernel's smoothness bias is fundamentally misaligned with the target's structure.
