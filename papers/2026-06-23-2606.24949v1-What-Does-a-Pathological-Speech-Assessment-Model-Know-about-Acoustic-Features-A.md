# What Does a Pathological Speech Assessment Model Know about Acoustic Features? A Case Study on Oral and Oropharyngeal Cancer Patients

**Authors:** Tuan Nguyen, Corinne Fredouille, Alain Ghio, Muriel Lalain, Virginie Woisard
**Published:** 2026-06-23 | **arXiv:** [2606.24949v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24949v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates what acoustic features a Wav2Vec 2.0-based neural network learns when trained to assess speech intelligibility in cancer patients, using canonical correlation analysis to map model embeddings to interpretable acoustic descriptors. The work reveals that the model prioritizes spectral and prosodic information, and provides practical guidance for acoustic feature selection in clinical speech assessment.

## Theoretical Background
Speech intelligibility assessment traditionally relies on acoustic features (pitch, formants, energy) extracted via signal processing. Recent advances use self-supervised learning models like Wav2Vec 2.0 — pre-trained neural networks that learn acoustic representations from raw audio without labeled data — to perform medical diagnosis tasks. The challenge is that these deep models operate as "black boxes," making it unclear which acoustic properties drive their predictions. Canonical correlation analysis (CCA) is a statistical technique that measures correlation between two sets of variables (here: model embeddings and hand-crafted acoustic features), enabling comparison between learned and human-interpretable representations. eGeMAPS (extended Geneva Minimalistic Acoustic Parameter Set) is a standard set of ~88 low-level acoustic descriptors (LLDs) widely used in speech emotion, pathology, and quality assessment research.

## Core Contributions
1. **Interpretability mapping**: Systematically correlates Wav2Vec 2.0 layer-wise embeddings with eGeMAPS descriptors using CCA to identify which acoustic dimensions the model actually encodes.
2. **Fine-grained analysis**: Provides both individual LLD correlations and grouped feature family analysis (prosodic, spectral, voice quality), revealing that spectral features (especially MFCC coefficients) dominate learned representations.
3. **Clinical guidance**: Translates interpretability findings into actionable recommendations for acoustic feature selection when designing pathological speech systems, reducing feature engineering overhead.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper sits at the intersection of [[mechanistic-interpretability]] (understanding what neural networks learn) and clinical speech processing. ## Practical Applications
1. **Clinical decision support**: Deploy interpretable speech assessment systems in oncology where clinicians need to understand model predictions for regulatory compliance and clinical trust.
2. **Patient recovery monitoring**: Track speech recovery post-surgery/radiotherapy by monitoring which acoustic features degrade most, using model insights to prioritize rehabilitation.
3. **Feature-efficient screening tools**: Use the identified high-correlation features (especially MFCC-1) to build lightweight, real-time screening systems for resource-limited clinical settings.
4. **Personalized therapy targeting**: Identify which acoustic dimensions (prosody vs. spectral) are most impaired per patient to customize speech therapy interventions.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Cloud-based clinical speech analytics platform**: Offer hospitals and cancer centers a SaaS service that assesses speech intelligibility over time with interpretable reports showing which acoustic dimensions are degrading (spectral flatness, pitch range, etc.).
2. **AI-augmented speech therapy app**: Mobile app for post-treatment patients with real-time feedback on which acoustic features to focus on, powered by interpretable model insights; integrate with wearable microphones.
3. **Acoustic feature profiling for medical device companies**: Provide feature extraction pipelines to hearing aid and voice prosthetics manufacturers, optimized for pathological speech based on this interpretability work.
4. **Biomarker discovery platform**: Use the CCA correlation insights to identify acoustic biomarkers for cancer treatment outcomes, licensing correlative data to pharma companies running post-treatment studies.
5. **Caregiver monitoring system**: Home-based system for family members to track speech changes in cancer patients between clinical visits, with alerts based on learned acoustic thresholds.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Limited generalization scope**: Model trained on oral/oropharyngeal cancer patients; unclear if findings transfer to other pathologies (stroke, Parkinson's, vocal folds paralysis) with different acoustic degradation patterns.
2. **CCA as proxy for interpretability**: CCA measures correlation with eGeMAPS but doesn't prove the model *uses* these features causally; high correlation ≠ causal importance. Ablation studies or attention analysis would strengthen claims.
3. **Static feature set**: eGeMAPS contains only 88 descriptors; model may encode sub-components or combinations not captured by this fixed set.
4. **Sample size and clinical validation**: Paper doesn't report patient cohort size, inter-rater reliability of ground-truth labels, or clinical outcome validation (does the model's feature emphasis actually predict patient recovery?).
5. **Pre-training bias**: Wav2Vec 2.0 pre-trained on non-pathological speech; unclear if representations are optimally suited for disease-specific acoustic patterns.

## Open Questions
1. **Causal importance**: Which eGeMAPS features are *necessary* for the model's predictions? Do ablation or SHAP-style analyses confirm CCA correlations translate to decision-relevant features?
2. **Temporal dynamics**: How do learned representations change during patient recovery? Can the model track acoustic improvement trajectories?
3. **Cross-pathology transfer**: Do the same spectral/prosodic features dominate for other speech pathologies, or are these cancer-specific?
4. **Layer-wise insights**: Which layers specialize in which features? Do early layers capture spectral detail while late layers integrate prosodic context?
5. **Clinical validation**: Does feature importance from this model align with clinician intuition or existing speech pathology literature on cancer-related dysarthria?

## Key Takeaway
Pre-trained self-supervised speech models learn to prioritize spectral and prosodic acoustic features for pathological speech assessment, and this interpretability can be extracted via CCA to guide practical feature engineering in clinical systems.
