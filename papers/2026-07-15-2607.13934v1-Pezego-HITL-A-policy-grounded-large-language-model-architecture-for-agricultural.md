# Pezego-HITL: A policy-grounded large language model architecture for agricultural extension in Ghana

**Authors:** Shunbao Li, Zhipeng Yuan, Amoako Ofori, Benedicta Y. Fosu-Mensah, Yang Li et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.13934v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13934v1)

## Summary
This paper presents Pezego-HITL, an LLM architecture for agricultural advisory in Ghana that prioritizes safety and policy compliance alongside helpfulness. The key innovation is P-EVAL, a validation framework that treats LLM deployment in high-stakes farming decisions as an adaptive compute allocation problem — balancing safety compliance, usefulness, response speed, and expert workload — rather than just measuring answer quality alone.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on several foundations: (1) **retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)** — a technique that grounds LLM outputs in external knowledge bases to reduce hallucination; (2) **policy alignment** — ensuring AI systems respect domain-specific rules and constraints (related to [[alignment]] in AI safety); (3) **human-in-the-loop (HITL) systems** — architectures where humans oversee or validate AI decisions, especially critical in high-stakes domains; and (4) **adaptive compute allocation** — frameworks that optimize multiple objectives (latency, accuracy, cost) simultaneously under resource constraints. The evaluation borrows from agreement metrics used in inter-rater reliability studies (Cohen's kappa, κ).

## Core Contributions
1. **P-EVAL framework**: A unified protocol for evaluating policy-constrained LLMs that explicitly surfaces trade-offs between safety (Policy Alignment Rate), utility (Agronomic Utility Rate), and latency — moving beyond single-metric benchmarks.
2. **Memory-routed architecture**: A structured RAG system with validated-memory routing that achieves 59.6% cache reuse, reducing P95 latency by 55% while maintaining safety compliance (PAR = 0.94, AUR = 0.95).
3. **Empirical validation in context**: Real-world calibration against gold-standard expert decisions (κ = 0.77) and direct socio-technical evaluation with 30 extension officers and 36 smallholder farmers in Ghana.
4. **Model generalizability**: Demonstration that the architecture works across different base models (including open-source Qwen), not just proprietary LLMs.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper sits at the intersection of [[alignment]] (ensuring LLMs respect safety constraints), **trustworthy AI** (making trade-offs explicit rather than hidden), and **AI for development** (applying LLMs to agriculture in the Global South). It extends traditional [[RAG]] approaches by adding a validation loop and memory routing. The multi-objective evaluation echoes frameworks in [[risk-management]] and [[optimization]] that must balance competing constraints. The human calibration step resembles approaches in learning-from-human-feedback and expert elicitation, though focused on deployment rather than training.

## Practical Applications
1. **Crop advisory hotlines**: SMS or voice-based systems where smallholder farmers query real-time pest/disease guidance; the system routes queries to cached expert advice or escalates to human agronomists based on policy risk.
2. **Extension officer workload management**: Automated triage of farmer queries so officers focus on novel or high-risk cases, improving coverage in under-resourced regions.
3. **Regulatory compliance**: Systems for regulated agricultural inputs (pesticide recommendations, fertilizer dosing) where policy compliance is non-negotiable and auditable.
4. **Climate-adaptive farming**: Real-time crop recommendations that adapt to region-specific weather, soil, and market conditions while respecting local agricultural policies.
5. **Multi-language agricultural extension**: Deployment in local languages (e.g., Twi, Hausa) with latency guarantees for bandwidth-limited rural contexts.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Policy-Aligned AgriAI SaaS**: A B2B platform for agricultural ministries/NGOs to deploy policy-compliant farming advisors with built-in audit trails and human escalation workflows — charge per query or per farmer.
2. **Extension-as-a-Service**: Managed service that handles the HITL pipeline — automatically routes farmer queries, aggregates extension officer feedback, retrains memory routing — allowing understaffed countries to scale advisory capacity.
3. **Offline-First Farming App**: A mobile app that works on 2G networks using memory-routed LLM inference locally on-device, syncing validated advice with cloud-based expert review.
4. **AgriPolicy Compliance Engine**: Horizontal B2B product that embeds policy-grounded validation (P-EVAL) into existing agricultural advisory tools — sell to agritech companies or government systems as an add-on module.
5. **Smallholder Farmer Credit Risk Scoring**: Use policy-compliant LLM outputs (crop recommendations, pest history) as alternative credit signals for informal farmers in regions with no credit bureaus.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability of expert calibration**: The framework requires gold-standard expert labeling (κ = 0.77) to calibrate the judge; this is expensive and may not scale to thousands of crop-soil-climate combinations.
2. **Domain specificity**: The policy constraints and memory routing are trained on Ghana-specific data; generalization to other regions or crops is unclear beyond the single Qwen3.5 experiment.
3. **Latency trade-offs under load**: P95 latency improvements assume cache hit rates; actual performance under real-world query bursts or seasonal spikes is untested.
4. **Limited to advisory, not causal reasoning**: The system provides recommendations but cannot deeply diagnose root causes (e.g., why a pest outbreak occurred) — it retrieves and ranks, it doesn't reason mechanistically.
5. **Human workload assumption**: The paper assumes extension officers are available for escalation; in very remote areas, this may not hold, breaking the HITL loop.
6. **Evaluation on simulated queries**: The 1,240-case dataset is simulated; real user interactions may have unexpected distribution shifts or adversarial queries.

## Open Questions
1. **How does performance degrade with out-of-distribution queries?** What happens when a farmer asks about a crop-pest combination not in the training memory?
2. **Can P-EVAL be automated further?** Is there a way to reduce expert calibration burden while maintaining policy alignment?
3. **What is the optimal human escalation threshold?** When should a query be routed to a human agronomist vs. answered by the system? How sensitive is this to context?
4. **Does policy alignment transfer across regions?** Can a model trained on Ghana's extension guidelines work in Nigeria or Malawi with minimal retuning?
5. **How do farmers perceive failures?** The paper surveys satisfaction, but not how farmers respond when the system gives bad advice — what trust recovery looks like.
6. **Can memory routing handle truly novel pests or climate events?** What happens during an unprecedented outbreak or drought?

## Key Takeaway
Policy-aligned retrieval-augmented generation with transparent safety-utility trade-offs can deliver trustworthy AI advisory for smallholder farming at scale.
