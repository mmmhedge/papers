# Heads, Not Backbones: Output Heads Dominate Architectures on Fat-Tailed Returns

**Authors:** Sichao He, Yansong Zhang
**Published:** 2026-06-29 | **arXiv:** [2606.30037v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30037v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates whether architectural backbone choices or output head designs matter more for forecasting fat-tailed financial returns. Through systematic comparison of four modern time-series backbones paired with three output heads (point, single-Gaussian, and Gaussian mixture), the authors demonstrate that output head design dominates backbone selection at short horizons, with mixture distributions providing substantial risk-capture gains during high-volatility regimes.

## Theoretical Background
Financial return forecasting faces a fundamental challenge: returns exhibit heavy tails (rare but extreme moves) and regime-dependent volatility that standard point forecasts cannot capture. Deep learning architectures for time-series forecasting typically consist of two components: a **backbone** (feature extraction engine like transformers or dilated convolutions) and an **output head** (final prediction layer). Traditional approaches use point forecasts (single scalar outputs), but density forecasting — predicting full probability distributions — better captures uncertainty. **CRPS** (Continuous Ranked Probability Score) and **pinball loss** are proper scoring rules that evaluate distributional predictions. **Gaussian mixture models** extend single unimodal Gaussians by combining K components, allowing multimodal distributions that can represent tail risk and regime-switching behavior. **Walk-forward validation** applies anchored expanding windows to simulate realistic out-of-sample performance on historical data.

## Core Contributions
1. **Quantitative head-vs-backbone dominance**: Establishes that output head switching produces 3.7 percentage points CRPS improvement (point→Gaussian→mixture) while backbone switching yields <1.5 percent, with mixture gains reaching 13.9 percent in crisis periods.

2. **Horizon-dependent trade-off discovery**: Demonstrates that head dominance holds at short horizons (h=1) but reverses at long horizons (h≥6), requiring practitioners to choose differently based on forecast target.

3. **Distributional metrics vs squared error decoupling**: Shows that Model Confidence Set analysis finds no statistical difference among 12 variants on squared error (undirected accuracy), but mixture heads separate cleanly on CRPS and coverage (risk-aware metrics).

4. **Tail regime validation**: Confirms mixture distributions capture genuine tail behavior beyond Gaussian assumptions, with largest relative gains during 1970s stagflation when risk decisions matter most.

## Practical Applications
1. **Hedge fund risk allocation**: Mixture-head forecasts enable dynamic position sizing; during crisis detection (elevated mixture component weights), reduce leverage and increase hedges by amounts justified by the predicted tail distribution.

2. **Volatility-targeting strategies**: Use mixture CRPS to set daily stop-losses and position limits that respond to predicted regime shifts, particularly in crisis detection where mixture adds 10%+ value.

3. **Portfolio rebalancing**: Monthly institutional rebalancing can incorporate mixture tail quantiles to set dynamic bands around target weights; saves transaction costs vs. fixed bands by adapting to predicted volatility regimes.

4. **Options market-making**: Use mixture forecasts to price implied volatility surfaces; mixture skew and kurtosis can sharpen bid-ask quotes during known high-volatility periods.

5. **Risk-limit monitoring**: Integrate mixture tail quantiles (e.g., 5th and 95th percentiles) into daily risk dashboards; flag when predicted tails widen faster than backbone models would suggest.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Historical stability**: Model trained on 1871–2023 S&P 500 monthly data; unclear if findings hold for other asset classes, frequencies, or markets with different structural characteristics (crypto, emerging markets, commodities).

2. **Regime definition ambiguity**: Paper uses "crisis" periods post-hoc (1970s stagflation) to validate mixture value; no explicit regime-detection mechanism, raising questions about real-time applicability.

3. **Computational cost not addressed**: Mixture heads require computing K Gaussian parameters + mixing weights; training and inference cost relative to point/single-Gaussian heads not reported; may be prohibitive for ultra-frequent rebalancing.

4. **Long-horizon backbone resurgence unexplained**: The paper documents that backbone dominates at h≥6 but provides limited mechanistic insight into why. This suggests the architecture-head trade-off is more complex than claimed.

5. **Model Confidence Set non-rejection**: The inability to statistically exclude any variant on squared error (5% level) undermines claims of practical separation; suggests real-world differences may be within noise for some practitioners.

6. **Overfitting risk**: Walk-forward on a single stock index (S&P 500) over 150 years; hyperparameter tuning not fully described; unclear if head rankings are robust to different train/validation splits.

## Open Questions
1. **Mechanism**: *Why* does the output head format dominate at short horizons? Is it a bias-variance trade-off, or does the backbone architecture genuinely struggle to extract the information needed for tail-risk quantiles at short scales?

2. **Generalization**: Do findings hold for:
   - Different asset classes (bonds, forex, commodities)?
   - Higher frequencies (daily, intraday)?
   - International markets with regime-switching?
   - Longer training windows (do structural breaks matter)?

3. **Online learning**: The paper uses offline walk-forward validation. What happens with online adaptation — does mixture head superiority persist when the model retrains monthly/quarterly in real trading?

4. **Mixture component interpretability**: Can the K=4 components be associated with market regimes (bull, bear, crisis, recovery)? If so, can practitioners use component assignment for regime forecasting separately?

5. **Threshold for head switching**: Is there a principled way to decide when to switch from point to Gaussian to mixture based on incoming data? A meta-learner to select head architecture dynamically?

6. **Tail-risk comparison**: How does mixture performance compare to other tail-modeling approaches: quantile regression, EVT (Extreme Value Theory), or copula-based methods?

## Key Takeaway
For short-horizon financial return forecasting, how you predict distributions (output head) matters far more than which backbone architecture you use, and mixture heads unlock genuine tail-risk capture during crises when risk decisions matter most.
