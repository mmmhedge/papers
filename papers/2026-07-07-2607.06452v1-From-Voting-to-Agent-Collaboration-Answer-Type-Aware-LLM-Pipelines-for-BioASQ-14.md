# From Voting to Agent Collaboration: Answer-Type-Aware LLM Pipelines for BioASQ 14b

**Authors:** Taeyun Roh, Eunha Lee, Wonjune Jang, Sohyun Chung, Junha Jung et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06452v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06452v1)

## Summary
This paper presents a question-type-aware LLM framework for biomedical question answering in the BioASQ challenge, where different answer formats (yes/no, factoid, list) receive tailored inference strategies rather than a one-size-fits-all approach. The work demonstrates that routing questions to specialized pipelines—including multi-agent collaboration for list questions—improves both answer accuracy and evidence grounding, achieving first place in the factoid subtask of BioASQ 14b Batch 4.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on several established ideas in NLP and LLM reasoning:

- **Large language models (LLMs)** are transformer-based neural networks that generate text by predicting sequences of tokens; they excel at knowledge retrieval and reasoning but can be sensitive to input formatting and evidence presentation order.
- **Chain-of-thought prompting** guides LLMs through intermediate reasoning steps, improving performance on tasks requiring multi-step inference (e.g., identifying biomedical entities).
- **Ensemble methods** combine multiple model outputs to reduce variance and improve robustness; in this context, voting and aggregation schemes stabilize predictions.
- **Multi-agent systems** decompose complex tasks into specialized agents that handle distinct subtasks (evidence extraction, candidate generation, verification) and coordinate results.
- **BioASQ** is an established benchmark for biomedical question answering over scientific literature, with subtasks spanning yes/no questions, factoid questions (single or multiple named entities), and list questions (multiple complex answers).

The paper assumes LLMs can be directed to different inference procedures via prompt engineering and that question type is a meaningful signal for routing.

## Core Contributions
1. **Question-type-specific routing framework**: Rather than applying uniform prompting, the paper systematically maps question types to distinct inference strategies, validated empirically on BioASQ 13b before official evaluation.

2. **Snippet shuffling and self-reflection for yes/no questions**: A technique to reduce sensitivity to evidence order and improve decision stability by presenting evidence in random sequences and asking the model to reflect on its reasoning.

3. **Multi-agent architecture for list questions**: A collaborative pipeline in which separate agents handle evidence extraction, candidate generation, answer verification, and final aggregation—effectively treating list-answer generation as a workflow optimization problem.

4. **Empirical validation on BioASQ 14b**: Competitive performance across batches with outright first-place ranking on the factoid subtask (Batch 4), demonstrating practical effectiveness.

## Connections to Other Work
- **[[Chain-of-Thought Prompting]]**: This work extends CoT by making it question-type-specific; it also combines CoT with ensemble voting for robustness.
- **[[Prompt Engineering]]**: The paper is fundamentally a sophisticated prompt engineering contribution, though it goes beyond single prompts to orchestrate multiple inference paths.
- **[[Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning]] and [[Multi-Agent Systems]]**: The agent-based list-question pipeline resembles task decomposition in multi-agent systems, though it uses deterministic routing rather than learned policies.
- **[[In-Context Learning]]**: The factoid strategy leverages few-shot examples in the prompt (in-context learning) to guide biomedical entity recognition.
- **Biomedical NLP benchmarks**: This complements other systems targeting BioASQ and similar medical QA tasks, but adds explicit question-type awareness as a design principle.

## Practical Applications
1. **Clinical decision-support systems**: Hospitals and clinics could use this framework to automatically answer structured medical queries (e.g., "Is drug X contraindicated in pregnancy?") by retrieving and synthesizing evidence from clinical guidelines and literature.

2. **Biomedical literature review automation**: Researchers conducting systematic reviews could apply the framework to rapidly answer factual questions about drug interactions, dosing, or gene function across thousands of papers.

3. **Pharmacovigilance and adverse event detection**: Identify and aggregate known side effects or safety signals by querying lists of adverse events associated with a drug from scientific literature.

4. **Medical student and resident education**: A tutoring system that answers anatomy, pharmacology, and pathology questions with evidence-grounded answers drawn from textbooks and journals.

5. **Regulatory submission support**: Pharmaceutical companies could use the framework to extract and aggregate evidence on efficacy and safety endpoints from clinical trial reports for FDA submissions.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **BioQA-Pro**: A B2B SaaS platform for biomedical researchers and clinicians offering a search interface that returns evidence-grounded answers to yes/no, factoid, and list questions across PubMed and proprietary literature databases, with question-type-aware routing under the hood.

2. **MedCompile**: An AI-powered tool for clinical operations teams to automatically extract and verify medical facts (e.g., contraindications, dosing guidelines) from guidelines and papers, with multi-agent verification for regulatory compliance.

3. **DrugInsight API**: A REST API and LLM plugin that biotech companies integrate into their discovery pipelines to rapidly answer drug-interaction and mechanism-of-action questions with confidence scores and evidence links.

4. **ResearchGPT (Biomedical)**: A ChatGPT-style interface specialized for biomedical questions, where users see not just answers but the question type, reasoning path (chain-of-thought), and cited evidence fragments with links to source papers.

5. **ComplianceBot**: A conversational AI for pharmaceutical legal and regulatory teams to query compliance rules, adverse event reports, and trial outcome data with structured answers and audit trails.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Question-type classification**: The framework assumes perfect or near-perfect identification of question type; misclassification would route questions to suboptimal inference pipelines. The paper does not discuss how sensitive results are to classification errors.

2. **Snippet-based evidence**: The approach relies on pre-retrieved snippets (abstract sentences or document passages); if retrieval is poor, even the best inference strategy cannot recover. Performance is thus bounded by retriever quality, which is not explored in detail.

3. **Hallucination and factual errors**: LLMs can generate plausible but incorrect biomedical claims. While multi-agent verification helps, it does not guarantee correctness, especially for rare or newly published findings not in the model's training data.

4. **Scalability of multi-agent pipelines**: List-question handling involves multiple sequential agent calls, each with latency; this may not scale to high-throughput production systems or real-time applications.

5. **Domain generalization**: The framework is validated on BioASQ; it is unclear how well it transfers to other biomedical QA datasets, different medical subdomains (e.g., radiology reports vs. drug literature), or non-English medical literature.

6. **Reproducibility and ablation gaps**: The paper reports final results but does not provide detailed ablation studies isolating the contribution of snippet shuffling, self-reflection, and each agent component in the list pipeline.

## Open Questions
1. **How sensitive is the framework to question-type classification errors?** An ablation or error analysis on misclassified questions would strengthen the work.

2. **Can the question-type routing be learned end-to-end** rather than hand-crafted, perhaps using reinforcement learning to optimize routing policies?

3. **How does performance degrade on out-of-domain biomedical literature** (e.g., clinical notes, veterinary medicine, or plants/agriculture)?

4. **What is the contribution of each component in the multi-agent pipeline for list questions?** A detailed ablation (removing agents one at a time) would clarify design choices.

5. **Can this approach be extended to more granular question types** (e.g., mechanistic questions, comparison questions) and how should routing adapt?

6. **How does the framework handle conflicting evidence across documents?** The paper mentions "reliable integration of evidence" but does not elaborate on consensus or confidence estimation when snippets disagree.

## Key Takeaway
Question-type-specific inference routing and multi-agent collaboration substantially improve LLM robustness and evidence grounding in biomedical QA, outperforming monolithic prompting strategies on established benchmarks.
