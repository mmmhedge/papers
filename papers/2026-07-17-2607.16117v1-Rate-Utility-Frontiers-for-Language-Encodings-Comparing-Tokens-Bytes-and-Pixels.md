# Rate-Utility Frontiers for Language Encodings: Comparing Tokens, Bytes, and Pixels Under Controlled Linguistic Content

**Authors:** Ingo Ziegler, Martin Krebs, Desmond Elliott
**Published:** 2026-07-17 | **arXiv:** [2607.16117v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16117v1)

## Summary

This paper compares three ways of converting text into sequences that language models can process: subword tokens (common in modern LLMs), raw bytes, and rendered pixels. Rather than comparing them under different conditions that favor different approaches, the authors use a fair experimental setup with identical linguistic content across thirteen languages, then deliberately constrain the downstream capacity to see which encoding preserves what information under compression. They find that no single encoding is best — the choice depends on the task, language, and how much compute you have.

## Theoretical Background

Language models require discrete input sequences. The **tokenization** problem — how to split text into meaningful units — has evolved over time. Early models used character-level representations; modern transformers typically use **subword tokenization** (e.g., BPE, SentencePiece), which breaks text into frequent subword units to balance vocabulary size and sequence length. Recently, some work has explored **byte-level** and **pixel-level** encodings as alternatives. The paper builds on information-theoretic concepts: **rate-utility frontiers** (from rate-distortion theory) measure the tradeoff between compression (fewer input positions, smaller "rate") and preservation of useful information ("utility"). The core insight is that sequence length alone does not determine how much meaningful information survives; a longer sequence can carry redundancy that compresses away, while a short sequence may discard task-relevant signal.

## Core Contributions

1. **Controlled comparison framework**: Uses verified parallel sentences in thirteen languages and five scripts to ensure all encodings see identical content, unlike prior comparisons that mixed language effects with encoding effects.

2. **Rate-utility methodology**: Sweeps latent bottleneck capacity to trace frontiers separately for each encoding, isolating three confounded quantities: (a) input sequence length, (b) available latent capacity after encoding, and (c) task-relevant information preserved under compression.

3. **Task-dependent findings**: Demonstrates that encoding choice depends on downstream utility. Pixels excel at surface form preservation; bytes preserve cross-lingual alignment (especially in same-script settings); tokens best support semantic tasks like topic classification. Performance differences are not explained by sequence length alone.

4. **Quantitative evidence that short inputs can discard meaning and long inputs can preserve compressible but not task-relevant information**, challenging the conflation of these properties.

## Connections to Other Work

This work relates to broader debates in [[large-language-models|LLM]] design around tokenization efficiency and universality. It echoes concerns in [[mechanistic-interpretability]] about what linguistic structure is preserved or lost during encoding. The rate-utility framework connects to information-theoretic approaches in [[NLP]] (e.g., probing and compression-based analysis of neural representations). The multilingual, multi-script evaluation also situates the work within studies of **multilingual transfer** and the question of whether encoding schemes favor certain languages or scripts. Unlike prior work that defaults to tokens for all tasks, this paper operationalizes the tradeoff explicitly.

## Practical Applications

1. **Multilingual model design**: Teams building LLMs for non-Latin scripts can use these results to choose encodings that preserve alignment signals, especially important for machine translation and cross-lingual retrieval.

2. **Compute-constrained deployment**: Organizations with limited inference budget can use the rate-utility frontiers to select an encoding that fits their latency and memory constraints while preserving task-relevant signal for their specific use case.

3. **Language preservation and accessibility**: For endangered or low-resource languages, pixel-based or byte-based encodings might better preserve orthographic structure while reducing dependency on large, language-specific tokenizer vocabularies.

4. **Adaptive encoding selection**: Dynamic systems that route different text to different encoders based on language, task, and available capacity, optimizing total throughput and accuracy.

## Product & Startup Ideas

5. **Cross-lingual search and alignment engine**: Embed-and-retrieve system that exploits byte-encoding superiority at alignment to build faster, cheaper multilingual semantic search without large dual encoders.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Limited to static encodings**: The comparison does not explore adaptive or learned encodings (e.g., learned tokenizers optimized end-to-end for rate-utility), which might outperform all three fixed schemes.

2. **Bottleneck design assumptions**: The controlled bottleneck architecture may not reflect real transformer behavior; actual models have skip connections, multiple layers, and attention patterns that could change how information flows differently across encodings.

3. **Task scope**: Only three utility functions are tested (surface form, alignment, topic). Other tasks (e.g., syntax, entity recognition, or zero-shot generalization) might show different tradeoffs.

4. **Parallel corpora dependency**: Results rely on high-quality parallel sentences across languages; many low-resource or under-resourced language pairs lack such resources, limiting generalization.

5. **Scale effects unknown**: Experiments likely use relatively small models and datasets; rate-utility frontiers may shift at the scale of modern billion-parameter LLMs or with much larger training corpora.

6. **No interaction with architecture**: The paper does not explore whether certain architectures (e.g., efficient Transformers, RNNs, or state-space models) interact differently with encodings.

## Open Questions

1. **Do rate-utility frontiers transfer across model scales?** Is the optimal encoding for a 7B model the same as for a 70B model?

2. **How do these results hold for in-context learning and few-shot tasks**, which rely on very different information preservation properties than supervised classification?

3. **Can learned encodings break the frontiers** established by tokens, bytes, and pixels? What would a jointly optimized encoding + bottleneck achieve?

4. **Do long-context models (with longer attention windows) show different tradeoffs**, since they can attend to redundant tokens more cheaply?

5. **How do these encodings interact with other design choices** like vocabulary size, subword merge strategy, or language-specific preprocessing (e.g., diacritics)?

6. **What explains the bytes-alignment advantage in same-script settings mechanistically?** Is it residual Unicode structure, or something deeper about cross-lingual phonetic preservation?

## Key Takeaway

Encoding choice is a task- and capacity-dependent tradeoff: tokens suit semantic tasks, bytes excel at cross-lingual alignment, pixels preserve orthography best.
