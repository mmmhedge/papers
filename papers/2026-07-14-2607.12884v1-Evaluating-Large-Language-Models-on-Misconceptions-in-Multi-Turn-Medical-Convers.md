# Evaluating Large Language Models on Misconceptions in Multi-Turn Medical Conversations

**Authors:** Monica Munnangi, Saiph Savage
**Published:** 2026-07-14 | **arXiv:** [2607.12884v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12884v1)

## Summary
This paper evaluates whether large language models can reliably detect and correct medical misconceptions during multi-turn conversations with patients. The authors introduce ThReadMed-QA, a dataset of 2,437 real patient-physician conversation threads, and find that even frontier models (like GPT-4 and Claude) drop from ~85% accuracy in correcting misconceptions on the first question to ~50% by the second or third follow-up, revealing a critical safety gap in patient-facing medical AI.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on research in conversational AI, medical NLP, and evaluation frameworks for language models. Multi-turn dialogue systems must maintain context and consistency across exchanges—a challenge different from single-turn question-answering. Safe medical communication (a concept from healthcare and patient safety) requires not just accurate answers but active correction of false beliefs embedded in patient questions. Prior work has developed rubric-based evaluation methods and "LLM-as-a-Judge" frameworks (using trained models to score model outputs), but these have typically focused on single-exchange tasks rather than temporal degradation in conversations.

## Core Contributions
1. **ThReadMed-QA dataset**: 2,437 multi-turn medical dialogue threads (8,204 QA pairs) sourced from real AskDocs interactions—a substantial resource for evaluating misconception handling over time.
2. **Empirical demonstration of degradation**: Systematic evidence that even state-of-the-art models (GPT-4, Claude-Haiku) exhibit sharp performance drops across 2–3 turns, not just occasional lapses.
3. **Error propagation analysis**: An oracle experiment showing that when prior model outputs are replaced with physician responses, performance improves but remains imperfect—indicating both error propagation *and* an irreducible modeling gap.
4. **Domain-specific evaluation rubric**: A rubric-based LLM-as-a-Judge framework tailored to medical misconception correction that captures both identification and correction quality.

## Connections to Other Work
This research relates to [[alignment]] concerns in AI safety—specifically, the challenge of building systems that remain reliable and safe over extended interactions. It echoes problems in [[RLHF]] (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) and instruction-following consistency. The work also connects to broader questions about [[hallucinations]] and [[context window]] limitations in LLMs. The multi-turn aspect mirrors challenges in [[agents]] and [[multi-agent-systems]] where coordination and consistency degrade over time. Error propagation has been studied in dialogue systems and machine translation, but its acute manifestation in medical settings is underexplored.

## Practical Applications
1. **Medical chatbot safety audits**: Systems like HealthTap, Woebot, or internal hospital chatbots could use ThReadMed-QA to stress-test misconception-handling before patient deployment.
2. **Clinical decision support**: Identifying which LLM architectures or retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) strategies maintain accuracy over multi-turn interactions could improve doctor-in-the-loop systems.
3. **Patient education platforms**: Developers building interactive symptom checkers or chronic disease management apps need benchmarks to ensure consistency across follow-up questions.
4. **Regulatory compliance**: Medical device regulators (FDA, EMA) could adopt this evaluation framework to certify conversational medical AI.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **MedAssurance**: A SaaS platform that audits medical chatbots and conversational AI using multi-turn evaluation datasets tailored to different medical domains (cardiology, oncology, mental health). Sells to healthcare providers and healthtech startups.
2. **SafeConverse**: Middleware/API wrapper that detects misconception-handling failures in real time and flags uncertain exchanges for human physician review before they reach patients.
3. **MedBench Pro**: Benchmark-as-a-service—provide hospitals and med-tech companies access to curated multi-turn evaluation datasets (similar to ThReadMed-QA) with automatic scoring and comparative dashboards.
4. **ContextGuard**: A fine-tuning service that specializes in hardening LLMs for medical conversation, using curriculum learning and adversarial multi-turn examples to reduce degradation.
5. **PhysicianValidator**: Software that pairs AI-generated medical responses with automated flagging of responses that contradict prior context or physician guidelines, enforcing consistency across dialogue threads.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Dataset scope**: ThReadMed-QA is sourced from AskDocs, a self-selected population; results may not generalize to other patient populations (e.g., different languages, health literacy levels, or socioeconomic backgrounds).
2. **Oracle analysis incompleteness**: Replacing model outputs with physician responses is unrealistic in practice—real physician responses may themselves contain errors or ambiguities, and the oracle doesn't capture how model-generated context interacts with subsequent reasoning.
3. **Misconception definition**: The rubric relies on manual annotation and LLM-as-a-Judge scoring, which introduces subjective judgment calls; inter-rater reliability and judge bias are not thoroughly discussed.
4. **Limited model coverage**: Only five models tested; newer or smaller open-source models are absent, making it unclear whether findings generalize across model families.
5. **No intervention strategies**: The paper diagnoses the problem but does not test potential solutions (e.g., explicit "acknowledge prior context" prompting, retrieval of prior turns, or fine-tuning).

## Open Questions
1. **Root cause analysis**: Is degradation primarily due to context window limits, attention collapse to recent turns, or fundamental reasoning instability? Mechanistic interpretability studies could help.
2. **Intervention efficacy**: Do prompt engineering (e.g., "Refer back to the initial question"), retrieval-augmented generation, or few-shot in-context examples improve multi-turn consistency?
3. **Cross-domain generalization**: Does this degradation pattern hold in other high-stakes domains (legal advice, financial planning) or is it specific to medical misconceptions?
4. **Human-AI hybrid workflows**: What human-in-the-loop architectures best mitigate these failures without overwhelming physicians?
5. **Model scaling**: Do larger models (or different architectures like mixture-of-experts) exhibit better or worse degradation curves?

## Key Takeaway
Frontier LLMs correctly identify and correct medical misconceptions in initial patient questions but fail to maintain this accuracy across follow-up exchanges, posing a patient safety risk.
