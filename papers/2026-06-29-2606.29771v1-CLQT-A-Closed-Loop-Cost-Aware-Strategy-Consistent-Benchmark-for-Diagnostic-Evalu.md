# CLQT: A Closed-Loop, Cost-Aware, Strategy-Consistent Benchmark for Diagnostic Evaluation of LLM Portfolio-Management Agents

**Authors:** Bo Qu, Mingguang Chen
**Published:** 2026-06-29 | **arXiv:** [2606.29771v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29771v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces CLQT, a diagnostic benchmark for evaluating LLM-based portfolio management agents that goes beyond simple return rankings by assessing the soundness of their reasoning and strategy consistency. Rather than certifying performance through historical returns (which are dominated by market movements), CLQT creates a closed-loop trading environment with institutional-grade cost modeling, temporal controls, and auditable decision trails to pinpoint where and why agents succeed or fail.

## Theoretical Background
Portfolio optimization traditionally relies on mean-variance frameworks and quantitative factor models, but the emergence of LLM agents — language models deployed as autonomous decision-makers — creates a new evaluation problem. Prior benchmarks in AI-for-finance focused on question-answering or simple sequential trading, but these suffer from **look-ahead bias** (models seeing future data during training) and **outcome conflation** (confusing market luck with agent skill). The paper builds on closed-loop simulation concepts from reinforcement learning and backtesting methodology from quantitative finance, but adds diagnostic rigor: rather than asking "did the agent beat the market?", CLQT asks "did the agent reason soundly given what it knew?"

## Core Contributions
1. **Diagnostic reframing**: Shifts evaluation from ranking agents by returns to diagnosing competencies across five dimensions (Coherence, Acuity, Composure, Discipline, Reliability).
2. **Fully auditable decision trail**: Every trading decision is sealed into a recompute-verifiable hash chain, enabling reconstruction of every metric and detection of contamination.
3. **Strategy-consistency scoring**: Quantifies whether an agent's portfolio decisions follow from its stated reasoning, separate from outcome quality.
4. **Institutional cost modeling**: Incorporates realistic transaction costs and financing costs (spreads, slippage, borrowing rates) rather than idealized frictionless markets.
5. **Three-tier memory + temporal gating**: A "hard TimeGate" prevents lookahead; memory is structured to test whether agents properly contextualize past decisions.
6. **Process scaffolding as experimental variable**: Tests whether agents perform better when constrained to specialized roles (committee) vs. full autonomy, isolating the effect of reasoning structure.
7. **Bias-corrected coherence scoring**: Uses a held-out, out-of-cohort LLM to judge logical consistency, reducing self-preference bias.

## Connections to Other Work
This builds on [[RLHF]] and agent evaluation traditions by treating LLM portfolio management as a process to be audited, not just an outcome to rank. It extends [[Attention Is All You Need]] era benchmarking (which often conflate model capacity with task performance) into the financial domain. Related to [[Constitutional AI]] in the sense that it creates an external auditor role. The hash-chain auditability parallels blockchain verification but applies it to ML reproducibility. The "decision round" concept echoes multi-step reasoning in chain-of-thought prompting, but grounds it in real trading mechanics. Differs from traditional backtesting (which assumes a fixed strategy) and from pure RL agent evaluation (which has ground-truth reward signals) by working in a space where correctness is ambiguous and outcomes are noisy.

## Practical Applications
1. **Institutional risk management**: Quant funds can use CLQT diagnostics to certify that autonomous trading agents are reasoning soundly before deploying capital, reducing tail risk from agents that got lucky on historical data.
2. **Regulatory compliance & explainability**: Asset managers face increasing pressure to explain portfolio decisions; CLQT's auditable trail provides regulators and clients with transparent reasoning logs.
3. **LLM vendor evaluation**: Finance firms selecting between Claude, GPT, Grok, or proprietary models for portfolio tasks can compare agents fairly on coherence and discipline, not just Sharpe ratio.
4. **Portfolio manager training**: Human PMs can review agent decision trails to understand failure modes and adopt or avoid the agent's heuristics.
5. **Hybrid human-AI workflows**: Identify tasks where agent reasoning is sound but execution fails (or vice versa), informing where to insert human oversight.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **LLM Audit-as-a-Service for Quant Funds**: A SaaS platform that runs trading agents through CLQT's five-axis scorecard before deployment, yielding a durable capability map and risk dashboard. Sold to hedge funds and asset managers as a pre-deployment quality gate.
2. **Agent Strategy Consistency Insurance**: An insurer pools capital to back the "composure" and "discipline" scores of pre-certified agents, creating a trust signal and enabling smaller funds to delegate portions of AUM to vetted LLM systems.
3. **Forensic Trading Audit Tool**: Post-trade analysis for regulators (SEC, CFTC) to quickly diagnose whether an agent's anomalous trading was a reasoning error, drift, or market manipulation — automating investigation of algo-trading incidents.
4. **Hybrid PM Benchmarking Platform**: A dashboard comparing human PMs and LLM agents on the same APM-CS scorecard, helping allocators decide which decisions to delegate and which to keep in-house.
5. **LLM Finance Model Zoo**: A curated marketplace of "CLQT-certified" agent templates (growth, value, momentum, macro) with published scorecards, so smaller funds can license battle-tested decision processes without building in-house.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Backtest-to-live degradation**: CLQT validates on post-cutoff broker data, but live market conditions (liquidity, sentiment, volatility regime shifts) can differ sharply from the training backtest window; the out-of-sample test may still not capture true future performance.
2. **Cost model incompleteness**: Institutional cost modeling may omit latency-sensitive microstructure (tick-by-tick slippage, adverse selection in thin markets) or evolving market structure (dark pool dynamics, market maker behavior changes).
3. **Strategy consistency as a circular metric**: If an agent is asked to judge its own coherence (or is evaluated by a similar-architecture LLM), self-preference bias may persist even with a "held-out" evaluator if both models share training data or architectural biases.
4. **Five-axis scorecard reductionism**: Real portfolio skill is multidimensional and context-dependent; collapsing it to five axes may miss interactions or domain-specific edge (e.g., an agent might be incoherent in calm markets but disciplined in crises).
5. **Limited to equity/liquid markets**: Applicability to illiquid assets (private equity, real estate, derivatives) is unclear; CLQT assumes price data and execution certainty.
6. **Temporal stationarity assumption**: The benchmark assumes agent skill is stable over time; in reality, LLM performance can drift with fine-tuning, prompt changes, or market regime shifts.

## Open Questions
1. **How does process scaffolding (committee vs. orchestrator) interact with market regime?** Does constraining an agent to specialized roles help in crisis (volatility spikes) but hurt in stable periods? Are there agent archetypes (risk-averse, opportunistic) for which one structure is universally better?
2. **Can Coherence scoring generalize across architectures?** Does a GPT-based evaluator fairly assess Claude-based agents, or do architectural differences bias the held-out auditor?
3. **What is the signal-to-noise ratio of the five axes?** How much of the variance in long-term returns is explained by Discipline vs. Acuity? Do all five axes matter equally?
4. **Can you transfer a CLQT-certified agent to a different market (e.g., trained on S&P 500, tested on Russell 2000)?** Is capability portable?
5. **How does the hash chain scale?** As agents run longer and accumulate decision trails, does verification overhead become prohibitive?
6. **What is the minimum backtest window needed for stable scorecard estimates?** How long before the APM-CS converges?

## Key Takeaway
CLQT separates agent *process quality* from *outcome luck*, enabling reproducible diagnosis of LLM trader reasoning via auditable decision trails and multi-axis capability scoring rather than rankings by returns alone.
