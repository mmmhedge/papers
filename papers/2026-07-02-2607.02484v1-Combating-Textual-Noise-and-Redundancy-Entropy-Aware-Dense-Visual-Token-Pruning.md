# Combating Textual Noise and Redundancy: Entropy-Aware Dense Visual Token Pruning

**Authors:** Xuehui Wang, Xuankun Yang, Wei Shen
**Published:** 2026-07-02 | **arXiv:** [2607.02484v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02484v1)

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental inefficiency in vision-language models (VLMs) — systems that process both images and text — by selectively removing redundant image patches while preserving details needed to answer specific questions. The key insight is that existing pruning methods fail because textual noise corrupts which patches matter, and naive selection breaks spatial coherence. The authors propose EADP, which uses information theory (entropy) to filter noise and frames token selection as a structured optimization problem, achieving better accuracy with fewer image patches.

## Theoretical Background
VLMs combine visual encoders (typically vision transformers — neural networks that treat images as sequences of patches) with language models to answer questions about images. These models are computationally expensive because they process many redundant image patches. Prior work has attempted to prune away unimportant patches using attention scores or language-vision alignment, but these methods struggle when instructions are complex (dense instructions) or require fine-grained perception (small objects, subtle details).

The paper builds on two theoretical foundations: (1) **information entropy** — a statistical measure of uncertainty or disorder in data, used here to quantify how much textual noise corrupts scoring signals, and (2) **submodular optimization** — a mathematical framework for selecting items (tokens) such that adding one more item provides diminishing returns, which naturally captures the idea that you want diverse, non-redundant selections.

## Core Contributions
1. **Identification of two failure modes**: Quantifies how textual noise (semantic inconsistencies in text representations) disperses across high-dimensional spaces and corrupts cross-modal scoring, and shows how feature fragmentation (splitting related visual information across dispersed patches) breaks holistic understanding under extreme compression.

2. **Entropy-based noise filtering**: Proposes a statistical method to detect and suppress noisy directions in text embeddings before computing image-text relevance scores, yielding more reliable fine-grained instruction signals.

3. **Submodular reformulation with spatial priors**: Recasts token selection from naive Top-K picking into a submodular maximization problem that explicitly enforces spatial coherence — patches should form contiguous regions, not scattered points. This is novel because prior work treats each patch independently.

4. **Empirical validation on dense tasks**: Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on multimodal benchmarks (particularly those requiring fine-grained reasoning like counting, small-object detection) where prior methods degrade sharply under tight token budgets.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[Vision Transformers]], efficient inference (model compression, pruning), and multimodal learning. It extends prior work on **token pruning for vision transformers** (which mostly ignored text context) and **cross-modal alignment** (which didn't account for noise in text embeddings). The submodular optimization framing relates to classical work in **structured sparsity** and **diversity-aware selection** in machine learning, but applies it in a novel cross-modal setting. The entropy-based filtering draws from robust statistics and connects to [[Alignment]] concerns in multimodal models — ensuring language and vision stay synchronized under compression.

## Practical Applications
1. **Mobile/edge deployment of VLMs**: Running vision-language models on smartphones or IoT devices where compute is severely limited; EADP could enable real-time visual Q&A with 50–80% fewer computations.

2. **Document understanding at scale**: Processing scanned documents or PDFs with dense text and images; pruning irrelevant regions while preserving text-centric patches accelerates OCR-free document QA systems.

3. **Medical image analysis**: Radiologists need fine-grained visual cues (tumors, lesions) and dense instructions (measurements, region comparisons); EADP preserves these under token constraints, enabling real-time diagnostic assistants.

4. **Autonomous vehicle perception**: Cameras feed high-resolution images to VLMs for scene understanding; aggressive pruning reduces latency while maintaining ability to detect pedestrians, signs, and hazards.

5. **Retail/checkout automation**: Identifying products in cluttered shelf images with questions like "count items in aisle 3"; requires both fine-grained vision and dense reasoning under compute budgets.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Entropy-based filtering assumes Gaussian or well-behaved distributions** in text embeddings; may fail if text noise follows multi-modal or heavy-tailed distributions, e.g., adversarial or out-of-domain prompts.

2. **Submodular optimization is NP-hard** — the paper likely uses greedy approximations (which give ~63% of optimal), but no analysis of approximation quality or failure cases is provided.

3. **Spatial priors are image-centric** — assumes patches form a 2D grid; fails for irregular patch layouts or non-image modalities (video, 3D scenes).

4. **Evaluation limited to static images** — no results on video, where temporal coherence and dynamics matter, or on long sequences where noise patterns may change.

5. **Hyperparameter sensitivity** — entropy threshold, submodular regularization weight, spatial prior strength are all tuning knobs; unclear how to set them without task-specific validation.

6. **No theoretical guarantees** — no formal analysis of when/why entropy filtering succeeds, or bounds on accuracy loss under extreme compression.

## Open Questions
1. **How does EADP degrade with out-of-distribution text instructions?** What happens with typos, slang, or languages unseen during training?

2. **Can you extend spatial priors to 3D or non-Euclidean modalities** (videos, point clouds, graphs) without redesigning the core algorithm?

3. **What is the actual approximation ratio of the greedy submodular solver?** Can you prove it achieves some fraction of the optimal dense representation?

4. **Does entropy filtering correlate with human judgment of importance?** Would a user study validate that filtered-out patches are truly irrelevant for answering questions?

5. **How does EADP interact with different VLM architectures?** Does it generalize beyond the tested model, or is it tightly coupled to specific attention patterns?

6. **Can you combine EADP with quantization or other compression techniques?** Is pruning + quantization compatible, or do they interfere?

## Key Takeaway
Textual noise and spatial fragmentation are overlooked culprits in vision-language model pruning; filtering noise with entropy and selecting tokens as a coherent submodular set — not just top-scoring patches — unlocks efficient, fine-grained multimodal reasoning.
