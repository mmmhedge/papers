# LLM-powered reasoning in agent-based modeling

**Authors:** Sifat Afroj Moon, Dakotah Maguire, Adam Spannaus, Joe Tuccillo, Maksudul Alam et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06757v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06757v1)

## Summary

This paper combines agent-based modeling (ABM) — a simulation technique that models thousands or millions of individuals and their interactions — with large language models (LLMs) to make those simulations more adaptive and realistic. Instead of relying on fixed rules for how people behave, the researchers use LLMs to predict human decision-making in real time. They demonstrate the approach on COVID-19 spread in Salt Lake County, showing that LLMs can help ABMs respond dynamically to changing conditions and inform policy decisions.

## Theoretical Background

Agent-based modeling is a long-established simulation paradigm where individual agents (people, organizations, etc.) follow rules and interact locally, producing emergent system-level behaviors. Traditionally, ABM relies on handcrafted rules or statistical priors — fixed assumptions about how agents will behave. LLMs, particularly transformer-based models (neural networks using attention mechanisms to process sequential data), have shown remarkable ability to simulate human-like reasoning and predict plausible responses to contextual prompts. This work bridges these two domains: using LLM inference to replace or augment the static decision logic that normally governs agents. The COVID-19 context is a natural test case because human behavior (lockdown compliance, vaccination decisions, social distancing) shifts in response to information, risk perception, and policy changes — exactly what static rules struggle to capture.

## Core Contributions

1. **HALE Framework**: A scalable architecture that integrates LLM-based reasoning directly into an ABM simulation loop, allowing agents to make contextually adaptive decisions rather than following fixed rules.

2. **Dynamic human decision modeling**: Demonstrates that LLMs can be queried in real time during simulation to generate plausible human responses to evolving epidemiological conditions.

3. **Empirical case study**: Provides proof-of-concept application to COVID-19 spread and intervention outcomes in a real county-level geography, bridging simulation research and public health policy.

4. **Scalability demonstration**: Shows that the hybrid approach remains computationally feasible despite the overhead of LLM inference at scale.

## Connections to Other Work

This work sits at the intersection of [[Agent-Based Modeling]], [[Large Language Models]], and applied epidemiology (computational disease modeling). It relates to recent efforts in using LLMs for reasoning and planning (e.g., chain-of-thought prompting), but applies that capability in a new domain: replacing hand-coded agent behavior. It also connects to [[Multi-Agent Systems]] research, where agent coordination and heterogeneous decision-making are core problems. The use of LLMs for human behavior simulation echoes work in behavioral economics and cognitive science, but grounds it in computational policy simulation. This is complementary to [[Reinforcement Learning]] approaches to ABM (agents learning policies via RL), but takes a different path: using pre-trained language understanding rather than learning from scratch.

## Practical Applications

1. **Pandemic preparedness and response**: Real-time simulation of public health interventions, vaccination campaigns, and lockdown scenarios by predicting how different populations will respond to policy and information changes.

2. **Urban planning and transportation**: Modeling pedestrian and vehicle flows, congestion patterns, and commute behavior under different infrastructure or policy scenarios (congestion pricing, transit investment) where behavior adapts to conditions.

3. **Financial market simulation**: Simulating trader and investor decision-making under varying market conditions, regulatory regimes, and information environments — more realistic than fixed-rule models.

4. **Disaster response and evacuation**: Predicting how populations will behave during emergencies (hurricanes, floods, fires) when they receive different warnings, shelter information, or evacuation orders.

5. **Social policy analysis**: Modeling labor market decisions, education choices, or benefit uptake in response to policy changes, where static rules fail because people reason about trade-offs.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **Policy Simulation SaaS for governments**: A platform where public health, urban planning, or economic agencies upload ABM templates and can run "what-if" scenarios with LLM-driven agent behavior — e.g., "what if we change mask guidelines?" — without hiring data scientists.

2. **Crisis response digital twin**: Real-time simulation engine for emergency management agencies that ingests live data (case counts, hospital capacity, mobility data) and uses LLMs to predict population responses to emergency declarations, generating scenario forecasts for decision-makers.

3. **Enterprise supply chain resilience simulator**: A tool for large manufacturers and logistics firms to model supplier and customer behavior under disruptions (port strikes, raw material shortages, tariffs) using LLMs to predict adaptive responses.

4. **Epidemiology / disease modeling consulting service**: Boutique firm offering bespoke HALE simulations for pharmaceutical companies, public health bodies, or nonprofits evaluating intervention strategies (vaccines, treatments, outreach) before rollout.

5. **Insurance and actuarial risk platform**: Tool for insurers to model claims behavior and population response to premiums, deductibles, or coverage changes under various economic scenarios, improving pricing and product design.

## Technical Limitations

1. **LLM consistency and bias**: LLMs are stochastic and may produce inconsistent or biased outputs for identical queries; no guarantee that responses are calibrated to real human behavior. Hallucinations or implausible decisions could corrupt simulation outcomes.

2. **Computational cost**: Querying an LLM for every decision by every agent in every simulation step is expensive and slow, limiting the number of agents, simulation duration, or policy scenarios evaluated.

3. **Lack of ground truth**: No direct validation that LLM-predicted behavior matches actual human choices in the scenario being simulated. The COVID-19 case may not validate against observed behavior due to confounding factors.

4. **Prompt engineering and sensitivity**: Results likely depend heavily on how agents' situations are described to the LLM. Subtle wording changes could alter outcomes; unclear how robust this is or how to systematically design prompts.

5. **Scalability to millions of agents**: While claimed as scalable, the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate that LLM-based ABM can actually run millions of agents in practice; batching or approximation strategies may be needed, introducing additional assumptions.

6. **Black-box interpretability**: Neither ABM nor LLM reasoning is fully transparent. It's hard to audit why a particular simulation outcome occurred or whether the agent's "reasoning" is sound.

## Open Questions

1. **How well does LLM-predicted behavior match real human decisions** in the domain being simulated? A validation study comparing LLM predictions to observed human responses (survey data, behavioral experiments, epidemiological records) is essential.

2. **What is the optimal batch size and query strategy** for LLM calls in large-scale ABM? Can you subsample agents or aggregate decisions without losing fidelity?

3. **How sensitive are simulation outcomes to prompt design and LLM choice?** Would results differ materially if you used a different LLM, a different prompt structure, or in-context examples?

4. **Can you incorporate uncertainty quantification**: confidence intervals or Bayesian posterior distributions over agent decisions, and how does that propagate to policy conclusions?

5. **How do you handle multi-step reasoning and planning** by agents? A single LLM call captures one decision; real policies involve longer deliberation and adaptation. Can you structure agent reasoning as a loop?

6. **Does this approach generalize beyond epidemiology?** Early validation on transportation, economics, or supply chain domains would strengthen claims of broad applicability.

## Key Takeaway

By replacing static rules with LLM-based reasoning, agent-based simulations can now adapt dynamically to changing conditions, making them more realistic for policy evaluation and opening a scalable path to more human-centric computational modeling.
