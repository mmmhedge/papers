# Markov Chain Monte Carlo with Diffusion Paths

**Authors:** Han Chen, Sifan Liu, Jun Yang
**Published:** 2026-07-13 | **arXiv:** [2607.11631v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11631v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes MAD-Path, a new sampling method for drawing from complex multimodal distributions (distributions with multiple peaks). Instead of the classical tempering approach used in Markov chain Monte Carlo, the authors use diffusion paths—smooth mathematical trajectories from a simple Gaussian distribution to the target—and combine them with a correction mechanism to eliminate sampling bias. The method is important because it better preserves the relative importance of different modes and mixes faster, which matters for Bayesian inference and other applications where you need reliable samples from complicated posterior distributions.

## Theoretical Background
Classical MCMC methods struggle with multimodal distributions because local moves (small random steps) can get trapped in individual modes. A standard fix is **tempering**, which temporarily flattens the distribution by raising it to a fractional power, making it easier to jump between modes. However, tempering distorts how much weight each mode should have.

Diffusion processes provide an alternative: imagine slowly adding noise to your target distribution until it becomes a simple Gaussian—the reverse of a denoising process. The **diffusion path** is the sequence of intermediate distributions along this journey. This paper exploits a key property: diffusion paths preserve relative mode weights naturally. The authors use **score-matching** (learning the gradient of the log-probability) to estimate the scores needed to sample along this path, then apply a **Metropolis correction** (a classical acceptance-rejection mechanism) to remove sampling bias from score estimation and discretization errors.

## Core Contributions
1. **Theoretical justification for diffusion paths in sampling**: A spectral-gap analysis showing that diffusion paths have favorable mixing properties compared to tempering, with precise mathematical guarantees.

2. **MAD-Path sampler**: A practical algorithm that combines diffusion-path proposals with Metropolis correction in an augmented space, ensuring the sampler remains unbiased even when the learned score function is inexact or discretization is coarse.

3. **Error quantification**: Explicit analysis of how score-estimation error and discretization error affect acceptance probability, providing concrete guidance for practitioners tuning the method.

4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrations on Bayesian posteriors showing improved mode-weight estimation and global exploration compared to both tempering-based MCMC and unadjusted diffusion samplers.

## Connections to Other Work
This work bridges diffusion models and classical MCMC. It relates to recent advances in **score-based generative models** (which learn to reverse diffusion) but applies them to sampling, not generation. The Metropolis correction is reminiscent of [[Metropolis-Hastings]] algorithms, ensuring the sampler inherits theoretical guarantees even with approximations. It also connects to ongoing research in combining learned proposals with Bayesian inference, similar in spirit to [[Normalizing Flows]] for variational inference but using diffusion instead of invertible transformations. The tempering alternative it improves upon is related to [[simulated annealing]] and parallel tempering (replica exchange MCMC).

## Practical Applications
1. **Bayesian posterior inference**: Sampling from posterior distributions in high-dimensional Bayesian models (e.g., neural network weight posteriors, hierarchical models) where standard MCMC fails.

2. **Bayesian inverse problems**: Recovering parameters from noisy measurements in engineering and physics, where posteriors are often multimodal.

3. **Model selection**: Computing marginal likelihoods (evidence) for comparing competing Bayesian models, which requires accurate mode-weight estimation.

4. **Uncertainty quantification**: Generating credible intervals and posterior samples for probabilistic forecasting in scientific and financial applications.

5. **Drug discovery**: Sampling molecular conformations and binding modes in computational chemistry, where multimodal distributions naturally arise.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Score function estimation**: Requires training a neural network to estimate scores via variational methods (e.g., score matching); if this learned function is poor, acceptance rates can drop significantly, though the method is still unbiased.

2. **Computational overhead**: Learning the score function adds a training phase before sampling begins; not beneficial for low-dimensional problems or those where classical MCMC is already fast.

3. **Path discretization**: The diffusion path must be discretized into steps; coarse discretization reduces acceptance probability (though bias is removed). Fine discretization increases computation.

4. **Scalability in very high dimensions**: While better than tempering, the method's practical performance on distributions in 10,000+ dimensions with many well-separated modes is untested.

5. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: Choosing the diffusion process schedule, score-network architecture, and number of discretization steps requires careful tuning; no fully automatic guidance given.

6. **Assumption of smooth target density**: Works best when the target has a known unnormalized density; doesn't apply to discrete or purely implicit models.

## Open Questions
1. **Adaptive score learning**: Can scores be learned online *during* sampling (not just before) to improve accuracy and acceptance dynamically?

2. **Parallel and distributed sampling**: How do diffusion paths interact with ensemble samplers (like parallel tempering) for further acceleration?

3. **Connections to neural sampling**: What is the precise relationship between diffusion-path MCMC and recent neural sampler proposals? Can they be unified?

4. **Very-high-dimensional regimes**: How does MAD-Path perform on 1000+ dimensional posteriors from modern machine learning models (e.g., Bayesian neural networks)?

5. **Theoretical convergence rates**: Can you prove explicit convergence rates (e.g., dimension-dependent rates) for MAD-Path with realistic score-estimation error?

6. **Application to constrained sampling**: How would you adapt diffusion paths to sample from distributions with hard constraints (e.g., simplices, manifolds)?

## Key Takeaway
Diffusion-path MCMC preserves mode weights and mixes faster than tempering while remaining provably unbiased despite approximation errors.
