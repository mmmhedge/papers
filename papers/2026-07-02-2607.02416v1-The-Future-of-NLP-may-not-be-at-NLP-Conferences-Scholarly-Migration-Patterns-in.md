# The Future of NLP may not be at NLP Conferences: Scholarly Migration Patterns in Natural Language Processing

**Authors:** David Jurgens
**Published:** 2026-07-02 | **arXiv:** [2607.02416v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02416v1)

## Summary
This paper documents a fundamental shift in where NLP research is being published: fewer papers appear at traditional NLP conferences (like ACL) and more appear at general machine learning venues. The author shows that both established researchers and newcomers are increasingly publishing outside NLP's traditional home, driven partly by citation advantages that general ML venues confer. This matters because it signals that NLP may be losing its identity as a distinct discipline.

## Theoretical Background
Academic disciplines maintain coherence through their publication venues — the conferences and journals where research is presented and evaluated. NLP emerged as a subfield of computational linguistics and developed its own conference ecosystem centered on venues like ACL (Association for Computational Linguistics), EMNLP, and NAACL. Before ~2018, there was relatively clear disciplinary separation: NLP research appeared at NLP venues, computer vision research at vision venues, and general machine learning at ML conferences (NeurIPS, ICML). The rise of large language models (LLMs — neural networks trained on massive text to generate or understand language) blurred these lines because LLM advances often required contributions from machine learning broadly, not NLP-specific methods. This paper uses bibliometric analysis and causal inference (statistical techniques to identify cause-and-effect rather than mere correlation) to quantify that shift.

## Core Contributions
1. **Quantifies migration at scale**: Compares publication patterns across 2010–2026, showing established authors lost 19.2 percentage points at ACL main conference tracks (the field's flagship) while general ML venues gained 8.6pp, even after controlling for field growth.
2. **Documents newcomer behavior change**: Among debut NLP authors, those publishing primarily at ACL venues dropped from 84% (2019) to 74% (2024), while those at general ML venues rose from 5% to 21% — a dramatic 4× increase in just five years.
3. **Identifies citation premium as causal mechanism**: Uses causal inference to estimate that general ML venues confer measurably higher citation counts, which influences where researchers choose to submit their work.
4. **Distinguishes ACL restructuring**: Shows that some shift is to ACL's newer "Findings" tracks (papers accepted with lower acceptance rates), not away from ACL entirely — suggesting NLP is stratifying internally.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper relates to broader discussions about [[Attention Is All You Need]] and the [[transformers]] revolution — the technical shift toward general-purpose neural architectures that work across domains (NLP, vision, multimodal) accelerated the disciplinary blurring documented here. It also connects to conversations about [[large-language-models]] homogenizing AI subdisciplines. The work echoes concerns raised in sociology of science literature about discipline formation and dissolution, but applies that lens empirically to a live research community. The citation premium finding echoes work on venue prestige and researcher incentives in academia.

## Practical Applications
1. **Grant funding allocation**: Funding agencies (NSF, DARPA, EU) could use these patterns to understand where NLP innovation is concentrating and adjust funding venue or track targets accordingly.
2. **Conference strategy**: ACL and other NLP venues could use this data to design tracks or incentives that retain high-impact work (e.g., special tracks for LLM-adjacent research with citation-based merit recognition).
3. **PhD curriculum design**: Universities could use migration data to inform whether to emphasize traditional NLP or position students as general ML researchers with language applications.
4. **Hiring and evaluation**: Academic departments hiring NLP researchers could calibrate expectations about where candidates' best work appears and adjust promotion criteria.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Academic venue recommender**: Tool that predicts optimal submission venue for an NLP paper based on topic, methodology, and author profile, surfacing where similar papers got the most citations.
2. **Disciplinary analytics platform**: SaaS for conferences and professional societies (ACL, ICML, etc.) to monitor in real-time where their researchers are publishing, by career stage and topic, with benchmarking across peer venues.
3. **NLP researcher positioning service**: Consultant firm advising early-career NLP researchers on venue strategy, co-authorship, and framing to maximize impact (citations) given venue-specific dynamics.
4. **Citation-weighted venue comparison engine**: Lightweight tool researchers consult before submitting, showing confidence intervals on expected citations by venue for papers in their topic cluster.
5. **Conference repositioning strategy firm**: Advise struggling NLP conferences on how to rebrand, create special tracks, or partner with ML venues to arrest the decline.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Citation as proxy for impact**: The paper infers that citation premium drives venue choice, but citations conflate true impact with popularity, novelty bias, and network effects (well-connected authors get cited more regardless of venue).
2. **Causality from observational data**: Even with causal inference techniques, the paper cannot definitively prove citation premiums *cause* migration — selection effects are hard to rule out (best authors may simply be choosing general ML venues for independent reasons, and those papers happen to get cited more).
3. **Survivor bias**: The analysis focuses on authors with ≥3 first-author papers; it misses one-time contributors or authors who left the field, whose venue choices might paint a different picture.
4. **Topic classification fidelity**: Determining whether a paper is "NLP-topic" vs. "general ML" is subjective and automated classification may misclassify papers at disciplinary boundaries (e.g., a paper on vision-language models).
5. **Confounding by technology**: The LLM era also coincided with increased funding, compute availability, and cross-disciplinary collaboration — venue choice could be driven by those factors rather than citation premiums per se.

## Open Questions
1. **Is migration permanent or cyclical?**: Will NLP reconstitute as a discipline around specific LLM challenges, or does this represent permanent dissolution into general ML?
2. **What is the quality divergence?**: Do general ML venues actually accept higher-quality NLP work, or do they accept broader work that happens to get cited more for non-scientific reasons? A qualitative review of accepted papers might clarify.
3. **How do other NLP-adjacent fields respond?**: Are computational linguistics, machine translation, information retrieval experiencing the same migration, or is it unique to NLP?
4. **Does citation premium persist?**: Is the citation advantage of general ML venues stable over time, or is it a transient effect of novelty? Will it persist as LLMs mature?
5. **What about funding and employment?**: Do researchers publishing at general ML venues secure more grants or faculty positions? Migration to venues is one thing; career outcomes are another.

## Key Takeaway
The center of gravity in NLP research is shifting away from traditional NLP conferences toward general machine learning venues, driven by citation incentives and the blurring of disciplinary boundaries in the LLM era.
