# It's Not What You Say, It's How You Say It: Evaluating LLM Responses to Expressions of Belief

**Authors:** Kevin Du, Clara Kümpel, Michelle Wastl, Alex Warstadt
**Published:** 2026-07-20 | **arXiv:** [2607.18232v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18232v1)

## Summary
This paper systematically studies how linguistic variation in user statements affects whether LLMs follow the user's stated beliefs or rely on their training knowledge. The authors create a controlled benchmark that pairs different linguistic expressions of belief (using presuppositions, certainty markers, tone) with factual queries, then test 16 models to identify which phrasings are most persuasive—finding that larger and instruction-tuned models are less susceptible to contextual framing than smaller base models.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on foundational NLP research on [[pragmatics]] and linguistic influence, combined with recent work on how LLMs integrate context and prior knowledge. It draws on theories from linguistics about how speakers signal certainty and belief through grammatical markers (evidentiality — linguistic devices that mark sources of information, like "I heard that…" vs "I know that…") and presuppositions (implicit assumptions baked into sentence structure). Prior work has shown that LLMs exhibit sensitivity to prompt wording, but this paper systematically characterizes *which linguistic features* drive that sensitivity using a principled typology grounded in linguistic theory.

## Core Contributions
- **Typology of belief expressions**: A four-dimensional framework (form, evidentiality, epistemic stance, tone) covering 17 fine-grained linguistic types that encode how humans express beliefs.
- **Controlled benchmark**: Pairs of semantically identical queries with different linguistic framings of the same belief, isolating the effect of form from content.
- **Systematic evaluation across model axes**: Tests 16 models varying in architecture, scale (1B–30B parameters), and training stage (base vs. instruct), identifying which model properties correlate with resistance to contextual persuasion.
- **Empirical ranking of persuasiveness**: Quantifies which specific expressions of belief statistically significantly move LLM responses toward context-following vs. prior knowledge.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to [[in-context learning]] (how well LLMs adapt to new information in prompts) and [[prompt engineering]] (the empirical study of phrasing effects on model outputs). It intersects with work on [[jailbreaking]] and adversarial prompts, but inverts the question: rather than asking how to bypass safety measures, it asks what linguistic signals naturally persuade models to shift their answers. It also connects to [[mechanistic interpretability]] — understanding *why* certain framings work may require investigating how model internals process presuppositions and epistemic markers.

## Practical Applications
- **Prompt engineering guidance**: Users and developers can learn which phrasings most reliably guide LLMs to adopt contextual information (useful for retrieval-augmented generation, customer support, and domain-specific applications).
- **Robustness testing**: AI safety teams can use the benchmark to identify which linguistic perturbations cause models to flip answers, helping flag models vulnerable to subtle manipulation.
- **Context-grounding in multi-turn dialogue**: Conversational AI systems can be tuned to recognize high-confidence user statements and weight them appropriately.
- **Accessibility and inclusivity**: Understanding how tone and form affect model responses helps design interfaces where non-native speakers or users with atypical phrasing patterns are still understood.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **LLM prompt optimization SaaS**: A tool that tests user prompts against a database of linguistic variants and recommends phrasing for maximum reliability in domain-specific tasks (legal, medical, financial).
2. **Robustness auditing service**: Offer enterprises a managed service to evaluate their deployed LLMs on the EoB benchmark, flagging linguistic attack surfaces and certifying resilience.
3. **Multilingual belief-expression toolkit**: Extend the typology to languages beyond English; sell a library for developers building truly multilingual chatbots that handle belief statements consistently across languages.
4. **Interactive prompt-engineering tutor**: A browser-based tool that shows users (in real-time) how different linguistic framings change an LLM's answers, with explanations grounded in the typology.
5. **Conversational AI quality control**: Integrate the benchmark into CI/CD pipelines for LLM-driven customer service; alert ops teams when model behavior drifts on belief-sensitive queries.

## Technical Limitations
- **Scope**: The benchmark focuses on factual queries paired with belief expressions; it may not generalize to opinion, ethical, or open-ended tasks where the notion of "prior knowledge" is ill-defined.
- **Model coverage**: Tests 16 models from a narrow slice of the landscape (Llama, Qwen, Gemma); behavior on smaller open-source or closed-source models (GPT-4, Claude) remains unexplored.
- **Language**: Typology grounded in English linguistics; presuppositions, evidential markers, and tone function differently across languages, limiting cross-lingual applicability.
- **Shallow causal claims**: The paper identifies *correlations* between linguistic features and persuasiveness but does not mechanistically explain *why* certain framings work—requires deeper interpretability work.
- **Single-turn queries**: Real-world context integration happens over multi-turn dialogue; the static prompt-pair setup may not capture dynamics of conversation and belief revision.

## Open Questions
- How do these linguistic effects interact with model fine-tuning objectives (RLHF vs. SFT vs. DPO)? Does alignment training systematically reduce susceptibility to belief-expression manipulation?
- Can we identify a causal mechanism by inspecting model activations or attention patterns when processing presuppositions vs. assertions?
- Do these patterns replicate in languages with richer evidential systems (Turkish, Japanese, Quechua)? Does the typology need expansion?
- How does multi-turn dialogue change the picture? Does a user's first statement "anchor" the model, or does it continually re-evaluate?
- Are there linguistic markers that reliably *increase* model reliance on prior knowledge (anti-persuasion), useful for safety-critical applications?

## Key Takeaway
Linguistic framing of user beliefs systematically shifts LLM responses; larger models resist context more than smaller ones.
