# Language-Critique Imitation Learning from Suboptimal Demonstrations

**Authors:** Chih-Han Yang, Dai-Jie Wu, Yun-Ping Huang, Ping-Chun Hsieh, Kenneth Marino et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-01 | **arXiv:** [2607.01225v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01225v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes using natural language feedback as structured supervision for training policies from suboptimal (imperfect) demonstrations. Instead of compressing feedback into single scores or weights, the method generates language descriptions of what went wrong and how to fix it, then trains policies to follow these critiques. It works on both standard behavior cloning and diffusion-based policies, and shows improvements over existing imitation learning approaches on navigation, manipulation, and game tasks.

## Theoretical Background
Imitation learning trains policies by mimicking expert demonstrations, but when those demonstrations are suboptimal — imperfect or mediocre rather than expert-level — naive copying performs poorly. Prior work addresses this by assigning quality scores to each demonstration (via discriminators, confidence estimates, or importance weighting) and down-weighting bad ones. However, these scalar signals discard information: they say "this trajectory is 60% good" but not *why* or *how to improve it*.

This paper builds on two established ideas: (1) behavior cloning and diffusion policies — methods that directly learn to reproduce actions from data without explicit reward signals; and (2) the observation that language can encode rich, compositional feedback about task progress and failure modes. The theoretical foundation includes standard imitation learning convergence theory, which the authors extend to show their loss function upper-bounds the gap between learned and expert performance.

## Core Contributions
1. **Language-critique framework**: A method to automatically construct natural language labels from suboptimal trajectories that describe current progress, identify failure modes, and suggest corrections — preserving information that scalar weights discard.

2. **Language-critique loss**: A novel training objective that directly incorporates structured language feedback into policy learning without reducing it to scalars, instantiated for both behavior cloning (LC-BC) and diffusion policies (LC-DP).

3. **Theoretical guarantee**: A convergence result showing the proposed objective upper-bounds the expert performance gap under standard assumptions (details not specified in abstract, but suggests principled grounding).

4. **Empirical validation**: Consistent improvements over imitation learning and offline RL baselines across continuous control domains (navigation, manipulation, gameplay).

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to several active areas:

- **[[Imitation Learning]]**: Extends classical behavior cloning by using richer feedback; complements discriminator-based approaches ([[GAIL]]-style methods) that use scalar classification scores.
- **[[Offline Reinforcement Learning]]**: Addresses the same problem (learning from fixed suboptimal data) but via reward modeling rather than language; this work shows language can compete or outperform learned rewards.
- **[[Language-conditioned policies]]**: Related to work on policies that take language instructions, but here language is used as *supervision signal* during training rather than a conditioning input at test time.
- **[[Diffusion Models for Control]]**: Extends recent diffusion-based policy learning with structured feedback mechanisms.
- **LLM-as-feedback**: Aligns with emerging work on using large language models to critique demonstrations, though this paper may construct critiques procedurally rather than via LLM calls.

## Practical Applications
1. **Robot learning from human demos**: A robot learns manipulation from videos of humans performing tasks imperfectly; language critiques (e.g., "gripper too slow," "approach angle wrong") guide the policy toward improvement without perfect examples.

2. **Autonomous driving simulation**: Learn driving policies from crowdsourced or cheap simulator trajectories that contain mistakes; language describes collision risks, traffic violations, or inefficient routing and trains safer policies.

3. **Game AI training**: Train game-playing agents from non-expert replays by providing language feedback about strategic errors ("overextended position," "suboptimal resource allocation"), enabling agents to surpass the original players.

4. **Surgical/medical training**: Critique recorded procedures (even by less experienced surgeons) with structured language feedback about technique, efficiency, or safety — without requiring expert-only data.

5. **Content creation & editing**: Train models to improve user-generated content (writing, design, music) by learning critiques of common flaws, enabling scalable refinement.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **"CritiqueBot for Robotics"**: A service that automatically labels robot demonstration videos with natural language critiques of suboptimal actions, enabling companies to train better policies from cheaper, non-expert data.

2. **AI coaching platform for autonomous systems**: SaaS offering language-based feedback generation and retraining for fleet management, delivery, or logistics companies to improve policies on real-world imperfect data.

3. **Surgical/medical simulation & training**: Startup selling software that learns from recorded procedures (including non-expert ones) using language critique, generating personalized coaching for trainees on specific failure modes.

4. **Game development tool**: Plugin for game engines that learns NPC behavior from designer-authored but imperfect demo videos, auto-critiquing and improving via language feedback — reducing hand-engineering of AI.

5. **Autonomous vehicle safety layer**: Real-time system that generates language critiques of edge cases from naturalistic driving logs, enabling continuous policy improvement without needing labeled expert data.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Language generation burden**: The method requires automatically constructing language labels for trajectories. The paper doesn't detail how critiques are generated (template-based? LLM-based? hand-annotated?), and this step likely requires domain knowledge and is non-trivial at scale.

2. **Scalability of language processing**: Training a policy to consume and act on natural language feedback adds computational overhead compared to scalar weights; unclear how this scales to high-dimensional or long-horizon tasks.

3. **Circular reasoning risk**: If critiques are generated from the same trajectory the policy learns from, there's risk of encoding the same errors rather than correcting them.

4. **Evaluation limited to continuous control**: Results shown on navigation, manipulation, and games — all relatively structured domains. Generalization to higher-dimensional or partially observable environments unclear.

5. **Dependence on language quality**: The method's success likely hinges on the quality and relevance of generated critiques; poor or misaligned language feedback could degrade learning, but robustness to critique errors is not analyzed.

6. **Theoretical assumptions**: The convergence guarantee relies on "standard assumptions" not detailed in the abstract; these may not hold in practice (e.g., function approximation error, limited data).

## Open Questions
1. **Critique generation**: How are language critiques actually constructed? Is there an LLM in the loop, or are they template-based? How sensitive is performance to critique quality or specificity?

2. **Comparison to LLM feedback**: Does using an LLM (GPT-style) to critique trajectories in natural language outperform or underperform this method? Is the gain from structure itself, or from the additional model capacity?

3. **Partial observability**: How does the method perform when the policy cannot fully observe the state (e.g., visual navigation with occlusions)? Can language critiques help bridge observability gaps?

4. **Long-horizon tasks**: Does language feedback help more for short-horizon tasks (where scalar weights suffice) or long-horizon ones (where intermediate milestones matter)? What's the horizon limit?

5. **Human-in-the-loop**: Can human annotators provide critiques more cheaply or accurately than automatic generation? Does human feedback improve over procedural critique?

6. **Failure modes**: What kinds of suboptimal demonstrations or task structures cause the method to fail? When is a scalar signal actually sufficient?

## Key Takeaway
Natural language feedback can serve as a richer supervision signal than scalar weights for learning policies from imperfect data, avoiding the information loss of scalar compression while providing interpretable, actionable guidance about failure modes and corrections.
