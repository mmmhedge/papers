# Relational and Sequential Conformal Inference for Energy Time Series over Graphs via Foundation Models

**Authors:** Keivan Faghih Niresi, Alice Cicirello, Olga Fink
**Published:** 2026-06-30 | **arXiv:** [2606.31804v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31804v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes STOIC, a framework that combines spatial-temporal graph neural networks with tabular foundation models to generate reliable uncertainty estimates for energy demand forecasting. Rather than just predicting single values, STOIC produces prediction intervals (ranges) with statistical guarantees, which is critical for operators managing power grids and heating networks under uncertainty.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two main pillars. First, spatial-temporal graph neural networks (STGNNs) — neural networks that model both time dynamics and relationships between connected nodes (like substations in a power grid). Second, conformal prediction — a statistical technique that wraps around any existing model to produce prediction intervals with guaranteed coverage rates, meaning they're provably reliable even without knowing the underlying data distribution. The innovation here is using foundation models (large pre-trained models that work across tasks) in a zero-shot manner, meaning they work without task-specific retraining. The key insight is reformulating spatial-temporal residuals (errors from the STGNN forecast) into tabular form so a foundation model can calibrate them using in-context learning (learning from examples in the input itself).

## Core Contributions
1. **STOIC framework**: A two-stage pipeline that first uses STGNNs for point forecasts, then uses a tabular foundation model to calibrate spatial-temporal residuals into prediction intervals without retraining.
2. **Reformulation strategy**: Converting graph-structured residuals into tabular representations that preserve sequential and relational dependencies, enabling foundation models to capture complex patterns.
3. **Empirical validation**: Comprehensive evaluation on five benchmarks (synthetic + real electricity/heating networks) showing consistent improvements over existing conformal prediction baselines.
4. **Zero-shot capability**: Demonstrating that foundation models can calibrate energy forecasts without task-specific fine-tuning, reducing computational overhead.

## Practical Applications
1. **Grid stability**: Operators use prediction intervals to set reserve margins and prevent blackouts when demand is uncertain.
2. **Economic dispatch**: Energy companies optimize generation and purchasing decisions while accounting for demand uncertainty, reducing both waste and cost.
3. **Renewable integration**: Wind/solar forecasters use uncertainty bands to plan storage and backup capacity for variable generation.
4. **District heating**: Facility operators schedule thermal generation and maintenance based on heating demand ranges rather than single guesses.
5. **Risk-aware billing**: Utilities can set dynamic pricing that reflects demand uncertainty, protecting profit margins during unpredictable periods.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Energy Risk Dashboard SaaS**: A cloud platform for grid operators and utilities that ingests real-time grid data, produces conformal prediction intervals for demand/generation 24-72 hours ahead, and recommends risk-mitigation actions (reserve levels, maintenance windows). Target: regional grid operators and municipal utilities.
2. **Predictive Maintenance for Substations**: Combine STOIC's uncertainty estimates with equipment stress models to forecast when transformer cooling or line capacity will be exceeded with high confidence, enabling proactive maintenance scheduling. Target: transmission operators.
3. **Renewable Energy Trading Bot**: Use STOIC's prediction intervals to trade electricity futures and intraday markets, exploiting the value of reliable uncertainty (wider intervals in volatile regions = better hedging). Target: energy trading firms.
4. **Microgrid Autonomy Layer**: Embed STOIC in distributed microgrid controllers so they can run resilient load-balancing and storage scheduling using confidence-bounded forecasts, without central authority. Target: industrial campuses, military bases.
5. **Insurance/Financial Products**: Develop structured financial products (demand hedges, capacity-shortage insurance) priced using STOIC's calibrated intervals, enabling utilities and large consumers to manage energy cost volatility. Target: energy finance firms.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Foundation model dependency**: STOIC's performance depends critically on the choice and quality of the tabular foundation model — the paper doesn't explore sensitivity to this choice or failure modes when the model is misaligned with energy data.
2. **Scalability to massive graphs**: Tests on five benchmarks; unclear how STOIC scales to continental-scale grids with tens of thousands of nodes. Graph encoding into tabular form may lose structure at very large scales.
3. **Out-of-distribution robustness**: Conformal prediction guarantees hold only if calibration and test data are exchangeable (same underlying distribution). Energy systems experience regime shifts (e.g., renewable penetration changes, weather patterns, policy shocks) that may violate this assumption.
4. **Computational cost not fully addressed**: Reformulating spatial-temporal residuals into tabular form and running foundation model inference adds overhead; wall-clock time comparisons vs. baselines are missing.
5. **Limited ablation studies**: The paper doesn't isolate the contribution of relational vs. sequential dependencies in the calibration step, making it unclear which aspect drives improvements.
6. **Synthetic data mismatch**: While synthetic simulations are controlled, they may not capture real grid dynamics (e.g., unmodeled constraints, operator behavior, cascading failures).

## Open Questions
1. How does STOIC perform during extreme events (heatwaves, ice storms, cyber-attacks) where the distribution shift is severe?
2. Can the framework be extended to joint forecasting of multiple interconnected variables (demand, wind generation, voltage) with correlated uncertainties?
3. How sensitive is the method to the choice of foundation model architecture and pre-training data? Would a domain-specific foundation model (pre-trained only on energy data) improve calibration?
4. Can conformal prediction intervals be tightened by incorporating domain constraints (e.g., physical laws, operational limits) or is there a fundamental trade-off with coverage guarantees?
5. How do prediction intervals degrade over longer horizons (e.g., week-ahead vs. day-ahead), and can adaptive calibration mitigate this?
6. Could adversarial examples or deliberately misreported grid data break the statistical guarantees, and how can STOIC be made robust to such attacks?

## Key Takeaway
STOIC combines spatial-temporal graph neural networks with zero-shot calibration from foundation models to produce provably reliable uncertainty estimates for energy forecasting without task-specific retraining, enabling risk-aware decision-making in critical infrastructure.
