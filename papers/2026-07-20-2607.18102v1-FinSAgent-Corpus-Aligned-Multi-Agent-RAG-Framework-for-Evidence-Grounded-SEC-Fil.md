# FinSAgent: Corpus-Aligned Multi-Agent RAG Framework for Evidence-Grounded SEC Filing Question Answering

**Authors:** Jijun Chi, Zhenghan Tai, Hanwei Wu, Tung Sum Thomas Kwok, Hailin He et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-20 | **arXiv:** [2607.18102v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18102v1)

## Summary
This paper presents FinSAgent, a multi-agent framework that answers questions about SEC financial filings by better aligning retrieval and reasoning with the actual structure and terminology of the filings themselves. The key insight is that existing systems fail because they rely on generic semantic similarity matching, which misses domain-specific evidence and retrieves irrelevant chunks — FinSAgent fixes this by conditioning each agent's searches on the filing's known structure (10-K items) and using a learned reranker that distinguishes evidential validity from topical similarity.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two foundational paradigms: retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) — using external document retrieval to ground language model outputs in evidence — and multi-agent systems, where specialized agents coordinate to solve complex tasks. It addresses a well-known problem in information retrieval: prior-corpus misalignment, the gap between what a model thinks is relevant (based on semantic similarity) and what a domain requires (based on evidential validity and structural fit). The 10-K filing structure referenced is a standardized SEC form with mandatory disclosure items (e.g., "Risk Factors," "Business Overview"), which provides fixed organizational anchors absent from unstructured corpora. The learned reranking approach extends classical semantic ranking by adding an explicit separation between topical relevance and domain-specific correctness.

## Core Contributions
1. **Corpus-aligned query decomposition**: Instead of generating queries directly from user questions, FinSAgent generates sub-queries per filing section, informed by a lightweight summary-level view of the corpus itself.
2. **Role-specialized multi-agent architecture**: Agents are anchored to mandatory 10-K item structure rather than generic roles, embedding domain constraints directly into agent design.
3. **Feature-gated reranker**: A learned model that explicitly separates evidential validity from semantic similarity, preventing false positives that are topically plausible but factually invalid.
4. **Empirical validation**: Improvements on five offline benchmarks and a three-arm randomized online experiment (1,000 user ratings), demonstrating both correctness and user preference gains.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[Retrieval-Augmented Generation]] systems and [[Multi-Agent Systems]]. It addresses the "query formulation gap" seen in prior RAG work and extends recent multi-agent reasoning approaches (e.g., [[Chain-of-Thought]] variants using specialized agents) by adding corpus awareness. Unlike generic question-answering systems, FinSAgent is domain-specific, similar in spirit to specialized [[Named Entity Recognition]] and relation extraction systems for finance. The reranking component echoes [[Dense Passage Retrieval]] and [[ColBERT]], but innovates by learning to weight evidential signals beyond semantic overlap. It also relates to work on structured information extraction from long documents, a challenge in finance and legal AI.

## Practical Applications
1. **Investor due diligence**: Institutional investors conducting M&A or equity research can query 10-Ks directly to extract financial metrics, risk factors, and competitive positioning without manual document review.
2. **Compliance and regulatory analysis**: Compliance teams can verify that filings contain required disclosures and extract specific regulatory commitments across multiple years.
3. **Financial analyst augmentation**: Sell-side and buy-side analysts use the system to accelerate report writing by automatically retrieving and synthesizing evidence from company filings.
4. **Shareholder litigation support**: Lawyers identifying evidence of misleading disclosures can search filings for inconsistencies or omissions in standardized sections.
5. **Academic finance research**: Researchers studying disclosure patterns, risk management practices, or management tone can systematically extract structured evidence across thousands of filings.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **FinAnalytics Pro**: SaaS platform where investors upload a list of ticker symbols and ask natural-language questions (e.g., "Which companies in my watchlist increased R&D spending and why?") — FinSAgent retrieves and synthesizes evidence across all filings, producing reports ready for investment committees.
2. **SEC Filing Compliance Checker**: Automated tool for law firms and corporates to verify that upcoming 10-K/10-Q filings contain all mandatory disclosures and highlight gaps before submission; uses corpus-aligned retrieval to flag missing sections.
3. **Financial Research API**: B2B API exposing FinSAgent's retrieval and QA capabilities so financial data aggregators (e.g., Bloomberg, FactSet competitors) can integrate evidence-grounded filing search into their platforms.
4. **RiskQuery**: Search engine for CFOs and FP&A teams to query their own and competitors' SEC filings for risk disclosures, covenant terms, and contingent liabilities; surfaces evidence flagged by domain specialists.
5. **Litigation Discovery Accelerator**: Legal tech tool using FinSAgent to search across thousands of archived filings and corporate documents for factual inconsistencies, evidence of knowledge, and timeline reconstruction in securities fraud cases.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scope limitation**: Works only on standardized, structured filings (10-K, 10-Q). Other financial documents (earnings call transcripts, proxy statements, 8-K filings) have different structures and are not addressed.
2. **Pre-training dependency**: The system's reranker and query decomposition are learned models; performance degrades if fine-tuning data is sparse or if new corpus distributions (e.g., micro-cap or international filings) diverge sharply from training.
3. **Hallucination risk**: Even with corpus conditioning, the underlying language model can still generate plausible-sounding false answers; the system does not guarantee factual accuracy, only improved retrieval grounding.
4. **Computational cost**: Multi-agent orchestration with database-aware query decomposition and multi-path retrieval incurs higher latency than single-pass RAG; real-time deployment for high-volume queries may be expensive.
5. **Redundancy handling**: While the paper acknowledges SEC filings are "highly redundant," the approach does not explicitly handle cross-filing temporal consistency or conflicting disclosures across fiscal years.
6. **Explainability**: The learned reranker is treated as a black box; users cannot easily inspect why a chunk was ranked high, limiting adoption in highly regulated or audit-sensitive settings.

## Open Questions
1. **Cross-filing temporal reasoning**: Can the framework be extended to synthesize evidence across multiple years of filings and flag inconsistencies or reversals in management disclosures?
2. **Generalization to other domains**: Does the corpus-aligned conditioning principle transfer to other standardized document corpora (e.g., medical records, legal contracts, patent databases)?
3. **Interactive refinement**: Can users iteratively refine queries based on intermediate results, and does interactive feedback improve the system's recall and precision in practice?
4. **Adversarial robustness**: How does the system perform when companies deliberately obscure or bury important disclosures in less-standard sections, and can it detect such evasion?
5. **Integration with numerical reasoning**: SEC filings contain tables, footnotes, and complex numerical relationships; does FinSAgent extend to answering questions requiring arithmetic or financial ratio calculation?
6. **Zero-shot domain transfer**: Can the same agents and reranker work on 10-Q or 8-K filings without retraining, or does the fixed 10-K structure limit flexibility?

## Key Takeaway
Aligning retrieval to a document corpus's structure and domain constraints, rather than generic similarity, dramatically improves financial Q&A accuracy and evidence quality.
