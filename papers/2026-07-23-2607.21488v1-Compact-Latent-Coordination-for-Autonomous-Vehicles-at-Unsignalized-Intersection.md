# Compact Latent Coordination for Autonomous Vehicles at Unsignalized Intersections

**Authors:** Gil Lifshits, Igal Bilik, Gilad Katz
**Published:** 2026-07-23 | **arXiv:** [2607.21488v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21488v1)

## Summary
This paper presents MAPS, a hierarchical deep reinforcement learning system for coordinating autonomous vehicles at unsignalized intersections without relying on traffic signals or privileged communication. A centralized "Master" agent learns a compact continuous embedding (proto-plan) that encodes coordination strategy, which decentralized "Worker" agents use alongside local observations to control individual vehicles. The approach achieves collision-free navigation with reduced travel times and generalizes remarkably well — a system trained on three agents works zero-shot on five-agent scenarios with 94% success.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), where multiple learning agents optimize policies simultaneously in shared environments. Traditional MARL struggles with: combinatorial explosion of joint action spaces (grows exponentially with agent count), the need for privileged information (global state unavailable to real vehicles), and rigid agent architectures that don't scale across different numbers of agents.

The paper leverages hierarchical reinforcement learning — where high-level strategic decisions (Master) decouple from low-level execution (Workers) — combined with continuous embedding spaces (encoding coordination as learned vectors rather than discrete symbols). This builds conceptually on prior work in centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE), where agents share information during training but act independently at deployment.

## Core Contributions
1. **Proto-plan abstraction**: A novel compact continuous embedding that encodes coordination strategy, allowing a single Master agent to communicate implicit coordination intent to multiple Workers without explicit message passing.
2. **Decoupling architecture**: Clean separation between strategic (Master) and tactical (Worker) optimization, enabling each module to improve independently.
3. **Strong generalization**: Empirical demonstration that proto-plan-based coordination generalizes to unseen agent counts (3→5 agents) zero-shot with 94% success, a significant advantage over prior MARL methods that typically overfit to specific team sizes.
4. **Unsignalized intersection focus**: Tackles a real autonomous driving problem where communication is unreliable and no external infrastructure is available.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to broader challenges in [[multi-agent reinforcement learning]] around scalability and communication efficiency. Prior approaches include:
- **Explicit communication**: Agents learn to send discrete messages (e.g., CommNet, QMIX), but this requires designing message protocols.
- **Graph neural networks**: Some MARL methods use GNNs to aggregate neighbor information, but assume fixed network topology.
- **Hierarchical RL**: Differs from prior hierarchical methods by emphasizing continuous embeddings rather than discrete skill/option libraries.

The work also connects to inverse reinforcement learning and imitation learning concepts — the proto-plan is analogous to learning an interpretable representation of the "intent" a coordinating planner would use.

## Practical Applications
1. **Unsignalized intersection management**: Deploy at rural intersections or developing regions where traffic signals are unavailable or unreliable.
2. **Autonomous vehicle fleets in mixed traffic**: Coordinate company-owned AVs navigating urban environments with human drivers and no signal support.
3. **Emergency response coordination**: Coordinate police, ambulance, and fire vehicles through intersections without relying on external infrastructure.
4. **Connected vehicle retrofits**: Enable coordination in legacy vehicles upgraded with simple sensors and V2X (vehicle-to-everything) radios, without requiring powerful central servers.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Autonomous intersection controller as a service**: Sell MAPS-based coordination stack to municipalities as a cloud service; vehicles upload local sensor data, receive steering recommendations in real-time. Minimal infrastructure cost vs. traditional traffic signals.
2. **Fleet coordination middleware for delivery companies**: Optimize delivery routing and intersection traversal for Uber Eats, Amazon Flex, or logistics fleets; licensing the proto-plan architecture per vehicle.
3. **Digital twin for traffic planning**: Provide city planners a simulation tool (similar to HighwayEnv) to test intersection designs against multi-agent coordination models before construction.
4. **V2X-enabled autonomous taxi dispatcher**: Operate autonomous taxi fleets in regions with poor signal infrastructure; proto-plans coordinate fleet-wide routing and intersection crossing.
5. **Safety validation platform**: Offer testing/certification services for autonomous vehicle manufacturers; run MAPS and competitors through standardized intersection scenarios to audit safety claims.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Limited realism**: Tested only in HighwayEnv (2D simplified simulator); real intersections have occlusions, uncertain sensor data, human unpredictability, and non-uniform road geometries.
2. **Master-Worker bottleneck**: The centralized Master agent could become a communication bottleneck or single point of failure in real deployments; latency or packet loss in proto-plan transmission isn't analyzed.
3. **Training data assumptions**: Assumes during training all agents have access to global state and can backpropagate through the Master. Real AVs have no such privilege.
4. **Modest agent count**: Tested on up to five agents; unclear if proto-plans scale to 10+ vehicles or handle heterogeneous vehicle types (cars vs. trucks vs. motorcycles).
5. **No human interaction**: Doesn't address how AVs should coordinate with human-driven vehicles at unsignalized intersections, which is the dominant real-world scenario.
6. **Proto-plan interpretability**: The paper doesn't explain what the learned embeddings actually encode — are they understandable to regulators or safety auditors?

## Open Questions
1. **Real-world validation**: Does MAPS transfer to actual traffic camera footage, real sensor noise, and human driver behavior? What is the sim-to-real gap?
2. **Scalability limits**: At what agent count does the proto-plan abstraction break? Can it handle 50+ vehicles in a parking garage or highway merge?
3. **Adversarial robustness**: How does MAPS perform against adversarial agents, communication delays, or sensor faults?
4. **Human-AV mixed scenarios**: What happens when some agents are human-driven (follow simple heuristics) and others are trained on MAPS?
5. **Proto-plan interpretation**: Can we interpret or visualize what proto-plans encode? Is there a semantic structure (e.g., "aggressive vs. cautious" or "northbound priority")?
6. **Comparison to classical control**: How does MAPS compare to handcrafted rule-based coordination or model-predictive control for intersection management?

## Key Takeaway
Hierarchical agents with learned compact embeddings can coordinate autonomous vehicles across unsignalized intersections and generalize to unseen team sizes.
