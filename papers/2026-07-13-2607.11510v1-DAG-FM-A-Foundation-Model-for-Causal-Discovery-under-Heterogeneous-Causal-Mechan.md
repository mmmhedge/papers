# DAG-FM: A Foundation Model for Causal Discovery under Heterogeneous Causal Mechanisms

**Authors:** Yikang Chen, Zhengkang Guan, Haoyuan Qian, Peng Cui, Yi Yang et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-13 | **arXiv:** [2607.11510v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11510v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces DAG-FM, a foundation model that discovers causal relationships in tabular data by learning to infer the structure of Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs — networks showing which variables cause which). The key innovation is handling *heterogeneous causal mechanisms* — real-world datasets where different variables follow different causal rules — using a mixture-of-experts approach that lets the model adapt to unknown assumptions about how causes produce effects.

## Theoretical Background
Causal discovery seeks to identify which variables influence which others from observational data alone, which is notoriously hard because many different causal structures can produce the same statistical patterns. Traditional approaches use constraint-based or score-based algorithms, but these struggle with high-dimensional data and don't scale well.

The paper builds on recent work using neural networks for causal discovery, particularly models that amortize the problem — training once to solve many instances rather than optimizing separately for each dataset. The core mathematical object is a DAG (a directed graph with no cycles), often paired with a Functional Causal Model (FCM) that specifies how each variable is computed from its causes plus noise.

The paper uses Transformers — neural networks with attention mechanisms that excel at capturing dependencies — and combines them with a Mixture-of-Experts layer (a technique where multiple specialized sub-networks compete to handle different input patterns).

## Core Contributions
1. **Decomposed auto-regressive framework**: Instead of directly predicting the entire causal graph, DAG-FM predicts causal structure step-by-step: first identifying "leaf nodes" (variables with no parents), then predicting parents for remaining nodes. This reduces the combinatorial search space.

2. **Mixture-of-Leaf-Experts (MoLE)**: A dynamic routing mechanism that allows the model to adapt to different functional causal assumptions (e.g., linear vs. nonlinear relationships, different noise distributions) without being told which assumption applies to each dataset.

3. **Tabular interaction block**: A module specifically designed to capture complex dependencies between rows and columns in tabular data, rather than treating data as a flat sequence.

4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets, substantially outperforming classical algorithms and recent neural baselines.

## Connections to Other Work
This extends recent neural approaches to causal discovery (which moved away from purely classical methods) by adding the flexibility of mixture-of-experts routing. It relates to work on [[Transformers]] for structured prediction and to broader efforts in amortized inference — the idea that a single trained model can efficiently solve many problem instances.

The heterogeneous mechanism challenge connects to broader robustness and domain adaptation questions in machine learning: how do we build models that work across different underlying data-generating processes?

## Practical Applications
1. **Scientific discovery**: Automatically identify causal relationships in biology, medicine, or economics datasets where experimental intervention is expensive or unethical.

2. **Policy evaluation**: Determine which policy levers (government spending, regulations) actually affect outcomes like unemployment or public health in real-world observational data.

3. **Root cause analysis in industry**: Identify which operational variables cause quality issues, system failures, or safety incidents in manufacturing or IT systems.

4. **Financial risk assessment**: Discover causal drivers of market movements or portfolio risk without assuming a fixed model structure.

5. **Healthcare**: Infer causal effects of treatments from electronic health records to guide clinical decision-making.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Causal Analytics SaaS**: A no-code platform where analysts upload tabular datasets and get causal graphs with uncertainty quantification; marketed to business intelligence and data science teams.

2. **Automated Root Cause Detection**: Integration with data warehouses/BI tools (Tableau, Looker, Snowflake) that automatically surfaces causal drivers of anomalies in KPIs.

3. **Clinical Decision Support**: A medical-grade tool that discovers treatment effects from hospital data; sold to health systems and integrated into EHRs.

4. **Policy Impact Modeling**: A service for government agencies and NGOs to rapidly estimate causal effects of policy interventions from administrative datasets.

5. **Pharmaceutical Causal Discovery**: A tool for pharma/biotech to accelerate target discovery by inferring causal pathways from omics and phenotype data, competing with traditional wet-lab approaches.

## Technical Limitations
1. **DAG identifiability**: The paper doesn't deeply address when causal structures are *not* identifiable from data alone — some DAGs will always produce identical statistical distributions. The model can't overcome fundamental information-theoretic limits.

2. **Mechanism assumption mismatch**: While MoLE adapts to different mechanisms, it's unclear how it performs when the true mechanism doesn't match any of the learned expert families.

3. **Scalability to very large graphs**: The auto-regressive inference still requires iterative prediction; true scalability to thousands of variables is unclear.

4. **Validation challenge**: The paper relies heavily on synthetic benchmarks where the ground-truth DAG is known. Real-world validation is harder — you can't easily verify causal claims.

5. **Tabular data only**: The approach is designed for structured tables; applicability to images, text, or mixed modalities is unclear.

6. **Computational cost of training**: Foundation models are expensive to train; the practical cost advantage over running classical algorithms on new datasets isn't quantified.

## Open Questions
1. How does DAG-FM degrade gracefully when data violate the model's assumptions about causal mechanisms? Can it detect when it's out-of-distribution?

2. Can you reliably recover causal graphs in the presence of hidden confounders (unmeasured variables that affect multiple measured ones)?

3. How does sample complexity scale with the number of variables and the complexity of causal relationships? What's the theoretical sample complexity?

4. How would you extend this to time-series data or dynamical causal systems where causes and effects are separated in time?

5. Can you combine this with domain knowledge (e.g., known constraints on which edges can exist) to improve sample efficiency and accuracy?

6. How does performance vary with different types of non-identifiability? Can the model quantify its confidence in different causal claims?

## Key Takeaway
A neural model discovers causal structures in tabular data by routing to the right mechanism assumption, outperforming classical methods.
