# When Words Are Safe But Actions Kill: Probing Physical Danger Beyond Text Safety in Hidden-State Risk Space

**Authors:** Weimeng Wang, Ziqiang Wang, Zihang Zhan, Chuanpu Fu, Qi Li et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.15218v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15218v1)

## Summary
This paper reveals that LLMs face two distinct safety problems: text-level harm (e.g., violent instructions) and physical-world danger (e.g., safe-sounding instructions that cause injury when a robot executes them). The authors show these dangers activate different parts of the model's internal representations, then build a lightweight detector (PRISM) that catches physical risks while avoiding false alarms on benign tasks—where standard safety filters fail.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on two research threads. First, mechanistic interpretability studies how neural networks encode concepts in their hidden states by analyzing learned directional patterns (similar to work on [[superposition]] and concept decomposition). Second, it extends text-level safety research (e.g., content moderation via classifiers) into the embodied AI domain, where language grounding introduces new failure modes. The key insight is that hidden-state probing — training linear classifiers on model activations — can reveal latent safety signals that language-level heuristics miss.

## Core Contributions
1. **CD/PD separability discovery**: Using hidden-state direction analysis, the authors prove that content danger and physical danger form orthogonal or near-orthogonal signals in LLM representations across multiple model scales (3B–32B parameters). This challenges the assumption that all safety issues are linguistically rooted.
2. **PRISM detector**: A single-layer L2-regularized logistic probe achieving 86–88% accuracy on SafeAgentBench with 11–14% false positive rate (vs. 25–39% for LLM judges), and 99.6% accuracy on their new physical-risk benchmark.
3. **PhysicalSafetyBench-1K (PSB-1K)**: A contrastive dataset of 1,000 physical-risk pairs designed to isolate physical grounding danger from explicit unsafe wording, enabling fairer evaluation.

## Connections to Other Work
This extends [[mechanistic interpretability]] into safety by treating dangerous knowledge as a latent direction. It complements recent work on [[RLHF]] safety—while RLHF trains models to refuse harmful queries at the token level, PRISM detects harm encoded in hidden states before generation. Relates to embodied AI safety (e.g., [[AI2-Thor]], robotics instruction grounding) and the broader alignment problem of ensuring agent actions match human intent. Similar in spirit to [[model editing]] and [[concept erasure]] but applied to safety detection rather than removal.

## Practical Applications
1. **Robotic planning systems**: Filter dangerous instructions before they reach embodied agents (e.g., "gently remove the object" interpreted as disassembling a live device).
2. **Autonomous vehicle planners**: Catch scenarios where text is innocuous but execution poses collision or injury risk.
3. **Industrial automation safety**: Monitor high-level task descriptions fed to factory robots to prevent accidents from benign-sounding commands.
4. **Dialogue systems for safety-critical domains**: Pre-deployment screening of LLM outputs in healthcare or aviation before human execution.
5. **Incident investigation**: Retroactively analyze logs to identify which instruction inputs triggered physical harm.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **PhysicalGuard (B2B SaaS)**: Cloud API that takes LLM outputs + task context and returns physical safety scores; white-label for robotics/autonomous vehicle companies.
2. **Safety Probe Marketplace**: Open-source library of pre-trained PRISM probes for different model sizes and domains (e.g., medical, construction, manufacturing); monetize via enterprise support.
3. **EmbodiedAI Safety Certification**: Third-party auditing service that validates LLM-based robotic systems before deployment by running PRISM + adversarial red-teaming.
4. **Real-time Guardrail Middleware**: Lightweight inference middleware that sits between LLM and robot controller, applying PRISM checks with <10ms latency.
5. **PhysicalSafetyBench Premium**: Commercial benchmark and continuous evaluation suite for enterprises building embodied AI, with curated adversarial physical-risk datasets per domain.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Probe generalization**: PRISM is trained on SafeAgentBench; transfer to novel robot morphologies, environments, or task domains unclear. PSB-1K is manually curated (1K pairs); scalability to broader physical contexts needs validation.
2. **Hidden-state access required**: Method assumes white-box access to LLM activations; does not work for API-only models (e.g., Claude, GPT-4).
3. **Context-dependency**: Physical safety is often contextual (a hammer is safe for construction, dangerous in a hospital). PRISM as presented may not encode rich task/environment context.
4. **Downstream agent compliance**: Detecting a risky instruction is only half the problem; ensuring the agent actually refuses or adapts remains an open control problem.
5. **Adversarial robustness**: No evaluation against adaptive attacks (e.g., obfuscated descriptions of dangerous actions). Text-level adversarial examples may fool the probe.
6. **Scalability to state-of-art models**: Evaluated on relatively small models (3B–32B); behavior on GPT-4, Claude-scale models unknown.

## Open Questions
1. Can PRISM generalize to new embodied platforms (e.g., humanoid robots, drones) without retraining? Is a single universal probe feasible?
2. How does probe performance degrade with model finetuning, quantization, or distillation?
3. Can CD/PD separability be leveraged to surgically remove physical danger without harming benign reasoning?
4. Does PRISM work on multimodal models (LLMs + vision) where danger grounding includes visual state?
5. Can hidden-state analysis predict which *sequences of actions* become unsafe in aggregate (multi-step plans)?
6. How does PRISM interact with adversarial prompting, jailbreaks, or in-context learning attempts to circumvent safety?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs encode physical danger as a distinct hidden signal separable from text-level harm; low-resource probes can detect it better than standard safety filters.
