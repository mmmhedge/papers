# Herding and Liquidity in Order-Book Markets. I. A Robust Liquidity-Stress Crossover and its Reflexive Mechanism

**Authors:** Jan Novotny
**Published:** 2026-07-09 | **arXiv:** [2607.08907v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08907v1)

## Summary
This paper uses agent-based modeling to study when order-book markets transition from liquid to stressed—specifically, when one side of the market dries up. The authors combine zero-intelligence traders (who follow fundamentals) with chartist herders (who follow price momentum), and carefully map out the conditions under which instability emerges. The key finding is a robust, smooth "liquidity-stress crossover" driven by herding feedback, where buying momentum begets more buying, creating self-reinforcing imbalances.

## Theoretical Background
Agent-based models (ABMs) simulate markets by letting heterogeneous traders interact, and often produce instabilities that mirror real crashes. The challenge, highlighted here, is distinguishing genuine emergent phenomena from numerical artifacts or parameter sensitivity. This paper applies Bouchaud's phase-diagram method—a rigorous technique from statistical physics—which requires mapping full parameter regions, testing robustness to rule changes, and ruling out degenerate cases before claiming a tipping point exists. The model combines two trader types: zero-intelligence liquidity providers (anchored to fundamental values) and mid-anchored chartists (momentum followers). The mechanism under study is reflexivity—the idea that price moves and order flow can reinforce each other in feedback loops.

## Core Contributions
1. **Robust crossover identification**: Maps a 7×6 parameter grid (336 simulations with null controls) and locates a genuine liquidity-stress crossover at high herder fractions (φ ≈ 0.9) and moderate herding strength (κ ≈ 1.0), where ~34% of events show one-sided order books.

2. **Methodological rigor**: Demonstrates robustness across rule variants (order-flow-imbalance rule vs. momentum rule), momentum horizons (16× range), and scrambled-sign nulls (zero crossover in all 42 control runs)—ruling out numerical artifacts.

3. **Mechanism decomposition**: Quantifies reflexivity: price-momentum herding has a large reflexive component (+0.29; positive feedback), while order-flow rules show near-zero contagion, proving the feedback loop is herding-specific.

4. **Monotone onset boundary**: Characterizes the critical herder fraction φ*(κ) = {0.55, 0.45, 0.36} across herding strengths, providing a clear phase boundary.

5. **Cross-market isolation**: Shows no directional contagion across markets linked only by signal herding, containing systemic risk to single-market instability.

## Connections to Other Work
This builds on classical market microstructure theory (bid-ask spreads, liquidity provision) and agent-based models of crashes. It extends [[Bouchaud]]'s methodological framework from physics to finance. The reflexivity concept echoes [[George Soros]]' market hypothesis but grounds it in quantitative herding dynamics. The work relates to literature on [[liquidity spirals]] and [[feedback trading]], and challenges explanations based on exogenous shocks by showing endogenous instability. The zero-intelligence baseline connects to [[Kyle's lambda]] models, while the chartist layer reflects [[heterogeneous agent models]] of financial markets.

## Practical Applications
- **Market surveillance**: Regulators can use the phase diagram to identify dangerous herder-fraction regimes (high φ) and set circuit breakers or position limits accordingly.
- **Liquidity risk metrics**: Banks can incorporate the one-sided-book probability as a stress indicator for internal capital adequacy models.
- **Flash crash prevention**: Market makers can tune inventory rebalancing rules to avoid the identified crossover region.
- **Trading venue design**: Exchanges can adjust order-matching rules (e.g., from momentum-based to order-flow-based) to shift away from the instability boundary.
- **Systemic risk modeling**: Central banks can use the two-market result (no cross-market contagion via signal herding) to calibrate whether herding or correlation in fundamentals drives systemic events.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Liquidity stress dashboard**: Real-time SaaS product monitoring live order-book asymmetry and herding signals, alerting traders and risk managers when markets approach the φ–κ crossover zone.

2. **Herding-aware execution algorithm**: Smart order router that detects directional herding (via mid-anchored momentum metrics) and routes away from herded venues or adjusts execution speed to avoid feedback loops.

3. **Micro-structure consulting**: Advisory service for exchanges and market makers to stress-test rule changes (order-flow imbalance rules, tick sizes, position limits) against the mapped phase diagram before deployment.

4. **Agent-based market simulator (enterprise)**: Licensed ABM tool for banks and hedge funds to backtest strategies in synthetic markets with controlled herding and liquidity conditions, avoiding real-market alpha leakage.

5. **Regulatory stress-testing platform**: Vendor service that runs Bouchaud-style phase-diagram analysis on regulatory proposals (e.g., position limits, circuit breaker thresholds) to quantify spillover risk and robustness before implementation.

## Technical Limitations
- **Scale and realism**: The model uses simplified zero-intelligence and chartist agents; real traders employ options, leverage, regime-switching, and correlated strategies not captured here.
- **Empirical calibration**: No clear guidance on how to set φ and κ from real market data; herding strength may vary across assets, regimes, and time.
- **Single instrument**: Analysis is mostly for a single asset; extension to multi-asset portfolios, option-stock links, or index rebalancing requires new work.
- **Discrete order model**: Continuous-double-auction order books may not generalize to call auctions, dark pools, or bilateral OTC trading.
- **Null control scope**: Scrambled-sign nulls rule out random noise but don't test alternative herding mechanisms (e.g., momentum contagion through correlated information).
- **Parameter smoothness**: The crossover is continuous rather than a sharp phase transition, raising questions about whether weak instabilities in the crossover region are practically relevant.

## Open Questions
1. **Empirical validation**: Can the predicted φ*(κ) boundaries be estimated from real limit-order-book data? Which assets and time periods exhibit high φ herding?
2. **Leverage and margins**: How do margin requirements and forced liquidations change the phase diagram? Is there a secondary instability?
3. **Information arrival**: What happens when herding is driven by correlated news rather than pure momentum? Does contagion emerge then?
4. **Multi-leg herding**: Can the framework extend to correlated herding across related assets (e.g., index constituents)? When does single-market herding spill over?
5. **Optimal regulation**: What is the socially optimal position in parameter space—maximizing liquidity provision while minimizing crash risk? Can regulators steer markets there?
6. **Learning and adaptation**: If traders learn the phase diagram and avoid high-φ regimes, how does the system evolve? Is there an equilibrium?

## Key Takeaway
Herding-driven liquidity crises are robust, emergent, and primarily driven by reflexive buying-begets-buying feedback rather than exogenous order-flow imbalances—and this instability can be precisely mapped and prevented by controlling the fraction and strength of trend-following traders.
