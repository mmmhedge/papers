# Attractor States Emerge in Multi-Turn LLM Conversations

**Authors:** Ting-Wen Ko, Jonas Geiping
**Published:** 2026-06-29 | **arXiv:** [2606.30571v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30571v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates whether large language models (LLMs) exhibit attractor-like behavior—stable behavioral patterns that conversations naturally settle into—when engaged in multi-turn debates with each other. By studying 7 different LLMs across 20 controversial topics in both self-play and mixed-play settings, the authors find that models develop distinctive "attractor states" that asymmetrically influence conversation partners, pulling them toward adopting the attractor model's stylistic traits and discourse patterns.

## Theoretical Background
The concept of attractor states comes from dynamical systems theory—mathematical frameworks that describe how systems evolve over time and whether they converge to stable configurations. In the context of LLMs, this framing is novel: rather than treating each model output as an independent prediction, the paper treats multi-turn conversations as trajectories through a high-dimensional representation space.

The work builds on prior understanding of:
- **LLM behavior in multi-agent settings**: Prior work has shown that LLMs can engage in coherent dialogue but dynamics across many turns remain underexplored.
- **Representation space analysis**: Using embeddings and latent space trajectories to understand model behavior (related to mechanistic interpretability — the study of what internal mechanisms drive model outputs).
- **Discourse analysis**: Tracking concrete linguistic traits (metacommentary, stance shifts, topic coherence) alongside learned representations.

The paper implicitly engages with emergence — how global behavioral patterns arise from local interactions without explicit coordination.

## Core Contributions
1. **Empirical discovery of model-specific attractors**: Demonstrating that individual LLMs exhibit stable behavioral signatures in multi-turn interaction that measurably influence partners, with quantified asymmetry (Claude Haiku is a strong attractor; GPT-4.1 nano is highly malleable).

2. **Mixed methodology**: Combining representation-space analysis (latent embeddings), discourse-level metrics (e.g., metacommentary frequency), and stance tracking to characterize attractors across multiple levels of description.

3. **Systematic comparison across 7 models and 20 topics**: Scale and breadth sufficient to establish generalizability beyond toy examples, with explicit self-play vs. mixed-play comparison to isolate model influence.

4. **Asymmetry characterization**: Showing that influence is not symmetric—certain models are consistently "pullers" while others are "followers"—rather than all models converging to a neutral equilibrium.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper touches on several active research areas:

- **[[Multi-agent reinforcement learning]]**: While this paper uses generative LLMs rather than RL agents, the core question—how do autonomous systems with different capabilities interact and influence each other?—is parallel.
- **[[Interpretability and representation analysis]]**: The use of latent space trajectories connects to mechanistic interpretability literature, though the focus here is behavioral rather than computational circuit-level.
- **[[Emergent behavior in language models]]**: Related to work on emergent capabilities and unexpected model interactions in scaled systems.
- **[[RLHF and alignment]]**: Indirectly relevant since model "personality" (attractor signature) may reflect training differences; understanding asymmetric influence has implications for safe multi-agent deployment.
- **Conversational dynamics**: Connected to dialogue systems research, though most prior work focuses on human-model or model-human interaction, not model-model.

## Practical Applications
1. **Autonomous debate and negotiation systems**: In business or policy contexts (contract negotiation, scenario planning), understanding which AI participant will dominate discussions helps structure multi-LLM teams predictably.

2. **Content moderation and filtering**: If certain models are strong attractors toward particular discourse styles (e.g., verbose, metacognitive), knowing this allows better calibration of multi-model review pipelines.

3. **Collaborative writing and research**: Multi-LLM drafting systems (e.g., for grant proposals or technical documentation) can be optimized by choosing attractor vs. non-attractor models for specific roles (primary author vs. critic).

4. **Customer service triage**: Routing customer conversations through models with different attractor signatures to maintain diversity of perspective or prevent conversational drift into unproductive loops.

5. **Multi-stage inference**: Using attractor profiles to sequence LLM calls—e.g., starting with a non-attractor model for brainstorming, then a strong attractor for refinement.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Attractor Profiling as a Service**: SaaS platform that characterizes any LLM's attractor signature (via lightweight benchmarking) and recommends optimal multi-model compositions for specific tasks (debate, negotiation, creative ideation).

2. **LLM Team Orchestration Engine**: Automated system that selects and sequences LLMs in multi-turn workflows based on desired outcomes—e.g., "maximize diversity" vs. "converge quickly"—using attractor theory as the underlying model.

3. **Debate & Consensus Platform**: B2B tool for organizations that need AI-assisted policy analysis or decision-making, where multi-LLM debates are structured to avoid convergence toward a single dominant model's perspective.

4. **Attractor-Resistant Fine-Tuning**: Consulting service or tool that fine-tunes or prompts LLMs to resist attractor influences from partner models, ensuring they maintain independent reasoning in collaborative AI systems.

5. **Multi-Agent Simulation & Forecasting**: Platform for modeling complex multi-stakeholder scenarios (market dynamics, policy outcomes, organizational change) by simulating interactions between models with known attractor properties, enabling better prediction of emergent behavior.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Limited model diversity**: Only 7 models tested; modern LLM ecosystem is vast and rapidly changing, and results may not generalize to very different architectures or training paradigms (e.g., open-source models with different alignment procedures).

2. **Short conversation horizons**: Multi-turn debates are finite; unclear if attractors persist over very long interactions (100+ turns) or whether new equilibria emerge at different timescales.

3. **Topic confounding**: 20 topics is broad, but political/controversial topics may behave differently from technical or creative domains where models might converge differently.

4. **Representation space ambiguity**: Using latent embeddings assumes the representation space is meaningful and stable; different embedding methods (e.g., different pooling strategies) might yield different trajectory descriptions.

5. **No causal mechanism**: The paper identifies correlations (model B adopts model A's traits when paired) but does not mechanistically explain *why* (is it due to prompt templates, training data similarity, architectural biases?).

6. **Self-selection bias in attractor strength**: Models described as "attractors" may simply have more consistent behavior due to training; causality is not established.

## Open Questions
1. **Why do attractors form?** What properties of model training or architecture make certain models pull others? Can we predict attractor strength from model size, data, or fine-tuning approach?

2. **Do attractors exist in longer horizons?** Do 50+ turn conversations show different attractor dynamics, or do they simply reinforce the same patterns?

3. **Can attractor behavior be controlled via prompting?** What kinds of system prompts or few-shot examples could suppress attractor effects or make models more resistant to influence?

4. **How do attractors change with model updates?** If GPT-4.1 is updated to 4.2, does its attractor signature change? Is there a stable "model identity" independent of version?

5. **Does attractor strength predict downstream alignment risk?** If a model is a strong attractor in debate, does that correlate with it being more persuasive or harder to align in adversarial settings?

6. **Multi-agent beyond pairs**: Do attractors generalize to 3+ model conversations, or do they break down in more complex interaction topologies?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs engaged in open-ended multi-turn conversations settle into model-specific attractor states that asymmetrically pull partners toward their behavioral traits, suggesting multi-agent LLM systems are partially predictable but shaped by structured power imbalances in influence.
