# Any-Dimensional Learning by Sampling

**Authors:** Eitan Levin, Venkat Chandrasekaran
**Published:** 2026-07-08 | **arXiv:** [2607.07680v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07680v1)

## Summary
This paper develops a unified theoretical framework for understanding how machine learning models generalize when trained on small inputs but evaluated on larger ones—a problem arising in sequences, graphs, and point clouds of varying sizes. The authors use random sampling maps to characterize when and how well models can scale from small to large inputs, and provide explicit generalization and sketching rates for important function classes like graph neural networks and transformers.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on classical statistical learning theory, particularly generalization bounds and sample complexity analysis. It extends these ideas to handle variable-size inputs by leveraging symmetries—permutation invariance, exchangeability, and graph isomorphism—that naturally arise in domains like sequences and graphs. The framework is grounded in probability theory, particularly de Finetti-type representation theorems that connect exchangeable random variables to latent measures. Key prior concepts include: sampling with replacement (classical bootstrap methods), species sampling (used in ecology and Bayesian nonparametrics), random binning, and graph homomorphism densities—the density of subgraph patterns in a larger graph. The paper also connects to recent work on permutation-invariant architectures like graph neural networks and transformers, which are designed to respect the symmetries present in variable-size inputs.

## Core Contributions
1. **Unified sampling framework**: The paper characterizes three fundamental types of sampling maps (sampling with replacement, random binning, species sampling) and shows when each is theoretically appropriate depending on the problem's symmetry structure.

2. **Generalization bounds**: Explicit finite-sample generalization rates for function classes that are continuous with respect to a chosen sampling notion, without requiring fixed-size assumptions.

3. **Sketching rates**: Constructive bounds on how well large inputs can be approximated by smaller sketches while preserving model predictions.

4. **Concrete instantiations**: Application of the framework to specific architectures and function classes—moment polynomials on measures, graph homomorphism densities, permutation-invariant transformers, and graph neural networks—with measurable rates.

## Connections to Other Work
This work intersects with [[graph-neural-networks]] literature (particularly permutation invariance and aggregation), [[transformers]] (especially permutation-invariant variants), and classical statistical learning theory. It relates to recent work on neural network extrapolation and length generalization in sequence models. The sketching perspective connects to data compression and randomized algorithms. The use of symmetries and exchangeability ties to deep work in probabilistic modeling and Bayesian nonparametrics.

## Practical Applications
- **Point cloud processing**: Training 3D models on small point clouds and evaluating on dense, large-scale scans without retraining.
- **Graph-based drug discovery**: Models trained on small molecules generalize to larger molecular graphs without expensive retraining.
- **Sequence models**: Language models or time-series models trained on short sequences reliably extend to longer sequences.
- **Data compression pipelines**: Automatically sketching large graphs or sensor data to smaller representations for efficient inference.
- **Federated learning on variable-size data**: Harmonizing model predictions across devices with different data sizes.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Adaptive model compression service**: Auto-sketch incoming data (sequences, graphs, point clouds) to optimal sizes based on learned model behavior, reducing latency and compute costs for inference APIs.
2. **Graph analytics platform**: Enable enterprises to train GNN models on small subgraphs of their knowledge bases and confidently query predictions on unseen, much larger graph snapshots.
3. **Cross-domain length generalization toolkit**: Library that certifies whether a trained sequence model (language, time-series) will reliably generalize to longer inputs, with automatic warning systems.
4. **3D model scaling service**: SaaS tool for 3D/robotics companies to validate that vision models trained on lower-resolution point clouds work correctly on high-resolution sensor data.
5. **Sketch-based data anonymization**: Generate mathematically-guaranteed smaller sketches of sensitive datasets that preserve downstream ML model behavior while protecting privacy.

## Technical Limitations
- **Architectural assumptions**: The framework assumes models respect certain symmetries (permutation invariance, exchangeability). Models that break these symmetries (e.g., position-dependent transformers without adaptation) may not fit the theory.
- **Continuity requirement**: Bounds require the target function class to be continuous w.r.t. the sampling notion; this may not hold for all architectures or loss functions.
- **Rate dependence on dimension**: While the paper provides explicit rates, the dimension-dependence of constants is not always characterized; rates may degrade sharply in high-dimensional problems.
- **Finite-sample tightness**: Bounds are worst-case; they may be loose for specific problem instances or architectures.
- **Computational cost of sketching**: The paper characterizes sketching *in theory* but does not address the computational cost of finding optimal sketches in practice.

## Open Questions
- **Empirical validation**: Do the predicted generalization and sketching rates match observed behavior in real large-scale trained models (transformers, GNNs)?
- **Adaptive sampling**: Can we learn the optimal sampling map during training rather than fixing it a priori?
- **Non-symmetric domains**: How does the framework extend to problems that lack strong symmetries or exchangeability?
- **Distribution shift**: How robust are the bounds when the distribution of large inputs differs from small ones?
- **Tightness**: Can the derived rates be improved, or are there fundamental limits that prevent better bounds?
- **Architecture design**: Can this theory guide the design of new architectures specifically optimized for variable-size generalization?

## Key Takeaway
Random sampling maps provide a principled, unified way to characterize when and how machine learning models trained on small variable-size inputs generalize to larger ones, with explicit rates tied to problem symmetries.
