# Width-Robust Learnability in Mean-Field Bayesian Neural Networks

**Authors:** Dmitry Vaintrob, Kaarel Hänni
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.05735v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05735v1)

## Summary
This paper proves that Bayesian neural networks at infinite width have the same learning complexity as large finite networks—they don't gain spurious power from their infinite capacity. The key insight is that at the "mean-field" scaling regime (where neural networks learn features rather than just adjusting output weights), learning a target function requires polynomial reduced entropy (a measure of prior cost) if and only if it's learnable from polynomial samples. This matters because it explains why infinite-width limits are theoretically justified: they capture the actual inductive bias of practical networks.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two major threads: (1) infinite-width limits of neural networks, which simplify analysis by treating networks as infinite-dimensional objects (the "neural tangent kernel" and "mean-field" regimes are standard approximations), and (2) Bayesian learning theory, which reasons about generalization through prior complexity and posterior concentration. The mean-field scaling is the intermediate regime between lazy learning (where networks barely adapt their features) and feature learning (where networks adapt significantly). The authors introduce **reduced entropy** — a notion from statistical mechanics that measures the "cost" of the prior distribution in encoding a target function to a given accuracy.

## Core Contributions
1. **Width-robust learnability theorem**: Establishes an equivalence: a target family is learnable at infinite width ⟺ learnable at polynomial width ⟺ has polynomially bounded reduced entropy.
2. **Subsampling construction**: Shows how to extract a polynomial-sized representative subset from infinite-width networks that preserves learned functions on all inputs.
3. **Active/lazy decomposition**: Decomposes the subsampling into an "active" component that preserves data-dependent low-dimensional structure and a "lazy" component that resamples entropy-dominated directions from the prior.
4. Proves that infinite-width mean-field limits don't introduce spurious generalization power compared to finite networks.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to broader questions in [[neural network generalization bounds]], [[mean-field theory of neural networks]], and [[feature learning vs. kernel regimes]]. The width-robustness result is a theoretical vindication that limit objects aren't misleading about finite-width behavior.

## Practical Applications
- **Justifying approximate inference**: Practitioners using mean-field approximations in Bayesian neural networks can now be confident these limits don't hide complexity; the predictions scale as expected.
- **Network pruning**: The subsampling construction gives a principled way to compress infinite-width solutions to finite size while preserving accuracy on held-out data.
- **Complexity certification**: For critical applications, one can verify that a learned model's complexity (via reduced entropy) is polynomially bounded, providing theoretical generalization guarantees.
- **Curriculum learning design**: Understanding which targets have low vs. high reduced entropy could guide construction of training curricula.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Bayesian NN verification tool**: SaaS that certifies whether a trained Bayesian network has polynomial-bounded reduced entropy, providing formal generalization bounds for regulated domains (finance, healthcare).
2. **Principled network compression**: Software that automatically extracts minimal representative subsets from large Bayesian networks using the active/lazy decomposition, with theoretical loss guarantees.
3. **Target complexity analyzer**: Tool that estimates reduced entropy of prediction tasks (e.g., from labeled data) to predict sample complexity and decide whether to invest in model training.
4. **Mean-field approximation validator**: Framework for practitioners to check when mean-field limits are theoretically justified for their specific architectures and target classes.
5. **Adaptive Bayesian learning platform**: System that dynamically reweights active (data-dependent) vs. lazy (prior-driven) components based on dataset size to optimize sample efficiency.

## Technical Limitations
- **Fixed depth assumption**: The theorem holds at fixed network depth; scaling depth introduces new questions about how reduced entropy evolves.
- **Boolean cube targets**: The main result focuses on discrete targets on Boolean hypercubes; extension to continuous or image domains is unclear.
- **Polynomial slack**: The equivalence is "up to polynomial slack"—doesn't pin down exact constants or log factors, which matter for practice.
- **Computational cost of subsampling**: While the subsampling construction is theoretically elegant, the paper doesn't address computational complexity of *finding* the polynomial-sized subset from the infinite-width solution.
- **Mean-field scaling specificity**: Results don't apply to other scaling regimes (lazy or other feature-learning scales), limiting scope.
- **Posterior concentration**: Assumes posterior is well-concentrated; doesn't address settings with poor mixing or multimodality.

## Open Questions
1. Can the width-robustness result extend to variable depth networks, and does reduced entropy scale predictably with depth?
2. What is the algorithmic complexity of finding the polynomial-sized representative subset? Is there a polynomial-time algorithm?
3. How does reduced entropy behave for natural data distributions (images, text) rather than Boolean cubes?
4. Can reduced entropy be estimated from finite data, enabling practitioners to predict learnability before training?
5. Does the active/lazy decomposition structure appear in other regimes (e.g., other scaling limits or architectures)?
6. How tight are the polynomial factors—is the slack log-space or polynomial in the dimension?

## Key Takeaway
Infinite-width mean-field limits of Bayesian neural networks faithfully preserve the computational complexity of learning—they don't gain spurious power, and what's learnable at infinite width is learnable at polynomial width.
