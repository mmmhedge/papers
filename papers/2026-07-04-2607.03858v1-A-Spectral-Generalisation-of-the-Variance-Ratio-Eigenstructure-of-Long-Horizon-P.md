# A Spectral Generalisation of the Variance Ratio: Eigenstructure of Long-Horizon Portfolio Covariance and a Multi-Memory Factor Model of U.S. Equity Returns

**Authors:** Anders G Frøseth
**Published:** 2026-07-04 | **arXiv:** [2607.03858v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03858v1)

## Summary
This paper develops a spectral generalization of the variance ratio test to decompose long-horizon equity returns into separate "memory channels" — one capturing return patterns and another capturing volatility persistence. The authors fit a five-factor model to U.S. and European stock portfolios that simultaneously explains seven empirical patterns in how returns and volatility behave over long time horizons, and discover that the characteristics predicting return momentum are fundamentally different from those predicting volatility persistence.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on the Lo-MacKinlay (1988) variance ratio, a statistical test that detects whether returns exhibit mean reversion (antipersistence) or momentum (persistence) by comparing variances over different holding periods. Classical variance ratios work on a single time series. The authors extend this to the cross-section — many assets at once — using spectral (eigenstructure) decomposition, which breaks down covariance matrices into their principal components to identify independent sources of variation. The framework treats returns and volatility separately, recognizing that momentum in price changes and persistence in volatility fluctuations may have distinct origins. This connects to long-memory processes (where shocks to a system decay very slowly) and cascade models (hierarchical structures where volatility operates at multiple timescales simultaneously).

## Core Contributions
1. **Spectral multivariate variance ratio**: Extends Lo-MacKinlay from univariate to cross-sectional setting, explicitly separating return-channel memory (price momentum/mean reversion) from volatility-channel memory (vol persistence).

2. **Parsimonious five-factor decomposition**: Identifies a universal factor structure capturing persistent, antipersistent, and multi-scale memory effects that replicates across five different portfolio universes (Fama-French 49-industry, pre/post-1998 subsamples, 100 size×value sort, and European equivalent).

3. **Late-1980s volatility regime transition**: Pinpoints a structural break in U.S. equity volatility dynamics (not at the conventional 1998 boundary) where the slowest volatility component doubled in duration from ~2 to ~4 years, localized with bootstrap confidence bands.

4. **Cross-channel independence**: Demonstrates that loadings predicting return memory have zero correlation (anti-alignment) with loadings predicting volatility memory, falsifying single-loading hypotheses and suggesting distinct economic drivers.

## Connections to Other Work
This extends [[Variance Ratio Tests]] (Lo-MacKinlay) into multivariate space. It relates to [[Factor Models]] in quantitative finance (Fama-French framework) but adds temporal structure through spectral decomposition. The multi-scale memory identification connects to [[Long-Memory Processes]] and cascade models in econophysics. The cross-sectional approach parallels [[Risk Management]] frameworks that decompose sources of portfolio risk, though here the focus is on temporal memory rather than static correlations. The volatility regime detection echoes work on structural breaks in financial time series but uses spectral methods rather than conventional GARCH or switching-model approaches.

## Practical Applications
1. **Long-horizon portfolio construction**: Investors holding stocks 2–4 year horizons can separately adjust for momentum versus volatility-cascade effects when allocating across industries or size-value sorts.

2. **Risk forecasting**: Asset managers can use the five-factor decomposition to forecast volatility regimes and persistence differently from return patterns, improving Value-at-Risk (VaR) and stress-testing models.

3. **Factor timing**: Hedge funds and systematic traders can exploit the decoupling of return and volatility loadings—long momentum factors while shorting vol-persistence factors, or vice versa, based on market regime.

4. **Index construction**: Passive fund managers designing smart-beta or factor indices can weight constituents by their return-memory vs. volatility-memory characteristics to target specific risk-return profiles.

5. **Regulatory capital models**: Banks and insurers can refine capital adequacy calculations by modeling return and vol memory separately, potentially reducing overconservative reserve requirements.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Spectral Memory Analytics Platform**: SaaS tool allowing asset managers to decompose their portfolio's return vs. volatility memory in real time, with alerts when the five-factor loadings shift—useful for risk committees and CIOs.

2. **Regime-Aware Factor Allocation Algorithm**: Algorithmic trading system that dynamically rebalances long/short factor exposures based on detected position in the volatility cascade (short, medium, long-memory phases), automatically adapting to late-1980s-style regime transitions.

3. **Cross-Channel Beta Inversion Signals**: Quant fund offering daily signals that exploit divergences between return-momentum and vol-persistence loadings across equity universes—for stat-arb or factor rotation strategies.

4. **Volatility Cascade Forecasting Model**: Specialized volatility forecasting service (sold to prop desks, options traders) that predicts the 2–4 year volatility persistence structure separately from return mean-reversion, improving option pricing.

5. **Institutional Portfolio Diagnostics Tool**: Consultant/software product that audits existing long-only portfolios to measure implicit exposure to return vs. volatility memory factors, recommending adjustments to hedge unintended exposures.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Historical data requirement**: The five-factor model is identified via spectral decomposition of realized covariance matrices; sparse or short-history data (e.g., new indices, small asset universes) will yield unreliable eigenstructures.

2. **Stationarity assumption**: The framework assumes the underlying covariance structure is stable over the fitting window; regime shifts (like the late-1980s transition the paper finds) break this, limiting out-of-sample predictive power.

3. **Cross-sectional dependence**: The method decomposes existing cross-sectional structure but does not address time-varying correlations; during crises when correlations spike to 1, the spectral decomposition may collapse.

4. **Economic interpretation of factors**: While the five factors are parsimonious, the paper does not link them to fundamental economic drivers (e.g., macro shocks, sentiment, liquidity)—so the model is largely descriptive.

5. **Forward-looking signal degradation**: Past eigenstructure does not guarantee future factor relevance; the model is purely historical and offers no mechanism to adapt to structural breaks in real time.

6. **Modest out-of-sample evidence**: The paper demonstrates in-sample fit across five panels but does not report forward-looking Sharpe ratios or transaction costs for realistic trading strategies.

## Open Questions
1. **What drives the decoupling?** Why do return-momentum and volatility-persistence have independent cross-sectional loadings? Is it market microstructure, behavioral (overreaction to different signal types), or macroeconomic?

2. **Real-time regime detection**: Can the late-1980s transition have been detected in real time without hindsight? What early-warning signals would have worked?

3. **Global and emerging markets**: Does the five-factor structure replicate in EM equities, FX, or commodity markets, or is it U.S./developed-market specific?

4. **Multi-horizon trading**: Can the return vs. volatility memory decomposition be exploited with specific holding periods (e.g., long return-momentum factor at 2-year horizon, hedge with short-vol at 4-year) to beat buy-and-hold?

5. **Factor rotation predictability**: Are there leading indicators (implied vol, credit spreads, macro data) that predict switches in the five-factor loadings, enabling tactical tilts?

6. **Causality and spillovers**: Does return memory cause volatility memory, or vice versa? Granger causality or instrumental variable analysis might clarify directionality.

## Key Takeaway
Returns and volatility have independent long-horizon memory structures, so portfolio models and risk forecasts must treat momentum and volatility persistence as separate economic phenomena, not byproducts of a single shared loading.
