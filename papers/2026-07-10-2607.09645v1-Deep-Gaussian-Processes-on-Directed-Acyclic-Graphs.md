# Deep Gaussian Processes on Directed Acyclic Graphs

**Authors:** Federico L. Perlino, Oliver Hamelijnck, Adam M. Johansen, Theodoros Damoulas
**Published:** 2026-07-10 | **arXiv:** [2607.09645v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09645v1)

## Summary
This paper develops Deep Gaussian Processes (DGPs) — probabilistic models that stack multiple layers of uncertainty — specifically designed to work on directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) where functions compose along edges and observations are scattered throughout the structure. The work matters because many real systems (causal mechanisms, multi-fidelity simulations, gene networks) have this DAG structure, and existing methods struggle with uncertainty propagation through such compositional systems while handling noisy, irregularly-sampled data.

## Theoretical Background
Gaussian Processes (GPs) are non-parametric Bayesian models that place probability distributions over functions, allowing principled uncertainty quantification. Deep Gaussian Processes extend this by stacking multiple GP layers, where the output of one layer becomes input to the next. However, standard DGPs suffer from *prior collapse* — a phenomenon where uncertainty vanishes as the network deepens, making deep models less expressive than shallow ones. This paper extends DGPs to DAG structures where multiple input paths and observations at intermediate nodes create new theoretical challenges. The work also connects to causal inference (where DAGs represent causal mechanisms) and multi-fidelity modeling (where different simulation fidelities form a hierarchy).

## Core Contributions
1. **Theoretical analysis of prior collapse on DAGs**: Proves almost-sure lower bounds on the frequency of depths where input information is preserved, accounting for graph topology and intermediate observations. Shows broad kernel classes where these guarantees hold.

2. **Role of input connections**: Formalizes an earlier observation that direct connections between distant nodes help information flow, mitigating depth-induced information loss.

3. **Structured variational approximation**: Proposes an inference algorithm that maintains computational tractability while preserving dependencies between nodes on the DAG, captures the *explaining-away effect* (a key causal phenomenon where observing a collider child makes parent causes conditionally dependent), and retains compositional uncertainty throughout the graph.

4. **Empirical validation on realistic applications**: Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on latent-collider DAGs, protein signaling networks, and multi-fidelity physics simulations, with interpretable recovery of contribution hierarchy.

## Practical Applications
1. **Drug discovery pipelines**: Model hierarchical signaling networks (gene regulation → protein interactions → phenotypic outcomes) with uncertainty; intermediate node observations (protein concentrations) improve prediction of unobserved downstream effects.

2. **Multi-fidelity engineering simulation**: Combine low-cost, low-accuracy simulations with expensive high-fidelity experiments; automatically infer which high-fidelity runs are most informative by propagating uncertainty through the fidelity hierarchy.

3. **Climate and earth science emulation**: Chain mechanistic models (radiation → atmosphere → ocean → ecosystem) where observations exist at some levels but not others; quantify how missing intermediate measurements affect final predictions.

4. **Systems biology and metabolic networks**: Model biochemical reaction networks with uncertain enzyme kinetics; use sparse protein concentration measurements to constrain predictions of downstream metabolite levels.

5. **Semiconductor yield modeling**: Represent process flows (deposition → lithography → etching → testing) as DAGs; fuse lab measurements at intermediate stages with final test data to improve defect prediction.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Probabilistic multi-fidelity simulation platform**: SaaS tool for engineering/scientific teams to combine heterogeneous data sources (simulations of different costs/accuracies, lab measurements, sensor data) with automatic uncertainty quantification and experiment design recommendations.

2. **Causal discovery and inference software**: Interactive tool for social scientists, economists, and domain experts to specify DAG structures, fit DGPs, and recover causal effect estimates with credible intervals — competitor to existing causal inference packages but with better handling of complex hierarchical systems.

3. **Bio-tech process optimization**: Consulting/software service for biotech manufacturers to model fermentation or cell culture process chains, use sparse intermediate measurements to predict yield, and recommend which monitoring points to add for maximum information gain.

4. **Climate impact modeling**: API/dashboard for corporate ESG teams and policy makers to model cascading climate impacts (emissions → atmospheric CO₂ → temperature → crop yield) with uncertainty, feeding into risk models and net-zero pathway planning.

5. **Digital twin middleware**: Software layer that sits on top of industrial IoT systems to fuse multi-fidelity sensor data (high-frequency cheap sensors, sparse expensive lab tests, simulation predictions) into a unified probabilistic model for predictive maintenance and anomaly detection.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability**: Variational inference still requires computing covariances and matrix inversions; applicability to very large DAGs (1000s of nodes) unclear; computational cost not detailed for high-dimensional problems.

2. **Model misspecification**: Theory assumes known DAG structure and kernel class; performance degrades if the true causal structure is unknown or if kernels don't match the data-generating process.

3. **Observation placement**: Theory bounds depend on how densely intermediate nodes are observed; if observations are sparse or concentrated in one part of the graph, information loss could be severe — no guidance on optimal observation design provided.

4. **Limited comparison baselines**: Empirical validation primarily against other GP methods; comparison to modern deep learning (neural networks trained end-to-end) or other causal inference methods (causal forests, instrumental variables) is absent.

5. **Gaussian assumption**: Assumes function distributions are Gaussian; may underfit heavy-tailed or multimodal posterior distributions. Non-Gaussian extensions not addressed.

6. **Intermediate observation noise**: While the paper allows noisy observations, it assumes observation noise is known or easily estimated; misspecified noise models could propagate error through the DAG.

## Open Questions
1. How does performance scale to DAGs with 100+ nodes and 1000+ observations? What are computational bottlenecks?

2. Can the framework handle unknown or partially known DAG structures, and how much data is needed to recover the true graph?

3. How do you optimally choose where to place new observations (experiment design) to maximize information gain for a downstream target?

4. Can non-Gaussian priors or other function space models (e.g., neural process variants) be used while preserving the theoretical guarantees?

5. Does the framework extend naturally to cyclic graphs or continuous-time DAGs (e.g., differential equations on DAGs)?

6. How does performance compare to end-to-end neural network training when the true DAG structure is given?

## Key Takeaway
Deep Gaussian Processes on DAGs provide a principled Bayesian framework for composing uncertain functions through causal/hierarchical structures, with theoretical guarantees on information preservation and practical utility for multi-fidelity and systems biology applications.
