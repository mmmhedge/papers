# Automatic Hard Example Synthesis with Multi-Level Agentic Data Curation

**Authors:** Genglin Liu, Muye Zhang, Krishnamurthy Viswanathan, Nichole J. Hansen, Blaž Bratanič et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.14256v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14256v1)

## Summary
This paper presents an automated system that uses multiple AI agents working together to create challenging test cases for multimodal AI models (systems that process both images and text). The goal is to make these models more robust against adversarial attacks and edge cases — tricky scenarios they might fail on. By synthesizing hard examples without human effort, the system improves safety model performance significantly (reducing errors from 41% to 24%).

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on several established ideas: **active learning** (selecting which data points to label for maximum learning), **red-teaming** (adversarial testing by trying to break systems), and **in-context learning** (teaching models through examples in their input). Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) combine vision and language understanding using transformer architectures. Traditional approaches to finding failure cases rely on manual annotation or random sampling, both of which struggle with the vast space of possible adversarial examples. The paper leverages **multi-agent systems** — coordinated groups of AI agents with different roles — to automate the discovery of these hard cases.

## Core Contributions
1. **Agentic red-teaming framework**: An automated system with specialized agents (Architect, image generator, verification committee) that iteratively proposes and mutates candidate adversarial examples rather than sampling randomly.
2. **Two-stage synthesis strategy**: Agents generate novel hypotheses *and* mutate past failed attempts, combining exploration with exploitation.
3. **Multi-level verification**: A committee of LLM raters validates synthesized examples, reducing false positives.
4. **Test-time retrieval**: Embedding hard examples as in-context demonstrations improves target model robustness without retraining or human labels.
5. **Empirical validation**: Substantial FNR reduction (41.2% → 24.5%) on a public safety benchmark with zero human annotation.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates closely to [[RLHF]] (reinforcement learning from human feedback) in that it improves model alignment, but replaces human judgment with automated verification. It extends [[red-teaming]] from manual adversarial attacks to systematic, scalable synthesis. The multi-agent coordination echoes work in collaborative multi-agent systems where agents divide labor. The use of in-context learning connects to [[prompt engineering]] and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). The work also touches on [[content moderation]] challenges and robustness evaluation, addressing similar pain points to adversarial training but with less computational overhead.

## Practical Applications
1. **Content safety at scale**: Platforms (YouTube, Facebook, TikTok) can use this to stress-test moderation models before deployment without hiring armies of annotators.
2. **Enterprise policy compliance**: Companies handling sensitive content (healthcare images, financial documents) can autonomously discover where their systems fail against policy violations.
3. **Regulatory testing**: Regulators could use agentic red-teaming to audit AI systems for bias, fairness violations, or safety lapses before approving deployment.
4. **Third-party auditing services**: Independent firms could offer automated robustness audits for MLLMs using this framework.
5. **Continuous model monitoring**: Production systems could periodically run agentic synthesis to find new edge cases emerging in live traffic.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **AI Safety Audit Platform**: SaaS tool offering automated red-teaming reports for any MLLM. Customers upload their model and safety policy; the system returns a report of vulnerabilities and a curated dataset of hard examples to retrain on.
2. **Adversarial Example Marketplace**: Sell synthetic hard examples and edge case datasets organized by modality, violation type, and difficulty level to ML teams building safety-critical systems.
3. **Robustness-as-a-Service**: API that wraps customer MLLMs and uses agentic synthesis in the background to continuously harden them, returning confidence scores and flagging new failure modes.
4. **Regulatory Compliance Testing**: Consulting firm automating compliance audits for AI systems in regulated industries (healthcare, finance, autonomous vehicles) by synthesizing adversarial scenarios matching regulatory frameworks.
5. **Model Benchmarking Suite**: Publish standardized adversarial benchmarks (like CIFAR or ImageNet but for safety/edge cases) generated via agentic synthesis, becoming the de facto evaluation standard.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Synthesis validity**: Generated adversarial examples may be unrealistic or outside the true distribution of real-world threats, limiting generalization to unseen attacks.
2. **Verification committee bias**: LLM raters can make mistakes or exhibit systematic biases (e.g., cultural blindness); a bad rater can poison the dataset.
3. **Scalability of mutation strategy**: As the example space grows, mutation-based exploration may hit diminishing returns; true novelty discovery remains hard.
4. **Image generation fidelity**: The quality and realism of synthesized images directly impact downstream model robustness; poor images may not transfer to real attacks.
5. **Circular dependency**: If the target model and the verification committee share similar biases, the framework may reinforce rather than expose gaps.
6. **Black-box assumption**: Requires only query access to the target model; unclear how well the approach works with white-box or interpretable systems where gradient-based attacks are possible.
7. **Policy definition**: Requires explicit encoding of safety policies; ambiguous or evolving policies (e.g., cultural norms around content) are hard to operationalize.

## Open Questions
1. **Generalization beyond safety**: Does agentic synthesis work for other model hardening tasks (robustness to distribution shift, OOD detection) or is it specific to safety/moderation?
2. **Comparison to gradient-based attacks**: How does agentic synthesis compare in efficiency and effectiveness to white-box adversarial attacks like PGD or AutoAttack?
3. **Human-in-the-loop hybrid**: What if a small fraction of examples are human-verified? Does the cost-benefit curve justify human annotation at some point?
4. **Mutation strategy analysis**: Which mutation operators (style transfer, object replacement, etc.) are most effective? Is there a principled way to prioritize them?
5. **Cross-model transfer**: Do adversarial examples synthesized against one MLLM transfer to others, or is each model's weakness unique?
6. **Scalability to video/longer sequences**: The paper focuses on images; how does the approach handle temporal or sequential modalities?

## Key Takeaway
Automated agents can systematically synthesize hard test cases for AI safety models without human effort, substantially improving robustness on real benchmarks.
