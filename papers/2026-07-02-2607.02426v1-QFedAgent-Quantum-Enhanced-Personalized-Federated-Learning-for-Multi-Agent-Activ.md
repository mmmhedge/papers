# QFedAgent: Quantum-Enhanced Personalized Federated Learning for Multi-Agent Activity Recognition

**Authors:** Quoc Bao Phan, Tuy Tan Nguyen
**Published:** 2026-07-02 | **arXiv:** [2607.02426v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02426v1)

## Summary
This paper presents QFedAgent, a federated learning system that combines quantum computing with classical machine learning to help multiple robots or distributed devices collaboratively learn activity recognition models while preserving privacy. The key innovation is using quantum circuits to fuse sensor data (accelerometer and gyroscope readings) with 72 parameters instead of 33,000 — a 450x reduction — while maintaining accuracy on real-world activity recognition benchmarks. This matters because federated learning is privacy-critical for robotics, but handling non-uniform data distributions across many agents has been a bottleneck.

## Theoretical Background
**Federated Learning (FL)** is a distributed training paradigm where multiple clients (e.g., robots, phones) train models locally and only share model updates, not raw data, with a central server. The challenge is that real-world data across agents is non-IID (non-independent and identically distributed) — different robots may see different activity patterns — which breaks many conventional FL algorithms.

**Personalized FL** extends this by allowing each agent to maintain a slightly different model tailored to its local data distribution, rather than forcing all agents to converge to a single global model.

**Sensor fusion** traditionally combines multiple sensor streams (accelerometer, gyroscope) using neural network layers. The paper proposes replacing this with **variational quantum circuits** — parameterized quantum programs that encode classical data into quantum states and leverage entanglement (correlation between qubits) to capture sensor interactions more compactly than classical networks.

**Non-IID data partitioning** simulates realistic scenarios where agents have heterogeneous activity labels or feature distributions, a known failure mode for vanilla FL.

## Core Contributions
1. **Quantum fusion module design**: A variational quantum circuit specifically architected to encode accelerometer–gyroscope pairs and model their interactions through quantum entanglement, reducing fusion parameters from 33K to 72 (450x compression).

2. **Personalized federated FL integration**: QFedAgent combines quantum fusion with a personalized federated learning algorithm that allows heterogeneous models per agent while maintaining a shared learned component.

3. **Empirical validation on non-IID data**: Demonstrates 97.7% mean test accuracy on the OPPORTUNITY human activity recognition dataset under realistic subject-based (agent-specific) data splits, showing quantum fusion remains competitive despite extreme parameter reduction.

4. **Concrete parameter-efficiency metric**: First quantified comparison of quantum vs. classical fusion overhead in the federated activity recognition setting (10x total model size reduction claimed across the full pipeline).

## Connections to Other Work
This bridges three communities:
- **Federated Learning**: Builds on standard FL optimization (e.g., FedAvg) but addresses the non-IID heterogeneity challenge that has motivated [[personalized federated learning]] variants.
- **Quantum Machine Learning**: Uses variational quantum circuits, a near-term approach to quantum advantage on small datasets (sometimes called NISQ algorithms — Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum).
- **Sensor Fusion & Activity Recognition**: Extends classical multimodal fusion (attention mechanisms, MLPs) with quantum encoding to reduce parameters for resource-constrained edge devices.

The work implicitly positions itself against classical federated multi-agent learning for robotics, though direct comparisons to recent work like federated graph neural networks or other parameter-efficient fusion (e.g., LoRA-style adapter modules) are not discussed.

## Practical Applications
1. **Smart Home Robotics**: Multiple home robots learn activity recognition (cooking, sleeping, exercise) from residents without uploading raw video or sensor streams; quantum fusion reduces on-device memory and communication bandwidth.

2. **Healthcare Wearables**: Fitness trackers and health monitors across a patient population collaboratively improve activity-of-daily-living detection while respecting privacy; quantum circuits compress fusion layers on battery-constrained wearables.

3. **Industrial IoT & Predictive Maintenance**: Distributed factory sensors (accelerometers on machinery) learn anomalous vibration patterns in a privacy-preserving federated setting; quantum fusion allows many sensors to train together with minimal parameter sharing overhead.

4. **Autonomous Vehicle Fleets**: Vehicles share learned activity/gesture recognition (pedestrian pose, cyclist balance) without exposing local sensor logs; quantum fusion reduces model update sizes for bandwidth-limited cellular communication.

5. **Elderly Care & Fall Detection**: Multiple care facilities train a shared activity recognition model for fall detection across residents without centralizing sensitive motion data; parameter efficiency enables real-time inference on edge devices.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Quantum Hardware Availability**: The paper does not specify whether experiments were run on actual quantum processors (IBM Qiskit, Rigetti) or classical simulators. Real quantum devices suffer from noise, limited qubit counts, and high error rates — the claimed parameter efficiency may not translate to practical speedup on current NISQ hardware.

2. **Scalability of Quantum Fusion**: The approach encodes pairs of sensors (accelerometer, gyroscope). Scaling to many more sensor modalities (video, audio, thermal) is not addressed — quantum circuit depth and entanglement overhead may explode.

3. **Non-IID Convergence Guarantees**: The paper claims personalized FL integration but provides no formal convergence analysis or comparison of non-IID robustness vs. classical personalized FL baselines (e.g., Per-FedAvg, FedProx). Empirical 97.7% accuracy alone does not prove convergence properties.

4. **Limited Dataset & Baseline Comparisons**: OPPORTUNITY is a single, relatively small activity recognition benchmark. No comparison to modern deep learning baselines (LSTMs, temporal CNNs, Transformers) or recent personalized FL methods in federated settings.

5. **Communication Cost Not Quantified**: While the paper mentions "communication cost," actual round-trip communication (model updates, gradients) is not measured. Federated learning is often communication-bound, not computation-bound.

6. **Assumption of Homogeneous Quantum Access**: Assumes all agents can execute identical quantum circuits; heterogeneous quantum capabilities (different qubit counts, calibration noise) across agents are not considered.

7. **Subject-Based Non-IID Only**: The partition strategy (non-IID by subject/agent) is one flavor; other realistic partitions (label-distribution skew, feature shift) are not tested.

## Open Questions
1. **Quantum Advantage on Real Hardware**: Can the quantum fusion module demonstrate a speedup (latency or energy) on actual quantum processors vs. classical simulators, accounting for noise and readout errors?

2. **Personalization Mechanism Details**: What is the exact personalization strategy? Is it per-agent parameter fine-tuning, or a federated meta-learning approach (e.g., MAML, Per-FedAvg)? How does it compare formally?

3. **Multi-Modality Scaling**: How do quantum circuits scale when fusing 5+ sensor types or high-dimensional embeddings? Is there an entanglement-depth vs. accuracy trade-off?

4. **Convergence under Non-IID Data**: Can you prove or empirically validate that personalized QFedAgent has faster or more stable convergence than personalized classical FL under label-skew or feature-shift non-IID scenarios?
