# No Subspace to Track: Non-Identifiability and Optimizer State in Low-Rank Training

**Authors:** Noel Thomas
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.05872v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05872v1)

## Summary
This paper reveals that low-rank subspace tracking—the core assumption behind memory-efficient optimizers like GaLore—is fundamentally unreliable due to estimator noise. The authors show that subspace estimates from different minibatches disagree as much as estimates separated by many training steps, meaning there is no stable "object" to track. Instead, they propose treating subspace refreshes as coordinate changes for optimizer state, leading to LDAdam, which outperforms patched GaLore while clarifying why and when low-rank training actually works.

## Theoretical Background
Low-rank training reduces memory and compute by projecting gradients onto a small r-dimensional subspace before updating model parameters. This approach is crucial for scaling large language models when full-rank optimization is infeasible. The foundational assumption is that the gradient's top-r subspace drifts slowly—like a weakly rotating object—so refreshing it every T steps gives meaningful savings without harming convergence. The paper builds on [[GaLore]] and optimizer state management in adaptive methods like Adam, which maintains first and second moment estimates across training. The key mathematical tool here is the chordal distance (a metric on subspaces) and spectral analysis of gradient covariance matrices.

## Core Contributions
1. **Non-identifiability discovery**: Empirical demonstration across four model families (70M–6.9B parameters) that the top-r subspace is not meaningfully reproducible—two independent estimates from the same step diverge by ~0.73 of maximal chordal distance, almost identical to divergence across T refresh steps. This holds at scale and across architectures (transformers, vision transformers).

2. **Noise characterization**: The spectral tail shrinks as N^(−1/4) under N-fold averaging, not N^(−1/2) as pure noise would predict, proving averaging cannot recover the lost structure—only ~39 of 128 directions are stable across minibatches.

3. **LDAdam algorithm**: A theoretically motivated fix treating each subspace refresh as a coordinate change. The first moment transports *exactly* through rotation (optimal under isotropy); the second moment is rotated by r-k* (where k* is reproducible rank), provably ~(r-k*)/2 better than blind transport.

4. **Empirical validation**: LDAdam reaches 18.7 perplexity vs. 19.3 for GaLore at 1B parameters over 40k steps, with clearer guidance on beta2 selection.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper directly critiques and refines [[GaLore]], which proposed low-rank gradient projection for memory efficiency. It extends the theory of [[Adam]] and adaptive optimizers by analyzing how optimizer state (first/second moments) must transform under coordinate changes. The work relates to broader questions in [[mechanistic-interpretability]] about what structure in neural networks is real vs. estimator artifact, and connects to the optimization literature on quasi-Newton methods and adaptive learning rates. The non-identifiability result echoes discussions in dimensionality reduction (PCA, subspace learning) about when low-rank models are well-defined.

## Practical Applications
1. **Memory-efficient LLM training**: LDAdam enables training of 1B+ parameter models on memory-constrained hardware (GPUs/TPUs) with better convergence than current low-rank methods.

2. **Hyperparameter tuning for low-rank optimizers**: The reproducible rank k* provides a diagnostic—practitioners can now measure which dimensions are stable and set r accordingly, avoiding silent failures where subspace assumptions break.

3. **Scaling efficient fine-tuning**: For domain-specific model adaptation (medical, legal LLMs), LDAdam could reduce per-step memory by 50–80% while maintaining convergence guarantees, making multi-tenant inference infrastructure more economical.

4. **Mobile and edge inference**: Smaller models (70M–160M) trained with LDAdam could run on-device with lower memory footprint and faster convergence during periodic retraining.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Reproducible rank k* is unknown a priori**: While the paper characterizes k*, practitioners need a method to measure it without running the full optimization. The paper doesn't propose fast diagnostic routines.

2. **Vision transformer weakness**: Non-identifiability is "more weak" in ViTs, suggesting the theory may not generalize equally to non-transformer architectures—scope unclear for CNNs or hybrid models.

3. **Limited task scope**: Experiments focus on language modeling (Pythia). It's unknown whether LDAdam's first-moment transport property holds under non-isotropic gradient distributions (e.g., in RL, sparse data, or imbalanced classification).

4. **Beta2 sensitivity remains empirical**: While the paper shows beta2=0.999 helps LDAdam, the theoretical guidance on when to use 0.99 vs. 0.999 is limited. This is still a hyperparameter that requires tuning.

5. **Computational overhead of rotation estimation**: Computing and applying the rotation (coordinate change) at each refresh step introduces non-trivial overhead—the paper doesn't quantify wall-clock time vs. memory savings tradeoff.

## Open Questions
1. **Fast k* estimation**: Can we diagnose reproducible rank cheaply (e.g., from a single minibatch or small samples) before committing to low-rank training?

2. **Non-isotropic gradients**: How does the first-moment transport result change when gradients are non-isotropic (e.g., in structured sparsity, outlier-heavy models)? Does LDAdam still apply?

3. **Generalization across architectures**: What architectural properties determine the reproducible rank k*? Can we predict it from model width, depth, or gradient statistics?

4. **Multi-scale low-rank**: Should r and k* vary across layers or training phases? Can we use layer-specific reproducible ranks for better efficiency?

5. **Theoretical convergence rates**: Does LDAdam have formal convergence guarantees, and are they better than GaLore? The paper is empirical on this front.

6. **Hybrid approaches**: What if we use full-rank for early training (when subspaces are unstable) and switch to LDAdam once k* stabilizes? Is there an adaptive switching rule?

## Key Takeaway
Low-rank subspace tracking fails not because of slow drift but because subspace estimates are dominated by irreducible noise; treating refreshes as optimizer state coordinate changes (LDAdam) fixes this and clarifies when low-rank training is reliable.
