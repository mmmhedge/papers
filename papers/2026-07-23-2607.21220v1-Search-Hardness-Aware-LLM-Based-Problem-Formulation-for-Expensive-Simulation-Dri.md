# Search Hardness-Aware LLM-Based Problem Formulation for Expensive Simulation-Driven Design

**Authors:** Yuchen Li, Handing Wang, Bing Xue, Mengjie Zhang
**Published:** 2026-07-23 | **arXiv:** [2607.21220v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21220v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes SHA-PF, a framework that uses large language models to automatically reformulate engineering design problems in ways that make them easier to solve. Rather than just translating requirements into objectives and constraints, the method discovers formulations that guide optimization algorithms toward efficient search by prioritizing samples with high potential for progress—dramatically reducing the number of expensive simulations needed to find good designs.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on two key ideas. First, **expensive simulation-driven design** — a common engineering practice where each evaluation of a candidate design is computationally costly, so the goal is to find satisfactory solutions using as few evaluations as possible. Second, **problem formulation** — the choice of how to represent objectives, constraints, and variables shapes the search landscape that an optimization algorithm explores. Recent work has used [[large language models]] to translate natural-language requirements into formal mathematical formulations, but prior methods focused on whether the formulation matched the designer's intent, not whether it led to efficient optimization. This paper introduces **search hardness** as a new criterion: a formulation is better if it guides the solver toward rare, high-value regions of the design space.

## Core Contributions
1. **Search hardness as a formulation metric**: The key insight that formulations should be scored by how efficiently they guide search toward progress, not just by design-intent alignment.
2. **SHA-PF framework**: An LLM-based method that iteratively generates, repairs, and refines problem formulations using evolutionary search, guided by search hardness scores.
3. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrations on multi-objective benchmarks and five real antenna design problems, showing that SHA-PF formulations require significantly fewer simulations than baseline approaches.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[optimization]] and [[large language models]]. It extends recent LLM-based automatic problem formulation methods by adding a performance-aware objective. The idea that problem structure shapes algorithmic efficiency relates to classical work on problem landscapes and fitness functions in evolutionary computation. The use of LLMs for generation and repair reflects broader trends in using generative models for code and constraint synthesis. The evolutionary refinement step echoes meta-learning and algorithm configuration literature, which similarly treat algorithm hyperparameters or design choices as objects to be optimized.

## Practical Applications
- **Aerospace engineering**: Designing aircraft wings or antenna arrays where each simulation takes hours; SHA-PF could reduce design cycles from weeks to days.
- **Automotive design**: Optimizing vehicle components (suspensions, aerodynamics) under expensive computational fluid dynamics simulations.
- **Materials discovery**: Finding novel material compositions where simulation is expensive; fewer evaluations translate directly to cost savings.
- **Microelectronics**: Circuit or chip layout optimization where full electromagnetic or thermal simulations are prohibitively costly.
- **Structural engineering**: Bridge or building design where expensive finite-element analysis dominates the design bottleneck.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability to high-dimensional spaces**: Experiments focus on moderate-dimensional problems (antenna design); performance in 100+ dimensional design spaces is unclear.
2. **Simulator dependency**: The method requires a working simulator and assumes expensive evaluations are the bottleneck; if simulation is cheap or parallelizable at scale, the benefit diminishes.
3. **LLM quality and consistency**: Formulations are only as good as the LLM's ability to generate and repair them; no formal guarantees on formulation correctness or robustness.
4. **Search hardness estimation**: Scoring formulations by search hardness requires sampling and evaluating candidate designs during the formulation search phase—this itself incurs simulation cost and may not fully predict real-world hardness.
5. **Limited to simulation-driven design**: Does not address cases where objectives are implicitly defined by physical laws or where the problem is poorly specified by the user.
6. **Generalization**: Unclear how well formulations discovered on one problem class transfer to similar but distinct problems.

## Open Questions
1. **How does formulation quality scale with problem complexity?** Do the discovered formulations remain effective for much larger, real industrial problems?
2. **Can the method handle multi-fidelity simulations?** Many engineering workflows use coarse and fine-fidelity simulators; can SHA-PF exploit that hierarchy?
3. **What is the cost-benefit tradeoff of formulation search?** How many simulations are spent discovering a good formulation vs. solving the final design problem, and is the total savings always positive?
4. **Can humans learn from discovered formulations?** Do the reformulated problems provide engineering insight, or are they opaque to domain experts?
5. **How robust are formulations to simulator noise or approximation errors?** Engineering simulators often have tuning parameters; how sensitive is the discovered formulation to those choices?
6. **Can this extend to multi-agent or collaborative design scenarios?** What if multiple engineers with different objectives need to agree on a shared formulation?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs can automatically discover problem formulations that guide expensive design optimization more efficiently by prioritizing high-potential regions of the search space.
