# The Illusion of Equivalency: Statistical Characterization of Quantization Effects in LLMs

**Authors:** Baha Rababah, Cuneyt Gurcan Akcora, Carson K. Leung
**Published:** 2026-07-09 | **arXiv:** [2607.08734v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08734v1)

## Summary
This paper challenges the conventional wisdom that quantization (compressing model weights to fewer bits) preserves model behavior when standard metrics like accuracy stay high. The authors show that quantized LLMs actually make different predictions than their full-precision counterparts on individual examples, even when overall performance appears identical—what they call an "illusion of equivalence." They propose new evaluation methods and analyze where in the model structure quantization causes the most damage.

## Theoretical Background
Large language models are typically trained and stored in full precision (32-bit floating point), which is expensive to deploy. **Quantization** reduces this to lower bit-widths (e.g., 8-bit, 4-bit, or 2-bit integers), dramatically cutting memory and computation costs. Prior work assumed that if aggregate metrics like accuracy or perplexity remain stable post-quantization, the models behave equivalently. However, this work builds on emerging understanding that aggregate metrics can mask behavioral drift—a concept familiar from fairness and robustness literature. The paper uses techniques from **mechanistic interpretability** (analyzing internal model structure, in this case attention weights) to understand *why* quantization breaks things, treating quantization as a structural operator that distorts the model's learned representations.

## Core Contributions
1. **Correctness agreement metric**: A decision-level measure showing what fraction of examples a base model and quantized model both get correct—independent of absolute accuracy. This reveals divergence invisible to standard metrics.
2. **Empirical evidence of behavioral divergence**: Across multiple models and quantization schemes, behavioral changes emerge at moderate compression even when task accuracy is preserved.
3. **Layer-wise structural analysis**: Quantization is analyzed as an operator on attention weights. Query and key projections are shown to be consistently more sensitive to quantization than value and output projections—a previously uncharacterized asymmetry.
4. **Non-linear breakpoints**: Discovery that quantization effects exhibit non-linear transitions at low bit-widths rather than graceful degradation.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to broader themes in [[Mechanistic Interpretability]] of transformers—understanding *where* in the architecture problems occur. It echoes concerns from [[RLHF]] and alignment work about whether proxy metrics (like accuracy) truly capture model behavior. The focus on agreement-level metrics parallels robustness evaluation in adversarial ML and fairness literature. The structural focus on attention mechanism distortions connects to recent work analyzing [[Attention Is All You Need]] and why attention patterns matter for model output.

## Practical Applications
- **Deployment safety**: Before shipping a quantized model to production, practitioners can now measure behavioral equivalence, not just accuracy, to catch edge cases where quantization changed decision boundaries.
- **Model card documentation**: Quantized models could be shipped with correctness agreement scores, helping users understand which predictions might differ from full-precision versions.
- **Compression strategy selection**: Engineers choosing between 8-bit, 4-bit, and 2-bit quantization can now measure actual behavioral drift, guiding the compression-accuracy tradeoff.
- **Debugging quantization failures**: Layer-wise sensitivity analysis enables targeted fixes—e.g., keeping query/key projections at higher precision while aggressively quantizing value/output.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Quantization validation SaaS**: A tool that automatically measures correctness agreement and layer-wise distortion for any LLM before deployment, surfacing risky bit-width choices.
2. **Behavioral testing suite for quantized models**: Extend to domain-specific tests (e.g., medical QA, legal reasoning) to certify that quantized models don't change their decision patterns on high-stakes examples.
3. **Adaptive quantization service**: Use layer-wise sensitivity findings to automatically assign different bit-widths per layer, maximizing compression while meeting a user-defined behavioral agreement threshold.
4. **Quantization insurance/auditing**: Offer post-hoc audits of deployed quantized models to measure actual behavioral drift and flag unexpected discrepancies in production.
5. **Efficient fine-tuning for quantized models**: Develop techniques to re-align quantized models to base model behavior, using correctness agreement as a fine-tuning objective.

## Technical Limitations
- **Metric interpretation**: Correctness agreement measures *overlap* but doesn't distinguish whether quantization fixes mistakes (good) or introduces new errors (bad)—context matters.
- **Computational cost of evaluation**: Computing correctness agreement requires running both base and quantized models on large test sets, adding overhead that may limit practical adoption.
- **Limited scope**: Analysis focuses on attention weights; other components (MLPs, embeddings) receive less scrutiny. Generalizability across model families and domains unclear.
- **Attribution gap**: The paper identifies that query/key projections are sensitive but doesn't fully explain *why* at a mechanistic level—correlation, not causation.
- **Assumptions about bit-width**: Results are empirical; no universal formula for predicting breakpoints across different models or quantization schemes (uniform vs. mixed-precision).

## Open Questions
- Why are query and key projections inherently more sensitive? Is this a property of attention mathematics or learned weight structure?
- Can correctness agreement be predicted *before* quantization, without actually quantizing and evaluating?
- Do behavioral divergences on mismatched examples correlate with known failure modes (hallucination, reasoning errors, etc.) or are they orthogonal?
- How do mixed-precision schemes (different bit-widths per layer) interact with behavioral agreement? Can layer-wise sensitivity be exploited to design optimal mixed-precision strategies?
- Does fine-tuning quantized models on correctness-disagreement examples improve behavioral alignment, or are some divergences irreversible?

## Key Takeaway
Standard accuracy metrics create a false sense of safety around quantized LLMs—behavioral divergence is far more common than performance metrics suggest, and understanding where this divergence occurs in model internals is essential for safe deployment.
