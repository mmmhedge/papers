# SciDiagramEdit: Learning to Edit Scientific Diagrams from Paper Revisions

**Authors:** Yasheng Sun, Zezi Zeng, Yifan Yang, Chong Luo, Wenyi Wang et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.15272v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15272v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces SciDiagramEdit, a system that automatically edits scientific figures based on natural-language instructions by learning from real paper revisions found on arXiv. The work matters because figure editing is tedious and repetitive for researchers, and automating it could save significant time while maintaining the visual grammar and semantic correctness that dense scientific diagrams require.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two key ideas: (1) **instruction-driven image editing** — systems that modify images based on text commands, which typically struggle with complex, structured visuals; and (2) **agentic learning** — training systems to refine their own behavior by iteratively proposing and executing skills. Scientific diagrams are harder to edit than natural images because they contain heterogeneous elements (plots, schematics, photos, arrows, captions) arranged under strict visual and semantic rules. The authors leverage **vector graphics** (editable primitive shapes) rather than raster images, allowing fine-grained control over individual components. The skill evolution framework uses an iterative loop where a proposer suggests improved instructions, the agent executes them, and performance on a validation set guides refinement.

## Core Contributions
- **SciDiagramEdit benchmark**: A large-scale dataset of before/after figure pairs mined from arXiv version histories, grounded in authors' actual revision intents rather than synthetic edits.
- **Skill evolution framework**: An agentic learning approach where an autonomous proposer continuously refines the agent's skill specification across multiple epochs, lifting accuracy without manual annotation.
- **Vector-based editing pipeline**: Operating on editable vector sources rather than pixels, enabling both interpretable agent actions and collaborative human–agent co-editing.
- **Evidence that natural revisions are effective training signals**: Demonstrates that real paper edits encode sufficient instruction diversity and visual patterns to train generalizable editing skills.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to broader work on [[instruction-driven image editing]], which typically focuses on natural images; SciDiagramEdit extends this to structured, domain-specific visuals. It also connects to [[multi-agent reinforcement learning]] and [[skill learning]], where agents autonomously propose and refine behaviors. The vector-graphics approach parallels work in [[neural rendering]] and [[neural scene representations]] that operate on compositional primitives rather than pixels. The use of real revision history as training signal echoes ideas in [[data programming]] and [[learning from human behavior]].

## Practical Applications
- **Manuscript submission workflow**: Authors revise figures iteratively; an integrated editor could suggest edits based on revision instructions, reducing manual labor.
- **Collaborative paper writing**: Multiple co-authors can use the tool to propagate stylistic or layout changes across figures consistently.
- **Paper template adaptation**: Authors copying figures between papers (or adapting templates) can quickly relabel and rearrange components via natural language.
- **Accessibility and reproducibility**: Researchers can systematically update figures when experimental parameters change (e.g., changing axis labels, legend entries, or panel orders).

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **ArXiv Assistant Plugin**: A browser extension or Overleaf integration that suggests figure edits as authors write revision notes, learning from each user's style.
2. **Scientific Diagram-as-a-Service**: Cloud API for researchers to upload figures and request edits in plain English; charges per edit or per manuscript.
3. **Paper Diff Tool for Figures**: Enterprise tool for research teams to visualize, track, and auto-generate changelogs for figures across paper revisions; pairs with version control systems.
4. **Figure Templating Platform**: Publishers (Nature, Science, JMLR) offer templates where authors describe edits once, system auto-applies to all affiliated figures across a journal submission.
5. **Consortium Dataset & Licensing**: Monetize the arXiv revision dataset by licensing it to academic institutions and AI companies for figure editing research.

## Technical Limitations
- **Vector graphics requirement**: Only works on figures available as editable vector sources (SVG, PDF with vector layers); rasterized figures or images embedded in PDFs are out of scope.
- **Semantic understanding**: The agent must infer visual grammar rules from examples; it may struggle with novel diagram types not well-represented in arXiv (e.g., specialized field-specific infographics).
- **Instruction ambiguity**: Natural-language revision intents from papers may be implicit or vague; the framework assumes enough revision pairs exist to learn consistent patterns.
- **Computational cost**: Skill evolution requires multiple training epochs and re-evaluation; scaling to very large, complex figures may be slow.
- **Generalization across domains**: Trained on arXiv (computer science, math, physics); transfer to other fields (biology, chemistry, medicine) with different diagram conventions is untested.

## Open Questions
- How does performance scale to three-dimensional diagrams, interactive figures, or videos?
- Can the framework learn to generate *novel* edits (not just refinements of existing patterns)?
- Does the agent learn truly compositional skills, or does it memorize figure-specific patterns? Can we measure compositional generalization rigorously?
- How sensitive is skill evolution to the quality and diversity of the initial revision corpus? What is the minimum dataset size?
- Can the human proposer (in skill evolution) be fully automated, or does human-in-the-loop feedback remain necessary for complex edits?
- Does the approach work on figures from fields outside computer science (biology, medicine), or is it overfitted to arXiv's domain?

## Key Takeaway
Learning to edit scientific diagrams from real paper revisions enables fast, language-driven figure updates grounded in actual research practice.
