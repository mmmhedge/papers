# PolarBM: Complex-valued Boltzmann Machine for Modeling Audio Signals in Polar and Log-polar Coordinates

**Authors:** Toru Nakashika, Kohei Yatabe
**Published:** 2026-07-14 | **arXiv:** [2607.12417v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12417v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces PolarBM, a Boltzmann machine designed to naturally handle complex-valued data (numbers with both magnitude and phase) using polar coordinates instead of converting to real numbers. The key insight is that amplitude and phase are physically linked in signals like audio spectrograms, and modeling this relationship directly improves performance on tasks like audio modeling compared to standard neural networks that treat them independently.

## Theoretical Background
Boltzmann machines are energy-based generative models that learn probability distributions over data by minimizing an energy function. Traditionally, they work with real-valued variables. Audio spectrograms are naturally represented as complex numbers (amplitude × phase), but most machine learning methods either discard the phase information or treat amplitude and phase as independent real dimensions, losing the constraint that they physically co-vary. The paper builds on the theory of complex-valued neural networks and probability distributions, introducing the power-weighted noncentral complex Gaussian (PW-NCCG) distribution — a flexible family that unifies several classical distributions (Rice, Nakagami, noncentral chi) as special cases.

## Core Contributions
1. **PolarBM**: A Boltzmann machine with an energy function defined in polar coordinates (amplitude, phase) where phase probability explicitly depends on amplitude, capturing their physical coupling.
2. **LogPolarBM**: An extension modeling amplitude on a logarithmic scale to align with human auditory perception (loudness is logarithmic).
3. **Restricted variants (PolarRBM, LogPolarRBM)**: Practical layered versions for tractable training on real audio data.
4. **Theoretical unification**: Shows the resulting marginal amplitude distributions generalize Rice, Nakagami, and noncentral chi families, providing a principled probabilistic framework for complex signals.
5. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates superior modeling accuracy compared to deep neural networks on audio datasets.

## Connections to Other Work
This extends [[Boltzmann machines|RBMs]] to the complex domain, complementing prior work on complex-valued neural networks (which typically don't model amplitude-phase coupling). It contrasts with standard [[deep-learning]] approaches that either discard phase or treat it as independent noise. The polar-coordinate formulation parallels work in [[computer-vision]] that uses polar/log-polar representations for rotation invariance, though applied here to a probabilistic generative model. The LogPolarBM variant echoes mel-scale spectrograms widely used in speech processing, but grounded in a principled statistical model rather than engineering heuristic.

## Practical Applications
1. **Audio enhancement & denoising**: Using learned amplitude-phase relationships to reconstruct clean speech from noisy recordings.
2. **Audio compression**: Efficiently encode spectrograms by exploiting learned dependencies between amplitude and phase.
3. **Speech synthesis & vocoding**: Generate natural-sounding waveforms by sampling from the learned complex-valued distribution.
4. **Wireless communication signal recovery**: Reconstruct transmitted complex-valued signals from noisy channel observations.
5. **Quantum state tomography**: Model quantum wavefunctions (inherently complex) for state estimation in quantum experiments.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Computational cost**: Boltzmann machines (especially unrestricted ones) are notoriously slow to train (MCMC sampling is expensive); scalability to very large audio datasets or long sequences remains unclear.
2. **Phase modeling assumptions**: The model assumes phase is conditionally Gaussian given amplitude, which may not hold for all signal types (e.g., highly nonlinear signals, music with strong phase discontinuities).
3. **Limited baselines**: Experiments compare mainly against feedforward DNNs; no comparison to modern state-of-the-art generative models (variational autoencoders, diffusion models, transformers) or phase-aware methods like Griffin-Lim.
4. **Audio experiments only**: Despite claims of broad applicability, only audio is demonstrated; wireless and quantum experiments are mentioned but not provided.
5. **No temporal modeling**: Treats each spectrogram frame independently; doesn't capture dynamics or temporal coherence in audio signals.

## Open Questions
1. How does PolarRBM scale to high-resolution spectrograms (e.g., 512+ frequency bins) or long audio sequences? What is the actual wall-clock training time?
2. Could a deeper variant (e.g., deep belief network in polar form) improve modeling further, and is the added complexity worth it?
3. How does phase modeling break down for different signal types (music vs. speech vs. noise)? Should models be task-specific?
4. Can this framework be combined with temporal models (RNNs, transformers) to capture audio dynamics while preserving amplitude-phase coupling?
5. Is the logarithmic amplitude assumption (LogPolarBM) optimal for all audio tasks, or should it be learned?
6. How do the learned distributions differ across speakers, languages, or recording conditions? Can they be used for speaker identification or signal classification?

## Key Takeaway
Complex audio signals have linked amplitude and phase; modeling this dependency directly in a Boltzmann machine beats standard neural networks.
