# Resample or Reroute? Budget-Aware Test-Time Model Selection for Large Language Models

**Authors:** Teng-Ruei Chen
**Published:** 2026-07-09 | **arXiv:** [2607.08665v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08665v1)

## Summary
This paper addresses a practical problem in deploying multiple large language models (LLMs): how to best spend a limited per-query budget on either resampling the same model or rerouting to a different one. The authors propose an online policy that dynamically allocates budget between these two strategies using an imperfect verifier, and show it achieves better cost-quality tradeoffs than existing approaches across multiple benchmarks.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on recent work showing that test-time resampling (generating multiple outputs and selecting the best) can recover performance gains beyond what a single committed router (a model that picks which LLM to use upfront) can achieve. However, prior work assumed unlimited budget and perfect correctness labels — unrealistic for production systems. The core tension is between two competing uses of finite budget: sampling more outputs from the current model versus switching to a potentially better model. The work uses marginal utility analysis — computing expected correctness gain per unit cost — to guide allocation decisions.

## Core Contributions
1. **Problem formulation**: Explicitly frames resampling vs. rerouting as competing allocation problems under a per-query cost budget with imperfect verification.
2. **RoR policy**: Proposes a concrete online allocation policy that estimates marginal correctness per unit cost and dynamically chooses between resampling and rerouting.
3. **Empirical validation**: Generates multi-draw correctness tensors across 11 open-weight models and 4 benchmarks (new experimental infrastructure), and shows RoR achieves favorable Pareto fronts.
4. **Verifier dependence analysis**: Ablations reveal that gains shrink with verifier quality and test robustness under label-free verifiers.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to [[dynamic routing]] in mixture-of-experts systems and [[best-of-K]] sampling strategies, but uniquely treats them as budget-constrained allocation problems. It connects to broader work on [[test-time computation]] and [[inference-time scaling]] — the observation that more computation at inference can improve quality. The oracle gap motivation aligns with work on [[learned routing]] for LLM serving and cost-quality tradeoffs in practical deployment.

## Practical Applications
- **Multi-LLM serving infrastructure**: Cloud providers (AWS, GCP, Azure) could use RoR to route customer queries across available models (GPT, Claude, Llama) within compute budgets.
- **Edge deployment with fallbacks**: Mobile or edge devices with access to both local and remote models could decide dynamically whether to recompute locally or send to cloud.
- **Agentic systems**: AI agents making tool calls could allocate budget between retrying with more reasoning or switching tools entirely.
- **Enterprise LLM platforms**: Internal tools routing between specialized fine-tuned models vs. general-purpose ones under SLA cost constraints.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Cost-aware LLM gateway**: A proxy service that sits between applications and LLM APIs, automatically tuning resampling vs. rerouting to meet cost and latency targets while maximizing correctness.
2. **Correctness verification as a service**: A dedicated verifier API (trained or ensemble-based) that charges per check, optimizing when to verify outputs and when to retry vs. reroute.
3. **LLM budget optimizer for enterprises**: SaaS tool for companies using multiple LLM providers; automatically negotiates provider pricing and dynamically allocates queries to minimize total cost while hitting quality SLAs.
4. **Inference marketplace matching**: Platform matching queries to the cheapest/best model combination dynamically, with built-in resampling and rerouting policies based on query type and budget.
5. **Agentic task planner**: Framework for AI agents that treats function calls and model retries as budget-constrained resources, optimizing plans to meet time/cost/quality constraints.

## Technical Limitations
- **Verifier quality assumption**: The entire approach depends on having a reasonably accurate correctness verifier; results degrade significantly with poor verifiers, and many domains lack cheap, reliable verifiers.
- **Offline to online gap**: Training is done on replay (batch) data; online performance under distribution shift or changing model availability is unexplored.
- **Limited model pool**: Experiments use 11 models, mostly open-weight; behavior with larger or more heterogeneous pools (commercial APIs with different latency profiles) unclear.
- **Benchmark diversity**: Four benchmarks tested; generalization to long-form generation, code, or reasoning tasks not demonstrated.
- **Marginal utility estimation**: The policy estimates marginal correctness per cost; estimation error under low-sample or novel-query regimes not analyzed.
- **Cold-start and exploration**: No discussion of how to handle new models or queries with no prior correctness distribution data.

## Open Questions
- How does RoR perform under **distribution shift** when the correctness landscape of models changes post-deployment?
- Can the policy be **learned end-to-end** (e.g., with RL) rather than relying on heuristic marginal-cost estimation?
- How sensitive is performance to **verifier calibration** and are there principled ways to choose verifier strength?
- Does the approach scale to **very large model pools** (100s of models) or is there a curse of dimensionality?
- Can we do **online exploration** to adaptively learn model correctness and improve allocation over time without too much exploration cost?
- What happens under **non-stationary** conditions (model updates, API outages, price changes)?

## Key Takeaway
When serving multiple LLMs under cost constraints, dynamically allocating budget between resampling and rerouting beats committing to a fixed strategy, but only if you have a reliable verifier and can estimate per-option marginal correctness per cost.
