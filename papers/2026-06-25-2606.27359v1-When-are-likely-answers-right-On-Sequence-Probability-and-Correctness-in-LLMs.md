# When are likely answers right? On Sequence Probability and Correctness in LLMs

**Authors:** Johannes Zenn, Jonas Geiping
**Published:** 2026-06-25 | **arXiv:** [2606.27359v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27359v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates the fundamental relationship between sequence probability (how likely an LLM assigns to an output) and correctness across different decoding strategies, models, and benchmarks. The authors find that while higher sequence probability correlates with correctness within a fixed dataset, this relationship breaks down when trying to improve accuracy through decoding hyperparameters or methods—a counterintuitive finding that challenges common assumptions underlying popular decoding techniques.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on the foundational understanding of [[autoregressive-models|autoregressive language models]] and their probability assignments to sequences. It engages with decoding method theory—the idea that methods like beam search, nucleus sampling, and temperature scaling work by manipulating probability mass toward higher-likelihood outputs. The work implicitly relies on classical notions of calibration and the assumption that model confidence correlates with correctness, which is well-studied in [[interpretability]] literature. The four-level analysis framework (methods, hyperparameters, prompt-answer pairs, repeated responses) provides a structured decomposition of where probability-correctness alignment might and might not hold.

## Core Contributions
1. **Systematic quantification** of the probability-correctness relationship at four distinct granularity levels, revealing where correlations exist and where they fail.
2. **Counterintuitive finding**: Higher sequence probability correlates with correctness *descriptively* (across static prompt-answer pairs) but *not prescriptively* (when actively optimizing hyperparameters/methods).
3. **Practical clarity** on when decoding improvements can/cannot be expected, directly challenging the theoretical justification for many existing methods.
4. **Evidence that sequence probability is a poor indicator within repeated responses to the same prompt**, suggesting model stochasticity and prompting effects overwhelm probability signals.

## Connections to Other Work
This work directly informs the design space of [[beam-search]], temperature-based sampling, and [[nucleus-sampling]]-style decoding methods. It relates to broader questions in [[mechanistic-interpretability]] about what model probabilities actually represent and whether they reflect world knowledge or surface-level pattern matching. The findings have implications for [[self-consistency]] methods (like self-consistency decoding) that aggregate multiple samples, and for [[reward-shaping]]-based approaches that use model confidence as a signal. It also touches on [[RLHF]] and [[rlhf-alternatives]], since those methods implicitly assume probability and correctness can be aligned through training. The work resonates with calibration studies in [[distribution-alignment]] and questions about when learned distributions truly reflect task performance.

## Practical Applications
1. **Smarter decoding configuration**: Systems can now understand that tuning temperature/top-p within a fixed method is unlikely to improve accuracy, avoiding wasted hyperparameter search.
2. **Verifier-free self-improvement systems**: Knowing that sequence probability alone is unreliable for single outputs, organizations can invest in explicit verifiers rather than relying on probability-based filtering.
3. **Efficient ensemble methods**: Self-consistency and voting-based decoding remain viable when probability doesn't help—practitioners should ensemble diverse samples rather than filtering by probability.
4. **Model evaluation protocols**: Benchmark design should account for the fact that probability rankings within a dataset are meaningful, but cannot be assumed to transfer across datasets or methods.
5. **Confidence-based UI design**: LLM applications can abandon probability-based confidence displays and instead use explicit uncertainty measures (verifiers, ensemble disagreement).

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Smart decoding orchestrator**: A tool that automatically selects decoding method + hyperparameters by learning dataset-specific probability-correctness relationships, then falls back to verifier-free ensembling when probability signals fail.
2. **Lightweight verifier marketplace**: A platform offering plug-and-play task-specific verifiers (trained on modest data) that replace probability-based filtering, with SaaS APIs for LLM applications.
3. **Benchmark-aware LLM API**: An inference service that maintains a database of probability-correctness calibration curves per benchmark/domain, advising users on decoding choices and confidence thresholds.
4. **Self-consistency as a service**: A managed platform optimizing self-consistency decoding (sampling + voting), with analytics showing where probability-based methods fail and where voting succeeds.
5. **LLM reliability monitor**: Enterprise tool that flags when a deployed LLM's decoding is misaligned with correctness on new domains, triggering alerts for retuning or verifier addition.

## Technical Limitations
- **Scope constraints**: Findings are empirical and benchmark-dependent; transferability to new domains, model architectures, or tasks is unclear.
- **Causality vs. correlation**: The paper documents that probability and correctness *don't* align under intervention, but doesn't deeply explain *why*—the mechanistic source remains opaque.
- **Limited theoretical framework**: No formal characterization of when probability-correctness alignment should or shouldn't exist; insights are largely observational.
- **Decoding method coverage**: Likely focuses on standard methods (temperature, top-p, beam search); newer approaches (speculative decoding, guided generation) may behave differently.
- **Single vs. multiple turns**: Analysis likely focuses on single-turn prompting; multi-turn dialogue or in-context learning dynamics may exhibit different patterns.
- **Model heterogeneity**: Unclear how findings scale across model sizes, architectures (e.g., different Transformer variants), or training procedures.

## Open Questions
1. **Mechanistic explanation**: Why does probability-correctness correlation hold within a dataset but not under hyperparameter intervention? Is it due to model calibration drift, distribution shift, or something else?
2. **Domain-specific calibration**: Are there domain/task properties (e.g., factuality vs. reasoning) where probability remains predictive even under optimization?
3. **Hybrid approaches**: Can we design decoding methods that maintain probability signals while being more robust to hyperparameter changes?
4. **Verifier design**: Given probability fails, what lightweight verifier signals (e.g., internal activations, token-level uncertainty) are most predictive of correctness?
5. **Transferability**: Do probability-correctness relationships learned on one benchmark transfer to others? Can we predict when they will?
6. **In-context learning dynamics**: How do probability-correctness relationships change with few-shot examples or chain-of-thought prompts?

## Key Takeaway
Sequence probability predicts correctness *descriptively* within a dataset but fails *prescriptively* when used to guide decoding decisions—meaning raw probability rankings should inform analysis but not hyperparameter tuning.
