# AdvancedMathBench: A Benchmark Suite for Advanced Mathematical Proof Generation and Verification

**Authors:** Lingkai Kong, Zijian Wu, Yuzhe Gu, Haiteng Zhao, Wenyong Huang et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-13 | **arXiv:** [2607.11849v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11849v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces AdvancedMathBench, a benchmark for evaluating how well large language models can generate and verify advanced mathematical proofs at the undergraduate and doctoral level. Current models struggle significantly on this task — even the best achieves only 66% on doctoral-level problems — and the paper also shows that models are poor at detecting errors in proofs, suggesting that reasoning verification remains a critical unsolved problem.

## Theoretical Background
Large language models have demonstrated strong performance on mathematical reasoning tasks, particularly on high-school and olympiad-level competition math. However, most existing benchmarks (like MATH, GSM8K, and others) focus on problems with short, verifiable final answers rather than extended logical arguments. The paper builds on the intuition that proof verification — checking not just whether an answer is correct, but whether the reasoning is sound — requires understanding proof structure and mathematical validity. The automatic verification pipeline uses expert-annotated proofs to train classifiers that can assess proof correctness and pinpoint specific error types, moving beyond simple binary "right or wrong" judgments.

## Core Contributions
1. **ProverBench**: A new benchmark with 296 advanced mathematics problems (undergraduate and doctoral qualifying exam levels) requiring multi-step proof generation, substantially harder than existing datasets.
2. **Automatic verification pipeline**: A trained model that assesses proofs with fine-grained error detection, showing strong agreement with human experts—moving beyond final-answer grading to evaluate the validity of reasoning steps.
3. **VerifierBench**: A dataset of 888 model-generated proofs paired with expert annotations, allowing evaluation of whether models can correctly identify when proofs are valid or invalid and provide sound reasoning for their judgments.
4. **Empirical findings**: Demonstration that frontier models (GPT-4.5-xhigh) achieve only 75.8% and 66.1% on undergraduate and doctoral problems respectively, and struggle with error detection (Balanced F1 of 65.1), revealing critical gaps in proof verification capability.

## Connections to Other Work
This work extends the tradition of mathematical reasoning benchmarks (e.g., [[MATH]], [[GSM8K]]) by targeting advanced rather than competition mathematics. It relates to recent work on formal verification and theorem proving, though it focuses on natural-language proof assessment rather than machine-checkable formal proofs. The automatic verification approach connects to work on [[RLHF]] (reward modeling) and process-based supervision, where models learn to evaluate intermediate steps rather than just final answers. It also touches on the broader challenge of [[mechanistic-interpretability]] — understanding why models fail at reasoning — and the alignment problem of ensuring models can justify their conclusions accurately.

## Practical Applications
1. **AI-assisted mathematics education**: Tools that provide students with generated proofs and automatically identify logical gaps, enabling personalized feedback.
2. **Automated theorem-proving assistants**: Systems that help mathematicians at research institutions verify intermediate steps in long-form proofs without manual checking.
3. **Curriculum validation**: Educational platforms using AdvancedMathBench to assess whether advanced math tutoring systems are truly teaching rigorous reasoning versus pattern matching.
4. **Proof-checking in academic publishing**: Pre-submission analysis of research papers to flag potential logical errors in mathematical arguments before peer review.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **MathVerify**: An API service for universities and research institutions that automatically validates mathematical proofs, offers fine-grained error reports, and suggests corrections—monetized as a SaaS for math departments and publishers.
2. **ProofTutor**: An interactive homework platform for advanced undergraduates that generates proofs on demand, explains each step with natural language, and asks students to identify logical gaps before revealing the answer key.
3. **Research Assistant for Mathematicians**: A Copilot-style tool that takes handwritten or LaTeX proofs, converts them to standardized form, runs automated verification, and flags suspicious steps for manual review—bundled with theorem-proving backends.
4. **Math Peer Review Automation**: A service for open-access math journals and preprint servers (arXiv) that flags potentially unsound proofs in submissions, reducing reviewer burden.
5. **Corporate Training in Formal Reasoning**: A B2B offering for finance, insurance, and consulting firms to train analysts and modelers on rigorous mathematical argumentation, using AdvancedMathBench-style problems as diagnostic tools.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scope constraint**: The benchmark covers only 296 problems; advanced mathematics spans many disciplines (topology, functional analysis, abstract algebra, number theory, etc.), so coverage within each area is sparse.
2. **Expert annotation bottleneck**: Fine-grained error annotations rely on manual expert review, making the benchmark expensive to extend and potentially introducing inconsistent grading standards across annotators.
3. **Natural-language vs. formal reasoning**: Proofs in natural language admit ambiguity and informal leaps that are harder to verify automatically than machine-checkable formal proofs; the verification pipeline may reward surface-level correctness over deep logical rigor.
4. **Generalization uncertainty**: Success on AdvancedMathBench does not guarantee that models can reason about novel mathematical domains, as memorization of proof patterns from training data may inflate performance.
5. **Error detection plateau**: The low true negative rate (failure to catch invalid proofs) suggests that models struggle with adversarial or subtly flawed arguments, and the benchmark may not adequately test edge cases.

## Open Questions
1. What architectural or training innovations (e.g., process-based [[reinforcement-learning]], intermediate supervision, or specialized tokenization for mathematical symbols) could close the gap between current performance (~66%) and near-human levels on doctoral-level proofs?
2. Does pre-training on formal mathematical languages (Lean, Coq, Isabelle) or hybrid formal-informal datasets improve proof verification accuracy, and if so, how much?
3. Can models be trained to generate confidence scores or uncertainty estimates for proofs, and does this calibration improve error detection?
4. How do different proof styles (constructive vs. non-constructive, direct vs. by contradiction) affect model performance, and are there systematic biases?
5. Does the automatic verification pipeline generalize to out-of-distribution proof types (e.g., from different mathematical fields or written by human mathematicians in uncommon styles)?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs struggle significantly with advanced mathematical proofs—even frontier models achieve only 66% on doctoral-level problems and fail to detect logical errors reliably.
