# LLM for the development of FCM

**Authors:** Alexis Kafantaris
**Published:** 2026-07-06 | **arXiv:** [2607.04983v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.04983v1)

## Summary
This paper demonstrates how a local large language model (Qwen2.5-32B) can automatically extract quantitative relationships from text to build fuzzy cognitive maps — a type of knowledge graph that models cause-and-effect connections between concepts. The authors validate their approach on hotel reviews, showing that the resulting map can predict customer satisfaction ratings without being explicitly trained on them.

## Theoretical Background
Fuzzy cognitive maps (FCMs) are a knowledge representation technique that combines fuzzy logic (reasoning with degrees of truth rather than strict true/false) with graph-based networks of concepts and their causal relationships. Large language models (LLMs) are neural networks trained on vast text corpora that can perform complex language understanding tasks, including information extraction — pulling structured data from unstructured text. This work sits at the intersection of two older fields: it repurposes modern LLMs (which have shown strong performance on structured prediction tasks) as extractors to populate the traditional fuzzy logic framework. The key insight is that an LLM's learned representations of language meaning can be converted into explicit quantitative relationships suitable for fuzzy graphs.

## Core Contributions
The paper's main contribution is a practical pipeline: (1) use a local LLM to extract entity relationships and their strengths from raw text without preprocessing or filtering, (2) construct a data-driven fuzzy cognitive map from those extractions, and (3) validate the map on an out-of-distribution task (predicting star ratings) to show the learned structure captures genuine semantic content. The work demonstrates this on Greek-language hotel reviews, creating a star-topology FCM that reflects reviewer preferences. The external validation—showing correlation between FCM predictions and ratings the model never saw—is the key empirical claim.

## Connections to Other Work
This bridges [[large-language-models|LLMs]] and knowledge representation systems. It relates to information extraction work in [[NLP]] more broadly, and to explainable AI approaches that seek interpretable, human-readable models rather than black-box neural networks. FCMs are an older symbolic/neuro-symbolic technique; this work is a modern instantiation using LLMs as the extraction engine rather than manual annotation or hand-coded rules. The external validation approach is similar to out-of-distribution testing in machine learning, and the application domain (sentiment/satisfaction from reviews) touches on opinion mining and aspect-based sentiment analysis.

## Practical Applications
- **Hotel and hospitality management**: Automatically extract guest pain points and satisfaction drivers from review text to guide operational improvements, without manual analysis.
- **Product feedback analysis**: Build interpretable cause-effect maps from customer reviews to identify which features or attributes drive satisfaction.
- **Market research and competitive analysis**: Extract entity relationships from unstructured competitor reviews or social media to model market sentiment and preference structures.
- **Recommendation systems**: Use the FCM to predict user satisfaction for aspects they haven't explicitly rated, based on inferred causal relationships.
- **Regulatory compliance and auditing**: Generate interpretable audit trails showing which textual elements drove conclusions in regulated industries (finance, healthcare).

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Review Intelligence SaaS**: A dashboard that ingests hotel/restaurant/e-commerce reviews, auto-builds fuzzy cognitive maps by entity (location, staff, cleanliness, price), and surfaces actionable insights ranked by causal strength.
2. **Explainable Feedback Analytics**: Enterprise tool for product teams that converts user feedback into causal dependency graphs, allowing non-technical stakeholders to see *why* satisfaction is declining without reading thousands of comments.
3. **Local-LLM Knowledge Extraction API**: Managed service wrapping local LLMs to extract structured relationships from domain-specific text (medical notes, legal documents, technical documentation) and return queryable FCM formats.
4. **Neuro-Symbolic AI for SMEs**: White-label solution for small/medium businesses (hotels, restaurants, local services) to auto-generate interpretable satisfaction models from their review data, competing with black-box sentiment tools.
5. **Compliance and Audit Trail Generator**: System for regulated industries (banking, healthcare) that builds auditable fuzzy maps from unstructured narratives (incident reports, patient feedback), satisfying explainability and traceability requirements.

## Technical Limitations
- **Language and domain scope**: Tested only on Greek hotel reviews; generalization to other languages, domains, and review styles is unclear. LLM extraction quality likely varies significantly by language and domain.
- **FCM scalability**: Star topology is simple; complex real-world domains may require far denser, more tangled graphs that become hard to interpret and validate.
- **Quantitative extraction fidelity**: The paper doesn't clearly report how often the LLM correctly extracts relationship strengths or even identifies relevant relationships. No baseline comparisons (e.g., vs. manual labeling, vs. supervised methods).
- **Circular validation**: External validation on star ratings is helpful, but limited to one proxy task. True out-of-distribution testing would use entirely different review sources or domains.
- **No filtering or post-processing**: While the authors claim to pass documents "unfiltered," this is a liability; hallucinated relationships or spurious correlations introduced by the LLM would propagate into the FCM unchecked.
- **Computational cost and latency**: No analysis of inference time, memory, or cost of running Qwen2.5-32B at scale on large review corpora.

## Open Questions
- How does extraction quality degrade across languages, especially low-resource ones?
- Can the method work with truly large FCMs (hundreds or thousands of entities), or does interpretability collapse?
- What is the inter-annotator agreement if humans manually label the same relationships? Does LLM extraction beat or underperform that baseline?
- Does the choice of LLM size and architecture matter? How would a smaller or larger model compare?
- Can you add human-in-the-loop feedback to iteratively refine the FCM, and does that improve downstream task performance?
- How do results change if you apply light filtering (e.g., removing low-confidence LLM outputs) before FCM construction?
- Can this approach transfer to non-review domains (e.g., scientific papers, technical documentation, social media)?

## Key Takeaway
Local LLMs can be repurposed as automatic extractors to build interpretable fuzzy cognitive maps from unstructured text, opening a practical bridge between black-box language models and explainable, causal knowledge representations.
