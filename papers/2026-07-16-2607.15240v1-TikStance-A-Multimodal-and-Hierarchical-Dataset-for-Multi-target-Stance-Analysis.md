# TikStance: A Multimodal and Hierarchical Dataset for Multi-target Stance Analysis in TikTok Political Conversations

**Authors:** Yazhi Zhang, Fuqiang Niu, Bowen Zhang
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.15240v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15240v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces TikStance, a dataset of 13,876 comments on 161 TikTok political videos for detecting stance (favor, against, or neutral) toward candidates in the 2024 U.S. election. The dataset is unique because it preserves both video and audio content alongside hierarchical comment threads, enabling research on how political opinions are expressed and debated across multimodal short-form video platforms—a gap in existing datasets.

## Theoretical Background
Stance detection is a natural language processing task that identifies whether a piece of text expresses support, opposition, or neutrality toward a target (e.g., a person or policy). Prior work has focused on Twitter, news comments, and debate forums, but these platforms either lack visual/audio context or don't preserve conversational structure. Multimodal analysis—combining text, video, and audio—requires models that can fuse information across modalities. Hierarchical comment trees (threads where replies nest within replies) reflect real social discourse but are underexplored in stance datasets. The authors measure inter-annotator agreement using Krippendorff's α, a standard metric for assessing label quality.

## Core Contributions
1. **First multimodal stance dataset for short-form video**: Combines TikTok videos (audio + visual) with associated comments, preserving the audiovisual context speakers intended.
2. **Hierarchical conversation structure**: Encodes parent-child relationships in comment threads, enabling analysis of how stances evolve within nested discussions (23.3% of comments are nested replies).
3. **Multi-target political coverage**: Annotates stance toward three 2024 candidates (Trump, Biden, Harris) with consistent labeling across all three targets, revealing target-dependent patterns in stance distribution and engagement depth.
4. **High-quality annotations**: Three independent annotators per item with disagreement re-annotation; final Krippendorff's α values (0.72–0.74) exceed typical thresholds for reliability.

## Connections to Other Work
This work extends prior stance detection efforts (e.g., SemEval stance detection tasks on Twitter) by incorporating multimodal signals and conversational hierarchy. It complements [[RLHF]] and content moderation research by providing a benchmark for understanding how political discourse actually unfolds in algorithm-driven platforms. The hierarchical structure connects to work on thread-level and comment-level analysis in computational social science. Unlike single-modality datasets, TikStance requires architectures that jointly reason over video frames, audio, text, and conversation structure—aligning with recent multimodal transformer advances.

## Practical Applications
1. **Content moderation at scale**: Social platforms can use this to detect and rank politically inflammatory content by stance intensity and hierarchical position.
2. **Election monitoring**: NGOs and researchers can track shifts in public sentiment toward candidates in real time across platforms.
3. **Misinformation detection**: Identify when high-reach videos spark coordinated opposing stances in comments, signaling potential disinformation campaigns.
4. **Political campaign analytics**: Campaigns can monitor how voters' stances cluster and propagate through nested comment discussions.
5. **Media literacy tools**: Platforms can highlight comments that shift stance through a conversation thread, helping users recognize persuasion tactics.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **TikTok Political Sentiment Dashboard**: Real-time visualization of stance distributions and conversation flow around political topics, sold to campaigns, pollsters, and news organizations.
2. **Controversy Early Warning System**: Detects videos likely to generate polarized comment threads (high stance conflict at shallow depths) and alerts platforms to monitor for coordination.
3. **Contextual Fact-Check Bot**: A bot that replies to high-engagement opposing-stance comments with verified claims, trained on TikStance to understand conversational context.
4. **Political Ad Transparency Tool**: Browser extension that shows users the stance distribution among comments on politically relevant TikToks, plus highlights bot-like comment patterns.
5. **Researcher-as-a-Service**: SaaS platform offering API access to TikStance embeddings and stance classifiers, enabling academics to study political discourse without platform scraping.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Small video sample (161)**: May not capture the full diversity of TikTok's creator ecosystem or algorithmic recommendation patterns; results may not generalize to non-U.S. contexts or non-election topics.
2. **Temporal snapshot**: Collected Sept 2023–Jan 2025 (U.S. election cycle); unclear how stance distributions shift outside election periods or during breaking news events.
3. **Three-candidate focus**: Only covers major U.S. candidates; doesn't address ballot measures, congressional races, or international politics.
4. **Annotation scheme limitations**: Three-class labels (Favor/Against/None) may miss nuance (e.g., conditional support, satire, or irony); Krippendorff's α values, while acceptable, suggest non-trivial disagreement (α < 0.80).
5. **No baseline models reported**: Paper presents dataset but doesn't train or benchmark state-of-the-art multimodal models, leaving performance metrics unknown.
6. **Potential demographic biases**: No analysis of whether annotators, creators, or comment authors represent broader populations; TikTok's algorithmic feed may oversample extreme or emotionally engaging content.

## Open Questions
1. How do multimodal signals (creator's facial expression, background, music) influence stance classification compared to text alone? Does video context reduce or amplify disagreement?
2. Can stance be predicted from a video without seeing its comments? (I.e., do creators' intentions match audience interpretation?)
3. How does stance propagate through comment threads—do nested replies tend to agree or disagree with parents, and does this differ by target?
4. Do bot or coordinated inauthentic behavior patterns differ across the three targets? (Requires network analysis beyond the current dataset.)
5. How do stances toward one target correlate with stances toward the others? Are Trump supporters reliably Biden opposers?
6. Can this dataset improve zero-shot or few-shot stance detection on new political figures outside the 2024 cycle?

## Key Takeaway
TikStance enables stance detection research on multimodal political videos with conversational context, filling a gap in datasets for short-form platforms.
