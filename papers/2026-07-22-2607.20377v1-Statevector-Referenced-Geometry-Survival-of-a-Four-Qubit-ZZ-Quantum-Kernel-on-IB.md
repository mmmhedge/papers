# Statevector-Referenced Geometry Survival of a Four-Qubit ZZ Quantum Kernel on IBM Quantum Hardware: A Fixed-Subset Diagnostic Across Three Execution Configurations

**Authors:** Rostyslav Sipakov
**Published:** 2026-07-22 | **arXiv:** [2607.20377v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20377v1)

## Summary
This paper diagnoses how well quantum kernel methods preserve geometric structure when executed on real quantum hardware. The authors measure a four-qubit ZZ kernel's Gram matrix across different error-mitigation configurations on IBM hardware, finding that gate twirling best preserves geometry—but warns that implementation fidelity and task relevance are separate concerns, and no quantum advantage should be claimed.

## Theoretical Background
Quantum-kernel methods are a machine-learning approach that encodes classical data into quantum states, then uses quantum circuits to compute similarity measures (Gram matrices) between data points. The core assumption is that the geometric structure of the data—captured by these Gram matrices—survives the noisy execution on real quantum hardware. Prior work has established that quantum circuits suffer from decoherence and gate errors; this paper specifically tests whether error-mitigation techniques (dynamical decoupling and gate twirling) preserve the intended "statevector geometry" when running on IBM Quantum hardware. The authors use centered kernel alignment (CKA)—a measure of how well computed Gram matrices match their ideal, noise-free versions—as a diagnostic.

## Core Contributions
1. **Empirical geometry-survival measurement**: Quantifies how much geometric structure a specific four-qubit ZZ kernel preserves under real hardware execution, using multiple geometry diagnostics (full-matrix CKA, Spearman correlation, mean absolute error).
2. **Comparative error-mitigation analysis**: Shows gate twirling outperforms dynamical decoupling alone on all geometry metrics, with jackknife-resolved statistical significance.
3. **Decoupling of fidelity from task relevance**: Critically demonstrates that the most hardware-faithful configuration had *lower* label alignment with the target task, revealing that implementation fidelity and predictive performance are independent axes.
4. **Methodological clarity**: Explicitly disclaims quantum advantage or superiority; frames results as descriptive diagnostics rather than causal mitigation-efficacy claims, setting a standard for rigorous quantum ML reporting.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[NISQ]] (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum) device characterization and quantum machine learning. It builds on prior quantum-kernel literature that assumes geometry preservation, while contributing to growing concerns about whether near-term quantum classifiers offer genuine advantage or merely absorb noise in ways that confound interpretation. Related diagnostics like kernel alignment have roots in classical deep-learning interpretability; the novelty here is applying them rigorously to quantum circuits under realistic hardware constraints.

## Practical Applications
1. **Quantum hardware benchmarking**: Operators of quantum systems can use this protocol to validate error-mitigation strategies for kernel-based workloads.
2. **Quantum ML model validation**: Teams building quantum classifiers on real hardware can diagnose whether their kernel geometry is preserved before investing in downstream training.
3. **Hardware-algorithm co-design**: Identifies which error-mitigation config best suits a given kernel on a given device, informing circuit redesign.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Quantum ML diagnostic-as-a-service**: Cloud offering that runs standardized geometry-survival tests on user circuits, returning fidelity + task-relevance scores before full training.
2. **Error-mitigation configurator**: Software that automatically tests multiple mitigation strategies on a frozen kernel and recommends the Pareto-optimal config for a user's task.
3. **Quantum circuit compiler with geometry awareness**: Compiler that optimizes circuits for both gate count and kernel geometry preservation.
4. **Quantum ML explainability dashboard**: Real-time visualization of geometry drift (via CKA, Spearman) as circuits execute, helping researchers debug kernel performance.
5. **Hybrid quantum-classical kernel tuner**: AutoML system that jointly optimizes classical kernel parameters *and* quantum mitigation settings to maximize label alignment.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Single kernel, single backend, single dataset**: Results are specific to one four-qubit ZZ kernel on IBM Fez with one air-quality dataset (N=24); generalization is unclear.
2. **Small dataset size**: N=24 windows limits statistical power and may not reflect behavior on larger, realistic datasets.
3. **No causal inference**: The study is observational (single non-interleaved jobs); cannot establish that gate twirling *causes* improved geometry survival without controlled experiments.
4. **Residual distortion dominance**: The paper identifies that hardware distortion, not sampling noise, drives errors—but doesn't propose or test solutions.
5. **Task-fidelity disconnect unexplained**: The inversion between geometric fidelity and label alignment is observed but not mechanistically understood.
6. **No quantum advantage claim**: By design, the paper makes no claim about whether quantum kernels outperform classical methods, limiting its applicability to quantum ML pipelines.

## Open Questions
1. **Scalability**: How do these results hold for larger kernels (8+ qubits) or datasets (N > 1000)?
2. **Mechanism of task misalignment**: Why does the most faithful geometric configuration yield the worst label alignment? Is it an artifact of the specific task, or a fundamental property of this kernel?
3. **Cross-backend generalization**: Do gate-twirling rankings hold on other IBM backends, or are they backend-specific?
4. **Hybrid optimization**: Can you jointly optimize error mitigation and classical kernel parameters to recover label alignment without sacrificing geometry?
5. **Causal mitigation efficacy**: Controlled experiments (e.g., randomized mitigation allocation) to establish which strategies causally improve real downstream performance.

## Key Takeaway
Quantum kernel geometry survives hardware execution better with gate twirling, but faithful geometry doesn't guarantee task performance—fidelity and relevance are separate axes.
