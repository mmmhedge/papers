# A Multi-Fidelity Convolutional Autoencoder-Transfer Learning Framework for Guided-Wave-Based Damage Diagnosis Using Large Simulated and Limited Experimental Datasets

**Authors:** Santosh Kapuria,  Abhishek
**Published:** 2026-06-25 | **arXiv:** [2606.27304v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27304v1)

## Summary
This paper presents a multi-fidelity transfer learning framework that combines lightweight physics-based simulations with convolutional autoencoders (CAE) to enable accurate damage diagnosis in structures using piezoelectric sensors. By leveraging large synthetic datasets from efficient simulations and fine-tuning on small experimental datasets, the approach achieves R² > 0.93 for damage localization and excellent generalization to unseen damage scenarios—addressing the critical practical constraint of limited labeled experimental data in structural health monitoring.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on several established foundations:
- **Physics-informed simulation**: One-dimensional spectral element methods provide computationally efficient synthetic data generation, grounded in wave mechanics and finite element theory
- **Transfer learning**: Leverages the principle that features learned from large synthetic datasets can adapt to real experimental domains with minimal fine-tuning (domain adaptation)
- **Convolutional autoencoders**: Unsupervised representation learning for extracting discriminative features from time-series guided-wave signals
- **Multi-fidelity learning**: Combines low-fidelity (fast, approximate) simulations with high-fidelity (expensive) experimental data to optimize the data-computation trade-off

## Core Contributions
1. **CAE-based transfer learning outperforms standard CNN approaches** for guided-wave damage diagnosis, suggesting that unsupervised feature extraction better captures the underlying physics
2. **Demonstrates practical viability** of physics-informed synthetic pretraining with minimal experimental data—a critical gap for deploying ML in costly experimental domains
3. **Generalizes to unseen damage scenarios** not present in training/fine-tuning sets, indicating the model captures generalizable damage signatures rather than memorizing patterns
4. **Establishes quantitative benchmarks** (R² > 0.93 localization, R² > 0.99 sizing) that position CAE-based transfer learning as competitive with domain-specific approaches

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of:
- **[[Domain adaptation]]**: Bridges the sim-to-real gap using limited experimental labels
- **[[Representation learning]]**: CAE learns compressed feature representations of guided-wave physics
- **[[Digital twins]]**: Uses high-fidelity simulation as virtual representation for real structure behavior

## Practical Applications
1. **Aerospace maintenance**: Real-time damage detection on aircraft wings/fuselage using onboard piezo sensors, reducing inspection downtime
2. **Wind turbine health monitoring**: Early warning system for blade cracks or delamination in off-shore installations with remote/limited access
3. **Bridge structural integrity**: Continuous SHM networks on aging civil infrastructure to prioritize repair budgets and prevent catastrophic failures
4. **Nuclear reactor vessel monitoring**: Non-destructive evaluation where experimental data is extremely costly and dangerous to acquire
5. **Railway track inspection**: Autonomous detection of rail defects/cracks from train-mounted sensor networks without track shutdown

## Technical Limitations
1. **Simulation fidelity gap**: Spectral element models may not capture all experimental variability (material heterogeneity, sensor nonlinearity, environmental noise). Unclear how model performs when sim-to-real gap widens
2. **Limited experimental validation scope**: Testing on plate-like structures with piezo transducers; generalization to complex geometries (composite laminates, curved surfaces) not demonstrated
3. **Damage type assumption**: Framework assumes damage manifests similarly in simulation vs. reality; may fail for novel damage modes (e.g., kissing bonds, micro-cracking) not represented in pretraining
4. **Scalability to larger structures**: Spectral element preprocessing and CAE inference cost growth with spatial/temporal resolution not quantified; applicability to full-scale aircraft/bridges unclear
5. **Transfer learning plateau**: No analysis of how performance degrades as experimental domain diverges from simulation assumptions (temperature, humidity, load conditions)
6. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: Limited discussion of CAE architecture sensitivity or how to select optimal fine-tuning epoch counts

## Open Questions
1. **How sensitive is the framework to simulation inaccuracies?** Ablation studies on spectral element discretization, damping assumptions, and transducer models would clarify the error budget
2. **What is the minimal experimental dataset size?** Precise characterization of R² decay as a function of labeled experimental samples would inform cost-benefit analysis
3. **Can this transfer across material types?** Can a model pretrained on aluminum transfer to composites or steel with minimal fine-tuning?
4. **How do environmental factors (temperature, humidity, pressure) degrade performance?** Real deployments experience drift; robustness testing is critical
5. **Can the framework detect out-of-distribution damage types?** Detecting unknown failure modes (not in training) is practically crucial but not addressed
6. **How does this compare to physics-informed neural networks (PINNs)?** Direct comparison would position this work within the broader physics-ML landscape

## Key Takeaway
Multi-fidelity transfer learning with CAEs enables practical deployment of deep learning for structural damage diagnosis by leveraging cheap physics-based simulation pretraining and minimal experimental fine-tuning, achieving excellent generalization without the prohibitive cost of large labeled datasets.
