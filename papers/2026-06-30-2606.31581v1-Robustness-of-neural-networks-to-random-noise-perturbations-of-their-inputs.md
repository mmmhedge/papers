# Robustness of neural networks to random noise perturbations of their inputs

**Authors:** Mark Levene, Martyn Harris
**Published:** 2026-06-30 | **arXiv:** [2606.31581v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31581v1)

## Summary
This paper studies how well neural networks maintain their performance when their inputs are corrupted by random noise. The authors propose a simple, computationally efficient metric that predicts how much a network's error will increase under input perturbations, treating the network as a black box. They validate their approach on real datasets and introduce "robustness curves" to visualize and compare robustness across different conditions.

## Theoretical Background
The paper addresses a fundamental problem in neural network reliability: understanding the relationship between model accuracy and input robustness. Prior work on adversarial examples and noise sensitivity shows that neural networks can be surprisingly fragile to input changes. This work differs by focusing on *random* noise perturbations rather than adversarial attacks, and by developing a practical measure that bounds generalization error under perturbation. The foundation rests on classical statistical learning theory — the idea that we can relate a network's performance on clean data to its performance on corrupted data via formal bounds.

## Core Contributions
1. **A computable robustness measure**: A metric that provides a high-probability upper bound on mean squared error degradation given a specified input perturbation magnitude. Critically, this works as a black-box analysis — no access to weights or gradients required.
2. **Robustness curves**: A visualization tool that plots how performance degrades as perturbation level increases, enabling systematic comparison within and across datasets.
3. **Empirical validation**: Demonstration on multiple real-world datasets that the proposed measure is both theoretically justified and practically informative.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to broader robustness research in [[deep learning]], including adversarial robustness and certified defenses, but takes a statistical rather than worst-case perspective. It complements work on [[Lipschitz constraints]] (bounding how much outputs can change given input changes) and connects to sensitivity analysis in machine learning. Unlike adversarial robustness literature which assumes an intelligent attacker, this assumes benign random noise — a different threat model with practical relevance for noisy real-world deployments (sensors, communication channels, measurement error).

## Practical Applications
- **Sensor-based systems**: Predicting performance degradation when physical sensors introduce measurement noise (robotics, autonomous vehicles, industrial monitoring).
- **Data quality assurance**: Assessing how sensitive deployed models are to data quality variations, guiding data collection standards.
- **Wireless communication systems**: Understanding ML model performance when signals are corrupted by channel noise.
- **Medical diagnostics**: Evaluating robustness to noise in imaging or signal data (EEG, ECG, imaging devices with inherent noise).
- **Model selection and validation**: Choosing between candidate models based on robustness-accuracy tradeoffs, not just clean-data performance.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Robustness Audit Tool**: SaaS platform that takes a trained model and dataset, computes robustness metrics, and generates reports on performance degradation under realistic noise scenarios — useful for regulated industries (finance, healthcare, autonomous systems).
2. **ML Model Insurance**: An underwriting platform that estimates model risk via robustness analysis; insurers pay out when deployed models fail due to input noise, pricing based on computed robustness bounds.
3. **Data Quality Monitoring Dashboard**: Real-time system that monitors incoming data quality and predicts model performance using robustness curves; alerts when data drift would cause unacceptable degradation.
4. **Edge Device Validator**: Tool for ML teams deploying to edge devices (phones, IoT, robots) to certify performance under realistic sensor noise before shipping.
5. **Synthetic Data Generator for Robustness Testing**: Automatically generates adversarial-but-random perturbation datasets matching specified noise profiles, enabling more rigorous offline testing before deployment.

## Technical Limitations
- **Black-box limitation turned strength**: While treating the network as a black box is computationally efficient, it may be loose compared to analysis that exploits model structure; tighter bounds might be possible with white-box access.
- **Gaussian noise assumption**: The method appears tailored to random perturbations; applicability to structured noise (e.g., adversarial, correlated) is unclear.
- **Limited to regression**: Framed for mean squared error (continuous outputs); adaptation to classification may require different analysis.
- **Scalability of bounds**: The paper doesn't fully address how tight or informative the bounds are for very high-dimensional inputs (images, text embeddings), where many dimensions might amplify perturbation effects.
- **Distribution mismatch**: Assumes perturbations are small and well-characterized; breaks down if noise distribution changes post-deployment.

## Open Questions
- How do the robustness bounds compare empirically to those from certified defense methods? Are they competitive?
- Can the approach extend to classification with meaningful per-class robustness analysis?
- Do robust networks (high robustness measure) learn meaningfully different internal representations, or are they just less sensitive by architecture?
- How does robustness interact with model capacity and regularization? Is there a fundamental accuracy-robustness tradeoff?
- Can this framework extend to structured perturbations (e.g., missing features, outliers) beyond random noise?
- How sensitive is the robustness measure to the choice of perturbation distribution? How would it perform under distribution shift?

## Key Takeaway
A simple, model-agnostic metric can efficiently predict and bound how much neural network performance degrades under random input noise, enabling practical robustness assessment for real-world deployments.
