# Understanding Reasoning from Pretraining to Post-Training

**Authors:** Jingyan Shen, Ang Li, Salman Rahman, Yifan Sun, Micah Goldblum et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-17 | **arXiv:** [2607.16097v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16097v1)

## Summary
This paper systematically studies how pretraining choices affect the performance gains from reinforcement learning in large language models, using chess as a controlled experimental domain. The key finding: post-RL performance is predictable from pretraining loss, and RL improvement scales linearly with pretraining tokens. This matters because RL is now central to making LLMs reason better, but we've understood little about how pretraining and RL interact—this work fills that gap with quantitative evidence.

## Theoretical Background
Large language models (LLMs) are neural networks trained in two phases: pretraining on massive text corpora to learn language patterns, then post-training via reinforcement learning (RL) to optimize for specific downstream tasks. Pretraining loss—the prediction error on unseen text—is a standard metric for model quality. Prior work studied each phase separately, but the interface between them remained unclear. This paper draws on scaling laws in LLMs (the observation that larger models and more training data yield predictable improvements) and applies RL theory in a domain with verifiable, ground-truth rewards.

## Core Contributions
1. **Controlled experimental framework**: Uses chess as a testbed—pretraining on 5M to 1B parameter models on human games, with exact puzzle solutions as ground truth. This avoids the confound of uncontrolled text corpora in standard LLM work.

2. **Quantitative pretraining-to-RL interface**: Demonstrates that post-RL performance at a fixed RL compute budget is predictable from pretraining loss alone, and RL improvement slope scales ~linearly with pretraining tokens. This provides a scaling law bridging the two phases.

3. **Mechanistic insight into RL behavior**: Shows RL does *not* uniformly sharpen the supervised fine-tuning (SFT) policy. Instead, on easy puzzles it amplifies already-preferred moves, while on hard puzzles it surfaces moves nearly invisible under SFT—a qualitative difference by problem difficulty.

4. **Transfer beyond chess**: Validates the same pretraining-loss predictability pattern on math-domain text with 1B models, suggesting findings are not domain-specific.

## Connections to Other Work
This builds on [[scaling laws]] in LLMs (e.g., Chinchilla, Kaplan et al.) by extending them across the pretraining-to-posttraining boundary. It relates to [[RLHF]] (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) pipelines used in models like ChatGPT, but isolates and measures the interaction with pretraining in a way prior work has not. The chess testbed approach echoes [[mechanistic-interpretability]] methodology—using simplified domains to study neural behavior—though here focused on scaling and phase interaction rather than circuit analysis. The finding that RL surfaces latent capabilities mirrors work on emergence in LLMs.

## Practical Applications
1. **Training efficiency**: Organizations can now estimate RL gains *before* investing in expensive RL runs, by measuring pretraining loss. This enables better allocation of compute across pretraining vs. RL stages.

2. **Model scaling decisions**: Teams can use pretraining-token scaling laws to decide: "Should we add more pretraining data or more RL compute?" This is directly actionable for frontier labs.

3. **Domain adaptation**: When entering a new reasoning domain (e.g., formal verification, theorem proving), practitioners can validate that chess/math scaling laws transfer, then plan RL budgets accordingly.

4. **Interpretable reasoning evaluation**: The chess framework with verifiable ground truth allows fine-grained diagnosis of what RL is learning—which could help debug reasoning failures in deployed systems.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Pretraining efficiency auditor**: SaaS tool that measures pretraining loss curves and predicts optimal RL budgets, helping ML teams avoid wasteful allocation.

2. **Domain-specific reasoning benchmarks**: Build modular, verifiable testbeds (chess-like) for other reasoning domains (formal math, code verification, planning) where users can measure and compare RL gains systematically.

3. **RL reward-shaping advisor**: Automated system that, given pretraining checkpoints at different scales, recommends RL curriculum and reward formulations most likely to succeed (based on pattern recognition across domains).

4. **Scaling law marketplace**: Platform where companies publish pretraining-to-RL transfer curves for their domains, allowing others to make informed compute allocation decisions without expensive replication.

5. **Interactive reasoning tutor**: Use chess/math testbeds to teach students or practitioners how pretraining and RL interact; package as an educational tool with interactive visualizations of the phase space.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Domain specificity remains unclear**: While chess and math transfer, it's untested on open-ended NLP (summarization, translation, instruction-following). RL with verifiable rewards is rare; most real applications have sparse or subjective feedback.

2. **Model scale ceiling**: Experiments max at 1B parameters; unclear if findings hold at 10B+ (where pretraining-RL dynamics may shift due to emergence phenomena).

3. **Pretraining loss as proxy**: Assumes pretraining loss *alone* predicts RL returns, ignoring other pretraining properties (data distribution, curriculum, architecture tweaks) that may matter.

4. **RL implementation details ignored**: Uses "standard" RL (likely PPO or similar) but doesn't isolate effects of reward scaling, KL penalties, or other hyperparameters—these could interact with pretraining in unexpected ways.

5. **Causality not proven**: Showing correlation between pretraining loss and RL gains doesn't prove pretraining loss *causes* it; confounding factors (e.g., latent capacity measures correlated with loss) are not ruled out.

## Open Questions
1. **Why does pretraining loss predict RL gains?** Is it encoding general reasoning capacity, or something more specific? Can you predict from other pretraining metrics (e.g., task-specific loss) with equal power?

2. **Does the linear scaling break?** At very high pretraining tokens or RL compute, do diminishing returns emerge? Where are the kink points?

3. **How do different RL algorithms interact with pretraining?** Would DQN, actor-critic, or other RL methods show the same patterns, or is this PPO-specific?

4. **Transfer to human feedback**: The paper uses ground-truth rewards (chess puzzles solved or not). How do findings change with noisy, subjective, or delayed rewards—the real setting for RLHF?

5. **Mechanistic explanation**: What circuits or attention patterns emerge during pretraining that RL then leverages? Can you interpret the "latent moves" RL surfaces?

6. **Inverse scaling for easy tasks**: Does the pattern reverse for trivial tasks? Is there a "sweet spot" difficulty range where this framework applies?

## Key Takeaway
Pretraining loss reliably predicts how much RL post-training will improve an LLM—offering a quantitative bridge between the two phases and a tool to optimize compute allocation.
