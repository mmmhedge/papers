# The Role of Input Dimensionality in the Emergence and Targeted Control of Adversarial Examples

**Authors:** Nasrin Malekzadeh Goradel, Niccolo Pancino, Yaser Gholizade Atani, Benedetta Tondi, Giovanni Bellettini et al.
**Published:** 2026-06-24 | **arXiv:** [2606.26207v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26207v1)

## Summary
This paper systematically investigates how input dimensionality drives the emergence and control of adversarial examples in deep neural networks. The authors combine theoretical analysis (examining concentration of measure frameworks) with extensive empirical evaluation across hierarchical datasets, demonstrating that adversarial vulnerability increases with dimensionality and that the additional cost of crafting *targeted* adversarial examples remains surprisingly small in high dimensions.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on theoretical frameworks from **concentration of measure** and **high-dimensional geometry**, which attempt to explain adversarial vulnerability through properties of high-dimensional spaces. It also engages with prior work examining why neural networks are vulnerable to small perturbations, drawing on the assumption that real data occupies lower-dimensional manifolds within high-dimensional input spaces. The authors critically examine whether these theoretical assumptions actually hold empirically in practice.

## Core Contributions
1. **Empirical validation of geometric intuitions**: Demonstrates that real image classes exhibit strong localization beyond what concentration-of-measure theory typically predicts, revealing gaps between theory and practice.

2. **Dimensionality as a unifying principle**: Establishes systematic evidence that higher input dimensionality consistently facilitates adversarial example construction across different architectures and datasets.

3. **Targeted vs. untargeted attacks**: Provides both theoretical arguments and experimental evidence that enforcing a specific target label adds only marginal distortion compared to untargeted attacks, and this gap *shrinks* with dimensionality.

4. **Methodological rigor**: Conducts extensive controlled experiments across hierarchical image datasets with diverse neural architectures, moving beyond isolated theoretical results.

## Connections to Other Work
This work relates to broader research on [[adversarial-robustness]], [[interpretability]] of neural networks, and the geometric properties underlying deep learning. It critiques existing theoretical frameworks (concentration of measure) that oversimplify real data distributions. The findings also connect to [[transfer-learning]] and [[domain-adaptation]] research, as understanding adversarial vulnerability across different dimensional regimes has implications for model generalization and architectural design choices.

## Practical Applications
- **Security auditing tools**: Develop dimensionality-aware vulnerability assessments for deployed computer vision systems.
- **Adversarial training**: Tailor data augmentation strategies based on input dimensionality to improve robustness.
- **Biometric authentication systems**: Assess risk of spoofing attacks for face/iris recognition in high-dimensional input spaces.
- **Autonomous driving perception**: Evaluate vulnerability of vision modules to crafted perturbations and design appropriate defenses.
- **Medical imaging**: Understand robustness of diagnostic models to input noise and adversarial perturbations.

2. **DimensionShield**: A neural network wrapper/defense layer that dynamically adjusts perturbation budgets and detection thresholds based on detected input dimensionality, providing adaptive robustness.

## Technical Limitations
- **Causality ambiguity**: The paper explicitly acknowledges that whether high-dimensional vulnerability arises from geometry+data-distribution interplay *or* from architectural properties of DNNs remains unresolved. This is a fundamental open question that limits mechanistic understanding.
- **Image-domain focus**: Evaluation concentrates on hierarchical image datasets; generalization to other modalities (text, audio, point clouds) is unclear.
- **Architecture diversity**: While multiple architectures are tested, the study may not capture behavior of very recent models (vision transformers, diffusion models) comprehensively.
- **Transferability assumptions**: Findings assume that adversarial examples generated in one setting transfer predictably; this may not hold across very different dimensional regimes or architectures.
- **Practical defense gap**: Understanding vulnerability mechanisms does not directly translate to practical defense strategies; the paper is diagnostic rather than prescriptive.

## Open Questions
1. **Geometry vs. architecture**: Can you empirically disentangle whether dimensionality effects arise from high-dimensional geometry principles or from specific architectural biases in deep networks?

2. **Non-image modalities**: Do the same dimensionality-vulnerability relationships hold for NLP, audio, or structured data where geometry may behave differently?

3. **Optimal input dimensionality**: Is there a "sweet spot" dimensionality that balances expressiveness and robustness? Can you design architectures that operate in a robustness-optimal dimensional regime?

4. **Defense design**: Given dimensionality insights, what *new* defense mechanisms can you construct that exploit high-dimensional geometry to make adversarial perturbations harder or more detectable?

5. **Certified robustness**: Can dimensionality-aware analysis improve certified robustness bounds, or provide dimension-specific certificates?

6. **Distribution properties**: How do the curvature, intrinsic dimensionality, and manifold properties of real data distributions interact with input dimensionality to determine vulnerability?

## Key Takeaway
High input dimensionality is a fundamental structural driver of adversarial vulnerability in neural networks, making attacks easier to construct and targeted attacks only marginally harder than untargeted ones—a phenomenon rooted in high-dimensional geometry that existing theory incompletely captures.
