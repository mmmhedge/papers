# Towards Developing a Multimodal Chat Assistant for University Stakeholders: RAG-based Approach

**Authors:** Md Abu Hanif Shaikh, Abdullah Al Shafi
**Published:** 2026-07-01 | **arXiv:** [2607.01115v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01115v1)

## Summary
This paper describes a multimodal chatbot system designed to help university stakeholders (students, staff, faculty) find answers to institutional questions using retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). The system combines large language models with semantic search over university documents, accepts both text and image queries, and runs efficiently on limited hardware. The key result: RAG reduces hallucinations (false confident statements) from 31.7% to 6.6%, making the system far more reliable for real institutional use.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on three foundational concepts: (1) **Large language models (LLMs)** — neural networks trained on vast text corpora that can generate human-like responses; (2) **Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)** — a technique that grounds LLM outputs by first retrieving relevant documents, then conditioning the model's response on those documents rather than relying solely on training data; and (3) **Vision-language models** — multimodal neural networks that process both images and text to generate unified representations. The core insight motivating RAG is that retrieval reduces hallucination (the tendency of LLMs to confidently generate false information) by anchoring responses to factual source material. Quantized inference (using lower-precision weights and activations) enables these large models to run on resource-constrained devices.

## Core Contributions
1. **Multimodal institutional chatbot**: A complete system that accepts text and image queries (via vision-language encoders) and routes them through a unified RAG pipeline—not just text-only.
2. **Quantized inference deployment**: Demonstrates practical deployment of LLMs + RAG on constrained university hardware without prohibitive latency.
3. **Empirical hallucination reduction**: Quantifies the concrete benefit of RAG in a real institutional setting (31.7% → 6.6% hallucination rate), validating the approach for high-stakes domains.
4. **Production architecture**: Full-stack system design (FastAPI backend, Next.js frontend, semantic retrieval) with user satisfaction validation across modalities.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits within the broader ecosystem of **[[Large Language Models|LLMs in production]]** and extends classical chatbot research by addressing the domain-specific grounding problem. It echoes approaches like **[[RLHF]]** and **[[Constitutional AI]]** in prioritizing factual reliability, but chooses the retrieval path rather than pure fine-tuning. The vision-language component connects to multimodal [[transformers]] (e.g., CLIP, LLaVA). The paper is pragmatic rather than novel algorithmically—its contribution is integrating existing techniques (RAG, quantization, vision-language models) into a production system for an underserved use case (universities in developing countries).

## Practical Applications
1. **University administrative support**: Students asking about enrollment deadlines, course requirements, or financial aid policies; staff querying handbook sections without manual lookup.
2. **Multilingual institutional Q&A**: Adapting the system to serve non-English-speaking students by indexing documents in multiple languages.
3. **Visual document queries**: Students uploading a photo of a physical notice board or handbook page to ask follow-up questions—important in contexts with poor digital infrastructure.
4. **Policy compliance auditing**: Internal teams querying whether institutional practices align with stated policies by searching both documents and images of official records.
5. **Onboarding and training**: New staff or faculty using the chatbot to learn institutional procedures quickly without waiting for human HR staff.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **University-as-a-Service admin layer**: SaaS platform that institutions deploy in-house—pre-indexed with their handbooks, policies, and FAQs—with white-label branding and multi-language support. Charge per institution + per-query tier.
2. **Knowledge automation for developing-country institutions**: Target underresourced universities by bundling the chatbot with offline capability and lower hardware requirements; partner with education NGOs for distribution.
3. **Institutional knowledge graph + chatbot**: Build on this foundation to auto-extract relationships (e.g., "which departments offer which degrees") and enable structured queries beyond plain text retrieval.
4. **Multimodal document processing for regulated industries**: Extend to hospitals, law firms, and government agencies that need to query both written policies and visual documents (X-rays, scanned contracts, blueprints) with high reliability.
5. **Hallucination-aware LLM audit tool**: Offer an auditing service that benchmarks institutional chatbots on hallucination rate before and after RAG deployment, helping organizations justify investment in grounded systems.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Semantic retrieval bottleneck**: The system's quality depends entirely on the relevance of retrieved documents. If the university handbook doesn't contain an answer, RAG cannot help—the LLM will still hallucinate or refuse.
2. **Image query modality under-evaluated**: The paper reports longer response times for visual inputs but provides limited detail on how vision-language encoders handle poor-quality images, handwritten text, or non-English documents—common in developing-country settings.
3. **Scalability assumptions**: No discussion of how the system scales if hundreds of thousands of documents must be indexed, or if concurrent users spike.
4. **Quantization trade-offs**: Quantized inference may degrade model quality (e.g., nuanced policy reasoning). The paper doesn't report accuracy loss from quantization.
5. **Institutional customization effort**: Each deployment likely requires significant effort to index, clean, and structure institutional documents—not a plug-and-play solution.

## Open Questions
1. How does performance degrade when the retrieved documents are incomplete or outdated? Can the system signal confidence in its answer?
2. What is the user acceptance for multimodal queries in practice? Do students actually upload images, or is text-only sufficient?
3. How does the system handle policy contradictions (e.g., two handbook versions)? Does it flag uncertainty?
4. Can the approach scale to very large document collections (millions of pages) without prohibitive retrieval latency?
5. What adaptation mechanisms exist for evolving policies without full retraining? Can users provide feedback to improve the index over time?

## Key Takeaway
Retrieval-augmented generation dramatically reduces hallucination in institutional chatbots (31.7% → 6.6%), making LLMs practical for high-stakes, domain-specific settings where accuracy matters more than raw generative power.
