# What Images Cannot Say: Language-Guided Olfactory Representation Learning

**Authors:** Eleftherios Tsonis, Xi Wang, Vicky Kalogeiton
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06402v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06402v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces SCENT, a framework that learns to represent odors (from electronic-nose sensors) by using language as a bridge between visual images and smell signals. The key insight is that many smells come from contextual environmental factors invisible in pixels — so the researchers use Vision-Language Models to describe what a scene looks like and what it might *smell* like, then train an olfactory encoder to align smell data with these multimodal descriptions. The work matters because it tackles a genuinely hard problem: making sensory systems (smell) learnable from visual data, which requires semantic reasoning beyond raw pixel values.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on three established areas. First, **multimodal learning** — the practice of aligning representations across different data modalities (vision, text, audio, etc.) into shared embedding spaces. Second, **Vision-Language Models (VLMs)** — neural networks like CLIP that jointly encode images and text, allowing semantic descriptions of visual scenes. Third, **contrastive learning** — a training technique where embeddings are pushed together for matching pairs and apart for non-matches, commonly used in multimodal alignment tasks (e.g., in CLIP itself). The novelty is applying these well-established techniques to olfaction, where direct visual-smell alignment is weak because smell is often invisible in images.

## Core Contributions
- **Language as a semantic bridge**: Uses VLM-generated scene descriptions (objects, context, plausible odors) to guide olfactory representation learning, sidestepping the problem that images don't directly show smells.
- **Language-guided latent decomposition**: A technique that separates smell representations into object-specific components and environmental/contextual components, enabling disentanglement of complex odor mixtures.
- **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on smell-to-image and smell-to-text retrieval on the New York Smells dataset, showing the framework outperforms vision-only baselines.
- **Interpretability**: Produces human-interpretable olfactory representations by decomposing smells into semantic factors.

## Connections to Other Work
This research connects to several threads:
- **[[CLIP]] and vision-language alignment**: Uses VLMs in the same spirit as contrastive vision-language models, but extends to a new modality.
- **Multimodal representation learning**: Related to work on aligning audio, text, and vision; extends this paradigm to smell (a relatively unexplored modality in deep learning).
- **Disentangled representations**: The latent decomposition approach echoes prior work on beta-VAEs and other disentanglement methods, but applied to olfactory data.
- **Sensor-to-semantic translation**: Connects to broader efforts to ground physical sensor signals (e.g., thermal, acoustic) in semantic understanding.

## Practical Applications
- **Retail & e-commerce**: Online stores could recommend scented products (perfumes, candles, diffusers) based on product images and user preferences, without requiring physical samples.
- **Culinary & food science**: Food companies could predict how a dish *smells* from images, aiding recipe development and product testing.
- **Virtual reality & immersive media**: VR experiences could generate realistic ambient odors based on scene visuals, enhancing immersion.
- **Assistive technology**: Help visually impaired users understand olfactory aspects of environments from image descriptions.
- **Museum & heritage**: Digital archives of artworks or historical spaces could include plausible olfactory metadata for augmented museum experiences.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Scent-Match for E-commerce**: A browser plugin that analyzes product images (perfumes, candles, foods) and recommends alternatives based on olfactory similarity, enabling smell-based discovery without physical testing.
2. **Virtual Scent Palette for Product Design**: Software for food/fragrance companies to explore smell profiles of visual mockups, reducing iteration cycles in R&D.
3. **Olfactory Metadata API**: A cloud service that generates smell descriptions and embeddings from product images, allowing B2B smell-based recommendation systems.
4. **Immersive VR/AR Platform**: A metaverse layer that adds realistic ambient odors to virtual scenes using SCENT-like models, licensing to game engines and social platforms.
5. **Heritage & Tourism App**: An AR guide that overlays historical odor descriptions on museum or architectural images, creating multisensory cultural experiences.

## Technical Limitations
- **Dataset dependence**: Performance relies on the New York Smells dataset, which is small and curated. Generalization to diverse geographies, seasons, or odor types is unvalidated.
- **Electronic-nose limitations**: Real electronic-nose sensors have noise, drift, and limited chemical sensitivity. The framework doesn't address robustness to sensor degradation.
- **Language-smell bottleneck**: Using language as a bridge assumes VLMs can accurately infer smells from images — but this is uncertain for many subtle or context-dependent odors (e.g., the smell of rain vs. petrichor).
- **Disentanglement assumption**: The latent decomposition assumes odors cleanly separate into object-specific and contextual components, which may not hold for complex, chemically intertwined mixtures.
- **Scalability**: Requires paired image-smell-text data; scaling to millions of samples is unclear, and collecting such data is expensive and labor-intensive.
- **Evaluation metrics**: Smell-to-image retrieval success is hard to validate — how do you confirm a retrieved image truly matches a smell's semantic meaning?

## Open Questions
- **Cross-dataset generalization**: Does SCENT trained on NYC smells transfer to other cities or environments? What about indoor vs. outdoor, or seasonal variations?
- **Fine-grained odor chemistry**: Can the model learn to predict specific volatile organic compounds (VOCs) or smell families (floral, fruity, woody) from images?
- **Real-world sensor integration**: How robust is the framework to real electronic-nose noise and drift? Does it work with commodity sensors or only lab-grade equipment?
- **Human evaluation**: What do humans say when they see the predicted olfactory representations? Are the decomposed components (object vs. context) actually interpretable to humans?
- **Language-smell alignment**: How much of performance comes from language guidance vs. the underlying contrastive learning? An ablation comparing to vision-only VLM embeddings would clarify this.
- **Temporal dynamics**: Can the framework handle how smells change over time (e.g., a candle's scent evolving as it burns)? Initial work focuses on snapshots.

## Key Takeaway
Language-guided multimodal learning can bridge the "visibility gap" in olfactory perception — enabling machines to infer and represent smells from images by reasoning about semantic context that is invisible in pixels.
