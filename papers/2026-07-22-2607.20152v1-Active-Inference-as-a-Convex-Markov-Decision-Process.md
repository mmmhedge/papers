# Active Inference as a Convex Markov Decision Process

**Authors:** Nikola Milosevic, Nicolás Hinrichs, Nico Scherf
**Published:** 2026-07-22 | **arXiv:** [2607.20152v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20152v1)

## Summary
This paper shows that Active Inference — a neuroscience-inspired framework where agents minimize expected free energy to make decisions — can be reformulated as a convex optimization problem within the standard reinforcement learning framework. By doing this, the authors make Active Inference amenable to modern RL algorithms and convergence guarantees, bridging two previously separate theoretical perspectives on adaptive behavior.

## Theoretical Background
Active Inference (AIF) is a principle from neuroscience and cognitive science proposing that agents act to minimize expected free energy (EFE), a quantity combining two objectives: reducing uncertainty about the world (epistemic value) and achieving desirable outcomes (pragmatic value). In contrast, standard reinforcement learning (RL) frames agent behavior as maximizing cumulative reward via policy optimization over Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) — mathematical models where an agent's future state depends only on its current state and action. The paper bridges these frameworks by showing how EFE minimization can be expressed as policy optimization in an MDP, leveraging convex optimization theory and mirror descent — a first-order optimization algorithm that works by reflecting the gradient through a convex mirror function.

## Core Contributions
1. **Convex MDP reformulation**: Demonstrates that closed-loop AIF can be cast as a convex MDP where pragmatic terms (goal-seeking) are linear in state marginals, making them equivalent to standard reward maximization, while epistemic terms (curiosity-driven) introduce a nonlinear, policy-dependent component.

2. **Epistemic drive as performative reward**: Reveals that the uncertainty-reduction component of AIF emerges as a policy-dependent (performative) reward — one that depends on the agent's own policy choices, distinguishing it from exogenous RL rewards.

3. **Mirror descent algorithm**: Derives a practical algorithm using mirror descent that locally linearizes the nonlinear epistemic objective, producing a policy-dependent reward compatible with actor-critic and dynamic programming methods.

4. **Performative RL connection**: Establishes that coupling world-model learning with policy optimization grounds Active Inference in modern RL and optimization theory, enabling convergence analysis and principled policy improvement guarantees.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of three communities:
- **Active Inference literature**: Extends foundational work on EFE minimization (e.g., Friston's Free Energy Principle) by making it algorithmically tractable and theoretically grounded in RL.
- **Reinforcement Learning**: Relates to [[Model-Based Reinforcement Learning]] and [[Actor-Critic Methods]] by incorporating learned world models, and to [[Curiosity-Driven Exploration]] / [[Intrinsic Motivation]] as these also combine extrinsic and intrinsic rewards.
- **Optimization theory**: Builds on convex optimization and mirror descent literature to provide convergence guarantees and principled policy improvement — moving AIF from heuristic to theoretically justified.

## Practical Applications
1. **Robotics & embodied AI**: Robots must explore their environment while achieving tasks — AIF with convergence guarantees could enable safer, more efficient autonomous systems in unstructured environments.

2. **Adaptive experimentation**: Clinical trials, A/B testing, and drug discovery benefit from balancing learning (epistemic value) with immediate outcomes (pragmatic value); this framework formalizes that trade-off.

3. **Active learning in machine learning**: Automated systems that choose which samples to label or which experiments to run could use this to provably balance informativeness and utility.

4. **Multi-agent coordination**: Agents that simultaneously learn models and optimize policies while accounting for uncertainty could improve collaborative tasks in uncertain environments.

5. **Autonomous scientific discovery**: Systems that autonomously design experiments must reduce uncertainty about mechanisms while achieving scientific objectives — a natural fit for the AIF-as-RL formulation.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Closed-loop policies only**: The convex formulation applies to policies that condition on observations; extension to partially observable settings with belief states remains unclear.

2. **Computational scalability**: Mirror descent requires computing state marginals and epistemic rewards; scaling to high-dimensional state spaces or long horizons is not addressed.

3. **World model accuracy**: The approach assumes a learned world model; performance degrades gracefully if the model is misspecified, but no robustness analysis is provided.

4. **Finite-horizon assumptions**: Convergence analysis likely requires bounded horizons or strong discount factors; behavior under infinite-horizon average-reward criteria may differ.

5. **Nonlinearity of epistemic term**: Although formulated as convex, the epistemic objective is still nonlinear, limiting applicability of standard convex solvers and requiring specialized algorithms.

6. **Assumption of stationarity**: The framework assumes a stationary environment; non-stationary or adversarial settings are not discussed.

## Open Questions
1. **Partially observable environments**: Can the convex MDP formulation extend to belief-state MDPs (POMDPs) where agents maintain uncertainty over hidden state?

2. **Sample complexity**: What is the sample complexity of learning the world model while optimizing policy in this framework, and how does it compare to standard RL?

3. **Deep RL integration**: How do the convergence guarantees translate when the policy and world model are deep neural networks rather than tabular representations?

4. **Multi-agent extensions**: Does the performative RL perspective extend to multi-agent settings where agents' epistemic rewards depend on other agents' policies?

5. **Empirical validation**: How do mirror-descent-based AIF policies compare in practice to standard RL and curiosity-driven methods on benchmark tasks?

6. **Reward specification**: In the pragmatic objective, how should practitioners specify or learn the reward function to align with human preferences?

## Key Takeaway
Active Inference can be optimized as a convex problem using reinforcement learning algorithms, with formal convergence guarantees.
