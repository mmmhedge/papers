# HCIG: A Hierarchical Cross-Modal Incongruity Graph Network for Multimodal Sarcasm and Cyberbullying Detection

**Authors:** Bhavana Verma, Priyanka Meel, Dinesh Kumar Vishwakarma
**Published:** 2026-07-17 | **arXiv:** [2607.16076v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16076v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes HCIG, a neural network framework that detects sarcasm and cyberbullying in social media by modeling the *mismatch* between what text and images say. Most existing systems treat text and images separately or combine them naively; this work captures hierarchical contradictions across multiple levels of meaning (word-level, phrase-level, whole-post level), which is where sarcasm and bullying often hide. The approach achieves strong results on two standard benchmarks and outperforms simpler fusion methods.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two established ideas: **multimodal learning** (combining information from text and images) and **graph neural networks** (GNNs — models that reason over structured relationships, treating data as nodes and edges rather than flat vectors). Prior work in multimodal fusion typically uses either concatenation of features or cross-modal attention mechanisms (techniques that let one modality "focus on" relevant parts of another). The key insight here is that *incongruity* — misalignment between modalities — is itself a signal worth modeling explicitly. The authors use graph attention networks (GATs), which apply attention weights to edges in a graph structure, and hierarchical attention mechanisms to combine representations at different semantic granularities.

## Core Contributions
1. **Hierarchical incongruity modeling**: Captures cross-modal mismatches at three distinct levels (token, phrase, document) rather than treating incongruity uniformly. This is novel — prior work doesn't explicitly structure contradiction detection across semantic scales.
2. **HCIG architecture**: Combines GATs for incongruity graph construction with learned hierarchical attention to weight and merge multi-granular representations.
3. **GCCN variant**: A complementary graph-based model using contradiction-aware pooling for efficient reasoning, showing the approach generalizes beyond one architecture.
4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates clear improvements over fusion baselines on both sarcasm (MMSD: 85.74% accuracy) and cyberbullying (MultiBully: 68.66% macro-F1) benchmarks, with ablation studies isolating the value of each component.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of **multimodal NLP** and **graph-based reasoning**. It implicitly critiques limitations of simpler cross-modal attention (e.g., [[Transformer]]-style attention for fusion) by showing that explicit incongruity graphs outperform them. The hierarchical angle also echoes work on hierarchical NLP models (e.g., hierarchical attention for document classification), but applied to the cross-modal setting. It connects to broader research on **sarcasm detection** and **hate speech/cyberbullying detection**, where prior work often ignored visual context or treated it as noise. The paper positions incongruity as a first-class modeling target, aligning with growing interest in contrastive and contradiction-aware learning in multimodal systems.

## Practical Applications
1. **Social media content moderation**: Automatically flag sarcastic or bullying posts on platforms like Twitter/X, Instagram, or TikTok, where visual memes often contain sarcasm or abusive undertones that text alone would miss.
2. **Toxicity detection for gaming platforms**: Discord, Twitch, or in-game chat systems where users mix images, video clips, and text to harass or mock — this system catches layered attacks.
3. **News credibility checking**: Identify misleading or sarcastic news stories that pair misleading headlines with contradictory images (e.g., clickbait with ironic visuals).
4. **Mental health monitoring**: Detect self-harm or suicidal ideation disguised as sarcasm or dark humor in multimodal posts shared by at-risk users.
5. **E-commerce review filtering**: Flag fraudulent or sarcastic product reviews that pair positive text with negative imagery (or vice versa).

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **SafeContent API**: A REST API for platforms to submit text + image pairs and receive sarcasm/toxicity scores with confidence and explanation of which modality contradicts which. Premium tier includes custom retraining on platform-specific slang.
2. **ParentGuard**: Browser extension and mobile app that analyzes children's social media feeds in real-time, highlighting sarcastic put-downs and cyberbullying (both obvious and veiled) and alerting parents with context.
3. **Brand Reputation Monitor**: SaaS tool that crawls social media for sarcastic or backhanded mentions of a brand (e.g., "love how your product breaks in 5 minutes" paired with a sad emoji meme), surfacing sentiment that simpler tools miss.
4. **Creator Safety Suite**: Tools for streamers/content creators to automatically moderate community posts and clips, catching mockery and toxic "banter" disguised as jokes.
5. **Misinformation Early Warning System**: B2B offering for newsrooms and fact-checkers that flags viral memes and image-text combos that are misleading or ironically constructed, before they spread.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Graph construction overhead**: Building and attending over token/phrase/document-level graphs is computationally expensive; scalability to very long posts or high-volume moderation may hit latency constraints.
2. **Dataset bias**: Both MMSD and MultiBully are English-language, platform-specific datasets; generalization to other languages, cultures, or platforms (TikTok, Discord) is untested.
3. **Semantic alignment assumption**: The model assumes clear token-level or phrase-level alignment between text and image regions. Real-world images are often abstract or symbolic, making correspondence implicit and fuzzy.
4. **Evaluation metric gaps**: Macro-F1 on imbalanced datasets (cyberbullying is rare) can be misleading; no analysis of false positive rates in production scenarios, where over-flagging content damages user experience.
5. **Interpretability**: The hierarchical attention weights are opaque; it's unclear *why* the model detects incongruity or *which* text-image pair caused a sarcasm decision — important for trust and debugging in moderation systems.
6. **Visual feature extraction**: The paper doesn't detail the backbone (ResNet? Vision Transformer?); choice of visual encoder likely affects what incongruities are discoverable.

## Open Questions
1. **Cross-lingual transfer**: Does HCIG trained on English MMSD transfer to sarcasm detection in Hindi, Spanish, or Mandarin, where sarcasm may be culturally specific?
2. **Adversarial robustness**: How does the model fare against adversarial images (slight perturbations) or paraphrased text designed to evade detection?
3. **Explainability**: Can the model be augmented to output human-readable explanations (e.g., "this phrase contradicts the image because [reason]")?
4. **Multimodal alignment**: Does the model work for posts with multiple images or video? How does it handle temporal incongruity (text first, then image reveal)?
5. **Few-shot and zero-shot scenarios**: Can hierarchical incongruity reasoning help detect new forms of sarcasm or bullying with few labeled examples?
6. **Real-world deployment**: What is the latency, memory, and false positive rate when deployed on 1M+ posts per day at a real platform? Can it be quantized or distilled?

## Key Takeaway
Modeling how text and images contradict each other at multiple levels beats simple fusion for detecting sarcasm and cyberbullying in social media.
