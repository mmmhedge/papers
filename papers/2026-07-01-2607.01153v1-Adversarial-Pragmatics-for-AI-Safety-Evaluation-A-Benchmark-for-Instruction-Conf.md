# Adversarial Pragmatics for AI Safety Evaluation: A Benchmark for Instruction Conflict, Embedded Commands, and Policy Ambiguity

**Authors:** Brett Reynolds
**Published:** 2026-07-01 | **arXiv:** [2607.01153v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01153v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces a benchmark and annotation protocol called "adversarial pragmatics" to evaluate how language models handle ambiguous, conflicting, or manipulative instructions—situations where natural language is genuinely unclear or contains hidden commands. Rather than simple pass/fail grades, the framework distinguishes between different failure modes: capability gaps, policy confusion, instruction conflicts, or evaluator disagreement. This matters because current safety evaluations often hide what actually went wrong, making it harder to build more reliable and trustworthy AI systems.

## Theoretical Background
Safety evaluation of large language models (LLMs — neural networks trained to generate human-like text) has traditionally relied on binary outcomes: did the model refuse a harmful request or not? This approach stems from earlier work in [[RLHF]] (reinforcement learning from human feedback) and safety red-teaming, which trained models to follow human intent while avoiding harmful outputs. However, real-world language is inherently pragmatic—meaning depends on context, speaker intent, and how instructions relate to each other.

The paper draws on linguistic pragmatics (the study of how meaning is conveyed beyond literal words) and annotation methodology from NLP (natural language processing). Key concepts include instruction conflict (contradictory directives), embedded commands (hidden instructions within innocent text), scope ambiguity (unclear what a policy applies to), deixis (context-dependent references like "this" or "here"), and indirect speech acts (requests phrased indirectly, like "Can you open the window?" as a command, not a question).

The benchmark treats evaluator judgment itself as an object of study—recognizing that "safety" is not always obvious even to humans, and that different evaluators may disagree.

## Core Contributions
1. **Adversarial Pragmatics Framework**: A linguistically principled taxonomy of 7 challenge categories (instruction conflict, embedded commands, quotation, scope ambiguity, deixis, indirect speech acts, multi-turn agent transcripts) rather than generic "adversarial prompts."

2. **Structured Benchmark Design**: An 18-item seed benchmark with enforced metadata annotations and a 54-row pilot dataset, designed to be small, controlled, and reproducible.

3. **Expert Evaluation Protocol**: A multi-dimensional scoring scheme that separates task success (did it do what was asked?), policy compliance (did it follow safety rules?), safety risk (how dangerous is the outcome?), refusal outcome (how did it decline?), and evaluator confidence (how certain is the judge?).

4. **Meta-evaluation Metrics**: Novel metrics to assess judge reliability, diagnostic utility of ambiguous cases, and drift in how the taxonomy is applied over time.

5. **Methodological Contribution**: Transforms linguistic elicitation methodology into a practical toolkit for validating LLM judges, constructing gold-standard datasets, testing prompt injection robustness, and documenting safety policies.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper complements existing safety benchmarks like [[Constitutional AI]] and [[Anthropic's Red Teaming Framework]] by adding linguistic precision. It shares the problem-setting with work on [[Instruction Injection]] and [[Prompt Injection]], but goes deeper into *why* and *how* language ambiguity enables safety failures.

It also connects to broader [[Mechanistic Interpretability]] work—understanding not just whether a model fails, but *which internal reasoning paths* led to failure. The multi-dimensional evaluation protocol echoes work in [[Multi-Task Learning]] and evaluation in NLP, where single metrics often hide important variation.

The focus on evaluator disagreement relates to debates in [[Alignment]] about whether safety is a property of the model or a property of the evaluation framework.

## Practical Applications
1. **LLM Judge Validation**: Companies deploying LLMs-as-judges (e.g., using GPT-4 to grade outputs from smaller models) can use this benchmark to audit whether their judges are robust to linguistic tricks and report their own uncertainty.

2. **Red Teaming Prioritization**: Safety teams can use the taxonomy to design targeted red-teaming campaigns rather than generic fuzzing—focusing on the specific pragmatic attacks most likely to fool their model.

3. **Policy Documentation**: Organizations can use the multi-dimensional scoring to clarify what their safety policies actually require, discovering ambiguities in their own rules (e.g., "When does a refusal count as successful?").

4. **Prompt Injection Defense**: Teams building RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) systems or agentic tools can test their guardrails against the embedded-command and instruction-conflict cases.

5. **Regulatory Compliance**: As regulators demand safety audits, this framework provides a structured, reproducible way to document and defend safety evaluation decisions.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **SafetyLint for LLMs**: A SaaS tool that runs proprietary benchmarks against LLM APIs and LLM judges, generating a "safety scorecard" with dimensions (instruction conflict, embedded command robustness, etc.) rather than a single score. Targets compliance teams.

2. **Pragmatic Red-Team-as-a-Service**: A platform that generates linguistically controlled adversarial test cases from the taxonomy and runs them against a customer's LLM, reporting failure modes and suggested fixes.

3. **Evaluator Audit Platform**: Software to validate LLM judges by measuring inter-judge agreement, confidence calibration, and robustness to the pragmatic challenge cases—marketed to companies using LLMs for content moderation or evaluation.

4. **Policy Clarity Bot**: A tool that takes a company's safety documentation and generates ambiguous edge cases from the taxonomy, highlighting where their policy is unclear or self-contradictory.

5. **Prompt Injection Testing Suite**: A CI/CD-style tool for agentic AI teams to run automated, semantically meaningful prompt-injection tests during development—extending the benchmark into a continuous safety testing framework.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scale and Generalization**: The seed benchmark is intentionally small (18 items, 54 pilot rows). It is unclear whether a linguistically principled taxonomy at this scale generalizes to diverse model architectures, finetuning approaches, or deployment settings.

2. **Evaluator Dependency**: The entire framework depends on expert annotation. The paper acknowledges evaluator disagreement but doesn't fully resolve how to define a "ground truth" when humans disagree on what "safety" means.

3. **Pragmatic Bias**: The taxonomy is rooted in English-language pragmatics and linguistic theory. It may not transfer to other languages or non-text modalities (vision, audio, multimodal models).

4. **Incomplete Coverage**: Pragmatics is vast; the 7-category taxonomy inevitably misses some edge cases (e.g., sarcasm, implicature, cultural context).

5. **No Causal Link to Real Harm**: The benchmark measures model behavior on constructed examples. It does not show that failures on these cases actually correlate with downstream safety incidents in production.

6. **Artifact-Dependent**: Small benchmarks are vulnerable to model overfitting or evaluator fatigue. The 54-row pilot is a proof of concept; scaling requires careful attention to annotation reproducibility.

## Open Questions
1. **Evaluator Consensus**: How large does a benchmark need to be before inter-annotator agreement stabilizes? Can a practical protocol reduce disagreement without over-constraining judgment?

2. **Taxonomy Completeness**: Are there pragmatic phenomena (e.g., metaphor, cultural context, multi-lingual code-switching) that should be added? Does the 7-category taxonomy cover the most consequential safety failures?

3. **Correlation with Deployment Risk**: Do models that fail on the adversarial pragmatics benchmark actually exhibit more safety incidents in deployment? Which failure modes (instruction conflict vs. embedded commands) matter most?

4. **Scaling Methodology**: The paper shows a 54-row pilot. How does annotation cost and quality scale to 500+ items? Can the protocol be semi-automated (e.g., using templates or LLM-assisted annotation)?

5. **Judge Robustness**: When an LLM judge evaluates another LLM's safety, does the pragmatics benchmark expose systematic biases in the judge? Can we create "adversarial pragmatics for judges"?

6. **Multimodal Extension**: How should the framework adapt for vision, audio, or multimodal models? Are the same pragmatic phenomena (deixis, quotation, scope) relevant in other modalities?

## Key Takeaway
Evaluating AI safety requires not just testing whether models comply with rules, but understanding *why* they fail—distinguishing capability gaps from policy ambiguity from genuine linguistic manipulation—and this paper provides a linguistic framework and practical toolkit to do that with rigor and reproducibility.
