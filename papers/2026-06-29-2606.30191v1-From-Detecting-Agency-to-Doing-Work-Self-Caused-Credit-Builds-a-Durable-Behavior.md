# From Detecting Agency to Doing Work: Self-Caused Credit Builds a Durable Behavioral Self in a Minimal Spiking Agent

**Authors:** Haoliang Han
**Published:** 2026-06-29 | **arXiv:** [2606.30191v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30191v1)

## Summary

This paper demonstrates that spiking neural networks can develop durable, self-preserving behavior when credit assignment is gated by the agent's own detected agency and drives slow parameter updates. The key finding is that "self-caused credit" — a multiplicative term combining agency detection, salience, and slow learning — produces behavioral residue that survives buffer removal and prevents catastrophic forgetting across sequential tasks, without replay or task-boundary mechanisms.

## Theoretical Background

The work builds on recent advances in agency detection (Ye, 2026) — the ability for predictive systems to distinguish self-caused from external events. It grounds itself in two classic ideas: (1) credit assignment in reinforcement learning, which solves how to assign learning signals to the right parameters; and (2) slow vs. fast learning timescales, a principle from neuroscience and meta-learning showing that different parameter populations learn at different rates. The paper uses spiking neural networks with the Nengo simulator (LIF/PES — Leaky Integrate-and-Fire neurons with Prescribed Error Sensitivity learning rule) — a biologically-plausible substrate where discrete spikes replace continuous activations. The core mechanism is "agency-gated slow credit": a multiplicative term (Own × Agency × Salience) that gates which learning signals influence slow-updating decoders, creating a dissociation between fast and slow credit channels.

## Core Contributions

1. **Agency-gated slow credit mechanism**: A concrete implementation showing that multiplicative agency gating (rather than simple additive pooling) on slow parameter updates produces durable behavioral residue post-buffer-removal, with 96% retention in minimal control tasks.

2. **Dissociation experiment**: By toggling *only* the slow-credit channel while holding agency gain constant, the paper isolates the causal role of self-caused slow work — behavior emerges *only* when slow credit is active (1.00 vs. 0.00 post-unload performance).

3. **Continual learning without replay**: Sequential learning across eight tasks with the agency-gated slow-credit mechanism prevents forgetting (0.88 final accuracy, 0.13 forgetting rate) where additive pooling, no-agency ablations, and episodic/replay baselines all collapse to chance.

4. **Basin deformation analysis**: A plastic-work analysis showing that net self-credit work directly corresponds to basin deformation, providing a mechanistic link between slow learning and behavioral persistence.

5. **Operational definition of behavioral self**: Formalizes the durable residue as an operational behavioral self — an abstraction that persists across episode boundaries without consciousness claims.

## Practical Applications

1. **Embodied robotics with limited compute**: Spiking networks draw less power than conventional ANNs; durable behavioral residue without replay enables long-horizon tasks on resource-constrained robots (e.g., micro-drones, edge-deployed systems).

2. **Incremental learning in autonomous vehicles**: Sequential task learning (lane-following → obstacle avoidance → traffic-sign recognition) without forgetting prior skills on neuromorphic chips.

3. **Adaptive agents in non-stationary environments**: Agents that must continually learn new behaviors without catastrophic forgetting of old ones (e.g., game-playing agents, industrial robotics with shifting task demands).

4. **Neuroscience-inspired brain-computer interfaces**: Using spiking networks to decode and reinforce agent intent ("self-agency") in neuroprosthetics, where the device learns to preserve learned control strategies without explicit memory replay.

5. **Lifelong learning in open-ended environments**: Agents that explore and adapt over extended timescales, relying on slow plasticity to build persistent world models without episodic buffering (useful for open-world video game agents, embodied AI in virtual worlds).

## Technical Limitations

1. **Scale and expressivity**: Experiments are on minimal control tasks (24-dim partially-observed) and simple sequential learning (8 tasks). Unclear whether the mechanism scales to high-dimensional state spaces or hundreds of sequential tasks.

2. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: The balance between slow and fast learning rates, agency gain, and salience weighting are likely critical tuning parameters; the paper doesn't systematically explore this landscape.

3. **Agency detection prerequisite**: The mechanism *requires* accurate agency detection (Ye, 2026) upstream. If that signal is noisy or misspecified, the entire self-credit mechanism may degrade gracefully or catastrophically — not explored.

4. **Biological realism vs. practicality trade-off**: Nengo LIF/PES is more biologically plausible than standard ANNs but still abstracts away many neural details (plasticity rules, neuromodulators, sparse connectivity). Claims of biological relevance are suggestive, not definitive.

5. **Task structure assumptions**: The paper assumes clear task boundaries and sequential presentation. In truly open-ended or multitask settings with overlapping tasks, the mechanism's behavior is unclear.

6. **Computational cost of slow learning**: Slow plasticity requires maintaining long integration windows or separate slow decoders. The actual wall-clock cost vs. standard backprop+replay is not quantified.

## Open Questions

1. **Does the agency gate prevent all catastrophic forgetting or just mitigate it?** How does performance degrade as task count or task dissimilarity increases?

2. **Can we characterize the "basin deformation" geometrically?** What loss landscape properties allow slow self-credit work to create durable basins?

3. **How does performance depend on the fidelity of agency detection?** What happens if the agency comparator (Ye, 2026) is noisy or partially broken?

4. **Does this mechanism work in competitive or multi-agent settings?** If two agents each apply self-caused credit, do they learn stable mutual models or oscillate?

5. **Can we scale this to 100k+-dimensional state spaces (e.g., image-based control)?** Do slow decoders remain interpretable at higher dimension?

6. **How does this compare to modern continual learning methods (e.g., experience replay with importance weighting, regularization-based approaches)?** A head-to-head benchmark on standard benchmarks (e.g., Permuted MNIST, Class-Incremental CIFAR) would help position the work.

7. **Is there an information-theoretic lower bound on forgetting?** Can slow self-credit work provably avoid forgetting, or only achieve a certain minimum rate?

## Key Takeaway

Self-caused credit driving slow plasticity — not agency detection alone — is the mechanism by which agents build durable, persistent behavioral self-models that survive memory wipeout and prevent catastrophic forgetting.
