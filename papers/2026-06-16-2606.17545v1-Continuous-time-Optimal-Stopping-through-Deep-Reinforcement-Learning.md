# Continuous-time Optimal Stopping through Deep Reinforcement Learning

**Authors:** Cosmin Borsa, Michael Ludkovski
**Published:** 2026-06-16 | **arXiv:** [2606.17545v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17545v1)

## Summary
This paper presents CARLOS, a deep reinforcement learning algorithm that solves continuous-time optimal stopping problems—such as American option pricing—by learning exercise rules at arbitrarily fine time resolution without suffering from discretization artifacts. Unlike classical dynamic programming approaches that face a trade-off between coarse grids (undervaluation) and fine grids (accumulation errors), CARLOS uses an aggregate deep neural network to progressively refine the space-time decision boundary, achieving near-American option upper bounds with computational efficiency.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on classical optimal stopping theory and dynamic programming, which traditionally solve Bermudan option pricing through backward recursion on discrete time grids. It combines this with modern deep reinforcement learning techniques, particularly the use of deep neural networks as function approximators for value estimation. The work leverages [[Monte Carlo methods]] for sampling trajectories and adaptive importance sampling to concentrate computational effort efficiently. The fundamental tension the paper addresses—between discretization error on coarse grids and approximation error accumulation on fine grids—is well-known in [[computational finance]].

## Core Contributions
1. **CARLOS algorithm**: A novel RL-inspired framework that progressively increases temporal resolution while training an aggregate deep neural network (ADNN), avoiding the traditional grid refinement dilemma.
2. **Adaptive sampling strategy**: A method that dynamically concentrates training effort near the optimal stopping boundary, reducing wasted computation on irrelevant regions of state space.
3. **Continuous-time approximation**: Enables learning exercise rules at arbitrary time granularity without explicit backward recursion, sidestepping error propagation.
4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates superior pricing relative to existing Bermudan solvers and approaches theoretical American option upper bounds with practical computational efficiency.

## Practical Applications
1. **American option pricing**: Direct application to pricing American-style equity and commodity options in trading desks, replacing slower classical methods.
2. **Real-time portfolio optimization**: Determining optimal rebalancing and exit times in dynamic portfolio management under real market constraints.
3. **Commodity futures hedging**: Optimal timing for unwinding futures positions in energy and agricultural markets where early exercise value is material.
4. **Credit derivatives**: Early exercise decisions in bond options, callable bonds, and credit default swap pricing.
5. **Executive stock option valuation**: Modeling optimal employee exercise behavior subject to vesting and market conditions.

## Technical Limitations
1. **High-dimensional curse**: Scalability to very high-dimensional state spaces (e.g., baskets of 100+ assets) remains unclear; empirical results likely limited to 2-5D problems.
2. **Neural network overfitting**: ADNN may overfit to training trajectories, especially near the boundary; generalization to out-of-sample market regimes untested.
3. **Convergence guarantees**: The paper likely lacks formal convergence proofs; only empirical benchmarking against upper bounds provided.
4. **Model risk**: Learned decision boundaries depend on assumed dynamics (e.g., geometric Brownian motion); real market jumps, stochastic volatility, or regime shifts could invalidate the policy.
5. **Computational cost of training**: While inference is efficient, the initial training phase with progressive grid refinement and adaptive sampling may still be expensive for one-off problems.
6. **Path dependency**: Unclear how the algorithm handles path-dependent payoffs (lookback, barrier options) or early stopping constraints.

## Open Questions
1. How does CARLOS perform on genuinely high-dimensional problems (10+ state dimensions)? Are there principled ways to exploit structure (e.g., separability)?
2. Can formal convergence rates be established, perhaps under smoothness assumptions on the value function and boundary?
3. How sensitive are learned policies to the underlying model assumptions (volatility, drift, distribution tail behavior)?
4. Can the method be extended to mean-reverting or jump-diffusion dynamics relevant to commodity and credit markets?
5. What is the sample complexity as a function of problem dimension and desired accuracy, compared to quasi-Monte Carlo alternatives?
6. How does the approach generalize across different option strikes or market parameters without retraining?

## Key Takeaway
CARLOS removes the discretization grid bottleneck in optimal stopping by progressively refining temporal resolution while training a neural boundary detector, enabling accurate American option valuations with computational efficiency unavailable in classical approaches.
