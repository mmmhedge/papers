# Reinforcement Learning for Risk-Sensitive Investment Management: a Free Energy--Entropy Duality Approach

**Authors:** Sebastien Lleo, Wolfgang Runggaldier
**Published:** 2026-06-18 | **arXiv:** [2606.20903v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20903v1)

## Summary
This paper develops a reinforcement-learning framework for continuous-time risk-sensitive portfolio optimization by reformulating a non-standard benchmarked asset allocation problem as a linear-quadratic-Gaussian stochastic differential game. Using free energy–entropy duality, the authors derive explicit optimal solutions and implement a continuous-time q-learning actor-critic algorithm that learns portfolio allocation and adversarial controls, validated on U.S. equity data with strong empirical accuracy.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on several foundational pillars:
- **Risk-sensitive stochastic control**: Extends classical Markovian control problems to handle risk preferences beyond simple expected returns
- **Free energy–entropy duality**: A mathematical technique from statistical physics and optimization that relates optimization problems to probability measures, enabling reformulation of difficult stochastic problems
- **Linear-quadratic-Gaussian (LQG) theory**: A classical framework from control theory that yields closed-form solutions for affine-quadratic problems
- **Benchmarked asset allocation**: A portfolio optimization paradigm where performance is measured relative to a benchmark rather than in absolute terms
- **Actor-critic methods**: A class of reinforcement-learning algorithms combining policy learning (actor) and value estimation (critic)
- **Kelly criterion**: Information-theoretic framework for optimal bet sizing, reinterpreted through the lens of fractional Kelly decompositions

## Core Contributions
1. **Novel problem reformulation**: The benchmarked risk-sensitive allocation problem does not fit standard Markovian templates (uncontrolled state, controlled terminal reward). The authors show how free energy–entropy duality converts this into an equivalent LQG game with explicit saddle-point solutions.

2. **Continuous-time q-learning algorithm**: A novel actor-critic method adapted for continuous-time SDEs, where the quadratic value function structure from the LQG reformulation directly motivates the critic architecture, and saddle-point controls motivate deterministic actors.

3. **Economic interpretation via Kelly decomposition**: The learned portfolio policy admits decomposition into fractional Kelly components, providing interpretability and connecting RL outputs to classical portfolio theory.

4. **Asymmetric learning dynamics**: Empirical discovery that the portfolio actor receives cleaner learning signals than the adversarial actor, suggesting a fundamental asymmetry in the problem structure.

## Connections to Other Work
- **Classical stochastic control literature** ([[Bellman equation]], dynamic programming): This paper extends DP to settings where the standard Markovian assumption is violated
- **Actor-critic algorithms** ([[Policy gradient methods]], [[Temporal difference learning]]): Adapts modern RL to continuous-time deterministic control landscapes
- **Variational inference and duality methods**: The free energy approach connects to [[Variational autoencoders]] and general Lagrangian relaxation techniques in optimization
- **Mean-variance portfolio optimization**: Extends classical [[Markowitz]] and [[Sharpe]] frameworks to dynamic, risk-sensitive settings with learning
- **Differential games**: Related to multi-agent RL and adversarial optimization, though here the adversary is fictitious (only for theoretical decomposition)
- **Physics-informed machine learning**: The statistical physics duality is a concrete example of [[Physics-informed-ml]] crossing into finance

## Practical Applications
1. **Algorithmic trading systems**: Real-time portfolio rebalancing for hedge funds and quantitative trading firms that need to adapt to market regimes while managing tail risk and drawdown constraints.

2. **Robo-advisor risk management**: Wealth management platforms can use this to automatically adjust asset allocation based on client risk profiles and market conditions, learning from historical data while respecting risk bounds.

3. **Pension fund optimization**: Long-dated liability-driven investment (LDI) strategies for institutional investors (pensions, endowments) that need to balance return targets against downside risk over decades.

4. **Corporate treasury optimization**: Dynamic cash management and liquidity reserve strategies for large corporations, learning optimal allocation between safe and growth assets subject to operational constraints.

5. **Insurance reserve management**: Optimal investment of insurance company reserves to meet future claim obligations while earning returns, with explicit risk-sensitivity to avoid insolvency.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Model calibration dependency**: The "partly model-based" approach still requires accurate calibration of drift and diffusion coefficients. Misspecification of the underlying SDE can degrade learning efficiency and saddle-point accuracy.

2. **Continuous-time implementation gap**: The theory assumes continuous trading and observation. Real markets operate on discrete ticks, and transaction costs are not explicitly modeled—both can erode the practical advantage of the learned policy.

3. **Scalability to high dimensions**: While tested on U.S. equities, the quadratic value function and LQG structure may not scale well to portfolios with hundreds of assets or with non-Gaussian returns.

4. **Adversarial control as a fiction**: The saddle-point formulation introduces a fictitious adversary for mathematical convenience. The authors note the adversary receives noisier learning signals, but it's unclear how this affects convergence guarantees or robustness to model error.

5. **Path-dependent constraints**: The framework assumes Markovian dynamics. Constraints with memory (e.g., maximum drawdown over rolling windows, turnover penalties) require extensions not covered here.

6. **Limited risk metric scope**: Risk-sensitivity is formalized through an exponential utility parameterization. Other risk measures (CVaR, maximum loss, Value-at-Risk) would require separate problem reformulations.

## Open Questions
1. **Convergence rates**: What are the finite-sample convergence guarantees for the continuous-time q-learning algorithm? How does the convergence rate scale with problem dimension and entropy parameters?

2. **Robustness to model misspecification**: How sensitive are the learned policies to errors in SDE drift/diffusion estimates? Can the RL algorithm detect and adapt to regime shifts online?

3. **Transaction costs and market impact**: How do proportional/fixed trading costs and market impact modify the optimal policy? Does the RL framework naturally handle these, or is a new reformulation needed?

4. **Multi-agent extension**: Can this framework handle competitive or cooperative settings where multiple agents execute correlated strategies (e.g., coordinated portfolio managers)?

5. **Why is the portfolio actor's signal cleaner?**: The empirical observation that the portfolio actor learns faster than the adversary deserves theoretical investigation. Does this persist for other asset classes or risk-sensitivity parameters?

6. **Explicit convergence to saddle-point**: Does the learned policy provably converge to the saddle-point solution derived from the LQG reformulation? Under what regularity conditions?

7. **Non-Gaussian returns**: How does the framework perform on real asset classes (crypto, commodities, emerging markets) with heavy tails and jumps?

## Key Takeaway
Free energy–entropy duality can reformulate intractable stochastic portfolio problems into LQG games with explicit solutions, enabling continuous-time actor-critic learning that provably recovers optimal risk-sensitive allocation policies with economic interpretability via Kelly decomposition.
