# Does Bielik Know What It Doesn't Know? Activation Dispersion Separates Entity Familiarity from Factual Reliability Across Model Scale

**Authors:** Grzegorz Brzezinka
**Published:** 2026-07-08 | **arXiv:** [2607.07670v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07670v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates whether large language models (LLMs) internally "know" when they're about to hallucinate, by examining neural activation patterns before generating answers about entities. Using Polish language models (Bielik, 1.5B–11B parameters), the authors discover that activation dispersion measures can detect whether a model has seen an entity before with near-perfect accuracy—but this internal awareness does NOT predict whether the model will actually answer correctly. The key finding: models are aware of entity familiarity but rarely abstain, revealing a gap between what models internally know and what they output.

## Theoretical Background
This work sits at the intersection of mechanistic interpretability (understanding how neural networks compute internally) and hallucination in LLMs. It builds on two key ideas: (1) activation analysis—examining the learned representations inside neural networks to understand what information they encode (prior work has shown MLPs in transformers store factual knowledge); and (2) the hallucination problem—LLMs confidently generate false information, especially about unfamiliar entities. The paper uses two classic dispersion measures from physics and information theory—inverse participation ratio (concentration of probability mass) and spectral entropy (disorder in a distribution)—applied to post-activation MLP hidden states. The authors assume that if a model's internal representations differ systematically between known and fabricated entities, those differences should be detectable without training (unsupervised).

## Core Contributions
1. **Unsupervised detection of entity familiarity**: Two simple dispersion metrics (inverse participation ratio, spectral entropy) applied to post-SwiGLU MLP activations achieve AUROC 0.95–1.00 for distinguishing known from fabricated entities—no training required, single forward pass.

2. **Clear separation from random baselines**: Both methods beat permutation floors (0.70–0.74) with high statistical confidence and survive held-out layer selection, showing the signal is robust and not an artifact.

3. **Decoupling familiarity from correctness**: Despite ceiling-level internal awareness at 1.5B parameters, behavioral correctness scales sharply with model size (0→19/42 for known athletes from 1.5B→11B). Within known entities, predicting *correct* answers is much harder (probe 0.93; dispersion barely beats chance).

4. **Signal transfers across entity types and domains**: Cross-domain generalization (mean AUROC 0.92–0.99) suggests the phenomenon is fundamental, not domain-specific. Counterfactual analysis shows template effects matter, but the core signal is diffuse across attention heads.

5. **Models almost never abstain despite internal uncertainty**: Audit of 2,520 answers reveals only 2 refusals and 1 hedge—models demonstrate internal awareness but fail to use it for self-regulation.

## Connections to Other Work
This work bridges mechanistic interpretability and the broader [[hallucination]] problem in LLMs. It relates to:
- **Activation analysis**: Recent work (e.g., sparse autoencoders, circuit analysis) has shown MLPs encode factual knowledge; this paper extends that by asking whether activation *geometry* (dispersion) encodes confidence or familiarity.
- **Uncertainty quantification**: Projects like [[semantic-entropy]] (cited as a baseline, achieving only 0.71–0.83 AUROC at 5x cost) attempt to detect when models are unsure. This paper shows internal signals exist but don't cleanly predict behavioral reliability.
- **Scaling laws for hallucination**: The observation that entity familiarity saturates at 1.5B while correctness scales to 11B mirrors broader findings that different capabilities scale differently.
- **Probing and interpretability**: The use of linear probes (supervised, 0.99–1.00 AUROC) vs. unsupervised dispersion measures echoes the debate in interpretability about what linear probes actually reveal.

## Practical Applications
1. **Hallucination detection at inference time**: Use dispersion measures as a lightweight, single-pass confidence score to flag risky outputs before generation completes, without 5x cost of semantic-entropy sampling.
2. **Selective model routing**: In multi-model systems, route queries to models that have higher activation dispersion for the relevant entity domains, increasing reliability without retraining.
3. **Data quality filtering for fine-tuning**: Identify which facts the model genuinely "knows" (high dispersion separation) vs. confidently confabulates, to curate high-signal training data for domain-specific adaptation.
4. **Auditing model knowledge**: For regulated domains (finance, medicine, law), systematically probe model activations to verify which entities it has reliable knowledge about before deployment.
5. **Training signal design**: Use the internal familiarity signal to weight training objectives—penalize confident-but-wrong answers more heavily when the model's activations should have detected unfamiliarity.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Confidence-Aware LLM API**: Offer a drop-in wrapper around LLM APIs that adds a real-time dispersion-based confidence score to each response, enabling downstream apps to decide whether to trust, hedge, or escalate to humans.

2. **Enterprise Knowledge Verification Service**: Help large organizations audit whether their fine-tuned LLMs actually know their proprietary facts. Scan internal knowledge bases and flag entities the model should know but activations suggest it doesn't.

3. **Self-Correcting Agent Middleware**: Build a system where LLMs check their own activations before answering. If dispersion is low, automatically trigger search, retrieval, or user confirmation before responding—no hallucination.

4. **Domain-Specific Model Selection Tool**: ML ops platform that profiles which models have strongest activation signals in which domains (athletes vs. cities), automatically selecting the best performer per query at inference time.

5. **Hallucination Insurance / Auditing SaaS**: Offer auditing-as-a-service to companies using LLMs in high-stakes settings. Pre-deployment, map model reliability by domain and entity type using activation analysis, then monitor drift over time.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Domain specificity of training**: Results are on Polish entities and Bielik models; unclear whether the 0.95–1.00 AUROC transfers to English, other languages, or other LLM architectures (GPT, Llama, etc.).

2. **Doesn't predict correctness within known entities**: The core limitation stated by authors—dispersion separates *known from fabricated*, not *correct from hallucinated*. This severely limits practical use for improving answer quality.

3. **Post-hoc evaluation only**: All measurements are unsupervised *after* training. The paper doesn't explore whether incorporation of this signal during training could improve the model's own self-awareness and abstention behavior.

4. **Assumes hallucination comes from unfamiliarity**: Entities the model has *seen* but misremembered aren't reliably detected. This conflates "knowing about" with "having the right representation"—a model might have high dispersion for a real person but output wrong facts.

5. **Computational cost of probing**: Requires running and instrumenting forward passes on hidden states. For real-time systems at scale, even a "single pass" overhead is non-trivial.

6. **No causal mechanism**: Paper shows *correlation* between activation dispersion and familiarity, not *why* this correlation exists or how to strengthen it. The signal is "diffuse across heads," limiting targeted intervention.

## Open Questions
1. **Does the signal exist in English-language models?** Bielik is Polish-specific; generalization to dominant LLMs (GPT-4, Llama, Mistral) is critical.

2. **Why doesn't activations-based familiarity improve correctness prediction?** If the model "knows" entity familiarity but can't use it to avoid hallucination, what's the bottleneck? Is it a separate circuit for confidence calibration?

3. **Can we train models to *use* this signal?** Can supervision on the dispersion signal during training teach models to abstain when activation dispersion is low?

4. **How does this scale to very long contexts?** Familiarity with 42 entities is simple; does the signal degrade when the model must track hundreds of entities across a long document?

5. **What about emerging or adversarial entities?** The paper tests known, obscure-but-real, and fabricated. What about newly famous entities (post-training) or adversarially constructed fake entities designed to fool activation-based detectors?
