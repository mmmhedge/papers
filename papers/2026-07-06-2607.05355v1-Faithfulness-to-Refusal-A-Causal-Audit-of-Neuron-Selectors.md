# Faithfulness to Refusal: A Causal Audit of Neuron Selectors

**Authors:** Ananth Eswar, Pratinav Seth, Utsav Avaiya, Vinay Kumar Sankarapu
**Published:** 2026-07-06 | **arXiv:** [2607.05355v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05355v1)

## Summary
This paper tests whether attribution methods (techniques that identify which neurons matter) actually identify *causally* important neurons in language models, rather than just statistically correlated ones. The authors show that popular attribution methods can fail to identify truly causal neurons, and they demonstrate this by testing whether neurons identified as important for "refusal" (rejecting harmful requests) actually install refusal behavior when edited—finding that many high-ranking neurons don't causally matter, and that refusal is implemented redundantly across different neuron sets.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two research threads: (1) **neuron attribution** — methods like integrated gradients and attention-based scores that rank neurons by importance for a model's outputs — and (2) **mechanistic interpretability** — the effort to reverse-engineer what internal computations in neural networks actually do. Prior work assumes attribution scores correlate with causal importance, but this assumption is rarely tested directly. The key insight is the distinction between *correlation* (a neuron's activation correlates with output) and *causation* (removing or editing that neuron changes the output). The paper also touches on **neural network safety editing** — the emerging practice of surgically modifying model weights to change behavior (e.g., to improve refusal of harmful requests).

## Core Contributions
1. **Direct causal audit methodology**: Introduces a one-shot neuron-row zeroing intervention that tests whether attributed neurons are *actually* causally important, not just correlated with outputs. This is a direct empirical test that prior work largely skipped.
2. **Ranking instability finding**: Shows that rank-stable attribution methods (those that consistently identify the same neurons) can be among the *least* causally valid — a surprising inversion of intuition.
3. **Redundancy discovery**: Demonstrates that refusal lives in a **redundant subspace** — different attribution methods identify largely disjoint sets of neurons that each suffice to install refusal. This means there's no single causal mechanism; instead, many paths achieve the same behavioral outcome.
4. **Empirical validation across five LLMs**: Systematic evaluation showing attribution methods beat activation/magnitude baselines at identifying dispensable neurons, but this doesn't always translate to causal correctness for safety-critical tasks like refusal.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper directly challenges implicit assumptions in [[mechanistic interpretability]] research and [[neural network interpretability]] more broadly. It relates to:
- **[[RLHF]] and safety editing**: Prior work on fine-tuning models for refusal; this paper shows the internal refusal mechanism is more complex than single-path causal stories suggest.
- **Causal inference in deep learning**: Builds on causal discovery methods adapted to neural networks, but goes further by testing sufficiency *and* specificity of attributed neurons.
- **Gradient-based attribution methods**: Indirectly critiques integrated gradients, attention rollout, and similar methods when applied naively to safety audits.
- **[[Mechanistic interpretability]]**: Complements work on finding circuits and causal subgraphs by showing that ranking neurons by correlation is insufficient.

## Practical Applications
1. **Safety-critical model editing**: Before surgically modifying a model to improve refusal or remove biases, practitioners can now directly test whether proposed edits are causal, reducing the risk of unintended side effects.
2. **Model pruning with guarantees**: When compressing LLMs by removing neurons, the audit method can verify that pruned neurons are truly dispensable, not just low-ranked by heuristics.
3. **Red-teaming attribution methods**: Companies can use this audit pipeline to stress-test their own interpretability tools before deploying them for high-stakes model modifications.
4. **Behavioral verification in safety benchmarks**: Formalize safety testing by confirming that identified refusal mechanisms actually prevent harmful outputs under adversarial conditions.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Safety audit-as-a-service**: A platform that takes a fine-tuned LLM and an attribution method, runs the causal audit pipeline, and reports which neurons are truly safe to edit — targeting enterprises deploying safety-critical models.
2. **Interpretability validation toolkit**: A library (like Nnsight or Circuits) that bundles causal audit tests, allowing researchers to ship interpretability papers with credible causal claims, not just correlations.
3. **Neural model compression optimizer**: Software that combines pruning heuristics with causal audits to guarantee safe neuron removal while maintaining task performance.
4. **Behavioral editing studio**: Interactive tool for model developers to visually explore redundant subspaces of refusal/bias/style, identify multiple safe edit paths, and choose the least side-effect-prone one.
5. **Model governance insurance**: A compliance service that certifies LLMs have been subjected to causal audits for safety properties, issuing reports that reduce liability for companies deploying edited models.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability**: Neuron-row zeroing requires forward passes for each neuron in each layer; this is expensive for billion-parameter models and may only be practical for targeted audits, not full-model scans.
2. **Attribution method dependence**: The findings are specific to particular selectors (e.g., integrated gradients, attention-based scores). A method not tested here might behave differently.
3. **Behavioral signal design**: The contrastive harmful-versus-benign signal for refusal is hand-crafted. Other safety properties (e.g., honesty, non-toxicity) may not decompose as cleanly, and generalization to those domains is unclear.
4. **Limited to language modeling level**: Primary audits are at the level of next-token prediction loss. Whether causal findings transfer to downstream tasks (e.g., instruction-following, RLHF) is not established.
5. **Redundancy hides uniqueness**: Discovering that refusal is redundant is valuable, but it also makes it harder to pinpoint *why* a particular neuron matters, limiting interpretability gains.
6. **No theoretical guarantees**: The paper is empirical; there's no formal framework predicting which attribution methods will be causally faithful a priori.

## Open Questions
1. **Does causal validity correlate with safety?** If we edit via a causally-validated attribution method vs. a naive one, does the safer model actually remain safer under adversarial attacks?
2. **How does redundancy arise?** Is it a function of training dynamics, architecture (e.g., skip connections), or something fundamental to the loss landscape? Can we predict redundancy without auditing?
3. **Generalization across tasks**: Do neurons identified as causal for refusal on hate speech remain causal for crime, or is causal importance task-specific within the same behavior class?
4. **Scalability to full models**: Can the audit be run on billion-parameter models efficiently, e.g., via gradient-based approximations or sketching?
5. **Editing robustness**: If we install refusal via one attribution method's disjoint neuron set vs. another's, which edit is more robust to distribution shift, adversarial prompts, or subsequent fine-tuning?
6. **Theoretical characterization**: Can we develop a theory of when rank-stability fails as a proxy for causal validity, or when redundancy should be expected?

## Key Takeaway
Attribution methods rank neurons by statistical correlation, not causal importance for safety-critical tasks — and the paper's causal audit framework reveals that rank-stability is a misleading proxy for the direct interventional tests that actually matter.
