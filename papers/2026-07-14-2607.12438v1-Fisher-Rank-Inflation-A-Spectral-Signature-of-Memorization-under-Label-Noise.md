# Fisher Rank Inflation: A Spectral Signature of Memorization under Label Noise

**Authors:** Satwik Bathula, Anand A. Joshi
**Published:** 2026-07-14 | **arXiv:** [2607.12438v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12438v1)

## Summary

This paper identifies "Fisher Rank Inflation" — a spectral pattern in neural network gradients that reveals when models transition from learning clean structure to memorizing mislabeled data. By analyzing the effective rank of per-example gradients in the last layer, the authors show that noisy labels temporarily inflate this rank, creating a detectable signature that tracks memorization and correlates with corruption severity.

## Theoretical Background

The work builds on two foundational ideas: (1) the observation that deep networks learn clean patterns before overfitting to noise (a phenomenon studied in implicit regularization literature), and (2) spectral analysis of neural network behavior, particularly using the Fisher information matrix — a Hessian-like object capturing the curvature of the loss landscape. The authors leverage the centered scatter of per-example gradients to construct a spectrum whose effective rank (a measure of how many dimensions carry significant variance) changes predictably during memorization. This connects to earlier work on neural collapse and feature learning trajectories but introduces a novel spectral signature that persists across architectures (CNNs, ResNets, Vision Transformers).

## Core Contributions

1. **Identification of Fisher Rank Inflation**: A precise characterization of how effective rank in the Fisher spectrum expands transiently during memorization and contracts afterward — creating an inflation–collapse trajectory tied to when corrupted labels are fit.

2. **First-order leave-one-out attribution formula**: A tractable spectral attribution method that identifies which examples most strongly influence the Fisher spectrum, showing corrupted examples are enriched among high-rank contributors (69–96% at peak rank).

3. **Theoretical predictions and explanations**: Derivation of conditions under which noisy examples inject spectral mass into previously unused eigendirections, increasing gradient entropy, and explaining why attribution signals weaken once the normalized spectrum stabilizes.

4. **Consistent empirical validation**: Demonstration across CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, CIFAR-10N, and multiple architectures that peak effective rank correlates monotonically with corruption severity and that rank inflation often precedes observable test degradation.

## Connections to Other Work

This work extends [[mechanistic interpretability]] of deep learning by providing a gradient-space lens on memorization dynamics. It relates to studies of the "critical period" and implicit bias in neural networks, but moves beyond loss-curve observations to a finer-grained spectral diagnostic. The paper complements recent work on neural collapse and feature learning trajectories by showing that memorization leaves a traceable fingerprint in the structure of per-example gradients. It also connects to [[memorization]] and [[double descent]] phenomena, offering a tool to detect the transition point between the two regimes.

## Practical Applications

1. **Early stopping and checkpoint selection**: Practitioners can monitor Fisher effective rank during training to identify peak-rank checkpoints and halt before corruption dominance, improving robustness without explicit validation sets.

2. **Label quality auditing**: Use the rank-inflation signature and top-rank samples as a diagnostic tool to identify and flag potentially mislabeled examples in real datasets without ground truth.

3. **Corruption severity estimation**: Estimate the level of label noise in a dataset by observing the peak effective rank, which correlates monotonically with corruption percentage.

4. **Robust model selection**: In noisy learning settings, use Fisher rank trajectories as a metric to select models that learn generalizable structure rather than memorizing noise.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **Label Quality Auditing Platform**: SaaS tool that monitors Fisher rank during training on user datasets, automatically flags suspicious labels, and recommends data cleaning actions — targeting ML ops teams managing real-world datasets.

2. **Adaptive Training Dashboard**: Real-time monitoring tool showing Fisher rank curves, allowing engineers to pause training at optimal checkpoints and visualize memorization risk before test degradation occurs.

3. **Synthetic Data Quality Checker**: Use Fisher rank inflation as a differentiable metric to optimize synthetic data generation pipelines, ensuring generated data doesn't trigger premature memorization.

4. **Noisy Label Denoising Engine**: Combine rank-based attribution with iterative relabeling to automatically correct corrupted labels, marketed to teams dealing with crowdsourced or weak annotations.

5. **Dataset Robustness Certification**: Offer a service that estimates dataset corruption levels and predicts model robustness by analyzing Fisher rank inflation curves, appealing to regulated industries (healthcare, finance) needing data quality guarantees.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Computational cost**: Computing per-example gradients and their spectrum scales with batch size and model size; impractical for very large models or datasets without efficient implementations.

2. **Architecture dependence**: While tested on CNNs, ResNets, and ViTs, behavior may differ in other architectures (RNNs, Transformers with different attention patterns) and the generalization is not fully characterized.

3. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: The method assumes standard training dynamics (e.g., learning rate schedules, batch size); different regimes may shift the timing or magnitude of rank inflation, reducing the reliability of early stopping heuristics.

4. **Attribution noise in Transformers**: The first-order spectral attribution shows weaker alignment with exact leave-one-out in Vision Transformers, limiting its utility for pinpointing corrupted examples in modern architectures.

5. **Synthetic vs. real noise**: Experiments focus on class-conditional label flips; real-world noise patterns (e.g., class-dependent confusion, partial labels) may not produce the same clear inflation–collapse trajectory.

6. **No direct mechanism for correcting labels**: The paper identifies which examples are likely corrupted but does not provide a method to correct or relabel them automatically.

## Open Questions

1. **Why does effective rank collapse after memorization peaks?** A deeper mechanistic explanation of the gradient dynamics that drive the rank decline could improve predictive power.

2. **Can Fisher rank inflation predict generalization gaps in clean training?** Does the method extend to detecting overfitting to clean but biased data, not just label noise?

3. **How does the signature change under different corruption models** (e.g., instance-dependent noise, symmetric vs. asymmetric flips, partial or soft labels)?

4. **Can the method scale to modern large-scale models** (e.g., billion-parameter transformers) without prohibitive computational cost, and does the signal remain detectable?

5. **How does Fisher rank interact with other regularization techniques** (dropout, weight decay, mixup) that suppress memorization? Does the inflation–collapse trajectory still hold?

6. **Can this be extended to detect other failure modes**, such as memorization of spurious correlations or shortcut learning, or is it specific to label noise?

## Key Takeaway

Neural networks learning noisy labels show a detectable spike in gradient spectrum rank — a spectral signature revealing when memorization begins and predicting which examples are corrupted.
