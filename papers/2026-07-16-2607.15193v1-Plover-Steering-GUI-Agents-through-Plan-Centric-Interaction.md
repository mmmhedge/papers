# Plover: Steering GUI Agents through Plan-Centric Interaction

**Authors:** Madhumitha Venkatesan, Shicheng Wen, Jiajing Guo, Jorge Piazentin Ono, Liu Ren et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.15193v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15193v1)

## Summary
Plover is a GUI automation system that makes AI agent behavior transparent and correctable by externalizing task plans as editable artifacts. Instead of having agents act as black boxes, Plover lets users see, inspect, and modify the agent's plan at each step, enabling human-in-the-loop correction when things go wrong. This matters because real-world GUI automation is fragile—unexpected dialogs and layout changes cause agents to fail—and human visibility into agent reasoning is critical for practical deployment.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on two converging threads: (1) vision-based multimodal agents that operate on screenshots and natural language (combining [[computer-vision]] with [[large-language-models]]), and (2) human-in-the-loop AI systems where humans supervise and correct autonomous behavior. The core insight is borrowed from explainable AI and plan-based systems: externalizing reasoning as persistent, editable artifacts (plans) rather than keeping it internal makes systems more debuggable and controllable. The architecture separates planning (deciding what to do) from execution (doing it), a classical pattern in robotics and autonomous systems. The paper also draws on interaction design principles from HCI, using formative user studies to inform the interface.

## Core Contributions
1. **Plan externalization for GUI agents**: Plover makes the agent's task decomposition visible and editable rather than implicit, enabling users to inspect intermediate reasoning.
2. **Localized repair mechanism**: When execution fails, users can edit the plan directly and resume from that point without restarting, preserving prior progress.
3. **Planner-executor architecture**: Explicit separation of planning and execution, with replanning triggered by failures or user intervention.
4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates through benchmark and scenario analysis that many autonomous failures are repairable when plans are visible, and that explicit replanning improves adaptability.
5. **HCI-grounded design**: Interaction patterns informed by formative study with six participants, bridging AI systems and usability.

## Connections to Other Work
Plover sits at the intersection of autonomous agents, [[computer-vision]]-based task understanding, and explainability. It addresses known weaknesses of end-to-end vision-language models (e.g., those based on [[transformers]]) when operating in dynamic environments. Related to [[RLHF]] and alignment work in that it emphasizes user control and interpretability, but differs by focusing on plan-level transparency rather than reward modeling. Connects to work on interactive AI, task decomposition, and replanning in robotics. The plan-centricity echoes classical AI planning systems but adapted for the multimodal era.

## Practical Applications
1. **Enterprise RPA (Robotic Process Automation) with auditing**: Customers need to see and validate what automation does before deployment; Plover's visible plans satisfy compliance and risk teams.
2. **Complex data-entry workflows**: When a form layout changes mid-task, users can edit the plan instead of scrapping the automation; particularly valuable in healthcare, legal, and financial services.
3. **Web testing and QA automation**: Testers can quickly patch broken test scripts by editing plans rather than rewriting code when UIs change.
4. **Personal-assistant applications**: Users interacting with diverse SaaS tools can supervise and correct agent behavior, building trust in high-stakes tasks like expense filing or travel booking.
5. **Accessibility tools**: Disabled users who rely on GUI automation can oversee and adjust agent plans to match their specific workflow preferences.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **"Plan Studio" — low-code RPA platform**: Sell visual plan editors for non-technical business users to build, debug, and maintain GUI automation without code. Revenue from SaaS seat licensing or per-workflow execution fees.
2. **"AutoRepair" — failure recovery as a service**: Offer a hosted service that detects GUI automation failures, suggests plan edits, and logs repairs for audit trails. Target regulated industries (finance, healthcare).
3. **"Supervise.AI" — embedded human-in-the-loop layer**: Provide an API/plugin that enterprises can drop into their existing RPA stacks to add plan visibility and user intervention without rewriting agents.
4. **"TestFix" — QA automation resilience tool**: Help QA teams instantly patch broken test scripts by editing high-level task plans rather than debugging low-level code. Integrate with Selenium/Cypress.
5. **"Workflow Debugger" — plan-centric debugging IDE**: Build a desktop IDE specifically for GUI automation that shows plan execution step-by-step, lets users pause/edit/rewind, and exports logs. Freemium model with premium features.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Plan representation scalability**: The paper doesn't clearly address how complex, multi-step workflows scale; large plans may become hard to inspect and edit.
2. **Generalization of planner**: It's unclear how well the planner generalizes to novel GUI patterns or workflows outside the training/evaluation set. Real GUIs are highly diverse.
3. **Latency of human-in-the-loop**: Every intervention introduces human delay; not suitable for real-time high-frequency automation where milliseconds matter.
4. **Screenshot-grounded grounding**: Reliance on screenshots limits applicability to text-only or voice interfaces; also vulnerable to rendering inconsistencies.
5. **Plan edit burden**: Users must understand and manually edit plans; if plans are poorly structured or hard to reason about, this becomes a bottleneck rather than a feature.
6. **Replanning cost**: Frequent replanning after edits could be computationally expensive; no discussion of computational overhead.

## Open Questions
1. How does plan complexity scale with workflow depth? At what point does visible planning become a cognitive overload for users?
2. Can Plover learn from user edits to improve its planner over time, or is it purely interactive?
3. How well do plans transfer across similar GUIs (e.g., different email clients)? Is there a notion of plan templates or plan reuse?
4. What is the latency overhead of plan externalization and replanning compared to pure end-to-end agents?
5. Can Plover be combined with [[reinforcement-learning]] to let agents learn better planning strategies from user corrections?
6. How robust is the system to adversarial or deceptive UI changes (e.g., phishing-like dialogs)?

## Key Takeaway
Making AI GUI agents' plans visible and editable lets humans catch and fix failures faster than waiting for end-to-end retraining.
