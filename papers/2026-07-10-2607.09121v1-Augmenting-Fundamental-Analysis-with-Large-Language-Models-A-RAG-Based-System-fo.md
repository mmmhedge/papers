# Augmenting Fundamental Analysis with Large Language Models: A RAG-Based System for Generating Investor Briefs

**Authors:** Bartosz Ziółko, Kacper Dobrzeniewski
**Published:** 2026-07-10 | **arXiv:** [2607.09121v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09121v1)

## Summary
This paper demonstrates how to use large language models (LLMs) combined with retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) to automatically generate investment briefs from company reports, macroeconomic data, and SEC filings. The authors built a working system using GPT-4o, tracked 9 companies over 4 weeks, and validated the approach with real individual investors—showing that LLMs can meaningfully assist fundamental analysis, which traditionally relies on manual document review and interpretation.

## Theoretical Background
Fundamental analysis evaluates companies by studying financial statements, competitive position, management quality, and macroeconomic factors—as opposed to technical analysis, which focuses on price patterns. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique where language models retrieve relevant documents from a knowledge base before generating responses, improving factual accuracy and grounding. The authors incorporated domain knowledge via Kitchin cycles—short-term economic cycles (3–5 years) used in macroeconomic forecasting. The system leverages transformer-based LLMs (like GPT-4o) which excel at synthesizing text across large documents.

## Core Contributions
- A practical RAG pipeline for fundamental analysis that ingests SEC filings (EDGAR), earnings reports, and macroeconomic indicators
- Integration of economic cycle theory (Kitchin cycles) as an explicit knowledge framework to guide LLM analysis
- A 4-week validation study with 9 companies and 9 individual investors, providing real-world feedback on whether automated briefs are genuinely useful
- Demonstration that GPT-4o can reliably extract and synthesize decision-relevant information from unstructured financial documents

## Practical Applications
- **Investment research outsourcing**: Hedge funds and asset managers could use such systems to accelerate due diligence on portfolio companies
- **Retail investor support**: Individual investors who lack access to professional research could receive AI-generated briefs on public companies quarterly
- **Compliance and risk monitoring**: Institutional investors could continuously monitor holdings and macro trends, triggering alerts when LLM-generated briefs flag deterioration
- **Financial advisor augmentation**: Wealth advisors could use the system to quickly brief clients on portfolio holdings or macro backdrop
- **Credit analysis automation**: Banks evaluating loan applicants could auto-generate financial summaries from applicant disclosures and market data

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **InvestorAssistant SaaS**: A subscription service that delivers automated, personalized investment briefs for a user's watchlist or portfolio, integrating real-time SEC filings, earnings transcripts, and macro data
2. **Fundamental Analysis API**: B2B API for fintech platforms, investment apps, and robo-advisors to embed AI-powered company analysis; charge per brief or per API call
3. **Macro-aware Portfolio Dashboard**: Real-time dashboard integrating Kitchin-cycle and other macro frameworks, with LLM-generated insights on how portfolio is exposed to upcoming cycles
4. **Institutional Research Licensing**: Sell pre-generated briefs on hundreds of publicly-traded companies to smaller investment firms that can't afford in-house research
5. **Financial Education Platform**: Interactive tool for retail investors to learn fundamental analysis by seeing LLM-generated briefs alongside explanations of *why* certain signals matter

## Technical Limitations
- **Hallucination risk**: LLMs can confidently generate plausible-sounding but incorrect financial facts; no guarantee briefs are factually accurate without post-hoc verification
- **Document quality dependency**: RAG performance degrades with messy, poorly-formatted source documents (common in older SEC filings or international data)
- **API cost at scale**: Running GPT-4o API calls for thousands of companies weekly is expensive; model fine-tuning or open-source alternatives may be necessary for cost-efficient deployment
- **Regulatory uncertainty**: SEC and investment regulators have not clarified liability if an AI-generated brief leads to losses; investment advice accountability is legally murky
- **Limited evaluation scope**: Only 9 companies, 9 evaluators, 4 weeks—small sample; unclear if approach generalizes across market regimes, sectors, or company sizes
- **No quantitative returns validation**: The paper validates usefulness via user feedback but does not show whether briefs actually improve investment returns

## Open Questions
- How do LLM-generated briefs compare in predictive power to human analyst reports? Do they miss subtleties that experienced analysts catch?
- Can the system handle non-English documents (e.g., international filings) or just English-language data?
- How does performance degrade with smaller, less-frequently-reported companies or micro-caps?
- What is the false-positive rate for red flags (e.g., fraud signals, deteriorating fundamentals)? Could users be misled?
- Does incorporating alternative data (earnings call transcripts, news sentiment, supply-chain tracking) meaningfully improve brief quality?
- How should the system update briefs dynamically? Quarterly (with earnings) or continuously?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs can reliably synthesize financial documents into actionable investment briefs when paired with RAG and domain knowledge frameworks, but real-world deployment requires addressing hallucination risk, cost, and regulatory clarity before institutional adoption.
