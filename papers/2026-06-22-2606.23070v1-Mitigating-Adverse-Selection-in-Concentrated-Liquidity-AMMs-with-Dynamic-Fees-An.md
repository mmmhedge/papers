# Mitigating Adverse Selection in Concentrated Liquidity AMMs with Dynamic Fees: An Agent-Based Model Approach

**Authors:** Daniele Maria Di Nosse, Fabrizio Lillo
**Published:** 2026-06-22 | **arXiv:** [2606.23070v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23070v1)

## Summary
This paper develops an agent-based model (ABM) of Uniswap v3 concentrated liquidity pools to study how dynamic fee schedules can compensate liquidity providers (LPs) for adverse selection losses. By simulating realistic blockchain microstructure—including mempool latency, block propagation, and heterogeneous trading agents—the authors demonstrate that volatility- and order-flow-based dynamic fees can improve LP profitability primarily by boosting fee income during periods of stale-price risk, rather than reducing the underlying loss-versus-rebalancing (LVR) costs.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on:
- **Loss-Versus-Rebalancing (LVR)** literature quantifying adverse selection costs faced by LPs in concentrated liquidity AMMs (e.g., Uniswap v3)
- **Heston volatility dynamics** for modeling realistic price dynamics with stochastic volatility clustering
- **Agent-Based Modeling** frameworks for capturing emergent behavior in financial markets with heterogeneous agents
- **Market microstructure theory**, particularly concepts of latency arbitrage, MEV (Maximal Extractable Value), and stale-price risk
- Foundational work on AMM mechanism design and the capital efficiency vs. risk trade-off in automated market makers

## Core Contributions
1. **Granular ABM of Uniswap v3**: First mechanistic model incorporating discrete blockchain infrastructure (block propagation delays, mempool latency) alongside heterogeneous agent populations (latency-arbitrageurs, smart routers, MEV searchers, rebalancing LPs)
2. **Dynamic fee design principles**: Proposes fee schedules parameterized by realized volatility and order-flow toxicity proxies specifically calibrated to hedge LVR exposure
3. **Empirical finding on compensation vs. mitigation**: Demonstrates that dynamic fees primarily increase fee income during adverse conditions rather than reducing the LVR mechanism itself—an important distinction for mechanism design
4. **Quantitative hedged P&L analysis**: Provides simulation-based evidence for conditions under which LPs achieve positive risk-adjusted returns under dynamic fee regimes

## Practical Applications
1. **Uniswap v3 (and similar AMM) governance parameter tuning**: Directly applicable to optimizing fee structures on live pools based on observed volatility regimes
2. **LP strategy advisory tools**: Real-time dashboards recommending position sizing and rebalancing frequency based on dynamic fee conditions
3. **Risk management for concentrated liquidity protocols**: Help LPs decide capital allocation across pools with different toxicity and volatility profiles
4. **MEV-aware routing and order placement**: Smart routers can use order-flow toxicity proxies to anticipate when LPs will face adverse selection and adjust routing accordingly
5. **Liquidity pool benchmarking**: Metrics for comparing pools and their fee structures relative to LVR exposure

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **LP Risk Intelligence Platform**: SaaS tool for institutional LPs that aggregates real-time volatility and toxicity data, recommends dynamic fee participation, and backtests fee strategies on historical data
2. **Autonomous LP Manager**: Smart contract that automatically adjusts concentrated liquidity positions and rebalancing frequency based on the paper's dynamic fee signals, packaging LPs against stale-price risk
3. **MEV-Aware Trading Router**: Routing engine that exploits the order-flow toxicity proxy to identify when LP spreads are widest (highest adverse selection) and routes orders optimally
4. **Simulation-as-a-Service for AMM Design**: Offer ABM simulation engine (parameterized by liquidity depth, agent types, volatility) for protocols designing new AMM mechanics or fee structures
5. **Dynamic Fee Recommendation Oracle**: Decentralized oracle service that publishes recommended fee updates based on on-chain volatility and toxicity, consumed by governance systems

## Technical Limitations
1. **Calibration and stylized facts**: Agent behaviors and parameters may not fully match empirical blockchain microstructure; mempool models are simplified
2. **Scalability of ABM**: Computational cost of simulating heterogeneous agents limits pool size and time-horizon realism
3. **Dynamic fee implementation complexity**: Requires oracle feeds for volatility and toxicity; gas costs and latency in updating fees on-chain could negate theoretical benefits
4. **Model risk**: Fee schedules optimized in simulation may underperform in live markets if agent behavior or market regimes shift
5. **Concentration on LVR**: Paper does not address other LP risks (e.g., impermanent loss, liquidity drains, cross-pool arbitrage)
6. **Heston dynamics assumption**: May not capture regime changes, jumps, or fat tails observed in crypto volatility

## Open Questions
1. **Empirical validation**: How do simulated LVR and fee impacts compare to historical Uniswap v3 pool data? Can the model be calibrated and back-tested against real logs?
2. **Optimal fee design**: Is there a principled (e.g., convex optimization) framework for designing fees, or are the heuristic proxies near-optimal?
3. **Multi-pool dynamics**: How do dynamic fees in one pool affect liquidity migration, and how do interactions between pools alter the picture?
4. **Adversarial robustness**: If fee schedules become public, do sophisticated agents (MEV searchers) learn to exploit the fee rules themselves?
5. **Welfare effects**: How do dynamic fees affect overall market efficiency, taker costs, and protocol revenue? Are there distributed winners/losers?
6. **Comparative fee mechanisms**: How do volatility-driven fees compare to other mechanisms (e.g., bonding curves, time-weighted average prices) for LP protection?

## Key Takeaway
Dynamic fees based on volatility and order-flow toxicity can improve concentrated liquidity LP returns, but primarily by boosting fee income during adverse conditions rather than reducing the fundamental adverse selection risk itself.
