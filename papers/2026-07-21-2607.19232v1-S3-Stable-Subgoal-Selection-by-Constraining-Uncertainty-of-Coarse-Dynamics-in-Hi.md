# S3: Stable Subgoal Selection by Constraining Uncertainty of Coarse Dynamics in Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning

**Authors:** Kshitij Kumar Srivastava, Kshitij Jerath
**Published:** 2026-07-21 | **arXiv:** [2607.19232v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19232v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes S3, a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) that helps high-level agents choose better subgoals by understanding the unpredictability of multi-step environment dynamics. Rather than trying to predict single-step transitions, the method predicts "coarse dynamics"—what happens several steps ahead—and encourages the high-level agent to select subgoals in regions where this prediction is confident. The approach outperforms existing HRL methods on long-horizon tasks in non-stationary environments.

## Theoretical Background
Hierarchical reinforcement learning decomposes difficult tasks into two levels: a high-level agent that chooses abstract subgoals, and low-level agents that execute primitive actions to reach those subgoals. The high-level agent traditionally receives only sparse rewards from task completion, making learning difficult. This paper builds on the intuition that intrinsic motivation—internal reward signals beyond the task objective—can guide the high-level agent better. Prior work has explored curiosity-driven exploration and uncertainty-based motivation, but those typically focus on single-step dynamics prediction. The paper introduces "coarse dynamics"—environment transitions at the temporal scale of the high-level agent (multiple primitive steps)—and uses a Mixture Density Network (MDN), a neural network that outputs probability distributions instead of point predictions, to estimate the uncertainty in these coarse transitions.

## Core Contributions
1. **Coarse Dynamics Framework**: Shifts from single-step to multi-step dynamics prediction at the high-level agent's temporal scale, making the prediction problem tractable while capturing meaningful environment structure.
2. **Uncertainty-Driven Intrinsic Motivation**: Uses dispersion metrics from MDNs (e.g., variance across mixture components) as intrinsic rewards—the high-level agent is incentivized to select subgoals in regions where coarse-step transitions are most predictable.
3. **Risk-Averse Subgoal Selection**: Empirically demonstrates that this uncertainty minimization leads to more stable, risk-averse behavior compared to state-of-the-art HRL baselines, particularly in non-stationary environments.
4. **Empirical Validation**: Tests on long-horizon continuous control tasks with environmental non-stationarity, showing improvements over baselines.

## Connections to Other Work
This work extends the tradition of intrinsic motivation in RL (e.g., curiosity-driven exploration, empowerment) by anchoring it in dynamics models rather than state visitation or information gain. It relates to work on learning dynamics models for planning and model-based RL, but uniquely applies this at the hierarchical level. The focus on uncertainty quantification connects to risk-sensitive RL and robust control. The use of MDNs for uncertainty estimation parallels recent work in Bayesian deep learning and aleatoric/epistemic uncertainty decomposition in RL. The paper implicitly addresses the exploration-exploitation tradeoff in hierarchical settings, contrasting with flat-RL approaches that struggle on long-horizon tasks.

## Practical Applications
1. **Robotic Manipulation in Changing Environments**: Long-horizon tasks like assembly or object rearrangement where the environment or object properties change unpredictably during execution; the risk-averse subgoal selection would avoid unreliable transitions.
2. **Autonomous Navigation in Dynamic Settings**: Robots navigating crowded or non-stationary environments (e.g., warehouses with moving obstacles, pedestrian areas) benefit from subgoals in predictable, stable regions.
3. **Game AI and Complex Strategy Games**: Multi-step planning in environments with partial observability or opponent adaptation; coarse dynamics help abstract away unreliable single-step outcomes.
4. **Industrial Process Control**: Multi-stage manufacturing or chemical processes where high-level setpoints should be chosen to minimize the variability of multi-step outcomes.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Adaptive Robot Control Platform**: Offer a cloud-based service that fine-tunes robot policies for new tasks/environments using S3-style hierarchical learning, reducing per-customer deployment time.
2. **Warehouse Automation Software**: Real-time path planning for autonomous forklifts/robots that automatically adapts to dynamic floor conditions, equipment failures, or personnel presence using coarse-dynamics intrinsic motivation.
3. **Game AI as a Service**: Provide a middleware library for game developers to quickly generate credible, strategic NPC behavior in open-world or procedurally-generated environments using HRL with uncertainty-aware subgoal selection.
4. **Supply Chain Optimization**: Digital twin simulation + optimization service for factories; S3 helps planners choose robust production stage targets that are resilient to machine variability and demand fluctuations.
5. **Reinforcement Learning Consulting for Manufacturing**: Specialized consulting that applies hierarchical RL with dynamics-aware reward shaping to optimize multi-step industrial processes (e.g., energy efficiency in HVAC systems, production scheduling).

## Technical Limitations
1. **Coarse Dynamics Granularity**: The choice of how many primitive steps to aggregate is left open; the paper doesn't provide principled guidelines for selecting this temporal abstraction level, which likely varies by task.
2. **MDN Scalability**: Mixture Density Networks can become computationally expensive in high-dimensional state-action spaces; the paper doesn't discuss scalability or approximation strategies.
3. **Non-Stationarity Assumption**: The method is tested in non-stationary environments but doesn't explicitly model or adapt to changes; it relies on online coarse-dynamics learning which may be slow to adjust to sudden shifts.
4. **Limited Baseline Comparisons**: While compared to HRL methods, the paper doesn't compare against recent model-based RL or other uncertainty-driven exploration methods at the same scale.
5. **Discrete vs. Continuous Environments**: Evaluation appears to focus on continuous control; applicability to discrete/combinatorial settings is unclear.
6. **Myopic Optimization**: Minimizing uncertainty at each high-level step may not align with long-horizon task success if stable-but-suboptimal subgoals are preferred.

## Open Questions
1. How sensitive is performance to the choice of coarse-step aggregation level, and can this be learned or adapted online?
2. Does S3 scale to very high-dimensional state-action spaces (e.g., image observations) without significant computational overhead?
3. Can the method be extended to non-stationary environments where the coarse dynamics themselves shift; should it incorporate change detection?
4. How does S3 compare to recent uncertainty-aware model-based RL methods (e.g., ensemble models, Bayesian neural networks) when both operate at the same temporal scale?
5. Does risk-averse subgoal selection sometimes prevent discovering better, riskier paths; can the method balance exploration of uncertain but promising subgoals?
6. Can the learned coarse-dynamics model be reused for transfer learning or multi-task learning across related environments?

## Key Takeaway
Hierarchical RL agents choose better subgoals when they avoid unpredictable transitions, using uncertainty in multi-step environment dynamics as intrinsic motivation.
