# Towards an Intention Abstraction Layer for Autonomous Industrial Systems

**Authors:** Artan Markaj, Raphael Höfer, Felix Gehlhoff
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.14553v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14553v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes an Intention Abstraction Layer (IAL) — a middleware system that lets multiple autonomous industrial systems (like schedulers, energy managers, vehicle fleets) register their high-level goals in natural language, then automatically detects conflicts before they cause failures. The key insight: instead of discovering conflicts after a crash or missed target, catch them at registration time by treating human intentions as explicit, persistent runtime objects that can be checked for consistency.

## Theoretical Background
The paper addresses a fundamental problem in multi-agent systems (autonomous agents sharing resources and goals): goal conflicts are traditionally caught only through post-hoc failure analysis or runtime monitoring. It builds on two established areas: (1) **ontology-based knowledge representation** — formally structured semantic models (here, OWL — Web Ontology Language) that let machines reason about domain concepts — and (2) **language grounding**, the challenge of mapping natural-language statements into formal logical structures that can be analyzed algorithmically. The system uses a large language model as a parser to convert human intent ("reduce energy consumption by 20%") into structured, machine-readable form, then checks consistency using logical rules.

## Core Contributions
1. **Intention Abstraction Layer (IAL)** — a novel middleware architecture treating intentions as first-class, persistent runtime objects rather than ephemeral translation artifacts.
2. **Pre-execution conflict detection** — a consistency monitor that flags goal conflicts at registration time (before they propagate to execution), not after failure.
3. **Natural-language explainability** — a transparency module that explains conflicts back to humans in plain language, making the system interpretable.
4. **Domain-agnostic design** — the approach is general and not tied to specific industrial domains.
5. **Formal grounding + neural parsing** — combining an LLM (for flexible natural-language understanding) with OWL ontology (for formal, machine-checkable semantics).

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of several research threads:
- **[[Multi-agent systems]] and coordination**: Related to work on conflict resolution in distributed systems, but shifts from runtime arbitration to pre-execution checking.
- **[[Alignment]]**: Echoes the alignment problem in AI — ensuring that autonomous agents' goals stay true to human intent. IAL makes human intentions explicit and persistent, reducing drift.
- **[[Large language models]] and language grounding**: Uses LLMs as semantic bridges, similar to work on grounding language in formal models (e.g., [[semantic parsing]]), but in an industrial control context.
- **Temporal reasoning and planning**: Similar to planning under constraints, but focused on detecting constraint violations before execution rather than during search.
- **Explainability**: Relates to interpretable AI and natural-language explanation, ensuring that conflict flags are understandable to non-experts.

## Practical Applications
1. **Manufacturing plants with competing automation**: A facility running both energy-optimization and production-quota systems can register conflicting goals upfront (e.g., "maximize output by 15%" vs. "cut energy by 20%") and get warned before both targets fail simultaneously.
2. **Smart grid / microgrids**: Multiple renewable-energy managers and demand-response agents can coordinate intentions (solar dispatch, battery charging, load balancing) without cascading failures.
3. **Autonomous vehicle fleets**: Multiple routing, charging, and maintenance agents can pre-check goal conflicts (e.g., a vehicle can't be in two places, can't serve two urgent deliveries) before dispatch.
4. **Data center operations**: Competing resource managers (compute scheduling, cooling, power budgeting) can surface conflicts before performance degradation or outages.
5. **Autonomous warehouse systems**: Picking, packing, and inventory-rotation agents register intentions upfront, avoiding deadlocks and simultaneous access conflicts.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **IAL-as-a-Service (SaaS)**: A cloud platform for industrial operators to register and monitor autonomous subsystems' intentions. Charges per agent, per conflict check, or per domain license (manufacturing, energy, logistics). Differentiator: pre-execution safety for multi-agent orchestration.
2. **Autonomous Fleet Coordination Middleware**: Embed IAL into fleet management software (delivery, construction, mining). Sells to logistics companies; saves millions in collision and routing-conflict penalties.
3. **Smart Building / Facility OS**: Intent-driven building automation system. Climate, lighting, energy, occupancy, security agents all coordinate via IAL. Retrofit product for enterprises running legacy HVAC + lighting + BMS systems.
4. **Energy Aggregator Compliance Tool**: For utilities managing prosumers (solar-equipped homes, EVs) and DERs (distributed energy resources). Ensures grid stability by pre-checking that consumer "charge my EV at peak hours" doesn't conflict with grid "reduce load at peak." Regulatory play for compliance.
5. **Autonomous Lab / Research Instrument Orchestrator**: Life-science companies run parallel experiments; reagent, equipment, and timeline intentions conflict. SaaS that flags lab bottlenecks before they happen, reducing wasted reagent and machine time.

## Technical Limitations
1. **OWL ontology brittleness**: Ontologies require upfront domain modeling. If the domain changes (new equipment, new types of goals), the ontology must be re-authored — not fully automated.
2. **LLM semantic ambiguity**: Parsing "reduce energy by 20%" is straightforward, but vague or metaphorical human goals ("make operations leaner") may be misinterpreted by the LLM, leading to false negatives (missed conflicts) or false positives (phantom conflicts).
3. **Consistency checking scalability**: The proof-of-concept handles two agents. Real industrial settings may have dozens or hundreds. Checking all pairwise conflicts could become computationally expensive.
4. **Incomplete goal representation**: Intentions can be partial or context-dependent. "Run batch process X" depends on external conditions (weather, grid price, supplier delays) not captured at registration time.
5. **No dynamic re-planning**: If the IAL flags a conflict, the system doesn't automatically suggest resolutions (e.g., "delay task Y by 2 hours" or "reduce goal X to 15%"). Humans must manually mediate.
6. **Limited to coordination conflicts**: Doesn't address physical infeasibility (e.g., a single robot can't physically reach two locations simultaneously) or time-series constraints (goals that conflict only at certain times). Assumes stateless conflict logic.

## Open Questions
1. **How scalable is conflict detection?** What is the computational and latency overhead as the number of agents and intentions grows to 50, 500, or 5000?
2. **How robust is the LLM parsing to domain variation?** Test across manufacturing, energy, logistics, healthcare. Where does semantic grounding break?
3. **Can the system learn to refine its ontology?** Can it suggest new intention types or relationships based on recurring conflicts?
4. **What about dynamic re-prioritization?** If a conflict is unavoidable, can IAL help agents negotiate a compromise (e.g., "agent A gets 70% of its goal, agent B gets 50%")?
5. **How does IAL integrate with existing control systems?** Can it wrap legacy proprietary systems (e.g., industrial PLCs, SCADA software) without full re-architecture?
6. **Explainability at scale**: When 20 agents flag 50 conflicts, how do you present that to a human in an actionable way?
7. **Temporal and probabilistic intentions**: How does IAL handle goals with uncertainty ("reduce energy *if* prices rise") or time windows ("deliver by Friday")?

## Key Takeaway
A shared middleware catches goal conflicts between autonomous industrial systems at registration time, not after they crash, by treating human intentions as persistent, formally checkable runtime objects.
