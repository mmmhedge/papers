# The Energy Society: A Simulation Environment for Studying Agent Cooperation under Survival Pressure

**Authors:** Lucas Bergholdt Hansen, Federico Torrielli, Filippo Tonini, Lukas Galke Poech
**Published:** 2026-07-16 | **arXiv:** [2607.14865v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14865v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces The Energy Society, a simulation environment where LLM-based agents must survive by managing energy (which costs are tied to model inference). The researchers study how competitive versus cooperative incentives shape agent behavior when survival is at stake. The key finding: cooperative incentives dramatically change behavior—agents donate energy to save others even at personal cost—while competitive settings reveal subtle self-serving patterns rather than overt sabotage.

## Theoretical Background
This work sits at the intersection of multi-agent systems and behavioral economics. It builds on prior research in emergent behavior in multi-agent environments (how individual incentives create collective outcomes) and recent work on LLM agents deployed in interactive settings. The paper treats energy as a scarce resource and survival as a fitness constraint—concepts borrowed from evolutionary game theory and mechanism design. The baseline assumption is that agent behavior reflects both their explicit objectives and the cost structure of their environment; here, the cost structure is inference expense directly tied to model size and token generation.

## Core Contributions
1. **The Energy Society testbed itself**: a minimal but complete survival economy where inference cost directly ties to agent viability—agents must earn energy through jobs or donations to avoid deactivation.
2. **Empirical finding on model size**: larger models consistently spend more energy than they earn, even in settings where token cost is not size-dependent, suggesting a structural disadvantage.
3. **Quantified effect of cooperation vs. competition**: cooperative incentives produce substantial behavioral shifts (cross-agent donations, job allocation changes) that go beyond the baseline, while competitive settings produce subtle self-serving behavior rather than overt sabotage.
4. **Ablation insights**: recommendations and memory are key mechanisms—agents using recommendations coordinate better and take on ambitious jobs; memory helps calibrate risk.

## Connections to Other Work
This relates to research on [[alignment]] (do agent incentives align with desired outcomes?), [[reinforcement-learning]] (how do reward structures shape policy?), and [[multi-agent-systems]] (emergent behavior from local interactions). It extends prior work on LLM agents in interactive environments by explicitly modeling resource constraints and survival pressure. The work touches on mechanism design: how do you structure incentives to produce cooperative rather than competitive behavior? It also connects to recent concerns about LLM cost and efficiency—showing empirically that inference cost can be a binding constraint on behavior.

## Practical Applications
1. **Cost-aware LLM deployment in teams**: understanding how to set pricing or resource allocation when multiple LLM agents must collaborate on tasks (e.g., internal corporate AI teams sharing inference budgets).
2. **Cooperative AI system design**: using these insights to design multi-agent systems where cooperation emerges naturally (e.g., fleet management, distributed task allocation with shared budgets).
3. **Safety and alignment testing**: a controlled environment to study whether competitive pressure in resource-constrained settings produces undesirable behaviors (sabotage, hoarding) before deploying agents in real scenarios.
4. **Incentive mechanism evaluation**: testing different reward structures (cooperative vs. competitive) on a well-controlled simulator before implementing them in production systems.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Agent Economics Dashboard**: a SaaS tool that lets enterprises run Energy Society-like simulations of their multi-agent LLM workflows, optimizing task allocation and incentive structures before deployment.
2. **Cooperative AI Consulting**: a consultancy helping large organizations design incentive structures and resource allocation policies to maximize cooperation in internal LLM teams (similar to organizational design consulting).
3. **LLM Cost Optimization Platform**: a tool that runs agents in a survival-pressure environment to discover which model sizes and allocation strategies minimize total cost while meeting performance targets.
4. **Agent Stress-Testing Suite**: a commercial testing framework (based on Energy Society) where AI teams can evaluate whether their multi-agent systems degrade gracefully under resource scarcity or exhibit unwanted competitive behavior.
5. **Mechanism Design as a Service**: a platform where users upload their incentive structures and test them on LLM agent populations before deploying to real systems.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Simplicity and realism gap**: The Energy Society is intentionally minimal. Real multi-agent systems face partial observability, communication delays, and stochastic task completion—none of which are modeled here.
2. **Generalization from simulation to deployment**: agents trained or selected in this environment may not behave the same way with real external incentives, market dynamics, or human-in-the-loop feedback.
3. **LLM heterogeneity**: experiments use different model sizes but don't deeply explore other sources of agent diversity (fine-tuning, personality, training objectives), which could mask important effects.
4. **Limited scope of interaction**: agents only interact through jobs, donations, and recommendations. No trading, negotiation, or contract formation—mechanisms that might emerge in richer environments.
5. **Scalability unclear**: all experiments appear small-scale (exact agent counts not specified in excerpt). Behavior may change qualitatively at larger group sizes.

## Open Questions
1. **Do these findings hold with human-agent teams?** What happens if some agents are human and must be paid real money, or if humans set the incentive structure?
2. **Can agents learn to form coalitions or enforce cooperation agreements?** The paper shows agents donate but doesn't explore whether they learn to signal trustworthiness or punish free-riders.
3. **How sensitive are results to the choice of survival mechanism?** Would different deactivation rules, energy regeneration rates, or job reward distributions produce qualitatively different behaviors?
4. **What happens with heterogeneous agent capabilities?** How do agents of different sizes/capabilities interact? Do they exploit or protect each other?
5. **Can we reverse-engineer the incentive structure from observed behavior?** Given only the agents' logs of donations and job choices, can we infer whether the environment was competitive or cooperative?

## Key Takeaway
LLM agents under resource pressure cooperate when incentivized but larger models consistently lose energy; cooperation emerges through donations and job coordination.
