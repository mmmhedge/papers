# Surrogate Fidelity: When Can Open LLMs Explain Closed Ones?

**Authors:** Philippe Chlenski, Zachariah Carmichael, Ayush Warikoo, Chia-Tse Shao, Yingxiao Ye et al.
**Published:** 2026-06-30 | **arXiv:** [2606.32008v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.32008v1)

## Summary
This paper asks a practical but underexplored question: when you can inspect an open-source language model's internals, can you use those insights to understand how closed proprietary models (like GPT or Gemini) work? The authors find a surprising answer — models often agree on *what* they output but disagree on *why*, meaning prediction-level similarity does not guarantee mechanistic similarity. This matters because mechanistic interpretability research has focused on open models while deployed systems remain opaque.

## Theoretical Background
Mechanistic interpretability (MI) aims to reverse-engineer neural networks by analyzing their internal computations — attention weights, activation patterns, causal flow of information. Most MI work examines fully transparent models (open weights). However, deployed LLMs like GPT-4 or Gemini expose only APIs returning log-probabilities over tokens, not internal states. The paper treats this as a *surrogate problem*: open models act as proxies to infer facts about closed ones. Prior work in MI has assumed direct access; this paper formalizes when that assumption breaks. The authors measure fidelity (agreement) across three levels: prediction (do models give the same output?), attribution (do they credit the same inputs for that output?), and representation (do their internal states encode information similarly?).

## Core Contributions
1. **Empirical demonstration of prediction–attribution divergence**: Across eleven models (Llama, Qwen, GPT, Gemini), models with high prediction agreement (≥90%) often have low attribution agreement—showing they reach the same answer by different reasoning paths.

2. **Access-validity inversion**: White-box signals (attention patterns, perturbation magnitudes) are *stable* across models but *weakly predictive* of causal attributions; conversely, black-box input ablations (which require no internals) actually capture causal effects better and transfer more reliably.

3. **Concrete API-compatible measurement scheme**: Log-odds ratios from token probabilities provide a scalar readout of a model's decision boundary without full internals, making surrogate fidelity measurable even for closed APIs.

4. **Systematic evaluation framework**: Operationalize surrogate fidelity rigorously, showing that prediction agreement is *insufficient* to warrant claims about mechanistic transfer.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper sits at the intersection of [[mechanistic-interpretability]] research and the interpretability-in-practice problem. It pushes back against the implicit assumption in recent MI work (e.g., studies of attention or circuits) that insights from transparent models transfer to deployed ones. Related to [[RLHF]] and model alignment research, which also care about understanding what closed models "really" learn. The access-validity inversion echoes findings in adversarial robustness and model distillation—high-level agreement masks lower-level divergence. Also connects to work on model steering and probing, which face similar questions about whether learned classifiers on one model's representations work on another.

## Practical Applications
1. **Interpretability auditing for deployed systems**: Organizations can run smaller open models alongside APIs to detect when interpretability insights from internal testing fail on production, flagging risky divergences before deployment.

2. **Mechanistic safety testing**: Safety teams can use open surrogates to stress-test attribution methods (e.g., does this ablation truly capture causal influence?), then carefully validate any safety insights claimed about closed models.

3. **API-efficient mechanistic research**: Researchers with limited API budgets can identify which surrogate fidelity metrics are most predictive, focusing expensive closed-model queries on high-confidence cases.

4. **Model selection and comparison**: When choosing between closed LLMs for a task, run fidelity checks on their open analogs to flag mechanistic divergence—useful if downstream reasoning transparency matters.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Surrogate Fidelity Audit Service**: SaaS tool that ingests open model weights + closed API credentials, automatically runs fidelity tests (prediction, attribution, representation), and outputs a report on mechanistic transferability—valuable for compliance and safety teams.

2. **Interpretability Insurance / Model Risk Ratings**: Rate closed LLMs on their mechanistic transparency via surrogate testing; sell risk scores to enterprises using GPT/Gemini for high-stakes decisions.

3. **API-Efficient Mechanistic Debugger**: IDE-like tool for LLM developers—compare behavior of open and closed models on failing test cases, highlight where mechanistic insights break, suggest debugging strategies.

4. **Open Model Fine-Tuning Marketplace**: Help users fine-tune open surrogates specifically to maximize fidelity to closed targets, creating better "explainability twins" that developers can inspect and monetize.

5. **Mechanistic Validation Framework for Model Distillation**: Product helping teams distill closed models to open ones while preserving mechanistic properties (not just accuracy), using this paper's fidelity metrics as the validation objective.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Binary classification focus**: Experiments are restricted to binary tasks; unclear how fidelity scales to multi-class or open-ended generation, where attribution becomes much harder to define.

2. **Limited model diversity**: Only four families tested (Llama, Qwen, GPT, Gemini); behavior on smaller models, specialist architectures, or older models remains unclear.

3. **Attribution methods assume causal clarity**: Leave-one-out and input ablation assume removing a token has a well-defined causal effect; this breaks for highly distributed representations or recurrent dependencies.

4. **No access to true internals of closed models**: Fidelity claims about closed models cannot be ground-truthed; the paper only compares surrogates against each other. We cannot verify if the open model's explanation is actually "right."

5. **API rate limits and cost**: Black-box testing (input ablation) requires many API calls; for large models, this becomes prohibitive, limiting practical adoption.

6. **Shallow temporal horizon**: Binary classification on single inputs; behavior on multi-step reasoning, in-context learning, or long context likely differs.

## Open Questions
1. **Do fidelity gaps widen with model capability?** The paper uses models of similar scale; what happens comparing a 7B open model to a 70B closed model? Is the divergence capability-dependent?

2. **Can you predict fidelity without running it?** Given model architecture and training data, can you forecast whether an open surrogate will be mechanistically faithful? Develop a theory.

3. **How do in-context learning and fine-tuning affect surrogate fidelity?** The paper tests base models; do few-shot prompting or LoRA adaptation change the prediction–attribution gap?

4. **What if you optimize an open model to maximize fidelity to a closed one?** Reverse the problem: can you distill or train a surrogate specifically for interpretability, and how much does that help?

5. **Do causal attribution methods transfer better than attention patterns?** The paper shows input ablation outperforms attention; does this hold for more sophisticated methods like integrated gradients or structural causal models?

6. **How do mechanistic differences correlate with downstream robustness/safety gaps?** If two models disagree on attributions, are they also likely to disagree on adversarial robustness, out-of-distribution behavior, or alignment?

## Key Takeaway
Open language models can reveal what closed models output, but not reliably why—and you cannot assume mechanistic insight transfers across model families without explicit validation.
