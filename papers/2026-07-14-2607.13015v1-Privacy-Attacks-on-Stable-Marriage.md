# Privacy Attacks on Stable Marriage

**Authors:** Stephan A. Fahrenkrog-Petersen, Aleksander Figiel, Darya Melnyk, Tijana Milentijević, Stefan Schmid
**Published:** 2026-07-14 | **arXiv:** [2607.13015v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13015v1)

## Summary
This paper reveals that the Gale-Shapley stable marriage algorithm — widely used in real-world matching systems like medical residency placement — leaks private preference information when one side can repeatedly query the algorithm. The authors show attackers can fully reconstruct honest participants' preferences through strategic interactions, highlighting a critical privacy vulnerability in a foundational algorithm used in sensitive domains.

## Theoretical Background
The stable marriage problem, formalized by Gale and Shapley, seeks a matching where no pair of unmatched agents would prefer each other to their current partners (a "stable" outcome). The classic Gale-Shapley algorithm has agents on one side (proposers) iteratively make offers to agents on the other side (acceptors) in order of preference. It's proven to always produce a stable matching, but this paper examines what happens when preference lists are treated as secret information that algorithms should protect — a privacy consideration largely absent from classical algorithm design. The work builds on emerging research in privacy-preserving algorithms and adversarial analysis of algorithmic mechanisms.

## Core Contributions
1. **Demonstrates complete preference inference**: Shows that when the proposer side is adversarial, they can fully reconstruct all acceptor preferences through repeated, strategic queries to the Gale-Shapley algorithm.
2. **Characterizes vulnerable preference distributions**: Identifies which preference orderings among honest agents are susceptible to attack, and shows the acceptor-proposes variant can be more robust against certain distributions.
3. **Extends to decentralized settings**: Proves that even in decentralized stable marriage protocols, an attacker can infer complete preference orderings.
4. **Validates on real-world data**: Demonstrates via experiments that synthetic and real-world preference distributions (including actual residency matching data) are indeed vulnerable.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of stable matching theory (Gale-Shapley) and privacy-preserving computation — areas that have rarely intersected before. It relates to broader concerns about [[mechanism design]] with privacy constraints and adversarial robustness of algorithms. The attack model (repeated queries to an algorithm to extract secret information) echoes membership inference attacks and model extraction attacks in machine learning, but applied to classical algorithmic problems. The paper motivates the need for [[differential privacy]] or other formal privacy guarantees in matching algorithms, a frontier largely unexplored in the stable matching literature.

## Practical Applications
- **Medical residency matching (NRMP)**: Hospitals or residents could exploit the algorithm to infer competitors' preferences, enabling strategic manipulation of their own preference lists.
- **School choice systems**: Public school assignment systems using stable matching could be gamed if administrators can run the algorithm repeatedly and reverse-engineer student/family priorities.
- **Job market platforms**: Online hiring platforms using matching algorithms (e.g., for fellowship or internship placement) risk exposing candidate preferences to competing institutions.
- **Organ transplant allocation**: In countries using stable matching for organ donor-recipient pairing, privacy breaches could compromise fairness and confidentiality.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Privacy-auditing service for matching platforms**: Offer institutions using stable marriage algorithms an independent audit to detect privacy vulnerabilities and test susceptibility to attacks on real deployment data.
2. **Differential privacy wrapper for legacy systems**: Provide a drop-in privacy layer that adds noise to stable marriage algorithm outputs, allowing institutions to deploy without full reimplementation.
3. **Privacy-preserving matching-as-a-service (MaaS)**: Build a trusted platform that runs stable marriage algorithms on encrypted or secret-shared preferences so no party (including the platform) learns others' preferences.
4. **Compliance & regulatory software**: Help healthcare networks and school districts document and certify that their matching systems meet data protection regulations (GDPR, HIPAA) with automated privacy verification tools.
5. **Adversarial testing toolkit**: Develop open-source tools that organizations can use to probe their own matching systems for privacy leaks before going live.

## Technical Limitations
- **Assumes repeated access**: The attack requires the attacker to run the algorithm many times with different inputs; a one-shot, sealed environment would be resistant.
- **Known algorithm assumption**: Attacks assume the attacker knows the algorithm being used; a proprietary or obfuscated algorithm might provide security-through-obscurity (though unreliably).
- **Honest-but-curious model**: The paper doesn't address collusion between multiple malicious parties or sophisticated defenses like cryptographic commitment schemes.
- **Scalability of attacks**: The number of queries required to fully reconstruct preferences scales with the size of preference lists; very large matchings might be expensive to attack, but still vulnerable.
- **No formal privacy bounds**: The paper doesn't quantify privacy loss (e.g., via differential privacy epsilon); it only shows complete preference leakage, not partial or noisy leakage scenarios.
- **Assumes static preferences**: The work doesn't consider dynamic or time-varying preferences or repeated matching over multiple rounds.

## Open Questions
1. Can formally privacy-preserving variants of stable marriage algorithms be designed that achieve non-trivial privacy guarantees (e.g., differential privacy) while maintaining stability and efficiency?
2. How do attacks generalize to many-to-one matchings (e.g., hospitals matching to multiple residents), which are used in practice?
3. Can approximate stability be traded off against privacy — i.e., do relaxed stability notions enable stronger privacy guarantees?
4. What is the minimum number of queries an attacker needs to infer preferences with high confidence, and how does this scale with problem size?
5. Can randomized stable matching algorithms (e.g., tie-breaking via randomness) provide meaningful privacy without sacrificing fairness?
6. How do these attacks perform against oblivious or online stable matching algorithms, which operate with limited information?

## Key Takeaway
Classic hospital-resident matching algorithms leak private preferences when attackers repeatedly query them, demanding privacy-preserving redesigns for sensitive real-world systems.
