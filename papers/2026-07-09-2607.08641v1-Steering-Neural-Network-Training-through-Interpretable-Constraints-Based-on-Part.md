# Steering Neural Network Training through Interpretable Constraints Based on Partial Dependence

**Authors:** Yann Claes, Pierre Geurts, Vân Anh Huynh-Thu
**Published:** 2026-07-09 | **arXiv:** [2607.08641v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08641v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes a method to steer neural network training using partial dependence constraints—statistical tools that measure how a model's output changes with specific input features. Rather than just explaining what a trained model learned, the authors use domain knowledge about desired input-output relationships as constraints during training, producing models that are simultaneously more accurate, data-efficient, and interpretable. The approach is validated on regression tasks including time-series forecasting.

## Theoretical Background
Neural networks often learn opaque, uninterpretable relationships between inputs and outputs. Partial dependence is a classical interpretability technique borrowed from statistics and ensemble learning (particularly random forests) that visualizes the marginal effect of a feature on predictions by averaging over other features. Domain knowledge here means prior understanding of how certain inputs *should* causally or functionally relate to outputs. The paper sits at the intersection of two growing areas: (1) mechanistic interpretability—understanding what neural networks learn—and (2) explanation-guided learning—using interpretability signals as training objectives to reshape model behavior.

## Core Contributions
1. **A concrete method for constraint-based training**: The authors formalize how to incorporate partial dependence alignment into the loss function, enabling users to specify desired input-output functional relationships and enforce them during training.
2. **Empirical validation on regression and dynamics**: Unlike most prior work (which focuses on classification), this demonstrates that constraint-guided training improves generalization and data efficiency on regression and forecasting tasks.
3. **Fidelity of explanations**: The paper shows that models trained with constraints produce interpretations that actually match the provided domain knowledge, whereas unconstrained models do not—addressing a gap between what we visualize in a model and what the model actually learned.

## Connections to Other Work
This work relates to [[RLHF|reward-guided learning]] in that both use external signals to shape model behavior, though here the signal is structural (partial dependence alignment) rather than reward-based. It also connects to physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), which embed domain knowledge as hard constraints during training, though the mechanism here is softer and focused on interpretability rather than governing equations. The paper addresses limitations in prior [[mechanistic-interpretability]] work that focuses only on post-hoc explanation without correcting model behavior. It differs from feature importance methods (e.g., SHAP, permutation importance) by using partial dependence as a *training objective* rather than a diagnostic.

## Practical Applications
1. **Scientific modeling**: Forecasting dynamical systems (climate, biology, physics) where scientists have strong priors about which variables causally influence outputs and in what functional form.
2. **Healthcare/medicine**: Training diagnostic models where medical domain knowledge specifies how patient biomarkers should relate to risk, ensuring learned relationships are clinically sensible.
3. **Finance**: Building regression models for asset pricing or risk prediction where practitioners know the expected monotonicity or functional shape of relationships (e.g., volatility surfaces).
4. **Safety-critical ML**: Autonomous systems where constraints on how perception features should influence control outputs can prevent dangerous learned behaviors.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Interpretability-as-a-Service platform**: A tool that lets domain experts define partial dependence constraints via UI, then automatically retrains models with those constraints applied, with visualization of before/after behavior.
2. **Regulatory compliance toolkit**: Software for financial institutions to ensure ML models comply with explainability requirements by letting compliance teams codify domain constraints and enforce alignment.
3. **Scientific ML IDE**: Integrated environment for computational scientists to train neural networks with built-in partial dependence constraint specification, reducing the gap between scientific intuition and learned models.
4. **Medical AI validator**: Tools for hospitals/pharma to validate that ML diagnostic models learned relationships that align with medical literature before deployment.
5. **Physics-aware neural network library**: Open-source framework combining this work with PINNs, enabling engineers to train networks constrained by both physics laws and interpretability requirements.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Partial dependence assumes feature independence**: The method marginalizes over other features; if features are highly correlated, the partial dependence curves may be misleading and constraints may not encode what users intend.
2. **Requires explicit prior knowledge**: Users must articulate expected functional relationships upfront; this is not always available or consensus-able, especially in novel domains.
3. **Scalability unclear**: No discussion of computational cost of computing partial dependence constraints during training; feasibility on very high-dimensional or large-scale datasets is unknown.
4. **Regression-focused**: The paper does not address classification or structured prediction; unclear how to generalize constraints to non-scalar outputs.
5. **Constraint specification challenge**: The paper does not provide principled guidance on *how precise* constraints must be or how to weight them against the data-fitting objective—hyperparameter selection could be difficult in practice.
6. **Local vs. global alignment**: Constraints are applied on average; localized deviations from domain knowledge might still occur and go undetected.

## Open Questions
1. How sensitive is the method to misspecified or slightly incorrect domain knowledge? Does poor prior knowledge hurt more than help?
2. Can the approach extend to classification tasks, and if so, how should partial dependence constraints be formulated?
3. What is the computational overhead, and does it scale to high-dimensional problems or very large datasets?
4. How do you balance competing constraints when multiple pieces of domain knowledge conflict?
5. Can this be combined with causal inference techniques to ensure constraints enforce causal rather than merely correlational relationships?
6. How do partial dependence constraints interact with modern optimization techniques (batch normalization, attention, residual connections)?

## Key Takeaway
Domain knowledge can be operationalized as partial dependence constraints during training, producing neural networks that are simultaneously more accurate, sample-efficient, and genuinely interpretable—not just post-hoc explainable.
