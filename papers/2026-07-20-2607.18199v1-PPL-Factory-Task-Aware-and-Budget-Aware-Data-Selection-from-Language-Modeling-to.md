# PPL-Factory: Task-Aware and Budget-Aware Data Selection from Language Modeling to Reasoning

**Authors:** Hang Zhang, Warren J. Gross
**Published:** 2026-07-20 | **arXiv:** [2607.18199v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18199v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces PPL-Factory, a data selection method that identifies the most valuable training samples for fine-tuning language models while using only a fraction of the full dataset. The key insight is that different tasks (like math reasoning vs. general language modeling) benefit from different sample-scoring strategies, and the method combines task-specific perplexity scores with budget constraints to achieve better performance with far fewer samples — reaching full-data accuracy using just 1-10% of training data.

## Theoretical Background
Language model fine-tuning traditionally uses all available training data, but recent work has shown that not all samples contribute equally to downstream performance. Prior data selection approaches fall into two categories: (1) heuristic-based methods that rely on proxy metrics like data quality, diversity, or reasoning trace length, which don't generalize well across tasks; and (2) perplexity-based selection, which uses the model's prediction loss on each sample as a difficulty metric. Perplexity — loosely, "how surprised the model is by a sample" — provides a model-aware signal, but existing methods treat all tokens equally and don't account for fundamental differences between language modeling (predicting next tokens) and reasoning tasks (solving step-by-step problems). This paper bridges that gap by making perplexity scoring task-aware.

## Core Contributions
1. **Task-aware perplexity scoring**: Recognizes that language modeling and reasoning tasks have different learning objectives and applies different scoring strategies — for reasoning, it focuses on perplexity of answer tokens rather than all tokens.
2. **Budget-aware selection**: Integrates sample-level perplexity scores with data budget constraints (e.g., "use only 10% of data") to select the most valuable subset.
3. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates substantial improvements on standard benchmarks (GSM8K, MATH) — matching or exceeding full-dataset fine-tuning with 1-10% of samples, which is a significant practical efficiency gain.

## Connections to Other Work
PPL-Factory extends the lineage of [[curriculum learning]] and sample-weighting approaches in deep learning by making them task-aware. It sits between simple heuristic-based data selection (which ignores model difficulty) and more complex learned selection strategies that may overfit to specific tasks. The work is related to broader efforts in efficient [[fine-tuning]] of large language models, including methods that use proxy metrics like entropy or gradient-based importance, but offers a simpler, more interpretable alternative grounded in model uncertainty.

## Practical Applications
1. **Cost-effective fine-tuning**: Organizations fine-tuning LLMs (e.g., for domain-specific tasks like legal document analysis or medical coding) can reduce GPU/compute costs by 90% while maintaining performance.
2. **Few-shot adaptation in resource-constrained settings**: Mobile or edge deployment scenarios where training budgets are limited can adopt better samples upfront.
3. **Rapid prototyping**: Teams iterating on LLM-based products can quickly identify which data matters most, accelerating development cycles.
4. **Synthetic data evaluation**: When generating or purchasing synthetic training data, practitioners can score and rank samples before expensive labeling or use.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **DataCurate**: A data-curation SaaS tool that takes raw datasets and applies PPL-Factory-style scoring to recommend which samples to label/use, lowering annotation costs.
2. **EfficientFineTune API**: A cloud service that accepts user data and automatically selects optimal subsets for fine-tuning open-source or proprietary models, charging by compute saved.
3. **SmartDataSelection for Enterprise**: Integrate PPL-Factory into data pipelines for regulated industries (healthcare, finance) where cost and data efficiency are critical; offer it as a middleware.
4. **Reasoning Task Optimizer**: A specialized tool for companies building reasoning-heavy LLM applications (code generation, math, planning) that auto-tunes which training samples matter most.
5. **Dataset Valuation Engine**: A platform that ranks/scores existing datasets by their utility for a given task, enabling data marketplaces to price and recommend datasets more intelligently.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Task-specificity trade-off**: While the method is more general than fixed heuristics, it still requires task-aware tuning (e.g., how to score reasoning vs. language modeling). It's unclear how well this transfers to novel, unseen task types.
2. **Perplexity as a proxy**: Perplexity measures model uncertainty, not ground-truth sample quality. A sample with high perplexity may be hard because it's noisy or ambiguous, not because it's informative.
3. **Computational cost of scoring**: The method requires computing perplexity for all samples before selection, which adds an upfront cost (though likely much less than full training).
4. **Limited scope of benchmarks**: Evaluation focuses on math/reasoning tasks (GSM8K, MATH). Generalization to other domains (code, dialogue, retrieval-augmented tasks) is unclear.
5. **Interaction with model size and architecture**: Results may be specific to the model and training setup used; robustness across LLM sizes and architectures is unexplored.

## Open Questions
1. How does PPL-Factory perform on tasks beyond mathematical reasoning (e.g., code generation, translation, dialogue)?
2. Can the task-aware scoring strategy be learned end-to-end rather than hand-designed for each task type?
3. What is the relationship between sample perplexity and downstream task performance across different types of errors (hallucinations, reasoning gaps, etc.)?
4. Does combining PPL-Factory with other efficient training methods (e.g., [[LoRA]], knowledge distillation) yield further improvements?
5. How sensitive is the method to the initial model checkpoint used for scoring? Does scoring with a smaller model (faster) yield similar selection quality?

## Key Takeaway
Smart data selection using task-aware perplexity scoring lets LLMs reach full-performance with 1-10% of training data, cutting fine-tuning costs dramatically.
