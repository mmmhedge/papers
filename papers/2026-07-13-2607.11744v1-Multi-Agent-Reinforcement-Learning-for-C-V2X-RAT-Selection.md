# Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning for C-V2X RAT Selection

**Authors:** Moritz Schaffenroth, Uwe Kölbel, Heike Lepke, Alexander Prinz, Alfred Höß
**Published:** 2026-07-13 | **arXiv:** [2607.11744v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11744v1)

## Summary

This paper applies multi-agent reinforcement learning (specifically MAPPO, a distributed variant) to the problem of choosing which communication technology vehicles should use in real-time: cellular networks, direct V2X sidelinks, or both. The work matters because connected vehicles need to balance conflicting demands (latency, reliability, bandwidth) across multiple applications, and the paper shows that learning-based channel selection outperforms static rules and single-agent deep RL approaches by 2–3% in delivery reliability while halving training time.

## Theoretical Background

V2X (Vehicle-to-Everything) communication enables cars to share sensor data and coordinate actions. Two main standards exist: cellular Uu (traditional mobile networks) and NR-V2X PC5 (a direct sidelink that doesn't require infrastructure). Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) trains multiple agents simultaneously to maximize a shared or individual reward; MAPPO (Multi-Agent Proximal Policy Optimization) is a distributed variant that scales better than centralized methods. The baseline approaches include single-agent DRL (which treats the problem as one agent deciding for one vehicle), static decision trees, and hand-coded heuristics. The paper frames hybrid V2X selection as a sequential decision problem where agents observe network conditions and application demands, then choose a channel to optimize on-time delivery.

## Core Contributions

1. **Problem formulation**: Casting real-time V2X RAT (radio access technology) selection for heterogeneous applications as a multi-agent RL task, where vehicles independently learn when to use cellular, sidelink, or both.

2. **MAPPO deployment**: Demonstrates that multi-agent modeling (where each vehicle learns its own policy) outperforms single-agent DRL on this problem—a non-obvious result for a distributed communication task.

3. **Empirical gains**: Shows 2.7–3.5% improvement in on-time delivery ratio over single-agent DRL, plus 50% faster training, validated on realistic urban scenarios and actual use-case workloads (CAMs, cooperative driving, sensor sharing).

4. **Generalization insight**: Identifies that gains are highest for advanced applications (cooperative driving, perception sharing) rather than legacy Cooperative Awareness Message (CAM) services, suggesting future V2X systems should invest in adaptive strategies.

## Connections to Other Work

This builds on the broader literature in [[Reinforcement Learning|multi-agent reinforcement learning]] for network optimization (e.g., spectrum access, resource allocation) and complements work in vehicular networks that has traditionally relied on heuristic or rule-based channel selection. Unlike centralized approaches ([[Multi-Agent Proximal Policy Optimization]] alternatives like QMIX), MAPPO avoids the need for a global coordinator, making it practical for decentralized vehicular systems. The comparison to single-agent [[Deep Reinforcement Learning|DRL]] highlights the value of explicitly modeling agent interactions in communication systems.

## Practical Applications

1. **In-vehicle middleware**: Embedded V2X stacks that dynamically switch or bundle channels based on learned policies, reducing latency for safety-critical messages (collision warnings) and reliability for cooperative maneuvers.

2. **Network operator decision support**: Helping telecom operators predict and manage congestion by understanding how vehicles' learned policies will distribute load across cellular and sidelink bands.

3. **Autonomous fleet coordination**: Ride-sharing or delivery fleets learning shared communication strategies to minimize cost and latency for cooperative navigation and platooning.

4. **V2X simulation and testing**: Training and validating RL policies in carmaker simulation environments before deployment, reducing real-world testing overhead.

5. **Hybrid 5G/6G testbeds**: Validating the approach on next-gen NR-V2X and cellular standards in smart city or highway corridors.

## Technical Limitations

1. **Scalability**: MAPPO assumes independent agents; with hundreds of vehicles in an area, communication overhead and state complexity may explode. The paper tests in "urban scenarios"—specifics on density and scale are unclear.

2. **Non-stationary environment**: Real network conditions (congestion, interference) change unpredictably. The policy was trained and tested in simulation; robustness to distribution shift is not addressed.

3. **Application model simplification**: Use cases (CAM, cooperative driving, sensor sharing) are abstracted. Real applications have heterogeneous QoS requirements and may require more sophisticated reward shaping than on-time delivery ratio alone.

4. **Centralized training assumption**: The paper does not clarify whether agents train in a centralized environment (unrealistic for real fleets) or fully decentralized (harder to achieve performance gains). Mixed assumptions may limit real-world transferability.

5. **No game-theoretic analysis**: With multiple vehicles learning policies in shared spectrum, equilibrium behavior and potential for harmful feedback loops (e.g., all vehicles oscillating between channels) are not analyzed.

6. **Hardware/standards constraints**: Assumes vehicles and infrastructure support both Uu and PC5 simultaneously and that switching is instantaneous; actual hardware latency, antenna switching costs, and standard limitations are ignored.

## Open Questions

1. How does performance degrade with increasing vehicle density? Is there a critical threshold beyond which MAPPO breaks down?

2. Can the learned policy transfer to different urban environments, weather conditions, or network topologies without retraining?

3. What happens if a subset of vehicles does not follow the learned policy (e.g., older cars with only cellular)? How robust is the multi-agent strategy to heterogeneous agent populations?

4. Can you characterize the equilibrium behavior of the learned policies? Do vehicles converge to a stable allocation of channels, or do they cycle?

5. How much does the reward design (on-time delivery ratio) matter? Would optimizing for different metrics (latency, energy, fairness) lead to qualitatively different policies?

6. Can centralized training + decentralized execution be formalized, and does it offer theoretical guarantees on convergence or performance?

7. How does this approach compare to other emerging methods, such as mean-field games or transformer-based attention mechanisms for channel assignment?

## Key Takeaway

Multi-agent RL learns to switch between cellular and sidelink V2X channels better than single-agent baselines, improving reliability by 3% for future connected vehicle applications.
