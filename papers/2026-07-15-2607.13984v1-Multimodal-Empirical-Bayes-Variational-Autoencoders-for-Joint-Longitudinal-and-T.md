# Multimodal Empirical Bayes Variational Autoencoders for Joint Longitudinal and Time-to-Event Modeling

**Authors:** Anders Sjöberg, Nils Olsson, Marcus Baaz, Mats Jirstrand
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.13984v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13984v1)

## Summary
This paper extends empirical Bayes variational autoencoders (EB-VAE) to jointly model tumor growth trajectories and patient dropout timing using longitudinal measurements, genetic data, and event times. It matters because clinical data comes in multiple forms—repeated measurements, dropouts, genetic information—and integrating them in a single probabilistic framework can improve treatment response prediction and identify genetic biomarkers.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on variational autoencoders (VAEs)—generative models that learn compressed latent representations of data by balancing reconstruction accuracy against a prior regularization term. Empirical Bayes VAEs (EB-VAE) extend this by letting the prior over latent variables adapt based on observed covariates, making the model more flexible when subject-level features (here, genetic data) are available. Longitudinal mixed-effects models are the classical pharmacometric approach: they capture individual variation in drug response using random effects regularized by population-level distributions. Time-to-event models (survival analysis) separately predict dropout or event timing. The key challenge this paper addresses is that dropout in clinical trials is often *informative*—patients drop out because they respond poorly—so ignoring the dropout mechanism biases trajectory estimates.

## Core Contributions
1. **Joint EB-VAE for longitudinal and time-to-event data**: Couples a latent variable model for tumor growth with a hazard model for dropout, enabling simultaneous inference on both processes while accounting for informative censoring.
2. **Hybrid semi-mechanistic decoders**: Compares fully neural decoders (learned dynamics) against hybrid decoders that embed mechanistic models (e.g., differential equations for tumor growth), recovering interpretable parameters like treatment effects.
3. **Genetics-conditioned priors**: Adapts the EB prior using high-dimensional genomic covariates, improving individual-level predictions and identifying biologically plausible genetic variants (BRAF, NRAS, NF1, MDM2) through stability selection.
4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates the framework on real melanoma and breast cancer data, showing the joint model reproduces both outcome distributions and dropout patterns in held-out subjects.

## Connections to Other Work
This work bridges [[Variational Autoencoders|VAEs]] and classical [[Pharmacokinetics|pharmacometric]] population modeling (nonlinear mixed-effects models). It parallels recent efforts in [[mechanistic-interpretability]] by using hybrid decoders that preserve interpretable parameters while leveraging neural flexibility. The informative censoring handling relates to modern causal inference and missing-data frameworks in biostatistics. The genetics conditioning resembles conditional generation in VAEs (e.g., class-conditioned image generation) but applied to high-dimensional covariate spaces.

## Practical Applications
1. **Personalized oncology**: Predict individual tumor trajectories and dropout risk at treatment initiation, identifying patients likely to respond poorly and enabling early intervention.
2. **Clinical trial design**: Use the joint model to simulate realistic trial outcomes under different dropout mechanisms, improving power calculations and retention strategies.
3. **Biomarker discovery**: Stability selection on genetic covariates accelerates identification of predictive variants, reducing need for large prospective validation cohorts.
4. **Pharmacometric regulatory submissions**: Hybrid decoders provide both predictive power and mechanistic interpretability, meeting regulatory expectations for dose justification and patient stratification.
5. **Drug development prioritization**: Estimate treatment-effect heterogeneity across genetic subgroups, informing go/no-go decisions for specific populations.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Clinical trial analytics platform**: SaaS tool that ingests longitudinal biomarker data, genetic profiles, and event times from ongoing trials; predicts dropout risk and outcome distributions in real time to alert sponsors to enrollment or efficacy issues.
2. **Precision oncology decision support**: Software-as-a-medical-device that takes a patient's baseline tumor burden, genetics, and treatment choice; outputs personalized survival and progression curves to support shared decision-making.
3. **Biomarker validation service**: Outsourced analytics service that applies stability selection and genetic conditioning to retrospective trial data, identifying and validating prognostic/predictive variants for sponsor partnerships.
4. **Population PK/PD modeling suite**: Modernizes legacy nonlinear mixed-effects tools (NONMEM) with neural flexibility and joint outcome modeling, marketed to CROs and pharma R&D.
5. **Patient stratification engine**: B2B tool for adaptive trial sponsors; clusters patients into risk/response groups in real time using the learned latent representations, enabling mid-trial protocol amendments.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Scalability to very high-dimensional genetics**: The framework conditions on genetic covariates, but doesn't discuss how it scales to genome-wide association studies (GWAS) with hundreds of thousands of variants; dimensionality reduction or feature selection may be necessary.
2. **Identifiability of latent effects vs. covariates**: The EB prior learns from covariate-conditioned regularization, but the paper doesn't deeply analyze when the model can distinguish between latent individual effects and measured genetic factors—potential for confounding in parameter recovery.
3. **Hybrid decoder interpretability**: While hybrid decoders recover parameters consistent with prior mixed-effects models, the paper doesn't quantify how much flexibility the neural component adds or how to know when it overfits mechanistic structure.
4. **Generalization across tumor types and treatments**: Experiments are on melanoma and breast cancer; it's unclear how sensitive the approach is to cancer biology differences or whether retraining is needed for new therapeutic classes.
5. **Computational cost and posterior inference**: No wall-clock time or convergence analysis; for a clinical tool, inference speed matters. Also, the paper doesn't discuss uncertainty quantification in downstream predictions (e.g., credible intervals on dropout risk).

## Open Questions
1. How does the framework perform under model misspecification—e.g., if the true tumor dynamics are non-Markovian or subject to unmeasured confounders?
2. Can the hybrid decoder architecture be made more transparent, e.g., via attention mechanisms or saliency maps, to explain which mechanistic components the model relies on vs. augments with neural terms?
3. Does genetic conditioning improve predictive performance when applied to *prospective* held-out cohorts with different treatment regimens, or is the benefit mostly in the training population?
4. How does the joint model compare to standard two-stage approaches (fit longitudinal model, then predict dropout independently) in terms of sample efficiency and bias correction?
5. Can the stability selection procedure be extended to detect gene–treatment or gene–gene interactions, or is it currently limited to main effects?

## Key Takeaway
Neural-probabilistic framework jointly models tumor growth and dropout, embedding genetic data, improves biomarker discovery in oncology.
