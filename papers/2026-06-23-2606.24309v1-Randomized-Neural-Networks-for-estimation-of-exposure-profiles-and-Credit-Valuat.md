# Randomized Neural Networks for estimation of exposure profiles and Credit Valuation Adjustment (CVA) for American Equity Options

**Authors:** Isidro Moroso Varona, Jakub Michańków, Paweł Sakowski
**Published:** 2026-06-23 | **arXiv:** [2606.24309v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24309v1)

## Summary
This paper applies randomized neural networks (RNNs) to estimate exposure profiles and Credit Valuation Adjustment (CVA) for American equity options under both Black-Scholes and Heston dynamics. The approach integrates neural network approximation into a Monte Carlo framework for exposure and CVA calculation, demonstrating superior computational scaling in high-dimensional problems compared to the classical Least-Squares Monte Carlo (LSM) benchmark.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on several foundational pillars:
- **Least-Squares Monte Carlo (LSM)**: The classical benchmark for American option valuation using regression to approximate continuation values along simulated paths
- **Credit Valuation Adjustment theory**: The methodology for pricing counterparty credit risk, requiring estimation of exposure profiles (Expected Exposure, Potential Future Exposure)
- **Randomized feedforward neural networks**: A computationally efficient variant of neural networks that uses random hidden layer weights and only trains output layer weights via least squares
- **Stochastic process modeling**: Black-Scholes and Heston models for underlying asset dynamics, with the latter capturing stochastic volatility effects

## Core Contributions
1. **Methodological extension**: Demonstrates that randomized neural networks can preserve convergence properties when extended from pricing to exposure and CVA estimation—a non-trivial leap
2. **High-dimensional scalability**: Shows empirical evidence that RNNs scale more efficiently than LSM in multi-asset scenarios, with measurably lower computational cost
3. **Model flexibility**: Validates the approach under two distinct stochastic frameworks (Black-Scholes and Heston), showing robustness
4. **Comprehensive numerical validation**: Includes path convergence analysis and sensitivity studies, providing practical confidence in the method

## Connections to Other Work
This paper sits at the intersection of:

## Practical Applications
1. **Counterparty risk management**: Banks and financial institutions can use RNNs to rapidly recompute CVA across large derivative portfolios during risk monitoring cycles
2. **Real-time trade pricing**: Dealers pricing American options to clients can incorporate CVA more efficiently, especially in equity baskets and multi-leg structures
3. **Regulatory capital calculation**: RNNs enable faster CVA computation for regulatory capital requirements (Basel III CVA RWA), reducing computational burdens in compliance
4. **Portfolio rebalancing**: Institutions can afford more frequent CVA-adjusted portfolio rebalancing when computational overhead is reduced
5. **Scenario analysis**: Risk managers can run extensive sensitivity and stress tests on exposure profiles in real time

## Technical Limitations
1. **Path convergence assumptions**: The convergence analysis assumes standard Monte Carlo path generation; breaks down if paths are correlated or use advanced variance reduction that isn't accounted for
2. **Random weight initialization**: Performance is sensitive to the random projection distribution; paper doesn't fully explore robustness to initialization schemes
3. **High-dimensional curse on features**: While scaling is better than LSM, the method still encodes basis functions implicitly; may struggle in ultra-high dimensions (100+) without careful network design
4. **Model risk in Heston**: Stochastic volatility adds parameters; paper doesn't address sensitivity to parameter misspecification or calibration error
5. **Portfolio netting structure**: CVA with netting requires careful treatment of path-wise correlations; unclear how RNNs handle complex netting agreements or collateral mechanics
6. **Training stability**: Randomized neural networks are simpler than deep networks but still require tuning hidden layer sizes; paper doesn't fully characterize hyperparameter sensitivity

## Open Questions
1. **Adaptive refinement**: Can randomized neural networks be combined with importance sampling or stratified sampling to further reduce variance in CVA estimation?
2. **Bilateral CVA and xVA extensions**: The paper addresses unilateral CVA; how do RNNs perform with bilateral CVA, FVA (funding valuation adjustment), or full xVA frameworks?
3. **Online learning**: Can RNNs be retrained incrementally as new paths are generated, or must they be rebuilt each computation cycle?
4. **Collateral and margining**: Real CVA calculations must account for initial margin and variation margin mechanics; how do these change the neural network design?
5. **Generalization across strike spaces**: Are networks trained on one strike range generalizable to others without retraining?
6. **Comparison to other modern methods**: How do RNNs compare to recent deep-learning CVA methods (e.g., using graph neural networks or Transformers for portfolio effects)?

## Key Takeaway
Randomized neural networks offer a computationally efficient alternative to Least-Squares Monte Carlo for CVA estimation in high-dimensional American option portfolios while maintaining convergence guarantees.
