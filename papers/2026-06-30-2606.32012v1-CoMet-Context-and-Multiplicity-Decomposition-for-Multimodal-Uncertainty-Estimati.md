# CoMet: Context and Multiplicity Decomposition for Multimodal Uncertainty Estimation

**Authors:** Sanghyuk Chun, William Yang, Amaya Dharmasiri, Olga Russakovsky
**Published:** 2026-06-30 | **arXiv:** [2606.32012v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.32012v1)

## Summary

This paper introduces CoMet, a method for estimating uncertainty in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) by decomposing it into two sources: context-induced ambiguity and answer multiplicity. Instead of generating multiple answers or sampling repeatedly, CoMet trains a lightweight post-hoc module that predicts these two uncertainty components, enabling efficient confidence estimation for open-ended tasks like visual question answering and hallucination detection.

## Theoretical Background

Uncertainty estimation in ML has roots in Bayesian approaches and ensemble methods, but becomes especially complex in large language models because answers are generated token-by-token and can be open-ended rather than discrete. Multimodal models — systems that process both images and text together — amplify this challenge because uncertainty can arise from the image, the text prompt, their interaction, or the inherent ambiguity of what counts as a correct answer. Prior work on uncertainty in vision and language has typically relied on sampling-based approaches (generating multiple candidate answers) or entropy-based metrics from the model's output distributions, both of which are computationally expensive. The key insight here is that uncertainty has structure: some ambiguity comes from the input context being inherently unclear, while other uncertainty stems from there being multiple valid answers given that context.

## Core Contributions

1. **Decomposition framework**: Formally separates uncertainty into context-specific (how ambiguous is the input?) and multiplicity-specific (how many valid answers exist?) components—a novel factorization not previously applied to MLLM uncertainty.

2. **Post-hoc lightweight module**: Trains a small neural network on top of frozen MLLM embeddings to predict both uncertainty components, avoiding costly autoregressive generation or repeated sampling at inference time.

3. **Empirical validation across multiple settings**: Demonstrates improvements over baselines on open-ended benchmarks, hallucination detection, and multiple-choice VQA; shows the method scales efficiently to real applications.

## Connections to Other Work

This work sits at the intersection of uncertainty quantification in [[transformers]] and multimodal vision-language modeling. It relates to prior efforts on [[calibration]] and confidence estimation in neural networks, and builds conceptually on work distinguishing aleatoric (data-inherent) vs. epistemic (model-inherent) uncertainty—though CoMet's decomposition is task-specific rather than model-centric. It also connects to the broader problem of hallucination in language models, where uncertainty estimates can flag likely false outputs. The post-hoc approach is similar in spirit to uncertainty quantification methods that wrap frozen pretrained models rather than retraining them.

## Practical Applications

- **Hallucination detection in MLLMs**: Flag when a model's answer is likely incorrect before users see it, critical for applications like medical image analysis or legal document review.
- **Interactive QA systems**: Highlight when the model is uncertain, prompting it to ask clarifying questions or refuse to answer rather than guessing.
- **Content moderation**: Identify uncertain model outputs that may contain fabricated details (e.g., fictional facts in image captions).
- **Educational tutoring**: MLLMs tutoring students can signal when it's unsure, encouraging students to seek additional sources.
- **Autonomous systems**: Robots or autonomous agents using vision-language models can defer uncertain decisions to humans.

## Product & Startup Ideas

1. **MLLM confidence API**: A cloud service that wraps any MLLM and returns both the answer and a calibrated uncertainty score; SaaS for companies deploying vision-language models.

2. **Hallucination detection for document processing**: A tool for enterprise document digitization (OCR + MLLM + CoMet) that flags uncertain outputs for human review, reducing downstream errors.

3. **Interactive chatbot with confidence feedback**: A consumer chatbot that shows confidence levels for image-related queries and asks clarifying questions when uncertain, improving trust and user experience.

4. **Quality control for synthetic data generation**: Use CoMet to identify low-confidence image captions or VQA annotations, automating quality filtering in data labeling pipelines.

5. **Adversarial robustness testing**: A tool for researchers/companies to audit MLLMs by identifying inputs where the model is artificially confident despite being wrong, improving safety testing workflows.

## Technical Limitations

- **Post-hoc design**: The method depends on the MLLM's internal embeddings and may not generalize well to radically different architectures or if the base model is retrained.
- **Limited to two uncertainty sources**: Real-world uncertainty may have finer structure; the context vs. multiplicity decomposition may oversimplify some scenarios.
- **Evaluation metric challenge**: Ground truth for "true multiplicity" (how many valid answers exist) is subjective and hard to define, especially in open-ended tasks; evaluation relies partly on proxy metrics like agreement between annotators.
- **Computational cost during training**: While inference is efficient, training the uncertainty module still requires forward passes and embedding extraction from the MLLM, which is expensive for large models.
- **Scaling to new domains**: The lightweight module is trained on specific benchmarks; transfer to out-of-distribution tasks or entirely new domains is untested.

## Open Questions

- **How does CoMet perform on truly novel or adversarial inputs** that differ from training data in structure or semantics? Does the decomposition still hold?
- **Can the method extend to other open-ended modalities** (e.g., audio + text, or video + text), or is it specific to vision-language?
- **What is the relationship between context and multiplicity?** Can they be formally disentangled, or do they interact in ways the model should learn?
- **How should CoMet be adapted when the MLLM is fine-tuned or updated?** Does the uncertainty module need retraining, or can it transfer?
- **Can this decomposition help improve the MLLM itself,** not just estimate its uncertainty? Could uncertainty signals be used for self-supervised learning or continual improvement?

## Key Takeaway

CoMet shows that decomposing uncertainty into context-induced ambiguity and answer multiplicity enables efficient, interpretable confidence estimation in multimodal models without expensive sampling or repeated generation.
