# Cluster-Aware Matching via Laplacian Optimal Transport

**Authors:** Gabriel Samberg, YoonHaeng Hur, Yuehaw Khoo, Nir Sharon
**Published:** 2026-07-17 | **arXiv:** [2607.16178v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16178v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes Laplacian Optimal Transport (LapOT), a method for matching point clouds that respects cluster structure rather than forcing point-by-point correspondences. The key insight is that when points naturally group into regions, aligning clusters is more meaningful than aligning individual points—think of matching two images where you care more about matching "the face region" than every pixel. The authors show this approach produces more stable and interpretable alignments.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on optimal transport (OT) — a mathematical framework for measuring and transforming one distribution into another by finding the cheapest way to "move mass" from one configuration to another. Classical OT finds point-to-point couplings by solving a linear program. This work extends OT by adding regularization terms based on Laplacian matrices (mathematical objects derived from graph structure that encode local connectivity). The Laplacian encodes which points are nearby in each point cloud, allowing the optimization to "know about" cluster structure. The paper also draws on spectral clustering and graph-based learning, which use Laplacian eigenvectors to discover natural groupings.

## Core Contributions
1. **Laplacian Optimal Transport (LapOT)**: A novel regularization of the OT problem using quadratic Laplacian terms that explicitly encourages the computed matching to preserve cluster boundaries within both point clouds.
2. **Refined Simultaneous Clustering (RSC)**: A post-processing method that uses the cluster-aware coupling from LapOT to produce consistent cluster assignments across both point sets, avoiding the fragmentation that arises when clustering each set independently.
3. **Theoretical and empirical validation**: Analysis and experiments demonstrating that LapOT produces more meaningful region-to-region alignments and more stable clusterings than baselines.

## Connections to Other Work
This work extends the classical [[optimal transport]] framework, which is foundational in machine learning for comparing distributions. It relates to **graph-based regularization** in matching problems (e.g., spectral matching) and **structure-preserving alignment** in computer vision and geometric data analysis. The cluster-aware perspective also connects to recent work on semi-supervised matching and the study of how geometric structure should be preserved during alignment. The use of Laplacian regularization echoes techniques in [[graph neural networks]] and spectral clustering.

## Practical Applications
- **Medical imaging**: Aligning anatomical structures (e.g., organs) across patient scans, where region-level alignment is clinically more relevant than voxel-level precision.
- **Single-cell biology**: Matching cell populations from different samples or conditions, where cell types (clusters) are more stable than individual cell identity.
- **Shape matching and morphing**: Aligning 3D models or point cloud scans where preserving part structure (head, torso, limbs) matters more than exact vertex correspondence.
- **Multi-modal data fusion**: Aligning point clouds from different sensors or modalities where natural groupings reflect semantic categories.
- **Protein structure alignment**: Matching functional domains across protein conformations.

## Technical Limitations
- **Scalability**: Optimal transport computation grows cubically with problem size; the method's performance on very large point clouds (millions of points) is unclear.
- **Graph construction**: The method's success depends on building a good similarity graph; it is sensitive to hyperparameters (e.g., neighborhood size, similarity metric) and can fail if the true cluster structure is not well-captured by simple graphs.
- **Limited theoretical guarantees**: While the paper provides analysis, it doesn't characterize when LapOT provably recovers the "true" clustering or matching, especially under noise or misspecified cluster models.
- **Assumption of clear clusters**: The method assumes point clouds have well-separated cluster structure; performance degrades when clusters overlap or are hierarchical.
- **Computational cost of RSC**: Refining clusterings adds a second optimization step; the trade-off between accuracy and runtime is not fully characterized.

## Open Questions
- How does LapOT scale to industrial-size datasets (100K+ points), and what are practical approximations?
- Can the method be extended to handle hierarchical or soft cluster structure (probabilistic clustering)?
- How robust is the approach to mismatch between the true number of clusters and the graph-based estimate?
- Can LapOT be integrated into end-to-end learning pipelines (e.g., as a differentiable layer in neural networks)?
- How does performance degrade under noise, and what noise models have been tested?

## Key Takeaway
Laplacian regularization lets optimal transport respect cluster structure, enabling region-to-region alignment that is more stable and interpretable than point-to-point matching.
