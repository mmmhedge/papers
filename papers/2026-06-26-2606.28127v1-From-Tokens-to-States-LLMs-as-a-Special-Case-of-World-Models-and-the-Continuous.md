# From Tokens to States: LLMs as a Special Case of World Models and the Continuous Path Beyond

**Authors:** Paul Dubois
**Published:** 2026-06-26 | **arXiv:** [2606.28127v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28127v1)

## Summary
This paper reframes the apparent divide between LLMs (which predict tokens) and world models (which simulate reality) as a false dichotomy, arguing instead that LLMs are a degenerate special case of world models constrained to discrete token spaces. It maps a continuous spectrum of architectures between next-token prediction (NTP) and joint-embedding predictive architectures (JEPA), positioning current research like multi-token and latent-space prediction as intermediate stations on this path.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on two conceptual foundations. First, the notion of *world models* — systems that learn compressed internal representations of reality to predict future states — popularized by work in model-based RL and Yann LeCun's vision of AI requiring abandonment of autoregressive prediction. Second, the existing paradigm of LLMs as *sequence predictors* that maximize likelihood of the next token given prior tokens. The paper also implicitly relies on formal notions of state spaces and action spaces from control theory: a state is the current configuration of a system, and an action transitions it to a new state.

## Core Contributions
1. **Reframes LLMs as world models**: Proves formally that LLMs fit the world model framework where the state space is token sequences, the only action is token appending, and transitions are deterministic (Markovian). This makes LLMs a strict *special case*, not a competitor.

2. **Maps a continuous spectrum**: Identifies and categorizes existing research (multi-token prediction, future-summary prediction, next-latent prediction) as lying on a natural path from NTP to JEPA, rather than treating them as isolated techniques.

3. **Identifies two critical tradeoffs**: Shows that moving along the spectrum progressively surrenders two key practical advantages:
   - **Data**: Internet-scale self-supervised text vs. the "cliff" to action-labeled environments
   - **Architecture**: Transformer efficiency on discrete tokens vs. the open question of whether transformers generalize to continuous-state prediction

## Connections to Other Work
This paper directly engages with [[Yann LeCun]]'s 2022 critique that token prediction is insufficient for AGI and his proposal of latent-space, energy-based world models. It also sits in dialogue with:
- [[JEPA]] (Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture) as the endpoint of the spectrum
- Recent work in multi-token prediction (Grangier & Auli; others) and latent prediction
- Model-based RL traditions that treat state prediction as the core learning signal
- Mechanistic understanding of how transformers process information sequentially

The paper's core insight is that LeCun's critique isn't wrong, just unnecessarily binary — the spectrum view suggests a gentler path with intermediate trade-offs rather than a cliff.

## Practical Applications
1. **Hybrid model architectures**: Build systems that predict multiple tokens or summary states, rather than single tokens, to capture longer-horizon reasoning while retaining transformer efficiency.

2. **Domain-specific world models**: Use the framework to guide development of world models for robotics, autonomous vehicles, and game AI where action-labeled data is available but expensive.

3. **Scaling laws for continuous prediction**: Investigate whether transformer-like efficiency curves apply to latent-space prediction, informing compute-optimal allocation between discrete and continuous modalities.

4. **Transfer learning bridges**: Pretrain on internet text (discrete, abundant), then fine-tune on action-labeled data (sparse, task-specific) by progressively moving along the spectrum.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Continuous-state foundation models**: Build pretrained models on intermediate-spectrum architectures (e.g., future-summary prediction) and license them to robotics/simulation companies as an alternative to pure LLMs or pure RL.

2. **Data labeling infrastructure**: Solve the "data cliff" by building tools to efficiently generate and standardize action-labeled training data for world models (e.g., synthetic environments, human-annotated trajectories).

3. **Architecture toolkit**: Develop a modular library that lets researchers smoothly interpolate between NTP and JEPA, measuring trade-offs in sample efficiency, compute, and generalization — sell to AI research labs.

4. **Simulation-to-reality optimization**: Use the spectrum framework to design better sim2real transfer by explicitly optimizing which level of abstraction (tokens, summaries, latents) transfers best across domains.

5. **Robotics-as-a-service**: Offer cloud-based world models trained via the continuous spectrum on customer-specific environments and action spaces, enabling small companies to avoid training from scratch.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Open architectural question**: The paper identifies but doesn't solve whether transformers actually generalize to continuous-state prediction or if fundamentally new primitives are needed. This is crucial and unresolved.

2. **Data availability bottleneck**: The abundance of internet text has no clear analog in action-labeled data. The paper frames this as an open question but doesn't propose solutions — this remains a hard practical constraint.

3. **Unclear intermediate points**: While the paper maps a spectrum, it doesn't rigorously define what "multi-token" or "future-summary" prediction *formally* looks like or how to measure progress along the spectrum quantitatively.

4. **Loss of theoretical grounding**: Moving away from likelihood-based objectives (NTP) loses the well-understood information-theoretic grounding. The paper doesn't address what objective function or loss should replace it at intermediate points.

5. **Computational cost of continuous prediction**: The paper doesn't empirically compare computational efficiency of discrete vs. continuous prediction at scale — this gap could be fundamental, not just an engineering problem.

## Open Questions
1. **Transformer generalization**: Can the transformer architecture (or a close variant) efficiently handle continuous-state prediction, or do we need entirely new inductive biases?

2. **Intermediate objectives**: What loss functions or auxiliary tasks should intermediate-spectrum models use? Is supervised learning on action labels sufficient, or do we need implicit objectives?

3. **Data generation at scale**: How can we generate the action-labeled, instrumented data needed for world models at internet scale? Synthetic environments? Human-in-the-loop? Reinforcement learning?

4. **Measuring progress**: How do we quantify where a model sits on the spectrum? Is there a unified metric of "discreteness" or "compression"?

5. **Scaling laws**: Do scaling laws (compute → performance) differ across the spectrum? Is continuous prediction more or less sample-efficient than NTP?

6. **Emergent capabilities**: At what point along the spectrum do planning, reasoning, and world understanding emerge? Is this threshold sharp or gradual?

## Key Takeaway
LLMs aren't a dead-end detour from world models; they're the tractable end of a continuous spectrum, and the path forward isn't revolutionary replacement but evolutionary refinement of which constraints to relax and when.
