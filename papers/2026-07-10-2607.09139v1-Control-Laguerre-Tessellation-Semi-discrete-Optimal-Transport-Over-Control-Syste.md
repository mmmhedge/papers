# Control Laguerre Tessellation: Semi-discrete Optimal Transport Over Control Systems

**Authors:** Ripon C. Sarker, Abhishek Halder
**Published:** 2026-07-10 | **arXiv:** [2607.09139v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09139v1)

## Summary
This paper extends optimal transport theory — the mathematical framework for efficiently moving probability mass from one distribution to another — to systems where agents move under control constraints (like robots or vehicles with limited acceleration). The key insight is that when transportation costs reflect the optimal control cost of agent motion, the solution has a clean geometric structure called a Laguerre tessellation (a partition of space based on distances to target points). The paper demonstrates this for linear systems with energy and time objectives, bridging control theory and optimal transport.

## Theoretical Background
Optimal transport, formalized by Monge and Kantorovich, asks: given a source distribution and target distribution, what is the cheapest way to "move" the source mass to match the target? The cost depends on a "ground cost" function — typically Euclidean distance, but here it is replaced by the optimal control cost of moving an agent from one state to another.

The paper builds on the classical result that when the ground cost satisfies the "twist condition" (a technical requirement roughly meaning costs increase smoothly and unambiguously with direction), the optimal transport map decomposes into regions, each associated with a target point. These regions form a Laguerre tessellation — a generalization of Voronoi diagrams where distances are weighted.

Control theory studies how to steer dynamical systems (agents with differential equations describing their motion) optimally. The paper connects this to transport: instead of Euclidean distance, the "cost" of moving from state A to state B is the minimum fuel (energy) or time needed for an optimally controlled agent to reach B from A.

## Core Contributions
1. **Control Laguerre Tessellation (CLT)**: A novel geometric structure that characterizes optimal transport when ground costs are induced by optimal control — extending classical Laguerre tessellation to control-constrained settings.
2. **Twist condition verification**: Shows that for linear controlled systems (minimum energy and minimum time objectives), the twist condition holds, guaranteeing CLT structure in the optimal solution.
3. **Explicit characterization**: Provides an almost-everywhere description of the optimal transport map via geometric partitioning, avoiding the need to solve the full transport problem numerically in some cases.
4. **Semi-discrete setting**: Addresses the practical case where the source is a continuous distribution (e.g., agents spread over a region) but targets are discrete (e.g., specific goal locations).

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of three communities:
- **Optimal transport theory**: Extends classical Monge–Kantorovich theory beyond standard Euclidean metrics. Related to [[Wasserstein distances]] and applications in machine learning.
- **Control theory**: Connects to optimal control problems (LQR — linear quadratic regulator, minimum time problems) by using control costs as transport metrics.
- **Multi-agent coordination**: The semi-discrete setting (many agents to discrete targets) is fundamental in swarm robotics and distributed systems, though here the focus is on the theoretical structure rather than decentralized algorithms.

The twist condition is classical in optimal transport; the novelty is proving it holds for non-Euclidean, control-induced metrics.

## Practical Applications
1. **Swarm robotics and drone coordination**: Deploying a swarm of agents (drones, robots) from a continuous initial region to discrete goal positions (landing pads, charging stations) with minimum energy or time — the CLT provides the optimal assignment structure.
2. **Autonomous vehicle fleet routing**: Repositioning vehicles from a dispersed geographic area to discrete depots or service stations, where fuel cost is the transport metric.
3. **Mobile sensor network deployment**: Moving a distributed set of mobile sensors to specific target locations in an environment where motion is energy-constrained.
4. **Warehouse automation**: Coordinating multiple robots or automated vehicles to transport goods from a continuous storage region to discrete checkout or shipping locations under energy budgets.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Swarm coordination software for drone logistics**: A SaaS platform that computes optimal CLT-based assignments for drone swarms, automatically generating routes that minimize fuel use or delivery time when moving packages from a warehouse to dispersed delivery zones.
2. **Autonomous fleet optimization engine**: A service for robotics or autonomous vehicle companies that uses CLT to optimize fleet repositioning (e.g., balance bikes/scooters across a city) given real vehicle dynamics and energy constraints.
3. **Supply chain routing solver**: A tool for logistics companies that extends standard routing software to handle constrained agent motion (energy, time, dynamics) and computes provably optimal multi-agent assignments.
4. **Robot task allocation system**: Middleware for multi-robot systems (warehouse, manufacturing) that automatically partitions tasks across robots using CLT, respecting their dynamics and energy limits.
5. **Research tool/library**: An open-source computational package implementing CLT for researchers in control and optimal transport, lowering barriers to application in robotics and distributed systems.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Compactly supported source**: Requires the source distribution to have bounded support — the method may not apply cleanly if agents start from unbounded regions.
2. **Absolutely continuous measures**: The theory assumes smooth probability densities; singular distributions (e.g., point masses mixed with continuous parts) require separate treatment.
3. **Twist condition verification**: While proven for linear systems with quadratic and time costs, extending to nonlinear dynamics requires new proofs; no general algorithm for arbitrary control systems.
4. **Discrete targets only**: The semi-discrete setting (continuous source, discrete targets) is practical but not fully general; the continuous–continuous case is not addressed.
5. **No decentralization**: The solution is centralized and global; implementing it in a decentralized, asynchronous multi-agent setting requires additional algorithmic work.
6. **Computational tractability**: For high-dimensional state spaces, computing the tessellation may become intractable; scalability to 10+ dimensions is unclear.

## Open Questions
1. **Nonlinear dynamics**: Can the CLT framework and twist condition verification be extended to nonlinear control systems, and under what conditions?
2. **Fully continuous case**: What is the structure of optimal transport in the continuous–continuous setting (continuous source and continuous target)?
3. **Decentralized algorithms**: Can agents compute their assigned regions and targets in a distributed, communication-efficient manner without central coordination?
4. **Robustness**: How does the optimal transport map degrade if agent dynamics are uncertain or if the twist condition is nearly violated?
5. **Scalability**: What are efficient algorithms for computing CLT partitions in high-dimensional spaces?
6. **Hybrid dynamics**: Can the framework handle agents with mixed discrete-continuous dynamics (e.g., mode switches)?

## Key Takeaway
Optimal transport maps for continuously distributed agents moving under control constraints have a clean geometric structure — a control-theoretic generalization of Laguerre tessellation — that can be computed explicitly when the control cost satisfies the twist condition.
