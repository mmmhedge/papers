# Screening of Biosecurity Features in Metagenomic Data with Evo 2 Probes

**Authors:** Jeremy Guntoro, Alexander Dack, Dylan Danno, Michaela Jančovičová, Križan Jurinović et al.
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.14070v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14070v1)

## Summary
This paper investigates whether genomic foundation models (Evo 2) learn biosecurity-relevant patterns that can be detected with simple linear probes, without retraining the model itself. The researchers show that these frozen representations encode strong signals for antimicrobial resistance detection (ROC-AUC 0.977 with attention probes) and weaker signals for bacterial virulence, suggesting foundation model embeddings could serve as a fast, cheap screening layer for pathogenic sequences in metagenomic data.

## Theoretical Background
Foundation models — neural networks trained on massive unlabeled datasets to learn general patterns — have recently been adapted for genomic sequences (e.g., Evo, ESM). The core insight here relies on the observation that if a frozen model's internal representations contain rich information, you can detect that information using lightweight "probes" — minimal supervised classifiers trained on top of the model's activations. This builds on mechanistic interpretability work showing that structure learned by large models is often linearly decodable. The paper uses two probe types: linear probes (single matrix multiplication) and single-head attention probes (slightly more expressive but still minimal). Metagenomic data is mixed DNA/RNA from environmental samples containing many bacterial species.

## Core Contributions
1. **Demonstrates AMR detectability in Evo 2 embeddings** — the first systematic evaluation of biosecurity signal in a genomic foundation model, achieving 0.888–0.977 ROC-AUC depending on probe type and aggregation strategy.
2. **Shows fine-grained discrimination** — probes separate AMR drug-class subcategories and distinguish AMR genes from other functional genes, ruling out that the signal is just generic "functional gene" detection.
3. **Establishes robustness to fragmented input** — probes work on simulated short reads (read-level ROC-AUC 0.898) without retraining, enabling pre-assembly screening where assembly is slow or unreliable.
4. **Maps current limits** — virulence is weakly decodable (0.833), and Evo 1.5-generated sequences don't clearly preserve AMR labels, indicating the approach has meaningful constraints.
5. **Contrasts sparse autoencoders with supervised probes** — finds supervised approaches more consistent, even though sparse autoencoders recover interpretable resistance features.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of [[mechanistic-interpretability]] (decoding information from frozen model activations) and genomic [[deep-learning]]. It extends recent work applying [[transformers]] to biology (ESM, Evo) by asking what biosecurity information these models capture — a novel angle. The probing methodology is standard in NLP interpretability but novel in genomics. The sparse autoencoder analysis relates to recent efforts in mechanistic interpretability to find interpretable latent features, though the paper finds supervised probes more reliable. It complements work on generative models for synthetic biology (SynGenome) by showing that generated sequences may not preserve semantic properties.

## Practical Applications
1. **Metagenomic biosurveillance** — rapid screening of environmental samples (wastewater, air filtration, soil) for AMR genes without expensive assembly pipelines.
2. **High-throughput sequence screening** — pre-filtering massive genomic datasets (e.g., from metagenomics projects) to flag high-risk regions before detailed downstream analysis.
3. **Synthetic biology safety checks** — screening generated or engineered sequences for unintended biosecurity hazards before synthesis.
4. **Public health monitoring** — integrating probes into routine genomic surveillance pipelines to detect emerging AMR threats in real-time.
5. **Laboratory automation** — incorporating probes as a computational checkpoint in automated sample-processing workflows to triage samples for manual review.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **MetaGuard** — A SaaS platform providing fast, lightweight AMR screening for clinical microbiology labs and wastewater utilities; integrates Evo 2 probes into a simple API that accepts FASTQ files and returns risk scores without requiring local compute or assembly.
2. **SynBio SafetyCheck** — B2B tool for synthetic biology design platforms (Ginkgo, Zymergen, etc.) that screens DNA designs for biosecurity risks before synthesis orders, using frozen probes as a first-pass filter.
3. **Pathogen Early Warning System** — Subscription service for public health agencies that continuously screens metagenomics data from environmental monitoring networks, with alerts for novel AMR patterns.
4. **EmbedHealth Analytics** — Consulting firm selling custom genomic foundation model probes (Evo 2 and successors) to pharmaceutical companies and biotech firms for rapid strain characterization and risk stratification.
5. **BioDeploy** — Open-source toolkit + commercial support offering lightweight, deployable probes for edge computing on sequencers and in-field devices, enabling real-time screening in remote or low-resource settings.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Limited to linearly accessible signal** — The approach only detects patterns that are straightforward to extract from frozen embeddings; more complex, non-linear biosecurity features may be invisible to lightweight probes.
2. **AMR-focused validation** — Strong results for AMR, but virulence is weakly decodable (0.833 ROC-AUC), and other biosecurity hazards (toxins, gain-of-function markers) are not evaluated.
3. **Generalization uncertain** — Evaluation is on held-out *metagenomic* test sets; generalization to novel organisms, engineered sequences, or out-of-distribution genera is not addressed.
4. **Dependence on model quality** — The approach is only as good as the underlying Evo 2 model; if the foundation model doesn't learn biosecurity-relevant patterns, probes won't recover them.
5. **Short-read degradation not fully characterized** — Read-level performance is promising, but real sequencing error modes (substitutions, indels, low-quality bases) are simulated, not empirically tested.
6. **Interpretability via sparse autoencoders is inconsistent** — The paper notes sparse autoencoders recover interpretable features but are "less consistent" than supervised probes, leaving mechanistic understanding incomplete.
7. **No false-positive/false-negative cost analysis** — The paper reports ROC-AUC but doesn't calibrate thresholds for operational settings where false positives (flag safe sequences) or false negatives (miss hazards) have asymmetric costs.

## Open Questions
1. **Can probes generalize to organisms outside training?** — How does performance degrade on novel bacterial families or engineered sequences far from natural metagenomic data?
2. **What makes virulence weaker than AMR?** — Is it biological (virulence is more heterogeneous or context-dependent), or does Evo 2 simply learn it less well? Can architectural changes (e.g., attention over multiple heads) improve virulence detection?
3. **Are there biosecurity hazards Evo 2 doesn't learn at all?** — Evaluate toxins, CRISPR guides, gain-of-function markers, and other biosecurity-relevant features not addressed here.
4. **How do probes behave on real sequencing errors?** — Test on actual low-quality reads and degraded ancient DNA, not just simulated reads.
5. **Can sparse autoencoders be made reliable?** — The paper dismisses them as inconsistent; is that a fundamental issue or a tuning problem? Can they be made reliable enough for interpretable detection?
6. **Do fine-tuned probes on smaller datasets beat frozen probes?** — What is the trade-off between computational cost and accuracy if you allow small amounts of supervised retraining on domain-specific data?
7. **How do successor models (Evo 3, ESM 3) perform?** — Are the findings stable across model versions, or are they Evo 2–specific?

## Key Takeaway
Frozen genomic foundation models encode detectable antimicrobial-resistance signals accessible via lightweight probes, enabling fast biosurveillance screening.
