# Poller: Are LLMs Suitable for Evaluating the Poetry Understanding Task?

**Authors:** Shanshan Wang, Derek F. Wong, Jingming Yao, Lidia S. Chao
**Published:** 2026-06-29 | **arXiv:** [2606.30556v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30556v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes Poller, a method for using large language models to evaluate poetry understanding by having LLMs adopt the perspective of a poem's author. The approach significantly reduces evaluation error compared to baseline LLM methods (up to 94.55% error reduction on some dimensions) while achieving results comparable to human evaluation, addressing the gap between cheap automated evaluation and expensive human expertise for literary analysis tasks.

## Theoretical Background
Automatic evaluation of natural language has long relied on surface-level metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR) that measure n-gram overlap with reference texts. However, poetry — particularly classical Chinese poetry — resists such quantitative methods because meaning is heavily dependent on implicit cultural knowledge, symbolism, and aesthetic judgment. Large language models (LLMs — neural networks trained on massive text corpora that can generate and understand natural language) have shown surprising capability at complex reasoning tasks, yet standard prompting approaches for evaluation often fail to capture the nuanced interpretive dimensions that human readers apply. The core insight here is that perspective-taking — instructing an LLM to role-play as the original author with biographical and contextual details — can anchor evaluations in deeper semantic understanding rather than surface pattern matching.

## Core Contributions
1. **Role-based prompting framework**: The key innovation is having LLMs evaluate poetry by adopting the author's perspective with detailed biographical and historical context, rather than evaluating from a generic external stance. This is distinct from standard evaluation prompting.
2. **Multi-dimensional evaluation**: Poller evaluates across eight specialized poetic dimensions (including rhetorical techniques and defamiliarization), not just single-metric scoring.
3. **Quantified error reduction**: Demonstrates that this approach achieves 89–94% error reduction on specific dimensions compared to baseline LLM evaluation methods, with performance approaching human consensus.
4. **Empirical validation**: Tests across multiple LLM architectures to show the method generalizes.

## Practical Applications
- **Literary education platforms**: Automated feedback systems for students analyzing poetry in classical Chinese literature courses, providing dimension-specific critique that mimics expert instruction.
- **Poetry translation evaluation**: Assessing how well poetry translations preserve aesthetic and thematic dimensions by having LLMs judge fidelity from the original author's perspective.
- **Digital humanities research**: Large-scale computational analysis of poetry corpora where human expert evaluation is infeasible, enabling statistical studies of stylistic trends across dynasties or schools.
- **Content moderation and cultural sensitivity**: Flagging culturally inappropriate interpretations of classical works in online literature communities.
- **Academic publishing workflows**: Pre-review screening of poetry analysis submissions by journals to reduce editorial burden.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **PoetryAI Tutor**: A subscription platform offering real-time, multi-dimensional feedback on poetry analysis essays for students and enthusiasts, with dimension-specific guidance (e.g., "Your reading of the metaphor is strong, but misses the historical context the author likely intended").
2. **Translation Quality Assurance Tool**: B2B SaaS for poetry publishers and translation agencies to validate translation quality across semantic, stylistic, and cultural dimensions before publication.
3. **Literary Analysis for Creators**: A tool for modern poets and writers to receive structured feedback on their work by comparing it against historical greats in the same genre — "your use of defamiliarization ranks in the 75th percentile against Song Dynasty poets."
4. **Cultural Heritage Platform**: A digital archive system (for museums, libraries) that automatically annotates classical poetry collections with expert-level interpretive essays, generated using Poller-style evaluation to ensure cultural authenticity.
5. **Licensing API for Publishing**: An evaluation API that academic publishers, online reading platforms, and educational software can integrate to automate peer review feedback on literary submissions.

## Technical Limitations
- **Author context dependency**: The method requires reliable biographical and historical information about the poet; it will degrade if this context is unavailable, inaccurate, or sparse. This limits applicability to lesser-known or anonymous works.
- **Cultural & language specificity**: The paper focuses on classical Chinese poetry; it is unclear whether the approach generalizes to other literary traditions, languages, or poetry forms (e.g., European sonnets, free verse).
- **LLM-human alignment assumption**: The assumption that role-playing as the author will produce evaluations closer to human judgment is heuristic and unproven for all dimensions and all poems. Some evaluations may simply reflect LLM statistical regularities rather than "author intent."
- **Scalability to new dimensions**: The eight dimensions are fixed; extending to novel evaluative criteria may require retraining or extensive prompt engineering.
- **Circular grounding**: The reference human evaluations used to validate Poller were themselves subjective; the true "ground truth" is philosophically unclear in literary criticism.
- **Computational cost**: LLM inference for every evaluation instance is more expensive than static metrics, though cheaper than human expert time.

## Open Questions
1. **Generalization across poetry types**: Does Poller work equally well for modern poetry, vernacular poetry, free verse, or only classical structured forms? How does performance degrade as temporal or cultural distance from training data increases?
2. **Comparison to other perspective-taking methods**: Are there alternative role-based prompts (e.g., "evaluate as a contemporary scholar," "as a student of the author") that outperform the author-perspective framing? How sensitive is the method to prompt wording?
3. **Interpretability of dimension scores**: What linguistic features does the LLM actually attend to when scoring each dimension? Are high scores on "rhetorical technique" driven by shallow pattern matching or genuine understanding?
4. **Cross-language transfer**: Can a single multilingual LLM evaluate poetry in multiple languages using the same framework, or does cultural knowledge require language-specific models?
5. **Human-LLM disagreement analysis**: When Poller and humans disagree, what kinds of poems or dimensions cause the largest gaps? Can we characterize systematic biases?
6. **Few-shot vs. zero-shot**: How much does performance improve if the LLM is shown 1–5 exemplar author-perspective evaluations before scoring a new poem?

## Key Takeaway
By instructing LLMs to evaluate poetry from the author's perspective using biographical context, rather than from a generic stance, this paper shows that automated evaluation can achieve human-level performance on subjective literary dimensions — proving that prompting strategy can dramatically improve LLM alignment with human expertise in nuanced domains.
