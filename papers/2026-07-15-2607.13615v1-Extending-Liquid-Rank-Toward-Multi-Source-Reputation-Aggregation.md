# Extending Liquid Rank Toward Multi-Source Reputation Aggregation

**Authors:** Nejc Znidar, Anton Kolonin
**Published:** 2026-07-15 | **arXiv:** [2607.13615v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13615v1)

## Summary
This paper extends liquid rank—a reputation system that allows influence to flow dynamically through networks—to handle multiple reputation sources at once. Instead of relying on a single reputation signal, the framework lets different types of reputation (from different platforms, communities, or contexts) be combined into one unified score, with adjustable weights for each source. This matters because real governance systems (DAOs, online communities, delegated authority structures) often need to balance reputation from many channels, and this work provides the technical machinery to do so fairly and flexibly.

## Theoretical Background
Liquid rank systems build on the concept of delegable voting and reputation flows. The core idea is that agents don't just have a fixed influence score; instead, their influence can be delegated transitively through networks, allowing reputation to "flow" to those who participate most constructively. Prior work in reputation systems and governance has typically focused on single, monolithic sources of reputation. This paper assumes familiarity with liquid democracy (a voting model allowing both direct voting and delegation), transitive trust propagation, and multi-source data fusion—the problem of combining signals from different sensors or feedback channels into a coherent estimate.

## Core Contributions
- **Multi-source aggregation framework**: Explicit mathematical formulation for blending heterogeneous reputation signals (e.g., forum karma, token-weighted votes, contribution metrics) into a single liquid rank score.
- **Configurable weighting scheme**: Introduction of fine-grained control mechanisms (weights, blending functions) that let designers specify how much each reputation source should contribute.
- **Socio-technical flexibility**: A generalizable approach that works with both human participants and machine agents, addressing hybrid governance systems.
- **Extensibility**: The extension maintains compatibility with existing liquid rank foundations while adding modularity for new reputation sources.

## Practical Applications
- **DAO governance**: Combine on-chain voting power with off-chain community reputation (Discord activity, GitHub contributions, forum presence) to determine proposal voting weight.
- **Federated identity & trust**: Aggregate reputation across multiple platforms (LinkedIn, GitHub, academic databases) for hiring or partnership decisions without centralizing data.
- **Community moderation**: Blend user tenure, post quality signals, and external expertise signals to assign moderation privileges fairly.
- **Peer review systems**: Combine reviewer historical accuracy, domain expertise badges, and collaborative filtering scores into a single reviewer reliability metric.
- **Decentralized social networks**: Allow users to carry reputation across interconnected communities with different contribution metrics (follows, likes, micropayments).

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Multi-Platform Reputation Aggregator**: A SaaS product that ingests reputation data from Discord, GitHub, Discourse, Telegram, and on-chain sources, outputting unified identity credentials usable across dApps and communities.
2. **DAO Governance-as-a-Service**: Plug-and-play reputation weighting engine for DAOs, allowing on-chain + off-chain signal mixing with A/B testing of governance weights.
3. **Enterprise Delegation Platform**: Internal tool for large organizations to blend manager endorsements, peer feedback, and objective metrics (code review count, project delivery) into leadership reputation scores for succession planning.
4. **Credibility Marketplace**: A protocol layer where reputation providers (platforms, auditors, credential issuers) publish schemas and weights, and applications subscribe to trusted reputation blends for their use case.
5. **Web3 Hiring Network**: Aggregate a candidate's GitHub commits, educational credentials, DAO contributions, and skill assessments into a single verifiable reputation portfolio for hiring and freelance matching.

## Technical Limitations
- **Source heterogeneity**: Reputation from different domains (votes, time, transactions, qualitative assessments) may use incommensurable scales; naive aggregation can hide or amplify disparities.
- **Weight selection problem**: The paper proposes mechanisms for weighting but does not deeply address how stakeholders should *choose* those weights without creating gaming incentives or inequity.
- **Sybil attacks**: Extending to multiple sources may increase surface area for Sybil resistance (creating fake identities to game scores across platforms).
- **Temporal stability**: Reputation from one source may evolve at different rates than another; synchronized blending may be unstable.
- **Interpretability**: As sources accumulate, the resulting score becomes less transparent—users may not understand why their reputation changed.
- **Cold start**: New agents with minimal history across all sources may be systematically disadvantaged if sources are not carefully weighted.

## Open Questions
- How should weights be set dynamically to prevent manipulation while maintaining fairness across diverse participant types?
- What happens when reputation sources conflict or give contradictory signals about the same agent?
- Can the framework prove Sybil resistance or Byzantine robustness guarantees when multiple sources are aggregated?
- How should the framework handle reputation decay or seasonal patterns that vary across sources?
- Can stakeholders verify the integrity of reputation sources and their weights through cryptographic or transparency mechanisms?
- Does multi-source aggregation improve governance outcomes (e.g., proposal quality, participation, fairness) empirically, and under what conditions?

## Key Takeaway
Liquid rank can now blend multiple reputation sources into one score, enabling fairer, context-aware governance in hybrid human-machine systems.
