# Heat-Kernel Entropy Profiles and Geometric Effective Sample Size for Weighted Measures on Manifolds

**Authors:** Kisung You
**Published:** 2026-07-07 | **arXiv:** [2607.06696v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06696v1)

## Summary
This paper introduces heat-kernel entropy profiles, a new way to measure the quality and geometry-awareness of weighted particle samples on curved spaces (manifolds). Unlike existing methods that only count effective particles, this approach understands the underlying geometry—it detects when particles cluster, duplicate, or spread unevenly across the curved surface. The work matters because weighted samples appear everywhere in machine learning: importance sampling, neural network approximations, and Bayesian inference all rely on good particle representations.

## Theoretical Background
The paper builds on several classical ideas. **Effective sample size (ESS)** is a standard concept in importance sampling and Monte Carlo methods—it tells you how many "real" independent samples your weighted particles are worth. **Rényi entropy** is an information-theoretic measure of diversity that generalizes Shannon entropy. **Heat kernels** are mathematical objects from differential geometry that diffuse information across a manifold (curved space) according to intrinsic geometry; they are fundamental to spectral methods and provide a multiscale perspective. The paper specifically uses order-two Rényi entropy with heat kernels to create a profile—a function across scales—rather than a single scalar summary. This connects to **spectral decomposition on manifolds**, where functions decompose into eigenbases like spherical harmonics on spheres.

## Core Contributions
1. **Heat-kernel entropy profiles**: A multiscale summary that tracks nonuniformity of weighted measures by diffusing particles via intrinsic heat flow, revealing structure at different scales.
2. **Geometric effective sample size**: A geometry-aware variant of ESS that penalizes nearby or duplicate particles while recovering standard ESS for well-separated ones—computable from pairwise heat-kernel overlaps.
3. **Theoretical guarantees**: Proofs of monotonicity, asymptotic behavior at small/large scales, consistency for deterministic weights, and a self-normalized importance-sampling extension on manifolds without boundary.
4. **Spherical interpretation**: On spheres, the profile decomposes into spherical-harmonic energies that recover classical summaries (mean direction, von Mises-Fisher, Bingham distributions).
5. **Empirical validation**: Experiments on spheres showing the profile detects antipodal clustering, girdle distributions, multimodality, and duplicate particles—structures invisible to weight-only methods.

## Practical Applications
1. **Importance sampling diagnostics**: Practitioners can now assess whether their weighted samples have good coverage in complex sample spaces (e.g., Bayesian posteriors on manifolds), not just average weight.
2. **Variational inference quality**: In neural networks trained with importance weighting or mixture models, the profile reveals if learned particle approximations have structural issues (clustering, antipodal spread).
3. **Particle filter diagnostics**: In sequential Monte Carlo and particle filters operating on manifold state spaces (e.g., directional data, pose estimation), detect degeneracy and multimodality early.
4. **Quadrature and cubature**: Evaluate and design integration schemes on manifolds by understanding how sample geometry affects approximation quality.
5. **Representation learning**: Diagnose whether learned embeddings of samples (as weighted points on a manifold) have desired geometric properties.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Computational cost**: Computing pairwise heat-kernel overlaps scales quadratically in the number of particles; for large N (>10K) this becomes expensive without approximation.
2. **Heat-kernel choice**: Results depend on the choice of manifold metric and diffusion time; no automatic principled selection scheme is provided.
3. **Manifold assumption**: Requires knowing or learning the manifold structure a priori; real data embeddings may not lie on a true manifold, invalidating theoretical guarantees.
4. **Boundary handling**: Proofs assume compact manifolds *without boundary*; many practical spaces (e.g., bounded domains, truncated spaces) have boundaries.
5. **High dimensions**: Heat kernels become harder to compute and interpret in very high dimensions; the method is most practical for low- to moderate-dimensional manifolds.
6. **Weight interpretation**: The profile is sensitive to absolute weight magnitudes; normalization conventions and numerical stability with very small/large weights need care.

## Open Questions
1. How do you efficiently approximate pairwise heat kernels for large N (e.g., via low-rank or local kernels)?
2. Can you learn or adaptively select the heat-kernel bandwidth and diffusion time scales from data?
3. How does the profile behave on manifolds with boundary, and can the theory be extended?
4. Can you use profiles to design better adaptive importance-sampling schemes in high dimensions?
5. What happens when the data only approximately lies on a manifold, and how robust are the diagnostics?
6. Can profiles be generalized beyond Rényi order-two entropy, and what trade-offs exist?

## Key Takeaway
Heat-kernel entropy profiles enable geometry-aware quality assessment of weighted particle approximations by detecting sample structure (clustering, multimodality, duplicates) that scalar effective sample size misses.
