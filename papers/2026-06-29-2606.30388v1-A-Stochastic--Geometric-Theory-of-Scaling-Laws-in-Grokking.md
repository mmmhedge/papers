# A Stochastic--Geometric Theory of Scaling Laws in Grokking

**Authors:** Róisín Luo, Christian Gagné, Jonas Ngnawé, Ihsan Ullah, Karyn Morrissey
**Published:** 2026-06-29 | **arXiv:** [2606.30388v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30388v1)

## Summary
This paper provides the first rigorous theoretical explanation for grokking — the phenomenon where neural networks suddenly generalize after long periods of overfitting — by characterizing the geometry of solution spaces under Adam optimization with weight decay. The authors prove that the reachable solution space has a shell-core topological structure (memorization solutions surrounded by generalization solutions), and they derive precise scaling laws predicting when the transition from memorization to generalization occurs.

## Theoretical Background
Grokking is an empirical mystery: networks fit training data quickly but delay generalizing for thousands of additional steps, then abruptly "get it." Prior work has observed this in modular arithmetic and other synthetic tasks but lacked mechanistic explanation. The paper builds on:

- **Implicit bias of gradient descent**: the tendency of optimization algorithms to find solutions with specific geometric properties even without explicit constraints
- **Adam optimizer with weight decay**: a regularizer that shrinks parameters toward zero, inducing a L2 penalty in the loss
- **Stopping-time theory** (from probability): a formal framework for analyzing when trajectories first hit a boundary or threshold
- **Neural network loss landscapes**: understanding how solutions cluster in parameter space (e.g., high-loss memorization vs. low-loss generalization solutions)

The key insight is that weight decay doesn't just prevent overfitting—it geometrically structures the solution space into concentric shells, creating a natural "escape path" from memorization to generalization.

## Core Contributions
1. **Shell-core topological characterization**: Proves that under Adam + weight decay, random initializations concentrate on an outer spherical shell, memorization solutions form an intermediate shell, and generalization solutions occupy the core. This is not assumed but derived from optimization dynamics.

2. **Quantitative scaling laws**: Derives explicit formulas relating grokking delay to hyperparameters:
   - How learning rate affects transition timing
   - How batch size scales the onset of generalization
   - How L2 regularization strength controls the topology
   
3. **Stopping-time analysis**: Uses first-passage time theory to formally analyze when optimization trajectories escape the memorization manifold and reach the generalization manifold boundary, connecting this to observed grokking delays.

4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates that predicted scaling laws match both synthetic tasks (modular arithmetic) and findings from prior grokking literature.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper directly addresses questions raised by [[Double Descent]] and [[Loss Landscapes of Neural Networks]] literature—why do some solutions generalize and others memorize? It complements [[Neural Collapse]] work (the tendency of features to form geometric structures) by showing optimization itself encodes geometry. The stopping-time methodology parallels work in [[Implicit Bias]] but applies it to delayed generalization. Related to [[Edge of Chaos]] phenomena in deep learning, where phase transitions reveal underlying structure. Differs from [[RLHF]] and [[Mechanistic Interpretability]] in LLMs by focusing on fundamental optimization geometry rather than behavioral alignment or circuit analysis.

## Practical Applications
1. **Hyperparameter selection for training stability**: Practitioners can use the derived scaling laws to predict safe learning rates and regularization values that avoid unexpected generalization delays in new domains.

2. **Early stopping and checkpoint selection**: Understanding when grokking occurs helps decide whether to continue training or conclude a model has failed, reducing wasted computation.

3. **Transfer learning initialization**: Since the paper characterizes where solutions live in parameter space, it could inform better initialization strategies for fine-tuning pre-trained models.

4. **Synthetic data curriculum design**: For tasks where grokking is problematic, the theory suggests how to adjust batch size or regularization to accelerate generalization.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **AutoML regularization tuner**: A tool that automatically adjusts learning rate, batch size, and weight decay based on grokking onset predictions, reducing manual hyperparameter search for companies training custom models.

2. **Neural network "health monitor"**: SaaS platform that watches training curves, predicts grokking delays using these scaling laws, and alerts engineers whether to continue or restart training—useful for companies with expensive GPU budgets.

3. **Synthetic task benchmark suite**: Commercialize a library of modular arithmetic and related tasks calibrated to reveal grokking behavior, sold to research teams and ML education platforms.

4. **ML training cost optimizer**: Enterprise tool that predicts total wall-clock time to generalization for a given model/dataset/hyperparameter combination, helping allocate cloud compute budgets more accurately.

5. **Academic textbook/course materials**: Structured educational content around grokking and optimization geometry, marketed to universities and online learning platforms (Coursera, Udacity).

## Technical Limitations
1. **Limited to synthetic tasks**: Theory is validated on modular arithmetic and similar symbolic tasks. Real-world generalization (vision, NLP) may not follow the same shell-core structure because data is not i.i.d. or because network capacity is not clearly divisible into memorization vs. generalization modes.

2. **Adam + L2 regularization specific**: Assumes weight decay is the primary mechanism. Modern techniques like [[Batch Normalization]] or other regularizers may disrupt the assumed topology.

3. **Infinite-width approximation not explicit**: The derivation likely relies on implicit smoothness or overparameterization assumptions not fully stated; finite-width effects could distort the predicted scaling laws.

4. **No phase transitions beyond grokking**: Does not address why memorization/generalization are the only two modes, or predict other phase transitions (e.g., feature learning vs. kernel regimes).

5. **Hyperparameter coupling**: The theory treats learning rate, batch size, and regularization somewhat independently; their interactions may be more complex in practice.

## Open Questions
1. **Does the shell-core structure hold for vision and language tasks?** Most experiments are on toy problems; generalization to realistic domains is unclear.

2. **Can we extend the theory to other optimizers** (SGD, AdaGrad, adaptive methods beyond Adam)?

3. **What happens with adaptive regularization** (e.g., L1, elastic net, data-dependent penalties)? Do they destroy the topological structure?

4. **Can stopping-time analysis predict *other* phase transitions** in neural network training, beyond grokking? (E.g., feature learning onset, double descent.)

5. **Is the shell-core structure deterministic or probabilistic?** The paper shows solutions concentrate there empirically—but is this a law of optimization or a statistical tendency that can fail?

6. **How does network width affect the theory?** Does the topology persist in under-parameterized regimes?

## Key Takeaway
Grokking is a direct consequence of Adam's weight decay inducing a shell-core geometry in solution space, and this geometry yields precise, testable scaling laws for when generalization suddenly emerges.
