# Simulation Based Reward Function Validation for Multi-Agent On Orbit Inspection

**Authors:** Patrick Quinn, Bala Prenith Reddy Gopu, George M. Nehma, Madhur Tiwari
**Published:** 2026-07-01 | **arXiv:** [2607.01367v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01367v1)

## Summary
This paper develops a better way to train groups of spacecraft to inspect objects in orbit using multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL). Instead of telling agents to visit fixed inspection points, the authors create a generalized reward function that evaluates the quality of images collected from any location, letting the agents autonomously decide where to position themselves and when to take pictures. The work matters because it moves MARL inspection from rigid, pre-planned tasks toward flexible, adaptive missions that could handle real-world orbital scenarios.

## Theoretical Background
Multi-agent reinforcement learning extends standard reinforcement learning — where a single agent learns to maximize rewards through trial and error — to scenarios with multiple independent agents learning simultaneously. Previous work on spacecraft inspection used MARL but relied on reward functions tied to visiting specific waypoints around a target object. This paper builds on the insight that inspection quality depends on image coverage and visibility, not just position. The authors ground their approach in 3D reconstruction principles: to validate whether images capture sufficient detail of an inspected object, you can reconstruct its 3D geometry and measure how well the collected images cover it. This connects to computer vision and photogrammetry concepts where multiple viewpoints enable accurate 3D scene understanding.

## Core Contributions
1. **Generalized reward function** — A reward formulation that evaluates image quality from arbitrary camera locations and orientations, not just predetermined inspection points. This enables agents to autonomously plan view trajectories.
2. **Agent control over timing** — Agents can decide *when* to collect images, not just where to go, making the task more realistic and flexible.
3. **Validation methodology** — Using 3D reconstructions of inspected objects as ground truth to assess whether collected images actually enable high-quality scene recovery. This ties reward feedback directly to mission success metrics.
4. **Best practices** — Empirical insights into reward design and multi-agent coordination for inspection, applicable beyond MARL to classical planning approaches.

## Connections to Other Work
This builds on prior MARL work in spacecraft control and planning. It addresses a limitation in earlier inspection approaches that used fixed waypoint inspection — essentially treating the problem as "visit these N points" rather than "gather sufficient visual information." The generalized reward design echoes principles in [[Active Vision]] and [[View Planning]] literature (classical robotics fields), adapting them to the multi-agent, learning-based setting. The 3D reconstruction validation step connects to [[Structure from Motion]] and scene understanding in [[Computer Vision]], grounding the reward in something physically meaningful. Related to broader questions in [[Reward Shaping]] and [[Inverse Reinforcement Learning]] — how do you define what "good" means in complex, open-ended tasks?

## Practical Applications
1. **On-orbit satellite servicing** — Autonomous inspection of damaged or malfunctioning satellites to assess repair needs before human crews or robotic arms intervene.
2. **Space debris characterization** — Groups of small inspector spacecraft autonomously gathering visual data on debris for collision risk assessment and potential removal planning.
3. **Large structure inspection** — Inspecting space stations, solar panels, or antenna arrays where exhaustive visual coverage is needed but pre-planned routes are inefficient.
4. **Autonomous rendezvous and proximity operations** — Training spacecraft swarms to safely approach and image objects in close orbit.
5. **Planetary lander surveys** — Groups of small rovers or drones inspecting landing sites or geological features with adaptive, learned inspection strategies.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Sim-to-real gap** — The paper validates in simulation using 3D reconstructions. Real orbital imagery suffers from lighting, occlusions, thermal noise, and unmodeled sensor dynamics that simulations may not capture.
2. **Scalability** — Multi-agent coordination complexity grows with swarm size. No discussion of how the approach scales beyond a handful of agents or how communication delays affect learning.
3. **Computational overhead** — Computing 3D reconstructions and evaluating reward signals for every agent action may be expensive. No runtime or sample efficiency benchmarks provided.
4. **Generalization** — Unclear how well policies trained on one target geometry transfer to different spacecraft shapes or orientations.
5. **Safety and constraints** — No explicit collision avoidance, fuel/battery constraints, or failure modes during training. Real missions have hard safety requirements that RL agents may violate before learning not to.
6. **Reward function design** — Still requires domain knowledge to define what image quality metrics (coverage, sharpness, geometric coverage) matter most. Not fully automated.

## Open Questions
1. How does policy performance degrade when deployed on real spacecraft with sensor noise, actuator uncertainty, and communication delays?
2. Can the learned inspection policies transfer across different target geometries with minimal retraining, or does each new satellite require from-scratch learning?
3. How do you incorporate safety constraints (no collisions, fuel limits) into the reward without hand-crafting penalty terms?
4. What is the sample efficiency cost of the generalized reward function compared to simpler waypoint-based approaches?
5. Do multi-agent learned policies exhibit coordinated behavior (e.g., explicit role assignment) or just emergent cooperation, and can you interpret what they've learned?
6. How much computational overhead does the 3D reconstruction validation add, and can it be approximated for faster training?

## Key Takeaway
Grounding MARL reward functions in physically meaningful outcomes (3D reconstruction quality) rather than geometric waypoints produces more flexible and generalizable spacecraft inspection policies.
