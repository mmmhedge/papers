# Governing Generative AI Across Financial Institutions: An SR 26-2-Compatible Framework for Generative AI Risk Control

**Authors:** Yiqing Wang, Yixin Kang, Luyun Lin, Siqi Mao
**Published:** 2026-07-05 | **arXiv:** [2607.04103v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.04103v1)

## Summary
This paper proposes a governance framework (GAICF) for using generative AI in regulated financial institutions, designed to comply with the U.S. Federal Reserve's new SR 26-2 supervisory guidance on model risk management. The work addresses a critical gap: while SR 26-2 modernizes how banks must manage AI models, it explicitly excludes generative AI—yet these tools (like LLMs) are increasingly embedded in banking workflows where their outputs influence credit decisions, compliance documentation, and regulatory explanations. The paper matters because it provides financial institutions with a practical roadmap to govern generative AI use while staying aligned with regulatory expectations.

## Theoretical Background
This work builds on two intersecting bodies of knowledge. First, U.S. banking regulation: SR 11-7 (the 2011 supervisory guidance on model risk management) established principles for controlling quantitative models used in lending and risk assessment. SR 26-2 (2026) modernizes this framework with a risk-based approach that ties control intensity to a model's materiality—how much it affects regulated decisions. Second, generative AI governance: the paper recognizes that large language models (neural networks trained to generate human-like text) create novel control challenges because they operate probabilistically, produce variable outputs, and are often used for tasks (like drafting compliance language or interpreting monitoring reports) that sit in a grey zone—not formal decision-making models, but consequential to how decisions are explained and challenged.

## Core Contributions
1. **Explicit acknowledgment of the SR 26-2 gap**: Demonstrates that generative AI is excluded from the new regulatory framework despite being embedded in banking workflows, creating genuine governance uncertainty.
2. **GAICF layered control structure**: Proposes a multi-level control architecture that translates traditional model risk management principles (governance, validation, monitoring, documentation) into practical controls for generative AI systems that fall outside formal model scope.
3. **Materiality-sensitive approach**: Extends SR 26-2's risk-based philosophy to generative AI by tying control rigor to how much an AI system's outputs affect downstream regulated decisions (e.g., LLM-assisted credit analysis vs. routine summarization).
4. **Workflow-embedded governance**: Provides concrete guidance on how to govern generative AI in realistic banking processes (e.g., when an LLM helps draft adverse-action notices, when it interprets regulatory reports) rather than in isolation.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper sits at the intersection of [[Alignment]] (ensuring AI systems behave as intended in high-stakes contexts), [[Risk-Management]] governance, and regulatory AI ethics. It implicitly builds on conversations about [[Mechanistic-Interpretability]] (understanding why LLMs produce specific outputs) and [[Generative-Models]] safety. The work is also adjacent to emerging frameworks in AI governance (e.g., NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act) but is uniquely tailored to U.S. banking regulation. It contrasts with purely technical interpretability research by showing how governance *processes* (not just model understanding) are necessary when AI affects regulated decisions.

## Practical Applications
1. **Credit risk assessment workflows**: Banks can now govern systems where LLMs help loan officers interpret credit files or draft risk summaries, with tiered controls based on materiality.
2. **Compliance and regulatory reporting**: Financial institutions can deploy LLMs to interpret regulatory requirements or draft compliance documentation while maintaining audit trails and validation standards.
3. **Model documentation and backtesting assistance**: LLMs can help finance teams generate model validation reports or interpret backtesting results, with controls ensuring accuracy and traceability.
4. **Monitoring and alert interpretation**: LLMs assisting risk teams in interpreting market data anomalies or stress-test results, with controls ensuring the interpretations don't drift from regulatory intent.
5. **Regulatory interaction and exam preparation**: Banks can use generative AI to draft responses to regulators or prepare for examinations, with frameworks ensuring truthfulness and governance visibility.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **Generative AI Governance-as-a-Service (GaaS)**: A SaaS platform that helps mid-sized and regional banks implement GAICF-aligned controls for their generative AI systems, including audit logging, control testing, and SR 26-2 compliance dashboards.
2. **LLM Risk Validation Toolkit**: Software that automates testing and documentation of generative AI systems used in banking, including prompt testing, output bias detection, and control evidence generation for regulators.
3. **Regulatory Compliance Audit Automation**: A consulting service (or software product) that maps a bank's existing generative AI workflows to GAICF requirements, identifies control gaps, and recommends governance improvements.
4. **Generative AI Model Registry for Finance**: A specialized platform where banks can register, document, and govern all their generative AI applications in one place, with built-in SR 26-2 compliance templates and controls.
5. **Banking-Specific LLM Fine-Tuning & Validation**: A service offering pre-fine-tuned, validated generative AI models specifically for banking use cases (credit analysis, compliance drafting, etc.) with built-in governance and audit trails.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Vagueness on "materiality"**: The paper does not provide quantitative or algorithmic definitions of when generative AI outputs are "material" enough to require stricter controls, leaving judgment calls to institutions.
2. **LLM output variability**: The framework assumes controls can govern stochastic, variable outputs from large language models, but does not deeply address how to validate or monitor probabilistic systems in a deterministic regulatory environment.
3. **Explainability remains hard**: The paper does not solve the core technical problem of why LLMs produce specific outputs (mechanistic interpretability is unresolved), limiting how thoroughly institutions can validate or audit AI decisions.
4. **Scope creep**: Generative AI is moving faster than regulation—systems may be deployed in novel ways (e.g., multi-agent workflows) not anticipated by GAICF.
5. **Calibration to institutions**: The framework is generic; real financial institutions will need substantial customization, and the paper does not provide guidance on how different institution sizes or risk appetites should adapt GAICF.

## Open Questions
1. How should institutions quantitatively measure when generative AI outputs are "material" to regulated decisions? Are there empirical thresholds or case studies?
2. What does effective monitoring of generative AI systems look like in practice? How often should outputs be sampled, tested for drift, or re-validated?
3. How should GAICF evolve if generative AI systems become truly agentic—i.e., making chains of decisions with minimal human oversight?
4. Are there specific failure modes (e.g., hallucination in regulatory reporting, bias in credit analysis) that GAICF should address with dedicated controls?
5. How does GAICF interact with data governance? If an LLM is trained on sensitive financial or customer data, what additional controls apply?
6. Should regulators provide clearer guidance on what constitutes acceptable validation evidence for generative AI in banking?

## Key Takeaway
Generative AI in banking needs governance that sits between full model risk management (for direct decision-making) and no governance (for general tools), and GAICF provides a practical regulatory-aligned framework for that middle ground.
