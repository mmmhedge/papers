# STEP: Career-Path Recommendation via Temporal and Educational Trajectory Modeling

**Authors:** Iman Johary, Guillaume Bied, Alexandru C. Mara, Tijl De Bie
**Published:** 2026-07-13 | **arXiv:** [2607.11722v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11722v1)

## Summary
This paper presents STEP, a system that predicts a person's next job by analyzing their career trajectory from resume data. It combines language models to extract structured career information from unstructured resumes with neural sequence models that learn temporal and educational patterns. The work enables large-scale career recommendation and labor market analysis, which matters for workforce planning, policy-making, and job-matching platforms.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on sequence modeling with recurrent neural networks (RNNs — networks that process data in sequence, maintaining memory of past inputs), specifically Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs — a type of RNN that selectively updates its memory). It leverages recent advances in large language models (LLMs — neural networks trained on vast text to understand and generate language) to extract structured data from unstructured text. The paper also employs Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM — a technique that conditions network behavior on additional information by scaling features), attention mechanisms (allowing models to focus on relevant parts of input), and contrastive learning (training where similar examples are pushed together and dissimilar ones pushed apart in representation space). Prior work on job recommendation and skill-based labor market analysis provides context, though most prior systems relied on hand-crafted features rather than end-to-end learning from raw resumes.

## Core Contributions
1. **STEP system**: A novel architecture combining time-decay GRU cells (modeling how older career events matter less), FiLM conditioning on education level, and attention-based pooling—specifically designed for next-job prediction with temporal and educational context.

2. **ROUTE procedure**: A two-stage unsupervised-then-supervised approach to improve occupation embeddings: (1) domain-adaptive denoising autoencoding on resumes, then (2) contrastive fine-tuning with guided negative selection (choosing hard negatives strategically).

3. **JobHop dataset**: An improved, publicly available benchmark for career trajectory research with multilingual resume data.

4. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance over baselines on four career trajectory datasets.

## Connections to Other Work
STEP relates to [[Attention Is All You Need]] (transformers) and recent work on [[RLHF]] in that it uses neural sequence models for practical prediction. It extends general sequence-to-sequence modeling into the labor domain. The contrastive learning component aligns with self-supervised pretraining methods common in NLP. The work also touches on representation learning — learning meaningful embeddings for occupations — which connects to broader literature on entity embeddings and domain adaptation in neural NLP systems.

## Practical Applications
1. **Job matching platforms** (LinkedIn, Indeed, Glassdoor): Recommend next-role candidates to users based on their trajectory, improving placement rates.

2. **Career coaching & mentoring**: Provide personalized next-step suggestions to workers, highlighting common paths and skill gaps.

3. **Workforce planning**: Help organizations forecast talent pipelines and succession planning by understanding typical role transitions in their industry.

4. **Labor market policy**: Governments use aggregate trajectory insights to design retraining programs and education policy aligned with job-market demand.

5. **Recruiting**: Help recruiters identify candidates with plausible trajectories to target roles, reducing bad hires.

## Product & Startup Ideas
1. **CareerPath.AI**: Freemium SaaS offering personalized next-job predictions and upskilling recommendations for individual workers, with premium features for employers.

2. **TalentFlow**: B2B platform selling workforce intelligence to HR departments—aggregate, anonymized trajectory analysis to forecast talent gaps and guide hiring.

3. **SkillBridge**: Career advisory chatbot powered by STEP, integrating job market data to guide mid-career transitions and retraining.

4. **OccupationMap**: Interactive labor market explorer for policymakers and educators showing skill-to-job-to-skill networks, powered by underlying trajectory models.

5. **RecruiterAssist**: Chrome extension helping recruiters source candidates by predicting who is likely to move into a target role next based on resume similarity.

## Technical Limitations
- **Resume bias**: Career trajectories in resume databases skew toward employed, formally educated, and digitally-literate populations; informal or gig work is underrepresented.
- **Data quality**: Resumes are noisy, incomplete, and sometimes fabricated; the LLM extraction step may propagate errors.
- **Multilingual challenges**: Despite claims of multilingual support, performance variance across languages is not thoroughly analyzed.
- **Temporal granularity**: The model works at job-level, not day-level; gaps, sabbaticals, and parallel roles are likely flattened or lost.
- **Cold-start problem**: New occupations or unusual role transitions may be rare in training data, limiting generalization.
- **Causality**: The model predicts correlations in trajectories, not causal drivers; it cannot distinguish between skills that cause role change vs. those acquired after.

## Open Questions
1. How does the model perform on out-of-distribution trajectories (e.g., career changers, people with atypical paths)?
2. Can the system generalize across different geographic labor markets, industries, or demographic groups without retraining?
3. What is the contribution of each component (time-decay, FiLM, attention) to overall performance? Are all necessary?
4. How sensitive is ROUTE to the unsupervised pretraining step? Could simpler pretrained embeddings (e.g., fastText, pretrained language model) suffice?
5. Can the system explain *why* it predicts a given next job (interpretability), crucial for user trust in career advice?
6. How do predictions change if you condition on external signals (e.g., market demand, salary trends, recession)?

## Key Takeaway
Large language models extract career patterns from resumes to predict workers' next jobs with temporal and educational context.
