# Efficient and Robust Spiking Neural Networks for sEMG-Based Muscle Fatigue Detection

**Authors:** Kaiwen Tang, Jiaqi Dong, Zhanglu Yan, Weng-Fai Wong
**Published:** 2026-07-13 | **arXiv:** [2607.11065v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11065v1)

## Summary
This paper develops Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs)—a brain-inspired neural architecture that processes information as sparse electrical spikes—to detect muscle fatigue from surface electromyography (sEMG) signals in wearable devices. The key innovation is a quantization-compatible training scheme that makes these networks robust to real-world noise while consuming 200x less energy than standard deep learning models, making them practical for always-on health monitoring.

## Theoretical Background
Spiking Neural Networks mimic biological neurons by firing discrete spikes only when their activation exceeds a threshold, unlike standard neural networks that compute continuous values at every step. This event-driven computation is inherently energy-efficient and naturally suited to temporal signal processing. Surface electromyography (sEMG) measures electrical activity in muscles and contains signatures of fatigue—gradual performance decline due to sustained activity. Traditional deep learning models applied to sEMG detection tend to be power-hungry and require large labeled datasets, making them impractical for embedded wearables. The paper builds on prior work in neuromorphic computing and low-power inference, adding a training scheme (SDH) that combines regularization terms to maintain accuracy when models are quantized (compressed to lower numerical precision for faster inference).

## Core Contributions
1. **Energy-efficient SNN framework**: Demonstrates that SNNs can match or exceed conventional deep learning baselines on sEMG fatigue detection while reducing energy by up to 201.77x—a dramatic efficiency gain for wearables.
2. **Quantization-compatible training scheme (SDH)**: Introduces a regularization-based training method that preserves model robustness when networks are quantized, addressing the practical challenge of deploying compressed models on resource-constrained hardware.
3. **Comprehensive noise robustness evaluation**: Tests the framework under seven noise conditions including physically motivated perturbations (not just synthetic noise), showing SNNs remain more stable than baselines—critical for real-world field deployment.

## Connections to Other Work
This work sits at the intersection of neuromorphic computing and biomedical signal processing. It relates to broader efforts in [[efficient neural networks]] for edge AI and [[low-power inference]], which aim to move computation from data centers to devices. ## Practical Applications
1. **Real-time wearable fatigue monitoring**: Smartwatches or armbands that detect muscle fatigue during workouts without draining battery, enabling immediate feedback to users.
2. **Rehabilitation systems**: Physical therapy clinics using wearable sEMG sensors to objectively track patient recovery and adjust exercise intensity dynamically.
3. **Occupational safety**: Factory workers or athletes monitored for cumulative fatigue to prevent injury-prone decisions in real time.
4. **Sports performance optimization**: Training systems that detect optimal exertion plateaus and suggest rest intervals to maximize gains while minimizing overtraining injury risk.

## Technical Limitations
- **sEMG signal variability**: Muscle physiology differs across individuals and changes with hydration, temperature, and electrode placement; the framework's generalization to unseen populations is unclear.
- **Noise model assumptions**: The seven noise conditions tested may not cover all real-world interference (e.g., EMI from industrial equipment, motion artifacts during extreme conditions). Purely synthetic perturbations may not capture true field behavior.
- **Limited dataset scale**: The paper evaluates on two public datasets; unclear if the approach scales to larger, more diverse populations or if it suffers from dataset-specific overfitting.
- **Fatigue definition ambiguity**: Different studies define fatigue biomechanically or perceptually; the ground truth labels in the datasets may conflate different fatigue mechanisms, limiting interpretability.
- **Hardware deployment gap**: Energy estimates are theoretical (from computational FLOPs); actual silicon implementation on specific microcontrollers (e.g., ARM Cortex-M4) may see overhead losses not captured by the model.

## Open Questions
1. How does SNN performance degrade when sEMG electrode placement varies across users? Does the framework need per-user calibration?
2. Can the quantization scheme (SDH) be pushed to even lower bit-widths (e.g., 2–4 bits) without accuracy loss, for truly ultra-low-power edge devices?
3. Does the framework generalize across muscle groups (e.g., trained on biceps, tested on quadriceps) or is fatigue signature too muscle-specific?
4. How sensitive are SNNs to changes in sEMG sampling rate or sensor hardware? Can the same trained model work across different wearable devices?
5. Could the learned SNN representations be used for other muscle-related tasks (e.g., gesture recognition, force estimation) in a transfer-learning setup?

## Key Takeaway
Spiking neural networks detect muscle fatigue in wearables with 200x less power than standard AI, enabling truly battery-efficient health monitoring.
