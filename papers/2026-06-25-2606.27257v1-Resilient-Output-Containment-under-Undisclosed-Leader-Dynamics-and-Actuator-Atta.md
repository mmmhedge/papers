# Resilient Output Containment under Undisclosed Leader Dynamics and Actuator Attacks

**Authors:** Mohammadreza Nematollahi, Khashayar Khorasani, Nader Meskin
**Published:** 2026-06-25 | **arXiv:** [2606.27257v1](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27257v1)

## Summary
This paper addresses resilient output containment for heterogeneous multi-agent systems where followers must track leaders whose dynamics and motion constraints are unknown, while the system faces actuator cyber-attacks. The authors propose a two-layer adaptive control architecture combining virtual-actuator reconfiguration for attack mitigation with a network interface protocol that achieves asymptotic containment without requiring global graph knowledge or knowledge of leader dynamics.

## Theoretical Background
The work builds on classical multi-agent consensus and containment control theory, extending it to handle:
- **Heterogeneous linear systems** with potentially different dynamics across agents
- **Directed network topologies** requiring leader-rooted spanning-tree conditions for stability
- **Lyapunov stability analysis**, specifically nonsmooth variants to handle discontinuities introduced by adaptive control and attacks
- **Resilient control** against actuator faults, extending prior work on Byzantine-robust consensus and attack-tolerant multi-agent systems
- **Unknown leader dynamics**, relating to problems in adaptive cooperative control where follower learning must compensate for unmodeled external agents

## Core Contributions
1. **Unknown leader dynamics framework**: Develops containment control where followers achieve bounded convergence to the leader convex hull without knowing leader trajectories, velocity bounds, or motion envelopes—only observing their outputs.
2. **Correlated actuator attack model**: Introduces a more realistic attack model combining state-correlated, input-correlated, and bounded exogenous false-data injection simultaneously.
3. **Two-layer adaptive architecture**: First layer reconfigures virtual actuators using only partial state measurements; second layer implements a lightweight network protocol whose parameters don't depend on global graph structure.
4. **Nonsmooth Lyapunov analysis for containment**: Provides asymptotic stability guarantees at the command level with explicit residual bounds tracking back to physical outputs.
5. **Scalability without global knowledge**: The network interface protocol operates with dimensionality matching plant outputs, avoiding the curse of dimensionality common in consensus protocols.

## Connections to Other Work
This paper sits at the intersection of:
- **[[Consensus and Containment Control]]**: Extends classical distributed control by handling unknown external agents and attacks simultaneously
- **[[Resilient Multi-Agent Systems]]**: Related to Byzantine-resilient consensus and attack-robust cooperative control, but novel in handling correlated attack models
- **[[Adaptive Control]]**: Uses adaptive protocol design similar to parameter-update rules in [[Reinforcement Learning|RL]] but in a distributed continuous-time setting
- **[[Cyber-Physical Systems Security]]**: Addresses actuator false-data injection, complementing prior work on sensor attacks and information-theoretic attack detection
- **[[Directed Graph Multi-Agent Control]]**: Builds on recent advances in stability analysis over directed topologies, requiring structural conditions like leader-rooted spanning trees

## Practical Applications
1. **Autonomous vehicle platoons**: Vehicles follow human drivers (unknown dynamics) while defending against sensor-spoofing or actuator hijacking attacks in urban/highway scenarios.
2. **Drone swarm operations**: Multiple UAVs maintain formation around unpredictable mobile targets while resisting GPS spoofing or actuator malfunction.
3. **Industrial process control networks**: Distributed chemical plant or power grid controllers track setpoints that change unpredictably while under adversarial cyber-attacks.
4. **Hospital equipment coordination**: Medical robots follow clinician-controlled (unknown) trajectories while maintaining safety despite potential hacking of motor commands.
5. **Smart grid frequency regulation**: Distributed generators track load-following requirements (undisclosed) while defending against false control signals from compromised communication channels.

## Technical Limitations
1. **Output-level convergence only**: Followers converge to leader convex hull up to a residual determined by local tracking controllers; perfect synchronization of *states* is not guaranteed, only outputs.
2. **Bounded trajectory assumption**: Leaders must generate locally absolutely continuous (bounded acceleration) trajectories; erratic or discontinuous leader motion may violate assumptions.
3. **Partial observability requirement**: Virtual-actuator reconfiguration requires access to partial state measurements; purely output-feedback scenarios are not addressed.
4. **Directed spanning-tree condition**: Network must have a leader-rooted united spanning tree; failures breaking this condition make the approach inapplicable (no graceful degradation analysis).
5. **Actuator-attack focus**: The attack model assumes actuators are the attack surface; sensor spoofing or communication delays/denials are not modeled and could destabilize the scheme.
6. **Heterogeneity limitations**: Works for heterogeneous *linear* systems; nonlinear followers or leaders would require extension.
7. **Parameter tuning guidance absent**: While the protocol avoids global graph knowledge, the paper provides limited practical guidance on choosing adaptive gain rates for real systems.

## Open Questions
1. **What happens under partial leader information loss?** Can the scheme tolerate cases where some followers lose the ability to observe leader outputs, and how do connectivity constraints translate to performance bounds?
2. **Can nonlinear leaders be handled?** Extension to Lipschitz-continuous leader dynamics with unknown bounds would broaden applicability.
3. **How does communication delay affect stability?** Real networks have quantization and transmission delays; what are the maximum tolerable delays before containment breaks?
4. **Can attack detection and localization be integrated?** Rather than just recovery, can followers collaboratively identify *which* agents are under attack?
5. **How robust is the scheme to attacks on the network protocol itself** (rather than just actuators)? Can adversaries corrupt the neighbor-exchanged interface states to cause containment failure?
6. **What is the optimal trade-off between attack recovery speed and tracking accuracy?** Is there a principled framework for tuning this trade-off?
7. **Can the framework be extended to time-varying or switching leader groups**? Many real scenarios involve multiple dynamic targets.

## Key Takeaway
A two-layer adaptive control architecture can achieve resilient containment of heterogeneous multi-agent systems around unknown leaders under sophisticated correlated actuator attacks, using only local neighbor communication without global network knowledge, provided the network maintains a leader-rooted spanning tree.
